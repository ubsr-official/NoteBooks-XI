# CHAPTER 7 — GLOSSARY & TERMINOLOGY

### Gravitation

---

> All terms arranged **alphabetically** with precise scientific definitions, dimensional formulae where applicable, and exam relevance tags.

## A

**Aphelion**
The point in a planet's elliptical orbit that is **farthest** from the Sun. At the aphelion, the planet is at maximum distance rA from the Sun and moves with minimum orbital speed vA. By conservation of angular momentum: rp·vp = rA·vA. For Earth, the aphelion distance ≈ 1.52 × 10¹¹ m (reached around July 4). *(All levels — critical)*

**Apogee**
The point in a satellite's orbit around Earth that is **farthest** from Earth's centre. The satellite's speed is minimum here. The term 'aphelion' is used for orbits around the Sun; 'apogee' is used for orbits around Earth. *(Board/NEET)*

---

## B

**Binding Energy (Satellite)**
The minimum energy required to remove a satellite from its orbit to infinity (i.e., to unbind it from Earth). For a circular orbit at radius r = RE + h:

Binding Energy = |E| = GMEm / 2(RE + h)

This equals the magnitude of the total mechanical energy of the satellite. A negative total energy implies a bound system. SI unit: J. *(NEET/JEE)*

---

## C

**Cavendish Experiment**
The first experimental determination of the gravitational constant G, performed by Henry Cavendish in 1798. Uses a **torsion balance**: two small lead spheres on a bar suspended by a fine wire; two large spheres brought close create a gravitational torque that twists the wire. By measuring the twist angle θ and the known restoring couple τ per unit angle, G is calculated from G = τθd²/MmL. This experiment effectively "weighed the Earth" by allowing ME to be calculated. *(Board/NEET)*

**Central Force**
A force that is always directed along the line joining the two interacting objects (along **r**). For a central force, the torque about the source = 0, so angular momentum is conserved. Gravity is a central force; this is why Kepler's second law (equal areas) holds. Any central force (not just gravity) obeys the equal-area law. *(All levels — critical)*

**Centre of Gravity (CG)**
The point at which the entire weight of a body can be assumed to act. In a **uniform gravitational field**, CG coincides with the **Centre of Mass (COM)**. For very large bodies where g varies from point to point (non-uniform g), CG ≠ COM. *(Board — conceptual)*

**Circular Orbit**
A special (limiting) case of an elliptical orbit in which the eccentricity is zero and the two foci coincide. For a circular orbit at distance r from Earth's centre: orbital speed V = √(GME/r), time period T = 2πr/V. *(All levels — critical)*

**Copernican Model (Heliocentric)**
The model of the solar system in which the **Sun is at the centre** and the planets revolve around it. First definitively proposed by Nicolas Copernicus (1473–1543). Superseded Ptolemy's geocentric model. Copernicus assumed circular orbits; Kepler later corrected these to ellipses. *(Board — historical)*

---

## E

**Eccentricity (e)**
A measure of how much an ellipse deviates from a circle. e = 0 for a circle; 0 < e < 1 for an ellipse; e = 1 for a parabola. For Earth's orbit e ≈ 0.0167 (nearly circular). Defined as e = (rA − rP)/(rA + rP) = distance between foci / major axis length. *(JEE)*

**Ellipse**
A closed curve for which the sum of distances from any point on the curve to the two foci (F₁ and F₂) is constant: TF₁ + TF₂ = 2a (where a is the semi-major axis). Kepler's first law states all planetary orbits are ellipses. A circle is a special ellipse with F₁ = F₂. *(All levels)*

**Escape Speed (vₑ)**
The minimum speed that must be given to an object at the surface of a planet (or other celestial body) so that it can escape to infinity against the planet's gravitational pull, never to return. Derived from energy conservation (KE + PE = 0 at minimum):

vₑ = √(2GME/RE) = √(2g₀RE) ≈ **11.2 km s⁻¹** for Earth.

Key properties: independent of the mass and the direction of projection; depends on the planet's mass and radius. SI unit: m s⁻¹. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## F

**Free Fall**
Motion of a body under the influence of gravity alone, with no other forces acting. In free fall, every part of the body has the same acceleration (g) → no internal stresses → the body is **weightless**. Orbiting satellites and their occupants are in perpetual free fall (falling toward Earth but also moving sideways fast enough to keep missing it). *(All levels — critical)*

**Focus (Foci)**
One of the two fixed points that define an ellipse. In Kepler's first law, the Sun is at one focus of each planet's elliptical orbit. The other focus is empty (no celestial body there). For a circle, both foci coincide at the centre. *(All levels)*

---

## G

**Geocentric Model**
The ancient model of the solar system proposed by Ptolemy (~150 AD) in which the **Earth is at the centre** and all other celestial objects (Sun, Moon, planets, stars) revolve around it. Required complex epicycles (circles within circles) to explain observed retrograde planetary motions. Eventually replaced by the heliocentric model. *(Board — historical)*

**Geostationary Satellite**
An Earth satellite in a circular equatorial orbit with a time period of exactly **24 hours** (same as Earth's rotation). To an observer on Earth, it appears to remain stationary above a fixed point on the equator. Orbit height ≈ 35,800 km above the equator. Used for communications and weather observation (e.g., INSAT series). *(Board/NEET)*

**Gravitational Constant (G)**
The universal constant of proportionality in Newton's law of gravitation: F = Gm₁m₂/r². Its value is:

G = **6.67 × 10⁻¹¹ N m² kg⁻²**

Dimensional formula: **[M⁻¹L³T⁻²]**. G is the same throughout the universe regardless of the nature of masses or the medium. First measured by Henry Cavendish (1798). *(All levels — critical)*

**Gravitational Field Intensity (g or E)**
The gravitational force per unit mass at a point in a gravitational field:

**g** = **F**/m = GM/r² (directed toward the mass creating the field)

It is a vector. At Earth's surface, g ≈ 9.8 m s⁻² directed toward Earth's centre. Dimensional formula: **[LT⁻²]**. *(All levels)*

**Gravitational Potential (U)**
The gravitational potential energy per unit mass at a point in a gravitational field:

U(r) = −GME/r (J kg⁻¹)

Taking V = 0 at infinity, U is always negative. The gravitational potential is a scalar. Dimensional formula: **[L²T⁻²]**. SI unit: J kg⁻¹. *(Board/NEET)*

**Gravitational Potential Energy (V or W)**
The work done by an external agent in bringing a mass m from infinity to a point at distance r from the source mass, with zero kinetic energy at both ends:

V(r) = −GMEm/r

This is always negative (V = 0 at r → ∞). The difference in V between two points gives the work done against gravity. Near Earth's surface (h ≪ RE), ΔV ≈ mgh. Dimensional formula: **[ML²T⁻²]**. SI unit: J. *(All levels — critical)*

**Gravity**
The attractive force of gravitation exerted by a large body (such as Earth) on smaller masses near its surface. Gravity at Earth's surface produces the acceleration g ≈ 9.8 m s⁻² (directed downward/toward centre). The word "gravity" is often used colloquially for the gravitational force, but technically gravitational force is the general phenomenon; gravity refers specifically to the effect of a large body's gravitational field. *(All levels)*

---

## H

**Heliocentric Model**
A model of the solar system in which the **Sun is at the centre** and the planets orbit around it. First mentioned by Aryabhatta; formally proposed by Copernicus (circular orbits); corrected to elliptical orbits by Kepler; explained physically by Newton. Now the accepted model. *(Board — historical)*

---

## I

**Inverse-Square Law**
The gravitational force between two masses varies as the inverse square of the distance between them: F ∝ 1/r². Newton deduced this from comparing g at Earth's surface with the Moon's centripetal acceleration (ratio = 3600 = (RM/RE)²). Many important physical forces (electrostatic, light intensity) also follow inverse-square laws. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## K

**Kepler's Laws**
Three empirical laws governing planetary motion, derived by Johannes Kepler (1571–1640) from Tycho Brahe's observations:
1. **Law of Orbits:** Planets move in elliptical orbits with the Sun at one focus.
2. **Law of Areas:** The radius vector sweeps equal areas in equal times (conservation of angular momentum).
3. **Law of Periods:** T² ∝ a³; equivalently T² = (4π²/GMS) × a³.

These laws are also applicable to satellite orbits (with Earth replacing the Sun). *(All levels — critical)*

---

## L

**Law of Periods**
See Kepler's Third Law. T² ∝ a³; the constant of proportionality is 4π²/GMS (same for all planets orbiting the same star). *(All levels)*

---

## N

**Natural Satellite**
A celestial body that orbits a planet under the influence of gravity. The Moon is Earth's only natural satellite, with an orbital period ≈ 27.3 days and a nearly circular orbit. *(Board)*

**Neutral Point**
The point between two massive bodies where the net gravitational force on a test mass is zero. For two masses M₁ and M₂ separated by distance d, the neutral point is at distance r from M₁ where:

GM₁/r² = GM₂/(d−r)² → r = d/(1 + √(M₂/M₁))

*(NEET/JEE)*

**Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation**
Every body in the universe attracts every other body with a force directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them:

F = Gm₁m₂/r²

This single law unifies terrestrial gravity (apple falling) and celestial mechanics (planetary orbits), and explains all of Kepler's laws. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## O

**Orbital Speed**
The speed required for a satellite to maintain a stable circular orbit at a given height h above Earth's surface:

V = √(GME/(RE + h))

For low Earth orbit (h ≈ 0): V₀ = √(gRE) ≈ **7.9 km s⁻¹** (also called the **first cosmic velocity**). Orbital speed decreases as height increases. SI unit: m s⁻¹. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## P

**Perihelion**
The point in a planet's elliptical orbit that is **closest** to the Sun. At perihelion, the planet is at minimum distance rp and moves with maximum orbital speed vp. For Earth, perihelion distance ≈ 1.47 × 10¹¹ m (reached around January 3). *(All levels — critical)*

**Perigee**
The point in a satellite's orbit around Earth that is **closest** to Earth's centre. The satellite moves fastest at perigee. Analogous to perihelion for orbits around the Sun. *(Board/NEET)*

**Principle of Superposition (Gravitation)**
The total gravitational force on a mass due to multiple other masses equals the **vector sum** of the individual gravitational forces:

**F**_total = **F**₁ + **F**₂ + … + **Fₙ**

Each force acts independently, uninfluenced by the presence of other masses. Similarly, total gravitational potential energy = algebraic sum of PE for all pairs. *(All levels — critical)*

**Ptolemaic Model**
See **Geocentric Model**. *(Board — historical)*

---

## R

**Radius of Orbit**
For a satellite in circular orbit: r = RE + h, where h is the height above Earth's surface and RE is Earth's radius. This is the distance from Earth's centre to the satellite. *(All levels)*

---

## S

**Semi-Major Axis (a)**
Half the length of the major axis of an ellipse. For a planetary orbit: a = (rp + rA)/2 where rp is the perihelion distance and rA is the aphelion distance. The semi-major axis is the key quantity in Kepler's third law (T² ∝ a³). For a circular orbit, a = radius of the circle. SI unit: m (or AU for astronomical distances). *(All levels — critical)*

**Shell Theorem**
Two important results for uniform spherical shells:
1. A uniform spherical shell attracts an external point mass as if all its mass were concentrated at its centre.
2. A uniform spherical shell exerts **zero** gravitational force on a point mass located anywhere inside it.

These results allow the Earth (treated as a set of concentric shells) to be treated as a point mass for external objects, and simplify the calculation of g at depth d. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## T

**Tidal Force**
A differential gravitational force: the difference in gravitational pull on the near and far sides of a body due to a massive object. Tidal force depends on the **gradient** of gravity (dF/dr ∝ 1/r³), not just F. Although the Sun exerts a larger gravitational force on Earth than the Moon, the Moon's tidal effect is greater because it is much closer (steeper gradient). *(Board — conceptual, Points to Ponder)*

**Time Period (Orbital)**
The time taken by a satellite to complete one full revolution around a central body:

T = 2π(RE + h)^(3/2) / √(GME)

For a low-Earth orbit (h ≈ 0): T₀ ≈ 85 minutes. For a geostationary orbit: T = 24 hours. T increases with orbital height. SI unit: s (or hours/days for large orbits). *(All levels — critical)*

---

## U

**Universal Gravitation**
The concept introduced by Newton that the same force of gravity that causes objects to fall on Earth also governs the motion of planets, moons, and all celestial objects. "Universal" means the law applies everywhere in the universe, for any two masses, regardless of their nature or the medium between them. *(All levels)*

---

## V

**Virial Theorem (Gravitational)**
For a bound gravitational system in a stable circular orbit:

KE = −E = −½ × PE

Where E is total mechanical energy. This means: |PE| = 2 × KE; Total energy E = −KE = PE/2. Always remember: for a satellite, **|PE| = 2KE** and **E is negative**. *(NEET/JEE)*

---

## W

**Weightlessness**
The condition in which an object appears to have no weight — it exerts no force on a support and a spring balance reads zero. This occurs when both the object and its support are in **free fall** (under gravity alone). An astronaut in an orbiting spacecraft is weightless because both the spacecraft and astronaut fall toward Earth together. Important: weightlessness does NOT mean absence of gravity. *(All levels — critical)*

## Common Misconceptions Clarified

| Misconception | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| Astronauts in space are weightless because there is no gravity | Gravity at orbital altitude is ~90% of surface value; weightlessness is due to free fall (both astronaut and spacecraft fall together) |
| Escape speed depends on the mass or direction of the projectile | Escape speed is independent of both; it depends only on the planet's mass and radius (and launch height) |
| A satellite in higher orbit moves faster | The opposite is true — higher orbit means lower orbital speed (V ∝ 1/√r) |
| Gravitational PE is zero at Earth's surface | By convention, GPE = 0 at infinity; GPE at any finite distance is negative |
| Supplying energy to a satellite always speeds it up | Supplying energy to raise orbit → satellite moves to higher orbit with LOWER speed (less KE but more PE) |
| The Moon causes weaker tides than the Sun because the Moon is smaller | Tidal force depends on gradient (dF/dr ∝ 1/r³), not just F; Moon is much closer → steeper gradient → stronger tides |
| g = 0 inside Earth | g = 0 only at Earth's centre; it varies linearly from g₀ (surface) to 0 (centre) |
| Kepler's 2nd law is unique to gravitation | Law of areas holds for ANY central force; it is equivalent to conservation of angular momentum |
