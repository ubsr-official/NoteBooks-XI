# CHAPTER 1 — GLOSSARY & TERMINOLOGY

### Units and Measurement

---

> All terms arranged **alphabetically** with precise scientific definitions, dimensional formulae where applicable, and exam relevance tags.

---

## A

**Accuracy**
The degree to which a measured value agrees with the **true or accepted value** of a quantity. A measurement can be precise but inaccurate (all readings cluster together but away from the true value). *(Board/NEET)*

**Ampere (A)**
The SI base unit of  **electric current** . Defined (since 2018) by fixing the elementary charge e = 1.602176634 × 10⁻¹⁹ C. Symbol is capitalised (A) because it is named after André-Marie Ampère.

**Angstrom (Å)**
A unit of length used to express atomic and molecular sizes.
1 Å = **10⁻¹⁰ m** = 0.1 nm. Not an SI unit but commonly used in spectroscopy. Example: hydrogen atom radius ≈ 0.5 Å.

**Arithmetic Mean (of measurements)**
The average of a set of repeated measurements of the same quantity. Used to reduce random errors. If measurements are a₁, a₂, ..., aₙ, then mean = Σaᵢ/n.

---

## B

**Barn (b)**
A unit of nuclear cross-sectional area.
1 barn = **10⁻²⁸ m²** = 100 fm². Used in nuclear and particle physics.

**Base Quantity**
A fundamental physical quantity that is defined independently of other quantities. The seven SI base quantities are:  **length, mass, time, electric current, thermodynamic temperature, amount of substance, and luminous intensity** . *(All levels)*

**Base Unit**
The SI unit corresponding to a base quantity. The seven base units are:  **metre, kilogram, second, ampere, kelvin, mole, candela** . All other SI units (derived units) can be expressed in terms of these seven.

**BIPM (Bureau International des Poids et Mesures)**
The International Bureau of Weights and Measures, located in Sèvres, France. Responsible for maintaining the international standards of measurement and defining the SI system. Established by the Metre Convention in 1875.

---

## C

**Candela (cd)**
The SI base unit of **luminous intensity** in a given direction. Defined by fixing the luminous efficacy of monochromatic radiation at frequency 540 × 10¹² Hz to be exactly 683 lm W⁻¹. *(Board)*

**CGS System**
A system of units based on the **centimetre (cm), gram (g), and second (s)** as the fundamental units of length, mass, and time respectively. Largely replaced by SI but still used in some areas of physics (e.g., electromagnetic units).

**CGPM (Conférence Générale des Poids et Mesures)**
The General Conference on Weights and Measures. An international body that meets periodically to update and maintain the SI system. The 11th CGPM in 1960 officially established the SI system; the 26th CGPM in 2018 approved the major redefinition of base units using fundamental constants.

---

## D

**Derived Quantity**
A physical quantity expressed in terms of **two or more base quantities** through algebraic relationships. Examples: velocity (length/time), force (mass × length/time²), density (mass/volume). *(All levels)*

**Derived Unit**
A unit obtained by combining base units mathematically. Examples: m s⁻¹ (velocity), N = kg m s⁻² (force), Pa = kg m⁻¹ s⁻² (pressure). Some derived units are given special names (joule, watt, newton). *(All levels)*

**Dimensional Analysis**
Also called the **factor label method** or  **unit factor method** . A mathematical technique using the principle of homogeneity to:

1. Check the correctness of physical equations
2. Derive relationships between physical quantities
3. Convert from one system of units to another. *(All levels — important)*

**Dimensional Equation**
An equation that expresses a physical quantity as a product of powers of the base quantity dimensions. Example: [F] = [M L T⁻²]. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Dimensional Formula**
The expression showing the powers to which the seven base quantities (M, L, T, A, K, mol, cd) are raised to represent a physical quantity. Written inside square brackets, e.g., [M L² T⁻²] for energy. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Dimensionless Quantity**
A physical quantity with no dimensions (all exponents are zero): [M⁰ L⁰ T⁰]. Examples: angle (ratio of arc to radius), refractive index, strain, relative density. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Dimensions**
The powers (exponents) to which base quantities [M], [L], [T], etc., are raised to represent a physical quantity. Describe the **nature** of a physical quantity, independent of the magnitude or system of units.

---

## E

**Error (Measurement)**
The difference between the measured value and the true value of a physical quantity. Types include systematic error (consistent bias), random error (unpredictable fluctuations), and gross error (blunders). *(Board/NEET)*

---

## F

**Factor Label Method**
See  *Dimensional Analysis* . Involves multiplying a measurement by unit factors (= 1) to convert between units while preserving the physical quantity. Example: 1 km h⁻¹ × (1000 m/1 km) × (1 h/3600 s) = 5/18 m s⁻¹. *(All levels)*

**Fermi (fm)**
A unit of length used in nuclear physics.
1 fermi = **10⁻¹⁵ m** = 1 femtometre. Named after physicist Enrico Fermi. Typical size of an atomic nucleus ≈ 1–10 fm.

**FPS System**
A system of units based on the **foot (ft), pound (lb), and second (s)** as fundamental units. Also called the British System. Largely obsolete for scientific work; partially used in everyday contexts in the UK and US.

**Fundamental Quantities**
See  *Base Quantities* .

---

## G

**Gram (g)**
The CGS unit of mass. Note: the SI unit of mass is the  **kilogram (kg)** , not the gram. 1 kg = 1000 g = 10³ g.

---

## H

**Homogeneity of Dimensions / Principle of Homogeneity**
The principle that in any physically valid equation,  **all terms must have the same dimensions** . Used as a test of dimensional consistency: if dimensions on both sides of an equation are equal, the equation may be correct; if different, it is definitely wrong. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## I

**International System of Units (SI)**
 *Système Internationale d'Unités* . The modern internationally agreed system of measurement based on  **7 base units** . Established in 1960 (11th CGPM) and most recently revised in November 2018 (26th CGPM) to define all base units using fundamental constants of nature.

---

## K

**Kelvin (K)**
The SI base unit of  **thermodynamic temperature** . Defined (since 2018) by fixing the Boltzmann constant k = 1.380649 × 10⁻²³ J K⁻¹. The Kelvin scale starts at **absolute zero** (0 K = −273.15°C). Temperature in Kelvin has no negative values.

**Kilogram (kg)**
The SI base unit of  **mass** . Previously defined by the International Prototype of the Kilogram (IPK), a physical artifact in Paris. Since 2018, defined by fixing Planck's constant h = 6.62607015 × 10⁻³⁴ J s. Symbol is **kg** (not capitalised, despite being named as a kilo-gram). *(Board/All)*

---

## L

**Least Count**
The smallest value that can be directly measured by a measuring instrument. Determines the **precision** of the instrument. A vernier calliper may have a least count of 0.01 cm; a screw gauge of 0.001 cm. *(Board/NEET)*

**Light Year (ly)**
The distance travelled by light in one year.
1 ly = c × 1 year = 3 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹ × 3.156 × 10⁷ s =  **9.46 × 10¹⁵ m** .
Used in astronomy. Not an SI unit.

---

## M

**Metre (m)**
The SI base unit of  **length** . Defined (since 1983, reaffirmed in 2018) by fixing the speed of light in vacuum c = 299,792,458 m s⁻¹ exactly. *(All levels)*

**MKS System**
A system of units based on the **metre (m), kilogram (kg), and second (s)** as fundamental units. The precursor to the modern SI system.

**Mole (mol)**
The SI base unit of  **amount of substance** . One mole contains exactly **6.02214076 × 10²³ elementary entities** (Avogadro constant Nₐ). The entity must be specified (atoms, molecules, ions, etc.). *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

---

## N

**Nanometre (nm)**
1 nm = 10⁻⁹ m. Commonly used in spectroscopy (visible light: 400–700 nm) and nanotechnology.

---

## O

**Order of Magnitude**
The power of 10 when a number is expressed in scientific notation and the coefficient is rounded to 1 (for coefficient ≤ 5) or to 10 (for coefficient > 5).
Example: Earth's diameter = 1.28 × 10⁷ m → order of magnitude =  **7** . *(Board/NEET)*

---

## P

**Parsec (pc)**
A unit of astronomical distance used in astrophysics.
1 parsec = 3.086 × 10¹⁶ m ≈ 3.26 light years. Defined as the distance at which 1 AU subtends an angle of 1 arcsecond.

**Physical Quantity**
Any property of a physical system that can be  **measured** . Expressed as a product of a numerical value and a unit. Classified as: scalar (only magnitude) or vector (magnitude + direction). *(All levels)*

**Planck's Constant (h)**
h =  **6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J s = [M L² T⁻¹]** . The fundamental quantum of action. Now used to define the kilogram (SI 2018 revision). Has the same dimensional formula as angular momentum.

**Precision**
The closeness of a set of repeated measurements **to each other** (reproducibility). Does not imply closeness to the true value. A precise instrument has a smaller least count. *(Board/NEET)*

**Pressure**
Force per unit area. SI unit:  **pascal (Pa) = N m⁻²** . Dimensional formula:  **[ML⁻¹T⁻²]** . Same dimensional formula as stress, Young's modulus, and energy density.

**Principle of Homogeneity**
See  *Homogeneity of Dimensions* .

---

## R

**Radian (rad)**
The SI supplementary unit for  **plane angle** . Defined as the angle subtended at the centre of a circle by an arc equal in length to the radius.
θ (rad) = arc length / radius = L/L →  **dimensionless** . 1 full circle = 2π rad = 360°. *(Board)*

**Relative Error**
The ratio of the absolute error to the mean measured value, often expressed as a percentage.
Relative error = (ΔA / A_mean) × 100%. A smaller relative error indicates a more precise measurement. *(Board/NEET)*

**Rounding Off**
The process of reducing the number of significant figures in a calculated result to match the precision of the input measurements. Rules: if the dropped digit > 5 → round up; < 5 → leave; = 5 → round to even (banker's rounding). *(All levels)*

---

## S

**Scientific Notation**
Expressing a number in the form **N × 10ⁿ** where 1 ≤ N < 10. Used for very large or very small numbers, and to unambiguously convey the number of significant figures. *(All levels)*

**Second (s)**
The SI base unit of  **time** . Defined by fixing the caesium-133 ground-state hyperfine transition frequency Δν_Cs = 9,192,631,770 Hz. *(Board)*

**SI System**
See  *International System of Units* .

**Significant Figures**
All certain digits in a measurement  **plus the first uncertain (estimated) digit** . Indicate the precision of a measurement. Number of SFs does not change with a change of units. *(All levels — very important)*

**Solid Angle (steradian, sr)**
The three-dimensional angle subtended at the centre of a sphere by a surface area equal to the square of its radius.
dΩ = dA/r² → **dimensionless** (sr). SI supplementary unit. 1 full sphere = 4π sr. *(Board/JEE)*

**Speed of Light (c)**
c = **2.998 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹ ≈ 3 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹** in vacuum. Used to define the metre. Has dimensional formula [LT⁻¹].

**Steradian (sr)**
See  *Solid Angle* .

**Systematic Error**
An error that is consistent and predictable, affecting all measurements in the same way. Sources include: instrument calibration error, zero error, parallax error. Can be reduced (but not eliminated by averaging). *(Board/NEET)*

---

## T

**True Value**
The actual (theoretically exact) value of a physical quantity. In practice, it is estimated by taking the arithmetic mean of a large number of precise measurements.

---

## U

**Unified Atomic Mass Unit (u)**
1 u = 1/12 × mass of one ¹²C atom =  **1.66056 × 10⁻²⁷ kg** . Used to express masses of atoms and molecules. Same as 'amu' (atomic mass unit). Dimensional formula: [M].

**Unit**
An arbitrarily chosen, internationally agreed-upon **standard** against which a physical quantity is measured. Must be: well-defined, reproducible, widely accessible, and invariant with time and environment. *(All levels)*

**Unit Factor**
A ratio equal to **1** used in dimensional analysis to convert between units. Both numerator and denominator represent the same physical quantity in different units.
Example: (1 km / 1000 m) = 1; (1 h / 3600 s) = 1.

---

## V

**Vernier Calliper**
A precision measuring instrument with a least count of typically  **0.1 mm = 0.01 cm** . Uses a sliding vernier scale alongside the main scale to improve precision beyond the main scale's minimum division. *(Board/NEET — Measurement practical)*

---

## W

**Weight**
The **gravitational force** on an object: W = mg. SI unit:  **Newton (N)** . Dimensional formula: [MLT⁻²]. Not the same as mass — weight varies with location (gravitational field), mass does not.
