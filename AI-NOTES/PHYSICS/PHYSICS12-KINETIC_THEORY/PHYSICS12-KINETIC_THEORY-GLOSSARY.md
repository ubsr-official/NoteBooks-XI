# CHAPTER 12 — GLOSSARY & TERMINOLOGY

### Kinetic Theory

---

> All terms arranged **alphabetically** with precise scientific definitions, dimensional formulae where applicable, and exam relevance tags.

---

## A

**Angstrom (Å)**
A unit of length equal to **10⁻¹⁰ m**, commonly used to express atomic and molecular sizes. The diameter of a typical atom is ~1–3 Å. Interatomic distances in solids and liquids are a few Å; in gases, they are tens of Å. Not an SI unit (the SI unit is the nanometre: 1 nm = 10 Å). *(All levels)*

**Atomic Hypothesis**
The foundational idea, attributed historically to Kanada (India, ~600 BC), Democritus (Greece, ~400 BC), and scientifically formalised by John Dalton (~1800), that **all matter is made of atoms** — tiny indivisible particles in perpetual motion, attracting each other at short distances and repelling when squeezed together. Richard Feynman considered this the most important scientific discovery. *(All levels)*

**Avogadro's Number (N_A)**
The number of molecules (or atoms) in **one mole** of any substance:
$$N_A = 6.02 \times 10^{23} \text{ mol}^{-1}$$
Named after Amedeo Avogadro. Together with the Boltzmann constant: $R = N_A k_B$. One mole of any ideal gas at STP occupies 22.4 litres. *(All levels — critical)*

**Avogadro's Hypothesis (Law)**
*Equal volumes of all gases at the same temperature and pressure contain the same number of molecules.*
Derivable from kinetic theory (since PV = Nk_BT; if P, V, T are the same, N must be the same). This was originally proposed by Avogadro from chemical evidence and later justified by kinetic theory. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## B

**Boltzmann Constant (k_B)**
The fundamental constant that relates temperature to energy at the molecular level:
$$k_B = \frac{R}{N_A} = 1.38 \times 10^{-23} \text{ J K}^{-1}$$
Named after Ludwig Boltzmann. It appears in the ideal gas equation (P = nk_BT), in the kinetic interpretation of temperature (½mv² = (3/2)k_BT), and in the equipartition theorem (½k_BT per DOF). Dimensional formula: [ML²T⁻²K⁻¹]. *(All levels — critical)*

**Boyle's Law**
At **constant temperature** (and fixed amount of gas), the pressure of a gas is **inversely proportional to its volume**:
$$PV = \text{constant} \quad (T, \mu \text{ fixed})$$
Discovered by Robert Boyle (1661). Follows directly from PV = μRT when T is fixed. Deviations at high pressure or near liquefaction point. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## C

**Charles' Law**
At **constant pressure** (and fixed amount of gas), the volume of a gas is **directly proportional to its absolute temperature**:
$$\frac{V}{T} = \text{constant} \quad (P, \mu \text{ fixed})$$
Follows from PV = μRT when P is fixed. Must use temperature in Kelvin. *(All levels — critical)*

**Collision Frequency**
The number of collisions a molecule undergoes per unit time:
$$Z = \frac{1}{\tau} = \sqrt{2} \pi n d^2 \langle v \rangle$$
where n is the number density, d is the molecular diameter, and ⟨v⟩ is the mean speed. Inversely related to mean free path. Units: s⁻¹. *(NEET/JEE)*

---

## D

**Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures**
*The total pressure exerted by a mixture of non-reacting ideal gases is equal to the sum of the partial pressures of each individual gas:*
$$P_{total} = P_1 + P_2 + P_3 + \ldots$$
Each partial pressure P_i = μ_i RT/V is the pressure that gas i would exert if it alone occupied the entire volume at the same temperature. Follows from kinetic theory because molecules of different gases do not interact. *(All levels — critical)*

**Degree of Freedom (DOF)**
The **minimum number of independent coordinates** required to completely specify the position and configuration of a molecule or mechanical system. Each independent quadratic energy term in the total energy expression corresponds to one degree of freedom. Types:
- **Translational:** 3 for any free particle (x, y, z)
- **Rotational:** 2 for linear molecules (diatomic); 3 for non-linear polyatomic
- **Vibrational:** each mode contributes 2 DOF (KE + PE both quadratic)
*(All levels — critical for specific heat calculations)*

**Dulong-Petit Law**
The empirical (and theoretically derivable) law stating that the **molar specific heat of a solid at ordinary temperatures** is approximately:
$$C \approx 3R \approx 24.9 \text{ J mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$$
Derived from the equipartition theorem: each atom vibrates in 3D, giving 3 vibrational modes × k_BT = 3k_BT per atom → 3RT per mole. **Exception: Carbon (diamond)** has C = 6.1 J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹ at room temperature (quantum effects). Also fails for all materials at very low temperatures. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## E

**Elastic Collision**
A collision in which **total kinetic energy is conserved** (in addition to momentum). In kinetic theory, all molecular collisions (molecule-molecule and molecule-wall) are assumed to be perfectly elastic. This ensures that the total energy of the gas remains constant in equilibrium (no energy is lost to heat during collisions). *(All levels)*

**Equipartition of Energy**
See **Law of Equipartition of Energy.** *(All levels — critical)*

---

## G

**Gay Lussac's Law of Combining Volumes**
When gases combine chemically to form another gas (at the same temperature and pressure), their volumes are in the ratios of small integers. Explained by Avogadro's hypothesis combined with Dalton's atomic theory. *(Board level)*

---

## I

**Ideal Gas**
A gas that **satisfies the equation PV = μRT exactly at all pressures and temperatures**. An ideal gas is a theoretical model in which:
- Molecules are point particles (negligible size)
- No intermolecular forces except during elastic collisions
- All collisions are instantaneous and elastic
No real gas is truly ideal. Real gases approach ideal behaviour at low pressures and high temperatures. *(All levels — critical)*

**Ideal Gas Equation (Perfect Gas Equation)**
$$PV = \mu RT = Nk_BT = nk_BTV$$
where μ = moles, N = number of molecules, n = number density. Forms:
- P = ρRT/M₀ (where ρ = mass density, M₀ = molar mass)
- PV = NkT (using total molecules N)
Dimensional formula of R: [ML²T⁻²K⁻¹mol⁻¹]. *(All levels — critical)*

**Interatomic/Intermolecular Distance**
The average distance between adjacent atoms or molecules. In solids: ~2 Å; liquids: ~2–3 Å; gases: ~tens of Å. In gases, this is 10× greater than in solids — hence intermolecular forces negligible. Not to be confused with mean free path (which is ~100× the interatomic distance in a gas). *(All levels)*

**Incessant Random Motion**
The perpetual, disorganised, random movement of gas molecules at all temperatures above 0 K. All directions of motion are equally probable (isotropic distribution). The equilibrium state of a gas is a *dynamic* equilibrium — molecules are always in motion and constantly colliding, but average macroscopic properties remain constant. *(All levels)*

---

## K

**Kinetic Energy of a Gas Molecule**
The translational kinetic energy of a single molecule: ½mv². The average translational KE per molecule in a gas at temperature T is:
$$\bar{\varepsilon} = \frac{1}{2}m\overline{v^2} = \frac{3}{2}k_BT$$
This is **independent of the molecular mass and nature of the gas** — it depends only on temperature. *(All levels — critical)*

**Kinetic Theory of Gases**
The theoretical framework that explains the macroscopic properties of gases (pressure, temperature, specific heat, viscosity, diffusion) in terms of the **microscopic behaviour of molecules** — their masses, speeds, and collisions. Key assumptions: large number of molecules in random motion, negligible intermolecular forces between collisions, elastic collisions. Developed by Maxwell, Clausius, and Boltzmann in the 19th century. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## L

**Law of Equipartition of Energy**
*In thermal equilibrium at absolute temperature T, the total energy is equally distributed in all possible modes of energy absorption. Each mode (degree of freedom) that involves a quadratic term in the energy expression contributes ½k_BT to the average energy.*

Stated compactly:
- **Translational DOF:** average energy per DOF = ½k_BT
- **Rotational DOF:** average energy per DOF = ½k_BT
- **Vibrational mode:** average energy per mode = k_BT (2 quadratic terms: KE and PE)

First proved by Maxwell; the proof is beyond school syllabus. It is a theorem of classical statistical mechanics, valid at temperatures where quantum effects are negligible. *(All levels — critical)*

## M

**Molar Volume**
The volume occupied by **1 mole of any ideal gas at STP** (standard temperature = 273 K, standard pressure = 1 atm):
$$V_{molar} = 22.4 \text{ litres} = 22.4 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}^3$$
This value is universal — the same for all ideal gases regardless of their molecular mass. Follows directly from PV = μRT. *(All levels)*

**Mean Free Path (l)**
The **average distance a molecule travels between two successive collisions**:
$$l = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}\pi n d^2}$$
where n is the number density and d is the molecular diameter. Depends inversely on n and d². For air at STP: l ≈ 2.9 × 10⁻⁷ m ≈ 1500 molecular diameters. Mean free path >> interatomic distance >> molecular diameter in a gas. The large value of mean free path is what gives gases their characteristic behaviour. Dimensional formula: [L]. *(NEET/JEE — critical)*

**Mean Speed (⟨v⟩ or v̄)**
The arithmetic mean of the speeds of all molecules in a gas:
$$\langle v \rangle = \sqrt{\frac{8k_BT}{\pi m}} = \sqrt{\frac{8RT}{\pi M_0}}$$
Related to RMS speed: ⟨v⟩ = v_rms × √(8/3π) ≈ 0.921 v_rms. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Mean Square Speed (v̄²)**
The arithmetic mean of the squares of all molecular speeds: $\overline{v^2} = \frac{3k_BT}{m}$. Not the same as (mean speed)². The square root gives the RMS speed. Appears directly in the pressure formula: P = (1/3)nmv̄². *(All levels)*

**Molecular Mass (M₀)**
The mass of one mole of a substance, in grams or kg/mol. Equal to the relative molecular mass (dimensionless, in u/amu) numerically. For example: O₂ → M₀ = 32 g/mol = 0.032 kg/mol. *(All levels)*

**Most Probable Speed (v_p)**
The speed at which the Maxwell-Boltzmann speed distribution has its maximum — the most likely speed for a molecule chosen at random:
$$v_p = \sqrt{\frac{2k_BT}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{2RT}{M_0}}$$
Order of speeds: v_p < ⟨v⟩ < v_rms. *(NEET/JEE)*

---

## N

**Number Density (n)**
The number of molecules per unit volume:
$$n = \frac{N}{V}$$
At STP: n = 2.7 × 10²⁵ m⁻³ for any ideal gas. Appears in the pressure formula P = nk_BT and the mean free path formula. Dimensional formula: [L⁻³]. *(All levels)*

---

## P

**Partial Pressure**
The pressure that a component gas of a mixture would exert if it alone occupied the entire volume of the container at the same temperature. For gas i: P_i = μ_i RT/V = n_i k_B T. See **Dalton's Law**. *(All levels)*

**Paramanu**
The Sanskrit term from the Vaiseshika school of Indian philosophy (Kanada, ~600 BC) for the smallest, indivisible particle — conceptually equivalent to the atom. Four types were postulated: Bhoomi (earth), Ap (water), Tejas (fire), Vayu (air). The size was estimated to be of the order of 10⁻¹⁰ m — close to the modern value. *(Board level — historical)*

---

## R

**Random Motion**
See **Incessant Random Motion.** At any instant, molecules in a gas move in all directions with a **distribution of speeds** (Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution). No direction is preferred — the gas is isotropic. This randomness is why macroscopic properties are uniform despite individual molecular chaos. *(All levels)*

**Real Gas**
A gas that shows deviations from the ideal gas equation PV = μRT. Real gases deviate more at:
- **High pressures** (molecules close → forces significant)
- **Low temperatures** (near the liquefaction point; PE ≈ KE)
At low pressures and high temperatures, real gases approach ideal behaviour. Graphs of PV/T vs P for a real gas deviate from the ideal horizontal line, approaching it from below or above depending on the conditions. *(All levels)*

**Rigid Rotator**
A diatomic molecule model in which the bond length is fixed (no vibration). For a rigid rotator: degrees of freedom = 5 (3 translational + 2 rotational). This model works well for O₂ and N₂ at ordinary temperatures. At high temperatures, vibrational modes become significant and the rigid rotator model breaks down. *(NEET/JEE — critical)*

**Root Mean Square Speed (v_rms)**
The square root of the mean of the squares of all molecular speeds:
$$v_{rms} = \sqrt{\overline{v^2}} = \sqrt{\frac{3k_BT}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{3RT}{M_0}}$$
Connected to temperature: v_rms² = 3k_BT/m. At the same temperature, **lighter molecules have greater v_rms** (v_rms ∝ 1/√m). For N₂ at 300 K: v_rms ≈ 516 m/s. Dimensional formula: [LT⁻¹]. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## S

**Specific Heat Capacity at Constant Volume (Cᵥ)**
The molar heat needed to raise the temperature of one mole of gas by 1 K at **constant volume** (no work done → all heat goes to internal energy):
$$C_v = \frac{dU}{dT} = \frac{f}{2}R$$
where f = total degrees of freedom. Values: monoatomic = 3R/2; rigid diatomic = 5R/2. *(All levels — critical)*

**Specific Heat Capacity at Constant Pressure (Cₚ)**
The molar heat needed to raise the temperature of one mole of gas by 1 K at **constant pressure** (gas also does work):
$$C_p = C_v + R = \frac{f+2}{2}R$$
Always Cₚ > Cᵥ. Values: monoatomic = 5R/2; rigid diatomic = 7R/2. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## T

**Temperature (Kinetic Interpretation)**
Macroscopically, temperature T is a thermodynamic variable. Microscopically (kinetic theory), temperature is a **measure of the average translational kinetic energy per molecule**:
$$T = \frac{2}{3k_B} \times \bar{\varepsilon} \qquad \text{where } \bar{\varepsilon} = \frac{1}{2}m\overline{v^2}$$
This is independent of the nature of the gas. At 0 K (absolute zero), the average translational KE would be zero — molecules would be at rest (though quantum mechanics modifies this slightly). *(All levels — critical)*

**Translational Kinetic Energy (E)**
The total kinetic energy associated with the translational (centre-of-mass) motion of all molecules in the gas:
$$E = \frac{3}{2}Nk_BT = \frac{3}{2}\mu RT$$
Related to pressure and volume by: PV = (2/3)E. For an ideal gas, the internal energy equals the total translational KE (no intermolecular PE, no rotational/vibrational contributions in the simplest monoatomic case). *(All levels — critical)*

---

## V

**Vibrational Mode**
The oscillatory motion of atoms within a molecule along the interatomic axis (like a spring). Each vibrational mode has **both kinetic and potential energy** — two quadratic terms — so it contributes **k_BT** (not ½k_BT) to the average molecular energy. In diatomic molecules, vibrational modes are usually inactive at room temperature but become active at high temperatures. *(NEET/JEE — critical)*

## Common Misconceptions Clarified

| Misconception | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| "Temperature = total heat content of gas" | Temperature = **average translational KE per molecule**. A larger quantity of gas at lower T can have more total energy than a small quantity at high T. |
| "All molecules in a gas move at the same speed" | There is a **distribution of speeds** (Maxwell-Boltzmann). v_rms, ⟨v⟩, and v_p are all different. |
| "Heavier gas molecules are hotter at the same T" | At the same T, ALL gas molecules have the **same average KE** = (3/2)k_BT regardless of mass. Heavier molecules just move slower. |
| "v_rms = ⟨v⟩" | v_rms = √(3k_BT/m) > ⟨v⟩ = √(8k_BT/πm). The RMS is always greater than the mean. |
| "Ideal gas = real gas at any conditions" | Ideal gas is a theoretical model. Real gases approximate it only at **low P, high T**. |
| "Vibrational DOF contribute ½k_BT like others" | Vibrational modes contribute **k_BT** (double), because they have both KE and PE terms. |
| "C_p − C_v = R only for monoatomic gases" | C_p − C_v = R for **any** ideal gas — monoatomic, diatomic, or polyatomic. |
| "Mean free path increases with temperature at fixed P" | At fixed P: n = P/(k_BT) decreases as T increases, so l = 1/(√2 π n d²) **increases with T** at constant pressure. |
| "Dulong-Petit applies to all solids" | Fails for carbon (diamond), silicon, and at **low temperatures** for all materials (quantum effects). |
| "Gas molecules fall to the ground due to gravity" | They don't accumulate at the bottom because their KE >> gravitational PE for ordinary heights. There is a slight density gradient (barometric formula), but it is small for normal room heights. |
