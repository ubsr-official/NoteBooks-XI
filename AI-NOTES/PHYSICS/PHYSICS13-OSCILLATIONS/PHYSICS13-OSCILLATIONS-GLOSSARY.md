# CHAPTER 13 — GLOSSARY & TERMINOLOGY

### Oscillations

---

> All terms arranged **alphabetically** with precise scientific definitions, dimensional formulae where applicable, and exam relevance tags.

---

## A

**Acceleration in SHM**
The instantaneous acceleration of a particle undergoing SHM:
$$a(t) = -\omega^2 A \cos(\omega t + \phi) = -\omega^2 x(t)$$
Acceleration is **always directed towards the mean position** (hence the negative sign). Its magnitude varies between 0 (at mean position) and ω²A (at extreme positions). The defining property of SHM is that **a ∝ −x** (acceleration proportional to displacement, directed oppositely). Dimensional formula: [LT⁻²]. SI unit: m s⁻². *(All levels — critical)*

**Amplitude (A)**
The **maximum displacement** of a particle from its equilibrium (mean) position during SHM. It is always a positive quantity. The particle oscillates between +A and −A. The total mechanical energy of SHM is directly proportional to the square of amplitude: E = ½kA². Dimensional formula: [L]. SI unit: m. *(All levels — critical)*

**Angular Frequency (ω)**
A measure of how rapidly an oscillation occurs, defined as:
$$\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T} = 2\pi\nu \quad \text{(unit: rad s⁻¹)}$$
Also called the **angular velocity** by analogy with circular motion. For a spring-mass system: ω = √(k/m). For a simple pendulum: ω = √(g/L). The SI unit is radians per second. Dimensional formula: [T⁻¹]. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## D

**Damped Oscillation**
An oscillation in which the amplitude **decreases progressively** with time due to energy loss from dissipative forces (friction, air resistance, viscous drag). The total mechanical energy is not conserved. Mathematically described by an exponentially decaying amplitude: A(t) = A₀ e^(−bt/2m), where b is the damping coefficient. Damped SHM is approximately simple harmonic only for time intervals much less than 2m/b. *(JEE awareness)*

**Displacement (in SHM)**
In the context of oscillations, displacement refers to the **change in any physical property** from its equilibrium value. This is a broader meaning than the usual position displacement. Examples: linear position x for a spring-mass system, angular position θ for a pendulum, voltage for an AC capacitor, pressure variation for a sound wave. Displacement can take positive and negative values. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## E

**Equilibrium Position (Mean Position)**
The position at which the net force on the oscillating body is **zero**. In SHM, this is the **centre** of oscillation. If the body is displaced from this position, a restoring force acts to bring it back. The body has maximum kinetic energy and zero potential energy at the mean position. For a spring-mass system, it is where the spring is at its natural length (in horizontal SHM); for a pendulum, it is the lowest point of the arc. *(All levels — critical)*

**Extreme Position (Turning Point)**
The position at maximum displacement (x = ±A) in SHM. At the extreme positions:
- Velocity = 0 (instantaneously at rest)
- Acceleration = ω²A (maximum magnitude)
- KE = 0 (all energy is potential)
- PE = ½kA² (maximum)
The particle momentarily stops and reverses direction at the extreme positions. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## F

**Forced Oscillation**
An oscillation in which the system is driven by an **external periodic force** at a frequency that may differ from the system's natural frequency. The system oscillates at the frequency of the external force, not its own natural frequency. If the external frequency approaches the natural frequency, the amplitude increases dramatically — this is **resonance**. *(JEE awareness)*

**Fourier's Theorem**
A mathematical result proved by French mathematician **Jean Baptiste Joseph Fourier** (1768–1830): *Any periodic function can be expressed as a superposition of sine and cosine functions of different time periods with suitable coefficients.* This is why any complex oscillation (like the sound of a musical instrument) can be decomposed into simple sinusoidal components. Fundamental to signal processing and wave analysis. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Frequency (ν)**
The **number of complete oscillations** (cycles) per unit time:
$$\nu = \frac{1}{T} \quad \text{(unit: Hz)}$$
Named after Heinrich Rudolph Hertz (1857–1894). 1 Hz = 1 oscillation per second = 1 s⁻¹. Frequency is not necessarily an integer — it can take any positive real value. Dimensional formula: [T⁻¹]. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## H

**Hertz (Hz)**
The SI unit of **frequency**, named after Heinrich Rudolph Hertz, discoverer of radio waves. 1 Hz = 1 cycle per second = 1 s⁻¹. For very fast oscillations (quartz crystals), microseconds (µs) are used. For very slow ones (Halley's comet, orbital period 76 years), years or other long time units are used. *(All levels)*

**Hooke's Law**
The empirical law (also the force law for SHM) stating that the restoring force exerted by a spring is proportional to the extension or compression:
$$F = -kx$$
where k is the **spring constant** (or force constant) and x is the displacement from the natural length. This linear force law is the condition for SHM. Named after Robert Hooke. k has dimensions [MT⁻²] and SI unit N m⁻¹. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## I

**Isochronism**
The property that the **period of oscillation is independent of amplitude**. A simple pendulum (for small angles) and a spring-mass system both exhibit isochronism — they oscillate with the same period regardless of whether the amplitude is large or small (within the SHM approximation). This was first observed by Galileo for the pendulum. *(All levels)*

---

## K

**Kinetic Energy in SHM**
The kinetic energy of a particle in SHM at displacement x from the mean position:
$$K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \frac{1}{2}k(A^2 - x^2)$$
Or in terms of time:
$$K = \frac{1}{2}kA^2\sin^2(\omega t + \phi)$$
K is maximum (= ½kA²) at the mean position and zero at the extremes. The period of K is T/2. Dimensional formula: [ML²T⁻²]. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## L

**Linear Harmonic Oscillator**
A system whose restoring force is **exactly linearly proportional** to displacement (F = −kx). A mass on an ideal spring is the standard example. The SHM is called "linear" because the force law is linear in x. In contrast, if the force contains terms in x², x³, etc., the oscillator is **non-linear**. *(NEET/JEE)*

---

## M

**Mean Position**
See **Equilibrium Position**. *(All levels)*

---

## N

**Natural Frequency**
The **intrinsic frequency** at which a system oscillates when disturbed and left free, without any external driving force. For a spring-mass system: ν₀ = (1/2π)√(k/m). For a simple pendulum: ν₀ = (1/2π)√(g/L). When a forced oscillation is applied at the natural frequency, **resonance** occurs. *(NEET/JEE)*

---

## O

**Oscillatory Motion**
A type of **periodic motion** in which a body moves **to and fro** about a fixed mean (equilibrium) position. The body is repeatedly displaced and returns to the mean position under the action of a restoring force. A body at the mean position, when displaced slightly, has a restoring force pulling/pushing it back.

Key condition: every oscillatory motion is periodic, but not vice versa. Circular motion is periodic but NOT oscillatory (it does not involve to-and-fro motion about a mean position). *(All levels — critical)*

---

## P

**Period (T)**
The **smallest time interval** after which the motion exactly repeats. The "smallest" is important — a motion with period T also repeats at 2T, 3T, etc., but T is the fundamental (minimum) period. SI unit: second (s). Dimensional formula: [T]. Related to frequency by T = 1/ν and to angular frequency by T = 2π/ω. *(All levels — critical)*

**Periodic Motion**
Any motion that **repeats itself at regular intervals of time**. The time interval is called the period T. All oscillatory motions are periodic, but periodic motions need not be oscillatory. Examples of periodic but non-oscillatory motion: uniform circular motion, revolution of planets. *(All levels — critical)*

**Phase ((ωt + φ))**
The **time-dependent quantity** (ωt + φ) in the expression x(t) = A cos(ωt + φ). It completely determines the state of motion (position and velocity) of the particle at any time t. Two oscillations with the same A and ω but different phases will be at different positions at the same time. Dimensional formula: dimensionless (radians). *(All levels — critical)*

**Phase Constant (φ)**
The **initial phase** — the value of the phase at t = 0. It is determined by the initial conditions (initial displacement and velocity). Given the amplitude A, the phase constant φ can be found from x(0) = A cos φ. Also called the **phase angle**. Symbol: φ. Unit: radian (dimensionless). *(All levels — critical)*

**Potential Energy in SHM**
The elastic potential energy stored in the restoring force field:
$$U = \frac{1}{2}kx^2 = \frac{1}{2}kA^2\cos^2(\omega t + \phi)$$
U is zero at the mean position and maximum (= ½kA²) at the extreme positions. Period of U is T/2. The spring force F = −kx is conservative, so PE is well-defined. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## R

**Reference Circle**
An imaginary circle used to represent SHM geometrically. A particle P moves uniformly along this circle with angular speed ω (= angular frequency of SHM) and radius equal to amplitude A. The **projection** of P onto any diameter of this circle executes SHM. The reference circle helps visualise and derive velocity and acceleration in SHM. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Resonance**
A phenomenon that occurs in forced oscillations when the **driving frequency equals the natural frequency** of the system. At resonance, the amplitude of oscillation becomes very large (theoretically infinite for zero damping). Practical importance: radio tuning (electrical resonance), MRI scanners, musical instruments. Danger: Tacoma Narrows Bridge collapse (1940) due to wind-driven resonance. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Restoring Force**
The force that always **acts towards the mean position** whenever the oscillating body is displaced from it. It is the force responsible for bringing the body back to equilibrium. In SHM, the restoring force is linearly proportional to displacement: F = −kx. The negative sign indicates the force is opposite to the displacement (directed towards mean). *(All levels — critical)*

---

## S

**Seconds Pendulum**
A simple pendulum with a **time period of 2 seconds** (so it takes 1 second for each half-oscillation — hence the name). On Earth (g = 9.8 m s⁻²), its length is:
$$L = \frac{gT^2}{4\pi^2} = \frac{9.8 \times 4}{4\pi^2} \approx 1 \text{ m}$$
A seconds pendulum is exactly 1 metre long at the Earth's surface. *(All levels)*

**Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM)**
The simplest form of oscillatory motion, defined by either of two equivalent conditions:
1. **Displacement form:** x(t) = A cos(ωt + φ) — displacement is a sinusoidal function of time
2. **Force form:** F = −kx — restoring force is linearly proportional to displacement and directed towards the mean position

SHM can also be viewed as the projection of uniform circular motion on a diameter. Key features: isochronism (period independent of amplitude), total energy constant, KE and PE exchange continuously. *(All levels — critical)*

**Simple Pendulum**
An idealised pendulum consisting of a **point mass (bob) suspended by a massless, inextensible string** from a rigid support. For small oscillations (θ < ~20°), it undergoes approximately SHM with period T = 2π√(L/g). The approximation sin θ ≈ θ (in radians) is used. Period is independent of the mass of the bob and (for small amplitudes) of the amplitude. *(All levels — critical)*

**Spring Constant (k)**
Also called the **force constant**, it is the proportionality constant in Hooke's law: F = −kx. It represents the **stiffness** of the spring — a larger k means the spring is stiffer and requires more force for the same extension. Dimensions: [MT⁻²]. SI unit: N m⁻¹. Connected to SHM by ω = √(k/m) and T = 2π√(m/k). *(All levels — critical)*

---

## T

**Time Period**
See **Period**. *(All levels)*

**Total Mechanical Energy in SHM**
$$E = K + U = \frac{1}{2}kA^2 = \text{constant}$$
The total energy depends only on the spring constant and amplitude — not on time, position, velocity, or phase. It is conserved because the spring force is conservative. E ∝ A². *(All levels — critical)*

---

## V

**Velocity in SHM**
The instantaneous velocity at displacement x from mean position:
$$v = \pm\omega\sqrt{A^2 - x^2}$$
Or as a function of time:
$$v(t) = -\omega A\sin(\omega t + \phi)$$
The + sign applies when moving towards the mean position, − when moving away. Maximum velocity = ωA (at mean position x = 0); minimum velocity = 0 (at extreme positions x = ±A). Velocity leads displacement by π/2 in phase. Dimensional formula: [LT⁻¹]. *(All levels — critical)*

**Vibration**
Essentially synonymous with **oscillation**. By convention, "oscillation" is used for lower-frequency motion (e.g., pendulum, oscillating branch), while "vibration" is used for higher-frequency motion (e.g., vibrating guitar string, molecular vibrations). There is no strict scientific distinction. *(Board level)*

## Common Misconceptions Clarified

| Misconception | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| "Period depends on amplitude" | Period T is **independent** of amplitude (isochronism) — T = 2π√(m/k) has no A. |
| "At mean position, acceleration is maximum" | At mean position, acceleration is **zero** (F = −kx = 0 at x = 0). Maximum acceleration is at the extreme positions. |
| "Velocity is maximum at extreme positions" | Velocity is **zero** at extreme positions (body momentarily stops). Velocity is maximum at the mean position. |
| "Total energy in SHM oscillates" | Total energy is **constant** = ½kA². Only KE and PE oscillate (with period T/2 each). |
| "sin θ = θ for all angles" | This approximation holds only for **small angles** (θ ≪ 1 radian ≈ 57°, practically valid up to ~20°). |
| "Pendulum period depends on bob mass" | T = 2π√(L/g) — **independent of mass** m. A heavier bob oscillates with the same period as a lighter one on the same length pendulum. |
| "SHM requires a spring" | SHM arises from any force of the form F = −kx. The pendulum (for small angles), a floating cork, mercury in a U-tube — all exhibit SHM without a spring. |
| "KE and PE both have period T" | KE and PE each have period **T/2** (they complete one cycle in half the oscillation period). |
| "Two SHMs always produce SHM when combined" | Combination of two SHMs is periodic only if the frequency ratio is rational. It produces SHM only if they have the **same frequency**. |
| "e^(−ωt) represents periodic motion" | This function decreases monotonically and is **non-periodic** (represents exponential decay, like damping). |
