# CHAPTER 14 — GLOSSARY & TERMINOLOGY

### Waves

---

> All terms arranged **alphabetically** with precise scientific definitions, dimensional formulae where applicable, and exam relevance tags.

---

## A

**Amplitude (a)**
The **maximum displacement** of a particle of the medium from its equilibrium position as a wave passes through it. In the wave equation $y(x,t) = a\sin(kx - \omega t + \phi)$, the constant $a$ is the amplitude. It is always positive and has dimensional formula [L]; SI unit: metre (m). The intensity of a wave is proportional to the **square of its amplitude** ($I \propto a^2$). *(All levels — critical)*

**Angular Frequency (ω)**
A measure of how fast the oscillation occurs at any fixed location. Defined as:
$$\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T} = 2\pi\nu$$
SI unit: rad s⁻¹. Dimensional formula: [T⁻¹]. The angular frequency of a wave is determined entirely by the **source** — a 440 Hz tuning fork always produces 440 Hz waves, regardless of the medium. *(All levels — critical)*

**Angular Wave Number (k)**
Also called the **propagation constant**. Defined as:
$$k = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda}$$
It represents 2π times the number of complete wavelengths per unit length — i.e., the spatial analogue of angular frequency. SI unit: rad m⁻¹ (or m⁻¹). Dimensional formula: [L⁻¹]. The wave speed is $v = \omega/k$. *(All levels — critical)*

**Antinode**
A point in a **standing wave** where the amplitude of oscillation is **maximum** (= 2a, twice the amplitude of each constituent travelling wave). At an antinode, displacement is maximum but pressure variation is minimum (zero). Antinodes are located at:
$$x = \left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right)\frac{\lambda}{2}, \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$$
The distance between two successive antinodes is λ/2. In a pipe, the open end is an antinode. *(All levels — critical)*

## B

**Beat Frequency (ν_beat)**
The frequency of the periodic rise and fall (waxing and waning) of intensity heard when two waves of **slightly different frequencies** ν₁ and ν₂ are superposed:
$$\nu_\text{beat} = |\nu_1 - \nu_2|$$
The ear perceives beats when the beat frequency is less than about 15–20 Hz. Musicians use beats to tune instruments — when two notes are in tune, no beats are heard. *(All levels — critical)*

**Beats**
The phenomenon of periodic variation in the **intensity of sound** (or any wave) arising from the superposition of two waves with slightly different frequencies. The resultant wave has an **amplitude that varies periodically** with time at the beat frequency. The ear hears a sound of the average frequency $\nu_a = (\nu_1 + \nu_2)/2$ whose loudness alternately swells and fades at the rate $\nu_b = |\nu_1 - \nu_2|$. *(All levels — critical)*

**Bulk Modulus (B)**
The elastic property of a medium relevant to longitudinal wave propagation. Defined as:
$$B = -\frac{\Delta P}{\Delta V/V}$$
where ΔP is the change in pressure and ΔV/V is the fractional change in volume. B is always positive (because pressure increase compresses the material: ΔV/V < 0 when ΔP > 0). SI unit: pascal (Pa = N m⁻²). Dimensional formula: [ML⁻¹T⁻²]. Speed of sound in a fluid: $v = \sqrt{B/\rho}$. *(NEET/JEE)*

---

## C

**Capillary Waves**
Short-wavelength (few centimetres) surface water waves whose restoring force is the **surface tension** of water. Distinct from gravity waves. *(Board level awareness)*

**Compression**
A region of **higher-than-normal density and pressure** in a longitudinal wave (e.g., sound). In a compression, the medium is squeezed — molecules are packed closer together. Compressions alternate with rarefactions to form the longitudinal wave pattern. *(All levels)*

**Constructive Interference**
The superposition of two waves that are **in phase** (phase difference φ = 0, or even multiples of 2π), producing a resultant wave with amplitude equal to the **sum** of individual amplitudes. For two equal-amplitude waves of amplitude a, the resultant amplitude = 2a. The waves reinforce each other. *(All levels — critical)*

**Crest**
The point of **maximum positive displacement** in a transverse wave. In the wave equation, it corresponds to $\sin(kx - \omega t) = +1$. The distance between successive crests equals one wavelength λ. *(Board/NEET)*

---

## D

**Destructive Interference**
The superposition of two waves that are **exactly out of phase** (phase difference φ = π, or odd multiples of π), producing a resultant wave with amplitude equal to the **difference** of individual amplitudes. For two equal-amplitude waves, the resultant amplitude = 0 (complete cancellation). *(All levels — critical)*

**Displacement Relation**
The mathematical equation describing the displacement of any particle of the medium at any position x and any time t for a sinusoidal progressive wave:
$$y(x,t) = a\sin(kx - \omega t + \phi)$$
This is also called the **wave function** or **wave equation**. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## E

**Echo**
The reflection of sound from a **rigid boundary** (e.g., a cliff, wall, or mountain). The reflected pulse is inverted (phase change of π). For an echo to be distinctly heard, the reflecting surface must be at least ~17 m away (so the reflected sound arrives at least 0.1 s after the direct sound, since the ear retains a sound for ~0.1 s). *(Board/NEET)*

**Electromagnetic Waves**
Waves that **do not require a material medium** for propagation — they can travel through vacuum. They are generated by oscillating electric and magnetic fields. Examples: light, radio waves, microwaves, X-rays, UV radiation. All electromagnetic waves travel at speed $c = 3 \times 10^8$ m s⁻¹ in vacuum. *(Contextual — Class XII topic)*

---

## F

**First Harmonic**
See **Fundamental Mode**. *(All levels)*

**Frequency (ν)**
The **number of complete oscillations** per unit time at any point in the medium as the wave passes. $\nu = 1/T = \omega/2\pi$. SI unit: hertz (Hz). The frequency of a wave is determined by the **source** and remains unchanged when the wave passes from one medium to another (only speed and wavelength change). *(All levels — critical)*

**Fundamental Mode**
The **lowest possible natural frequency** at which a bounded system (string, air column) can vibrate. Also called the **first harmonic**. For a string fixed at both ends or a pipe open at both ends: $\nu_1 = v/2L$. For a pipe closed at one end: $\nu_1 = v/4L$. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## G

**Gravity Waves**
Long-wavelength surface water waves (wavelengths from several metres to hundreds of metres) whose restoring force is **gravity** (which tends to keep the water surface at its lowest level). The waves in the open ocean are gravity waves. *(Board level awareness)*

---

## H

**Harmonic**
A natural frequency of oscillation of a bounded system that is an **integer multiple** of the fundamental frequency. The nth harmonic has frequency νₙ = nν₁. A string fixed at both ends can support harmonics: ν₁, 2ν₁, 3ν₁, ... A pipe closed at one end supports only **odd harmonics**: ν₁, 3ν₁, 5ν₁, ... *(All levels — critical)*

---

## I

**Interference**
The phenomenon that occurs when two or more waves overlap in the same region of space simultaneously. The resultant displacement is the algebraic sum of individual displacements (principle of superposition). Interference can be **constructive** (amplitude increases) or **destructive** (amplitude decreases), depending on the phase difference. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## L

**Laplace Correction**
The modification of Newton's formula for the speed of sound in gases, proposed by **Laplace** (Simon-Pierre Laplace, 1749–1827). Newton assumed pressure variations are **isothermal** (B = P), giving $v = \sqrt{P/\rho}$ ≈ 280 m s⁻¹ — about 15% too low. Laplace correctly argued that the process is **adiabatic** (too rapid for heat exchange): $B_\text{ad} = \gamma P$, giving:
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{\gamma P}{\rho}} \approx 331 \text{ m s}^{-1} \quad (\text{for air at STP})$$
This agrees with experiment. *(NEET/JEE — critical)*

**Linear Mass Density (µ)**
Mass per unit length of a string: $\mu = m/L$. Dimensional formula: [ML⁻¹]; SI unit: kg m⁻¹. The speed of transverse waves on a stretched string: $v = \sqrt{T/\mu}$. A denser string (higher µ) supports slower waves. *(All levels — critical)*

**Longitudinal Wave**
A wave in which the particles of the medium oscillate **parallel to (along)** the direction of wave propagation. These waves propagate as alternating compressions and rarefactions. Sound waves are longitudinal. Longitudinal waves can propagate in all elastic media — solids, liquids, and gases — because all can be compressed. Also called **pressure waves** or **compressional waves**. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## M

**Matter Waves**
A type of wave associated with **moving subatomic particles** (electrons, protons, neutrons, atoms) in quantum mechanics. Their existence was predicted by de Broglie and confirmed experimentally. They are described by a wave function in quantum mechanics and have wavelength $\lambda = h/mv$ (de Broglie relation). Practical application: electron microscopes. *(Awareness only — not in Class 11 scope)*

**Mechanical Wave**
A wave that requires a **material medium** for its propagation. The wave travels through the medium via elastic forces between neighbouring particles — the medium must be elastic and have inertia. Examples: sound waves, water waves, seismic waves, string waves. Mechanical waves cannot travel through vacuum. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## N

**Newton's Formula**
The formula for the speed of sound in a gas derived by Newton, assuming **isothermal** pressure changes:
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{P}{\rho}}$$
For air at STP, this gives ~280 m s⁻¹, which is about 15% lower than the measured 331 m s⁻¹. Newton's assumption is incorrect — Laplace later corrected it. *(NEET/JEE — important for "where did Newton go wrong?" questions)*

**Node**
A point in a **standing wave** where the displacement is **permanently zero** — the medium never moves at this point. Nodes are located at:
$$x = \frac{n\lambda}{2}, \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$$
The distance between two successive nodes is λ/2. At a node, displacement is minimum (zero) but pressure variation is **maximum**. In a pipe, a closed end is a node. *(All levels — critical)*

**Normal Modes**
The set of **discrete natural frequencies** at which a bounded system (string, air column) can oscillate. Each normal mode has a specific pattern of nodes and antinodes. The system cannot vibrate at arbitrary frequencies — only at these specific resonant frequencies. Also called **harmonics** or **overtones**. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## O

**Overtone**
A frequency of vibration of a system **above** its fundamental (first harmonic). The first overtone is the second harmonic; the second overtone is the third harmonic; etc. In a closed pipe, only odd harmonics exist, so the first overtone is the 3rd harmonic. *(NEET/JEE)*

---

## P

**Period (T)**
The **time** for any one particle of the medium to complete one full oscillation as the wave passes. Also, the time for the wave pattern to advance by one complete wavelength. $T = 2\pi/\omega = 1/\nu = \lambda/v$. SI unit: second (s). Dimensional formula: [T]. *(All levels — critical)*

**Phase**
The quantity $(kx - \omega t + \phi)$ appearing as the argument of the sine function in the wave equation. It determines the **state of displacement** of a particle at position x at time t. Two points with the same phase are in the same state of motion. *(All levels — critical)*

**Phase Difference**
The difference in phase between two particles of the medium at the same instant, or between two instants at the same particle. Two points separated by distance d have phase difference $\Delta\phi = kd = 2\pi d/\lambda$. Points separated by exactly λ are in phase (Δφ = 2π); those separated by λ/2 are out of phase (Δφ = π). *(NEET/JEE)*

**Phase Reversal**
The change of phase by **π (180°)** that occurs when a wave is reflected at a **rigid boundary**. The reflected wave is inverted (upward displacement becomes downward and vice versa). There is NO phase reversal at a free/open boundary. *(All levels — critical)*

**Plane Wave**
A wave in which the wavefronts (surfaces of constant phase) are **plane (flat) surfaces** perpendicular to the direction of propagation. A one-dimensional wave along the x-axis is a plane wave. *(Conceptual)*

**Pressure Antinode**
A point in a sound wave where the pressure variation is **maximum**. Pressure antinodes coincide with displacement **nodes** (since where particles don't move, pressure piles up). In a closed pipe, the closed end is a pressure antinode. *(NEET/JEE — important distinction)*

**Pressure Node**
A point in a sound wave where the pressure variation is **zero**. Pressure nodes coincide with displacement **antinodes**. In a pipe, the open end is a pressure node (open to atmosphere → pressure variation ≈ 0). *(NEET/JEE — important distinction)*

**Progressive (Travelling) Wave**
A wave that transfers energy by moving from one region of the medium to another. The wave pattern moves with a definite speed v. Contrast with a **standing wave** which does not travel. Examples: a wave on an infinite string, sound from a loudspeaker. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## R

**Rarefaction**
A region of **lower-than-normal density and pressure** in a longitudinal wave. In a rarefaction, molecules are more spread out than normal. Rarefactions alternate with compressions to form a longitudinal wave. *(All levels)*

**Reflection of Waves**
The phenomenon in which a wave pulse or wave, on meeting a boundary, **bounces back** into the original medium. At a rigid boundary, the reflected wave is **inverted** (phase change π). At an open boundary, the reflected wave is **non-inverted** (no phase change). The law of reflection holds for all waves. *(All levels — critical)*

**Refraction of Waves**
When a wave is incident obliquely on the boundary between two different media, part of it passes into the second medium as the **refracted wave**, which travels in a different direction. The incident and refracted waves obey **Snell's law** of refraction. *(Board/NEET awareness)*

**Resonance**
A special case of forced oscillation in which the **driving frequency equals the natural frequency** of the system. At resonance, the amplitude of vibration becomes very large. Examples: tuning a radio (electrical resonance), resonance columns, musical instruments responding to specific frequencies. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## S

**Seismic Waves**
Mechanical waves produced by earthquakes, propagating through the Earth. **P-waves** (primary waves) are longitudinal and travel through all media (solids, liquids, gases). **S-waves** (secondary/shear waves) are transverse and travel only through solids. The study of seismic waves reveals information about the Earth's interior. *(NEET/JEE contextual)*

**Sinusoidal Wave**
A wave whose displacement varies as a sine or cosine function of position and time. The simplest and most fundamental type of wave. Any periodic wave can be decomposed into sinusoidal components (Fourier's theorem). Also called a **harmonic wave**. *(All levels)*

**Speed of Sound**
The speed at which a sound (longitudinal) wave propagates through a medium. In air at STP (~0°C): 331 m s⁻¹; at 20°C: 343 m s⁻¹. In water (20°C): 1482 m s⁻¹. In steel: 5941 m s⁻¹. Speed increases with: (a) increasing elastic modulus (stiffness), (b) decreasing density, (c) increasing temperature, (d) increasing humidity. *(All levels — critical)*

**Standing Wave (Stationary Wave)**
A wave pattern formed by the **superposition of two identical waves travelling in opposite directions**. The pattern does not travel — it oscillates in place. Characterised by fixed **nodes** (zero displacement) and **antinodes** (maximum displacement). Standing wave equation: $y(x,t) = 2a\sin kx\cos\omega t$. No net energy transport. *(All levels — critical)*

**Superposition Principle**
The fundamental principle stating that when two or more waves overlap in a medium, the **net displacement** of any particle is the **algebraic sum** of the individual displacements due to each wave:
$$y = y_1 + y_2 + \ldots + y_n$$
Each wave retains its own identity after the overlap region. This principle is valid for linear media (restoring force ∝ displacement). *(All levels — critical)*

---

## T

**Transverse Wave**
A wave in which the particles of the medium oscillate **perpendicular to** the direction of wave propagation. These waves involve **shear strain** — layers of the medium slide sideways. Therefore, transverse mechanical waves can only propagate in **solids** (which can sustain shear stress), not in fluids. Examples: waves on a string, seismic S-waves, all electromagnetic waves. *(All levels — critical)*

**Travelling Wave**
See **Progressive Wave**. *(All levels)*

**Trough**
The point of **maximum negative displacement** in a transverse wave. In the wave equation, it corresponds to $\sin(kx - \omega t) = -1$. The distance between successive troughs equals one wavelength λ. *(Board/NEET)*

---

## W

**Wave**
A disturbance that propagates through a medium (or vacuum) transferring **energy and information** without the net transfer of matter. The constituents of the medium oscillate about their equilibrium positions; they do not travel with the wave. *(All levels — critical)*

**Wave Function**
The mathematical expression $y(x,t)$ that gives the displacement of any particle at position x at time t. For a sinusoidal progressive wave: $y(x,t) = a\sin(kx - \omega t + \phi)$. The wave function completely describes the wave. *(All levels — critical)*

**Wavelength (λ)**
The **minimum distance** between two points in a wave that have the same phase at a given instant. Equivalently, the distance between two consecutive crests, two consecutive troughs, or any two consecutive points in the same state of motion. $\lambda = 2\pi/k = vT = v/\nu$. SI unit: metre (m). Dimensional formula: [L]. *(All levels — critical)*

**Wave Number (k)**
See **Angular Wave Number**. *(All levels)*

**Wave Speed (v)**
The speed at which the wave pattern (a particular phase of the wave) moves through the medium. $v = \omega/k = \lambda\nu = \lambda/T$. The wave speed is determined entirely by the **properties of the medium** (elastic modulus and density). The source determines frequency; the medium determines speed; wavelength adjusts: $\lambda = v/\nu$. *(All levels — critical)*

## Common Misconceptions Clarified

| Misconception | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| "Newton's formula for speed of sound is correct" | Newton's formula ($v = \sqrt{P/\rho}$) is **wrong** — it underestimates by ~15%. Laplace's correction ($v = \sqrt{\gamma P/\rho}$) is correct. |
| "Sound travels faster at higher pressure" | Speed of sound in a gas is **independent of pressure** at constant temperature — both P and ρ increase proportionally, so P/ρ stays constant. |
| "Transverse waves can travel in any medium" | Transverse mechanical waves require **shear modulus** — they **cannot** travel in fluids (liquids and gases). |
| "Nodes in a standing wave vibrate slowly" | Nodes are points of **zero displacement** at all times — they never vibrate at all. |
| "Closed pipe supports all harmonics" | A pipe closed at one end supports **only odd harmonics** (1st, 3rd, 5th, ...). A pipe open at both ends supports all harmonics. |
| "Wave speed increases with frequency" | Wave speed in a given medium is **independent of frequency** (determined only by medium properties). Wavelength adjusts: λ = v/ν. |
| "The medium moves with the wave" | The medium particles merely **oscillate about equilibrium** — they do not travel with the wave. Only the disturbance (energy) travels. |
| "Displacement node = pressure node" | A **displacement node** is a **pressure antinode** (and vice versa). Where particles cannot move, pressure builds up. |
| "All standing wave frequencies are possible" | Only **discrete frequencies** (normal modes) are allowed — the system is constrained by boundary conditions. |
| "Beat frequency = average of two frequencies" | Beat frequency = **difference** of two frequencies (|ν₁ − ν₂|). The average is what we perceive as the pitch. |
