### Thermal Properties of Matter

---

> All terms arranged **alphabetically** with precise scientific definitions, dimensional formulae where applicable, and exam relevance tags.

## A

**Absolute Scale / Kelvin Scale**
A temperature scale in which the zero corresponds to **absolute zero** (−273.15°C) — the lowest theoretically possible temperature. Named after Lord Kelvin (William Thomson, 1824–1907). The Kelvin unit (K) is the same size as the degree Celsius, but the scales differ in origin:

$$T(\text{K}) = t_C + 273.15$$

The modern Kelvin scale uses the **triple point of water** (273.16 K) as its single fixed reference point. Unlike Celsius and Fahrenheit, the Kelvin scale has no negative values. *(All levels — critical)*

**Absolute Zero (0 K)**
The theoretical minimum temperature, equal to −273.15°C or −459.69°F. At absolute zero, molecular kinetic energy approaches its minimum value. Obtained by extrapolating the pressure vs temperature graph of an ideal gas (at constant volume) to zero pressure. **No substance can be cooled to exactly absolute zero** (third law of thermodynamics — beyond this syllabus). *(All levels — critical)*

**Anomalous Expansion of Water**
The unusual behaviour of water between 0°C and 4°C, in which it **contracts upon heating** (density increases). At 4°C, water reaches its **maximum density** (~1000 kg m⁻³). Above 4°C, it expands normally. This anomaly arises from the partially disrupted hydrogen-bond structure of liquid water. Ecologically critical — allows lakes to freeze from the top down, preserving aquatic life. *(All levels — critical)*

## B

**Blackbody**
An ideal body that **absorbs all incident radiation** (emissivity e = 1; reflectivity = 0) and also emits the maximum possible radiation at any given temperature. No real body is a perfect blackbody, but **lamp black** comes close. The radiation emitted by a blackbody depends only on its temperature, not on the material. Blackbody radiation led Planck to propose quantum theory (1900). *(All levels — critical)*

**Blackbody Radiation**
The thermal electromagnetic radiation emitted by a blackbody. Key properties:
- Covers a **continuous spectrum** of wavelengths
- Energy distribution depends only on T (universal curves)
- Peak wavelength λm decreases with increasing T (Wien's law)
- Total power ∝ T⁴ (Stefan-Boltzmann law)

*(All levels — critical)*

**Boiling Point**
The temperature at which a liquid's vapour pressure equals the external (atmospheric) pressure, and the liquid undergoes **vaporisation** throughout its volume. At the boiling point, both liquid and vapour states coexist in thermal equilibrium. Normal boiling point = boiling point at 1 atm pressure. **Boiling point increases with pressure** (pressure cooker) and **decreases with pressure decrease** (high altitude). *(All levels — critical)*

**Boyle's Law**
For a fixed amount of ideal gas at **constant temperature**: PV = constant. Discovered by Robert Boyle (1627–1691). Combined with Charles' law it gives the ideal gas equation. *(All levels)*

## C

**Calorimeter**
A device used to **measure heat energy** changes in physical or chemical processes. Typically a thermally insulated metallic vessel (copper or aluminium) with a stirrer and thermometer. Based on the principle: heat lost by hot body = heat gained by cold body (in an isolated system). *(All levels — critical)*

**Calorimetry**
The science of measuring heat. The fundamental equation used is:
$$Q = ms\Delta T \quad \text{(no phase change)}$$
$$Q = mL \quad \text{(during phase change)}$$
Principle: In an isolated system, **heat lost = heat gained**. *(All levels — critical)*

**Change of State (Phase Transition)**
A transition between states of matter (solid ↔ liquid ↔ gas). During a phase transition, temperature remains constant even though heat is being supplied or removed (the heat goes into latent heat). Common transitions: melting/fusion, freezing, vaporisation, condensation, sublimation, deposition. *(All levels — critical)*

**Charles' Law**
For a fixed amount of ideal gas at **constant pressure**: V/T = constant (T in Kelvin). Named after Jacques Charles (1747–1823). Directly implies that volume extrapolates to zero at T = 0 K (absolute zero). *(All levels)*

**Coefficient of Linear Expansion (αl)**
The fractional change in length per unit change in temperature:
$$\alpha_l = \frac{\Delta l / l}{\Delta T}$$
SI unit: K⁻¹. For most metals, αl ≈ 10⁻⁵ to 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹. Not truly constant — varies slightly with temperature. *(All levels — critical)*

**Coefficient of Volume Expansion (αv)**
The fractional change in volume per unit change in temperature:
$$\alpha_v = \frac{\Delta V / V}{\Delta T}$$
SI unit: K⁻¹. Relation to linear expansion: αv = 3αl (for isotropic solids). For an ideal gas at constant pressure: αv = 1/T. *(All levels — critical)*

**Conduction (Thermal)**
Transfer of heat through a material by **molecular collisions and vibrations**, without bulk movement of matter. Governed by Fourier's law:
$$H = KA\frac{T_C - T_D}{L}$$
Possible in solids, liquids, and gases. Most efficient in solids, particularly metals (free electrons carry heat). Coefficient K = **thermal conductivity**. *(All levels — critical)*

**Convection**
Transfer of heat by **actual bulk movement of a fluid** (liquid or gas). Not possible in solids. Two types:
- **Natural convection:** Driven by density differences due to temperature (buoyancy)
- **Forced convection:** Driven by external agent (pump, fan)
Examples: sea breezes, trade winds, car engine cooling, circulatory system. *(All levels — critical)*

## D

**Dewar Flask (Thermos Bottle)**
An insulated container that minimises all three heat transfer modes:
- **Vacuum** between double walls: eliminates conduction and convection
- **Silvered** inner and outer walls: reflects thermal radiation (high reflectivity → low emissivity → low radiation loss)
- **Insulating support**: minimises conduction at the base
Invented by Sir James Dewar (1842–1923). *(All levels)*

---

## E

**Emissivity (e)**
A dimensionless ratio (0 ≤ e ≤ 1) that measures how well a body emits (or absorbs) thermal radiation **compared to an ideal blackbody** at the same temperature. By Kirchhoff's law, emissivity = absorptivity. e = 1 for a perfect blackbody; e ≈ 0.97 for human skin; e ≈ 0.04 for polished silver. High e → good emitter/absorber; low e → poor emitter (good reflector). *(NEET/JEE)*

---

## F

**Fixed Points (of a Temperature Scale)**
Two reproducible temperature values used to define a temperature scale and calibrate thermometers. Traditional fixed points: ice point (0°C, 32°F) and steam point (100°C, 212°F). The modern Kelvin scale uses only ONE fixed point: **triple point of water = 273.16 K**. Fixed points must correspond to unique, reproducible physical phenomena. *(All levels)*

**Freezing Point**
See **Melting Point.** The temperature at which liquid → solid transition occurs. For a pure substance, freezing point = melting point at the same pressure. Freezing point generally decreases with increasing pressure (notable exception: water's freezing point decreases with pressure — important for regelation and skating). *(All levels)*

**Fusion**
See **Melting/Fusion.** The phase transition from solid → liquid. The latent heat absorbed during this process is the **latent heat of fusion (Lf)**. *(All levels)*

---

## H

**Heat**
The form of energy **transferred between two systems (or a system and its surroundings) by virtue of a temperature difference**. Heat is NOT a state property of the system — it only exists during the process of transfer. SI unit: Joule (J). Heat always flows from higher temperature to lower temperature (2nd law). *(All levels — critical)*

**Heat Capacity (S)**
The amount of heat required to raise the temperature of a **given body** by 1 K (or 1°C):
$$S = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta T}$$
SI unit: J K⁻¹. Heat capacity depends on both the mass AND the nature of the material. *(All levels)*

**Heat of Fusion**
See **Latent Heat of Fusion.** *(All levels)*

**Heat of Vaporisation**
See **Latent Heat of Vaporisation.** *(All levels)*

## I

**Ideal Gas Equation**
The equation of state for a low-density (ideal) gas:
$$PV = \mu RT$$
where µ = number of moles, R = 8.31 J mol⁻¹K⁻¹, T = absolute temperature (K). Combines Boyle's law and Charles' law. All ideal gas thermometers agree → the basis of the absolute temperature scale. For real gases, deviations appear especially at high pressures and low temperatures. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## K

**Kelvin (K)**
The SI unit of temperature. Defined so that 1 K is the same size as 1°C, but the zero point is at −273.15°C (absolute zero). The triple point of water = 273.16 K is the defining fixed point. Kelvin values are always positive. *(All levels — critical)*

**Kirchhoff's Law of Radiation**
At thermal equilibrium, the **emissivity** of a body equals its **absorptivity** for any given wavelength. Consequence: a good absorber is also a good emitter; a good reflector (poor absorber) is a poor emitter. Explains why a blackbody (perfect absorber) is also the best possible emitter. *(NEET/JEE)*

---

## L

**Land Breeze**
A convection current at night: land cools faster than sea → air above sea is warmer → rises → cooler air from land moves toward sea to replace it. The reverse of a sea breeze. *(All levels)*

**Latent Heat (L)**
The heat per unit mass absorbed or released by a substance during a **change of state**, without change in temperature:
$$Q = mL \quad \Rightarrow \quad L = Q/m$$
SI unit: J kg⁻¹. Two types: **Latent Heat of Fusion (Lf)** and **Latent Heat of Vaporisation (Lv)**. For water: Lf = 3.33 × 10⁵ J kg⁻¹; Lv = 22.6 × 10⁵ J kg⁻¹. Lv >> Lf because vaporisation requires complete separation of molecules. *(All levels — critical)*

**Latent Heat of Fusion (Lf)**
The heat per unit mass required to convert a solid into a liquid **at its melting point** without change in temperature. During melting, this energy goes into increasing molecular **potential energy** (breaking lattice bonds), not kinetic energy (hence T stays constant). *(All levels — critical)*

**Latent Heat of Vaporisation (Lv)**
The heat per unit mass required to convert a liquid into vapour **at its boiling point** without change in temperature. Much larger than Lf because all intermolecular forces must be overcome. For water, Lv = 22.6 × 10⁵ J kg⁻¹ — the reason steam burns are so severe. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## M

**Melting / Fusion**
The phase transition from solid to liquid. During melting, the temperature remains constant at the **melting point**, and both solid and liquid phases coexist in thermal equilibrium. Melting point of ice = 0°C = 273.15 K at 1 atm pressure. *(All levels — critical)*

**Melting Point**
The temperature at which a substance's solid and liquid phases are in **thermal equilibrium**. At standard atmospheric pressure = **normal melting point**. The melting point of most substances increases with pressure; for water it **decreases** with increasing pressure (anomalous). *(All levels — critical)*

**Molar Specific Heat Capacity (C)**
Heat required per **mole** of substance per unit temperature change:
$$C = \frac{1}{\mu}\frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta T}$$
SI unit: J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹. For gases: Cp (constant pressure) > Cv (constant volume); difference Cp − Cv = R. *(NEET/JEE — critical)*

## N

**Newton's Law of Cooling**
For small temperature excesses above surroundings, the **rate of loss of heat** is proportional to the excess temperature:
$$-\frac{dQ}{dt} = k(T_2 - T_1)$$
Results in exponential temperature decay: T₂(t) = T₁ + C'e^{−Kt}.
Valid only for **small ΔT**. For large ΔT, the full Stefan-Boltzmann law must be used. Named after Sir Isaac Newton. *(All levels — critical)*

**Normal Boiling Point**
The boiling point of a substance at **standard atmospheric pressure** (1 atm = 1.013 × 10⁵ Pa). Normal boiling point of water = 100°C. *(All levels)*

**Normal Melting Point**
The melting point of a substance at **standard atmospheric pressure**. Normal melting point of water = 0°C. *(All levels)*

---

## R

**Radiation (Thermal)**
Transfer of heat through **electromagnetic waves** without requiring any medium. All bodies at T > 0 K emit thermal radiation. The energy emitted follows the Stefan-Boltzmann law (H = AeσT⁴). Thermal radiation spans the infrared and visible spectrum depending on temperature. The only heat transfer mode that works in vacuum (e.g., solar energy reaching Earth). *(All levels — critical)*

**Regelation**
The process of **re-freezing** of ice after it has melted under pressure (when pressure is removed). Explained by the fact that water's melting point decreases with increasing pressure. Example: A weighted wire passes through an ice slab — ice melts under the wire (due to pressure), water refreezes above the wire (pressure removed); the wire passes through and the slab remains intact. Skating on ice exploits a similar effect. *(NEET/JEE)*

---

## S

**Sea Breeze**
A wind blowing from sea to land during the day, created by **natural convection**. Land heats up faster than sea (lower specific heat capacity) → hot air over land rises → cool moist air from the sea rushes in. Reverses at night (land breeze). *(All levels)*

**Specific Heat Capacity (s)**
The amount of heat required to raise the temperature of **unit mass** of a substance by **1 K** (or 1°C):
$$s = \frac{1}{m}\frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta T} = \frac{S}{m}$$
SI unit: J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹. Dimensional formula: [L²T⁻²Θ⁻¹]. Water has the highest specific heat capacity among common liquids (4186 J kg⁻¹K⁻¹), making it important as a coolant, heater, and climate regulator. *(All levels — critical)*

**Stefan-Boltzmann Constant (σ)**
The proportionality constant in the Stefan-Boltzmann law:
$$\sigma = 5.67 \times 10^{-8} \text{ W m}^{-2} \text{K}^{-4}$$
Relates the total radiated power of a blackbody to T⁴. Established experimentally by Josef Stefan (1879) and derived theoretically by Ludwig Boltzmann (1884). *(NEET/JEE — critical)*

**Stefan-Boltzmann Law**
The total electromagnetic energy radiated per unit time by a body:
$$H = Ae\sigma T^4$$
For net radiation from a body at T in surroundings at Ts:
$$H_{net} = Ae\sigma(T^4 - T_s^4)$$
T⁴ dependence means a small temperature increase causes a dramatic increase in radiation. *(All levels — critical)*

**Sublimation**
Direct transition from **solid to vapour** without passing through the liquid phase. Occurs at temperatures and pressures below the triple point. Reverse process (vapour → solid) = **deposition**. Examples: dry ice (CO₂), iodine, naphthalene (mothballs), ice in very dry/cold conditions. During sublimation, solid and vapour coexist in thermal equilibrium. *(All levels)*

## T

**Temperature**
A physical quantity that measures the **degree of hotness or coldness** of a body on some scale. More precisely, for an ideal gas thermometer: T is the quantity such that P ∝ T at constant volume. Temperature determines the **direction of heat flow**: heat flows from higher to lower temperature. SI unit: Kelvin (K). *(All levels — critical)*

**Thermal Conductivity (K)**
A material property that quantifies the rate of heat conduction per unit area per unit temperature gradient:
$$K = \frac{HL}{A\Delta T}$$
SI unit: W m⁻¹ K⁻¹. Higher K → better conductor. Ranges from ~406 (silver) to ~0.024 (air). Metals have high K (due to free electrons); insulators like glass wool have very low K. *(All levels — critical)*

**Thermal Equilibrium**
A state in which **two or more bodies (or parts of a system) in contact have the same temperature** and there is no net heat flow between them. Thermal equilibrium is the basis of the Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics. *(All levels — critical)*

**Thermal Expansion**
The increase in dimensions of a body due to increase in temperature, caused by increased molecular kinetic energy → greater average intermolecular separation. Three types: linear (αl), area (αA = 2αl), volume (αv = 3αl). *(All levels — critical)*

**Thermal Radiation**
See **Radiation (Thermal).** *(All levels)*

**Thermal Stress**
The compressive or tensile stress set up in a body when it is prevented from expanding or contracting freely during a temperature change:
$$\text{Thermal stress} = Y \cdot \alpha_l \cdot \Delta T$$
where Y = Young's modulus. Can be enormous — railway tracks must have expansion gaps to prevent buckling. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Thermometer**
A device used to measure temperature, exploiting a **thermometric property** — any property that changes reproducibly and measurably with temperature (volume of mercury, pressure of gas, electrical resistance, thermoelectric EMF). For a temperature scale to be defined, two fixed reference points are needed. *(All levels)*

**Triple Point**
The unique temperature and pressure at which **all three phases** (solid, liquid, vapour) of a substance coexist in thermal equilibrium. For water: T = 273.16 K, P = 6.11 × 10⁻³ atm. The triple point has a unique T and P (unlike melting/boiling points which vary with pressure) → ideal as a standard fixed point for the Kelvin scale. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## V

**Vaporisation**
The phase transition from **liquid to vapour (gas)**. Can occur as:
- **Boiling:** Occurs at the boiling point throughout the liquid volume
- **Evaporation:** Occurs at the surface at any temperature
Latent heat of vaporisation Lv must be supplied. Temperature remains constant during boiling. *(All levels)*

---

## W

**Wien's Displacement Law**
The wavelength of maximum intensity (λm) of blackbody radiation is **inversely proportional to absolute temperature**:
$$\lambda_m T = 2.9 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m K}$$
As T increases, λm shifts to shorter wavelengths → iron glows: red → orange → yellow → white. Used to estimate surface temperatures of stars and other celestial bodies from their spectral peak. Named after Wilhelm Wien (1864–1928). *(NEET/JEE — critical)*

## Common Misconceptions Clarified

| Misconception | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| Heat and temperature are the same | Heat is energy in transit; temperature measures degree of hotness. A large body at lower T can have more total thermal energy than a small body at higher T. |
| Temperature is the total thermal energy of a body | Temperature reflects *average* kinetic energy per molecule, not total energy. |
| Water freezes at 0°C always | The melting/freezing point depends on pressure. Under high pressure, ice melts at temperatures *below* 0°C (regelation). |
| αv = 2αl for isotropic solids | αv = *3*αl (for 3D). αA = 2αl (for 2D — area). |
| During melting, heat increases kinetic energy | During melting, heat goes to increase *potential energy* (to break lattice bonds). KE and temperature stay constant during phase change. |
| Good absorbers are poor emitters | By Kirchhoff's law, a good absorber is always a *good emitter* at thermal equilibrium. A perfect blackbody is both a perfect absorber AND perfect emitter. |
| Newton's law of cooling applies universally | It is valid only for *small* excess temperatures. For large ΔT, use the Stefan-Boltzmann law. |
| Lf and Lv are comparable for water | Lv (22.6 × 10⁵) is nearly 7 times Lf (3.33 × 10⁵) because vaporisation requires breaking all intermolecular bonds, not just partially disrupting the crystal lattice. |
| All modes of heat transfer need a medium | Radiation travels through vacuum (no medium needed). Conduction and convection require a medium. |
| Boiling point is always 100°C | Normal BP of water is 100°C at 1 atm. It changes with pressure — lower at high altitude, higher in a pressure cooker. |
