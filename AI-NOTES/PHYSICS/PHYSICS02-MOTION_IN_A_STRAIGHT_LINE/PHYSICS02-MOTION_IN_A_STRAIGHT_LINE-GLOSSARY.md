# CHAPTER 2 — GLOSSARY & TERMINOLOGY

### Motion in a Straight Line

---

> All terms arranged **alphabetically** with precise scientific definitions, dimensional formulae where applicable, and exam relevance tags.

---

## A

**Acceleration**
The rate of change of velocity with respect to time. Average acceleration = Δv/Δt; instantaneous acceleration = dv/dt. SI unit: **m s⁻²**. Dimensional formula: **[M⁰LT⁻²]**. A vector quantity — can be positive, negative, or zero. *(All levels)*

**Average Acceleration**
The total change in velocity divided by the total time taken: ā = (v₂ − v₁)/(t₂ − t₁). On a v–t graph, it equals the **slope of the chord** connecting two points. *(Board/NEET)*

**Average Speed**
The total path length (distance) divided by total time elapsed. Always a **scalar**, always ≥ 0. Average speed ≥ |average velocity|; equality holds only for unidirectional motion. *(Board)*

**Average Velocity**
The total displacement divided by total time: v̄ = Δx/Δt. A **vector** quantity that can be positive, negative, or zero. Not the same as average speed when the object reverses direction. *(All levels)*

---

## C

**Calculus (in Kinematics)**
The mathematical tool for handling variable acceleration. Key results: v = dx/dt; a = dv/dt = v(dv/dx). Displacement x = ∫v dt; velocity change = ∫a dt. Essential for JEE. *(NEET/JEE)*

---

## D

**Deceleration**
A non-standard (informal) term for **retardation** — an acceleration that acts opposite to the direction of velocity, causing the object to slow down. Precisely described as "negative acceleration" when velocity is in the positive direction. *(Board)*

**Displacement**
The **change in position** of an object: Δx = x₂ − x₁. A **vector** quantity with both magnitude and direction. Can be positive, negative, or zero. SI unit: **m**. Dimensional formula: **[L]**. *(All levels)*

**Distance** (see also: Path Length)
The total length of the actual path traversed. A **scalar**, always ≥ 0. In one dimension, path length ≥ |displacement|. *(All levels)*

---

## F

**Free Fall**
The motion of an object under the influence of **gravity alone**, with no air resistance. It is a case of uniformly accelerated motion with acceleration a = g = 9.8 m s⁻² (downward). *(Board/NEET)*

**Frame of Reference**
A coordinate system with respect to which motion is described. In one-dimensional kinematics, a single x-axis suffices. The choice of origin and positive direction is arbitrary. *(Board)*

---

## G

**Galileo's Law of Odd Numbers**
The distances traversed by a body falling from rest during successive equal intervals of time are in the ratio **1 : 3 : 5 : 7 : 9 ...** Established by Galileo Galilei (1564–1642). *(Board/NEET)*

**Galileo Galilei (1564–1642)**
Italian physicist who made the first quantitative studies of free fall, established that all objects fall with the same acceleration (in absence of air resistance), and derived the law of odd numbers. *(Historical/Board)*

---

## I

**Instantaneous Acceleration**
Acceleration at a specific instant of time:
a = lim(Δt→0) Δv/Δt = dv/dt
On a v–t graph: instantaneous acceleration = **slope of the tangent** at that point. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Instantaneous Speed**
The magnitude of instantaneous velocity. **Always equal** to |instantaneous velocity| (unlike average speed vs. average velocity). *(All levels)*

**Instantaneous Velocity**
The velocity at a specific instant: v = lim(Δt→0) Δx/Δt = dx/dt.
On an x–t graph: instantaneous velocity = **slope of the tangent** at that point.
For uniform motion: instantaneous velocity = average velocity. *(All levels)*

---

## K

**Kinematics**
The branch of mechanics dealing with the description of motion **without** reference to the causes (forces) of motion. Concerned with position, displacement, velocity, and acceleration as functions of time. (Causes are studied in Dynamics.) *(Board)*

**Kinematic Equations**
The three equations of motion for **constant (uniform) acceleration**:
1. v = v₀ + at
2. x = v₀t + ½at²
3. v² = v₀² + 2ax
Valid only when acceleration is constant in both magnitude and direction. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## M

**Motion**
Change in **position** of an object with respect to time. In kinematics, described without reference to the cause of the change. *(All levels)*

---

## O

**Origin**
The reference point from which positions are measured on a coordinate axis. The choice of origin is arbitrary — physical results are independent of this choice. *(Board)*

---

## P

**Path Length**
See *Distance*. The actual length of the trajectory followed by an object. A scalar quantity. For straight-line motion without reversal: path length = |displacement|. *(All levels)*

**Point Object**
An idealisation where the size of an object is ignored because it is negligible compared to the distances involved. Valid approximation in many real-world situations. *(Board)*

**Position**
The location of an object on the coordinate axis, measured from the origin. Symbol: x. SI unit: **m**. *(All levels)*

---

## R

**Reaction Time**
The time elapsed between a stimulus (e.g., seeing an obstacle) and the initiation of a response (e.g., applying brakes). Measurable by the ruler-drop experiment: t_r = √(2d/g) where d is the distance the ruler falls. *(Board/NEET)*

**Rectilinear Motion**
Motion along a **straight line**. The subject of Chapter 2. Positions, velocities, and accelerations are described along a single axis. *(All levels)*

**Reference Frame**
See *Frame of Reference*.

**Relative Velocity**
The velocity of one object as measured from the reference frame of another object. For objects A and B with velocities v_A and v_B relative to the ground: v_AB = v_A − v_B. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Retardation**
See *Deceleration*. A common but informal term. Strictly, retardation is the magnitude of deceleration. *(Board)*

---

## S

**Scalar**
A quantity having only magnitude, no direction. Examples in this chapter: distance, speed, time. *(All levels)*

**Slope (of a Graph)**
The tangent of the angle made by a line (or tangent to a curve) with the positive x-axis. In kinematics: slope of x–t graph = velocity; slope of v–t graph = acceleration. *(All levels)*

**Speed**
The magnitude of velocity. A **scalar** quantity. Instantaneous speed = |instantaneous velocity|; average speed ≥ |average velocity|. *(All levels)*

**Stopping Distance**
The distance an object travels after brakes are applied before coming to rest. Given by dₛ = v₀²/(2a) where v₀ is initial speed and a is the (magnitude of) deceleration. Stopping distance is **proportional to v₀²** — doubling speed quadruples stopping distance. *(Board/NEET)*

---

## U

**Uniform Acceleration**
Acceleration that is **constant** in both magnitude and direction throughout the motion. Only in this case are the three kinematic equations (v = v₀ + at, etc.) valid. *(All levels)*

**Uniform Motion**
Motion with **zero acceleration** — constant velocity. The x–t graph is a straight line; v–t graph is a horizontal line. Instantaneous velocity = average velocity at all instants. *(Board)*

---

## V

**Variable Acceleration**
Acceleration that changes with time. Kinematic equations cannot be used; **calculus** (integration/differentiation) is required. v = ∫a dt; x = ∫v dt. *(JEE)*

**Vector**
A quantity having both magnitude and direction. Examples in this chapter: displacement, velocity, acceleration. *(All levels)*

**Velocity**
The rate of change of position with respect to time. A **vector** quantity. Instantaneous velocity v = dx/dt; average velocity v̄ = Δx/Δt. SI unit: **m s⁻¹**. Dimensional formula: **[M⁰LT⁻¹]**. *(All levels)*

**v–t Graph** (Velocity-Time Graph)
A graph showing velocity on the y-axis and time on the x-axis.
- Slope of v–t graph = instantaneous acceleration
- Area under v–t graph between t₁ and t₂ = displacement in that interval
- Area using |v| = total distance *(All levels — very important)*

---

## X

**x–t Graph** (Position-Time Graph)
A graph showing position on the y-axis and time on the x-axis.
- Slope of tangent at any point = instantaneous velocity
- A straight line indicates uniform velocity (constant slope)
- A curve (parabola) indicates uniform acceleration
- A vertical line is physically impossible (object at two places at once) *(All levels — very important)*
