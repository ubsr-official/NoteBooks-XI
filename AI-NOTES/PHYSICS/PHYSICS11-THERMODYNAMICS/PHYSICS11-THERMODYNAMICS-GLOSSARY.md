### Thermodynamics

---

> All terms arranged **alphabetically** with precise scientific definitions, dimensional formulae where applicable, and exam relevance tags.

## A

**Adiabatic Process**
A thermodynamic process in which **no heat exchange** occurs between the system and its surroundings (ΔQ = 0). The system is thermally insulated. From the First Law: ΔU = −ΔW — work done by the gas comes entirely from a decrease in internal energy, so the gas **cools during adiabatic expansion** and **heats during adiabatic compression**. For an ideal gas: PVᵞ = constant, where γ = Cₚ/Cᵥ. Work done: W = μR(T₁ − T₂)/(γ − 1). Examples: sound wave propagation in air, diesel engine compression, pumping a bicycle tyre. *(All levels — critical)*

**Adiabatic Wall**
An insulating wall that does **not allow the flow of energy (heat)** between the systems it separates. A system enclosed by adiabatic walls is thermally isolated. Contrast with a diathermic wall. Ideal adiabatic walls are a theoretical construct; practical insulation (like polystyrene, glass wool, vacuum) approximates adiabatic behaviour. *(All levels)*

**Adiabatic Index (γ)**
The ratio of molar specific heat at constant pressure to molar specific heat at constant volume:
$$\gamma = \frac{C_p}{C_v}$$
Since Cₚ > Cᵥ always, γ > 1 always. Typical values: γ = 5/3 (monoatomic ideal gas); γ = 7/5 = 1.4 (diatomic ideal gas at room temperature); γ = 4/3 (triatomic/polyatomic). Appears in PVᵞ = constant (adiabatic equation) and the adiabatic work formula. Also called the **adiabatic exponent** or **heat capacity ratio**. *(NEET/JEE — critical)*

## C

**Calorie**
The old unit of heat energy, defined as the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of **1 gram of water from 14.5°C to 15.5°C**. Precisely:
$$1 \text{ cal} = 4.186 \text{ J}$$
The "mechanical equivalent of heat" (J = 4.186 J/cal) is now simply a unit conversion factor since heat is recognised as a form of energy. In SI units, the joule (J) is used for all forms of energy. *(All levels)*

**Carnot Cycle**
The idealised reversible thermodynamic cycle of maximum efficiency, proposed by Sadi Carnot (1824). Consists of four quasi-static processes:
1. Isothermal expansion at T₁ (absorb Q₁)
2. Adiabatic expansion T₁ → T₂
3. Isothermal compression at T₂ (release Q₂)
4. Adiabatic compression T₂ → T₁

The working substance returns to its initial state after one complete cycle. *(NEET/JEE — critical)*

**Carnot Engine**
A reversible heat engine operating between exactly two temperature reservoirs (T₁ and T₂). Its efficiency is:
$$\eta_{Carnot} = 1 - \frac{T_2}{T_1}$$
**Carnot's theorem** states: (a) no engine working between two temperatures can have efficiency greater than a Carnot engine between the same temperatures; (b) the efficiency of a Carnot engine is independent of the working substance. *(All levels — critical)*

**Carnot's Theorem**
See **Carnot Engine.** The two key statements: (a) Maximum possible efficiency between T₁ and T₂ = 1 − T₂/T₁. (b) Efficiency of any Carnot engine depends only on T₁ and T₂, not on the working substance. Proved using the Second Law of Thermodynamics by assuming the opposite and showing it leads to a violation. *(NEET/JEE — critical)*

**Clausius Statement**
One of the two classical statements of the Second Law of Thermodynamics:
> *No process is possible whose sole result is the transfer of heat from a colder object to a hotter object.*
Named after Rudolf Clausius (1822–1888). Denies the possibility of a perfect refrigerator. Equivalent to the Kelvin-Planck statement. *(All levels — critical)*

**Coefficient of Performance (COP)**
A measure of the efficiency of a refrigerator or heat pump:
- For a refrigerator: $\beta = Q_2/W = Q_2/(Q_1 - Q_2)$ (heat removed from cold body per unit work input)
- For a heat pump: $\beta' = Q_1/W = Q_1/(Q_1 - Q_2)$ (heat delivered to hot body per unit work input)

For a Carnot refrigerator: β = T₂/(T₁ − T₂). Second Law states β can never be infinite (W can never be zero). *(NEET/JEE)*

**Conservation of Energy (First Law)**
See **First Law of Thermodynamics.** *(All levels)*

**Cyclic Process**
A thermodynamic process in which the system **returns to its initial state** after a series of changes. Since internal energy is a state variable: ΔU = 0 for any cyclic process. Therefore ΔQ = ΔW — the total heat absorbed by the system equals the total work done by it. On a P-V diagram, a cyclic process traces a **closed loop**; the work done equals the area enclosed by the loop. *(All levels — critical)*

## D

**Diathermic Wall**
A conducting wall that **allows the flow of energy (heat)** between the systems it separates. In contact via a diathermic wall, two systems exchange heat until they reach thermal equilibrium (same temperature). Contrast with an adiabatic wall. A thin metal sheet is a good approximation. *(All levels)*

**Dissipative Effects**
Irreversible processes (friction, viscosity, turbulence, electrical resistance) that convert organised mechanical energy into disordered thermal energy (heat). Dissipative effects make a process **irreversible** — the original state cannot be recovered without external effort. Since dissipative effects are present everywhere in real systems, truly reversible processes are an idealization. *(All levels)*

**Dulong-Petit Law**
The empirical law stating that the molar specific heat of solids at constant pressure is approximately:
$$C \approx 3R \approx 24.9 \text{ J mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$$
Derived from the law of equipartition of energy applied to a 3D harmonic oscillator. Valid at room temperature for most metals. Exceptions include carbon (diamond), silicon, and other materials where quantum effects are significant even at room temperature. Breaks down at low temperatures for all materials. *(NEET/JEE)*

## E

**Efficiency (of a Heat Engine) (η)**
The ratio of work output to the heat absorbed from the hot source:
$$\eta = \frac{W}{Q_1} = 1 - \frac{Q_2}{Q_1}$$
Dimensionless (0 < η < 1; often expressed as percentage). The Second Law states η < 1 always. Maximum efficiency (for Carnot engine): η = 1 − T₂/T₁. For a perfect engine: η would = 1 — but this is forbidden by the Second Law. *(All levels — critical)*

**Entropy (S)**
A state variable (beyond this syllabus level, introduced conceptually): the measure of the degree of disorder or randomness of a system. For a reversible process: dS = dQ_rev/T. Entropy always increases (or remains constant) for any spontaneous process in an isolated system — this is another way to state the Second Law. The direction of irreversibility is always towards increasing entropy. Unit: J K⁻¹. Dimensional formula: [ML²T⁻²K⁻¹]. *(JEE Advanced — conceptual introduction)*

**Equation of State**
A mathematical relation connecting the state variables of a system in thermodynamic equilibrium. For an ideal gas: PV = μRT. For a real gas: various more complex equations. Connects pressure, volume, and temperature — only two are independent for a fixed quantity of gas. The equation of state can be used to plot isotherms (PV = constant at fixed T) or adiabats (PVᵞ = constant for adiabatic process). *(All levels)*

**Extensive Variable**
A thermodynamic state variable whose value is proportional to the **size (amount) of the system**. Examples: internal energy U, volume V, total mass M, total entropy S, heat capacity. Halving the system halves each extensive variable. Product of an intensive variable and an extensive variable is extensive (e.g., PΔV). *(NEET/JEE)*

## F

**First Law of Thermodynamics**
The general law of conservation of energy applied to thermodynamic systems:
$$\Delta Q = \Delta U + \Delta W$$
where ΔQ = heat supplied to the system, ΔU = change in internal energy, ΔW = work done by the system. Stated differently: energy is neither created nor destroyed — it is only converted. The First Law does not predict which direction a process will proceed — that is the role of the Second Law. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## H

**Heat (ΔQ)**
Energy transferred between a system and its surroundings due to a **temperature difference**. Heat is NOT stored in a body — it only exists as energy in transit. "A body contains heat" is a meaningless statement; "heat is supplied to a body" is meaningful. Not a state variable (path-dependent). SI unit: J. Dimensional formula: [ML²T⁻²]. *(All levels — critical)*

**Heat Capacity (S)**
The amount of heat required to raise the temperature of a given body by 1 K:
$$S = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta T}$$
SI unit: J K⁻¹. Depends on the mass and nature of the material AND the process (constant P or constant V). Distinguish from specific heat capacity (per kg) and molar heat capacity (per mole). *(All levels)*

**Heat Engine**
A device that **converts heat energy into mechanical work** in a cyclic process. Operates between a hot source (T₁) and a cold sink (T₂). Absorbs Q₁ from source, performs work W = Q₁ − Q₂, and rejects Q₂ to the sink. Efficiency η = W/Q₁ = 1 − Q₂/Q₁. By the Second Law, Q₂ > 0 always — not all absorbed heat can be converted to work. Real examples: steam engine, petrol engine, diesel engine. *(All levels — critical)*

**Heat Reservoir**
A large body with such a large heat capacity that its temperature remains essentially unchanged regardless of how much heat is absorbed from or given to it. The ocean, a large body of water, or the atmosphere can approximate a heat reservoir. In Carnot cycle analysis, the hot and cold reservoirs are ideal heat reservoirs at fixed temperatures T₁ and T₂. *(All levels)*

## I

**Internal Energy (U)**
The **sum of the kinetic and potential energies of all the molecular constituents** of a system, in the frame of reference in which the centre of mass of the system is at rest. Includes translational KE, rotational KE, vibrational KE of molecules, and intermolecular PE. Does NOT include bulk kinetic energy of the system as a whole. Key property: **U is a state variable** — depends only on the current thermodynamic state (P, V, T), not on the path taken to reach it. For an ideal gas: U depends only on temperature. SI unit: J. Dimensional formula: [ML²T⁻²]. *(All levels — critical)*

**Intensive Variable**
A thermodynamic state variable that is **independent of the size (amount) of the system**. Examples: pressure P, temperature T, density ρ, molar heat capacity C, specific heat capacity s. Dividing the system into halves does not change intensive variables. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Irreversible Process**
A thermodynamic process that **cannot be reversed** such that both the system and surroundings return to their original states without any other change anywhere. Irreversibility arises from: (1) non-equilibrium states (free expansion, explosive reaction), and (2) dissipative effects (friction, viscosity). All naturally occurring spontaneous processes are irreversible. Irreversibility is the rule, not the exception. *(All levels — critical)*

**Isobaric Process**
A thermodynamic process in which **pressure remains constant** (ΔP = 0). Work done by the gas: W = PΔV = μR(T₂ − T₁). Heat absorbed: ΔQ = μCₚΔT. Since temperature changes, internal energy changes too. Specific heat at constant pressure (Cₚ) governs heat requirements. Example: boiling water at standard atmospheric pressure, heating gas in a balloon. *(All levels — critical)*

**Isochoric Process (Isovolumetric)**
A thermodynamic process in which **volume remains constant** (ΔV = 0). No work is done: ΔW = PΔV = 0. From First Law: ΔQ = ΔU = μCᵥΔT. All supplied heat goes to changing internal energy and temperature. Govened by specific heat at constant volume (Cᵥ). Example: heating gas in a rigid sealed container. *(All levels — critical)*

**Isotherm**
A curve on a P-V diagram representing states of a system at **constant temperature**. For an ideal gas, the isotherm is a hyperbola (PV = μRT = constant at fixed T). Different temperatures give different hyperbolas — higher temperature → isotherm further from origin. Relevant in Carnot cycle analysis. *(All levels)*

**Isothermal Process**
A thermodynamic process in which **temperature remains constant** (ΔT = 0). For an ideal gas: PV = constant (Boyle's Law). Since U depends only on T for an ideal gas: ΔU = 0. By First Law: ΔQ = ΔW. Work done:
$$W = \mu RT \ln\frac{V_2}{V_1}$$
The gas must be in contact with a large reservoir at constant temperature to maintain isothermal conditions. Example: expansion of gas in a metallic cylinder placed in a temperature bath. *(All levels — critical)*

## K

**Kelvin-Planck Statement**
One of the two classical statements of the Second Law of Thermodynamics:
> *No process is possible whose sole result is the absorption of heat from a reservoir and the complete conversion of the heat into work.*
Named after Lord Kelvin (William Thomson) and Max Planck. Denies the possibility of a perfect heat engine (η = 100%). Equivalent to the Clausius statement. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## M

**Mayer's Relation**
The relation between molar specific heat capacities of an ideal gas at constant pressure and constant volume:
$$C_p - C_v = R$$
where R = 8.31 J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹ is the universal gas constant. Derived by Julius Robert von Mayer. The difference arises because at constant pressure, the gas must also do work (PΔV = RΔT per mole) in addition to increasing its internal energy, requiring additional heat. *(All levels — critical)*

**Molar Specific Heat Capacity (C)**
The amount of heat required to raise the temperature of **1 mole** of a substance by 1 K:
$$C = \frac{1}{\mu}\frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta T}$$
SI unit: J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹. For gases: Cₚ (at constant pressure) and Cᵥ (at constant volume) must be specified separately since heat capacity depends on the process. For solids: C ≈ 3R (Dulong-Petit law). *(All levels — critical)*

---

## P

**Path Function / Path-Dependent Quantity**
A quantity whose value depends on the **path taken** between two states, not just on the initial and final states. Examples: work (ΔW) and heat (ΔQ) are path functions — the same initial and final states can be connected by different processes giving different ΔQ and ΔW. Contrast with state functions (U, P, T, V). *(NEET/JEE — critical)*

**Perfect Heat Engine**
A hypothetical engine that converts ALL absorbed heat into work (η = 1 or 100%). Forbidden by the Kelvin-Planck statement of the Second Law of Thermodynamics. Would violate the Second Law because it would require no heat rejection to a cold reservoir (Q₂ = 0). *(All levels)*

**Perfect Refrigerator**
A hypothetical refrigerator that transfers heat from a cold to a hot body without any external work input (COP = ∞). Forbidden by the Clausius statement of the Second Law. *(All levels)*

---

## Q

**Quasi-Static Process**
An idealised thermodynamic process that is **infinitely slow** such that the system remains in **thermal and mechanical equilibrium with its surroundings at every instant**. At every stage, the pressure and temperature of the system differ only infinitesimally from those of the surroundings. Can be represented as a continuous path on a P-V diagram. A quasi-static process is not necessarily reversible (it also needs to be non-dissipative). Quasi-static processes are idealisations — real processes are approximations. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## R

**Refrigerator**
A device that uses external work to **transfer heat from a cold body to a hot body** (opposite of a heat engine). Coefficient of performance: β = Q₂/W = Q₂/(Q₁ − Q₂). Second Law: β can never be infinite. Carnot (ideal) refrigerator: β = T₂/(T₁ − T₂). A heat pump is a refrigerator used for heating — same device, different focus. *(All levels)*

**Reversible Process**
A thermodynamic process (state i → state f) that can be **reversed** such that both the system and its surroundings return to their original states with **no other change anywhere in the universe**. A process is reversible only if it is: (1) **quasi-static** AND (2) **free of dissipative effects** (friction, viscosity, etc.). Reversible processes are idealisations — all real processes are irreversible to some degree. A reversible engine achieves maximum possible efficiency. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## S

**Second Law of Thermodynamics**
A fundamental law of nature with two equivalent classical statements (Kelvin-Planck and Clausius) that determine the **direction of spontaneous processes** and limit the efficiency of heat engines and refrigerators. The Second Law cannot be derived from the First Law — it is an independent empirical fact about nature. It implies: (i) no perfect heat engine; (ii) no perfect refrigerator; (iii) all spontaneous processes are irreversible; (iv) entropy of an isolated system never decreases. *(All levels — critical)*

**Specific Heat Capacity (s)**
The amount of heat required to raise the temperature of **1 kg** of a substance by 1 K:
$$s = \frac{1}{m}\frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta T}$$
SI unit: J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹. Dimensional formula: [L²T⁻²K⁻¹]. Depends on the nature of the substance and (slightly) on temperature and process. For gases, s depends strongly on the process (constant P or constant V). Water has the highest specific heat capacity among common substances: s = 4186 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹. *(All levels — critical)*

**State Variable (State Function)**
A thermodynamic quantity whose value depends only on the **current state** of the system, not on the path taken to reach that state. Examples: internal energy U, pressure P, volume V, temperature T. ΔU between any two states is the same regardless of path. Contrast with path functions (ΔQ, ΔW). *(All levels — critical)*

---

## T

**Thermal Equilibrium**
The condition of a system (or two systems in contact) when the macroscopic variables no longer change with time. Two systems are in thermal equilibrium when they have the same temperature. The basis of the Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics. *(All levels — critical)*

**Thermodynamic System**
Any portion of matter (or space) that is under study. Described by macroscopic state variables (P, V, T, etc.) when in equilibrium. The rest of the universe (excluding the system) is the **surroundings** or **environment**. Interactions between system and surroundings occur via heat and work exchanges. *(All levels)*

**Thermodynamics**
The branch of physics dealing with the concepts of **heat, temperature, and the inter-conversion of heat and other forms of energy**. A macroscopic science — does not use molecular descriptions. Its four laws (Zeroth through Third) govern all thermodynamic processes. Central to understanding engines, refrigerators, phase transitions, and chemical reactions. *(All levels)*

---

## W

**Work (ΔW) in Thermodynamics**
Energy transferred to/from a system by means OTHER than temperature difference. For a gas in a cylinder with a movable piston at external pressure P:
$$\Delta W = P\Delta V$$
Positive when the system expands (gas does work); negative when the system is compressed (work done on gas). ΔW is a path function (depends on the process). Not a state variable. SI unit: J. Dimensional formula: [ML²T⁻²]. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## Z

**Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics**
*Two systems in thermal equilibrium with a third system separately are in thermal equilibrium with each other.*
Formulated by R.H. Fowler in 1931. Named "zeroth" because it logically precedes the First and Second Laws, yet was recognised after them. The Zeroth Law provides the conceptual foundation for the definition of **temperature** as a measurable, comparable quantity. If Tₐ = T꜀ and T_B = T꜀, then Tₐ = T_B. *(All levels — critical)*

## Common Misconceptions Clarified

| Misconception | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| "A body contains heat" | Heat is energy IN TRANSIT. Bodies contain *internal energy*, not heat. |
| U and Q are the same thing | U is a *state variable* (stored energy). Q is *energy in transit* due to ΔT — a path function. |
| ΔW and ΔQ are state functions | They are *path functions* — their values depend on the process, not just initial/final states. |
| Isothermal → no energy change | For an ideal gas, ΔU = 0 isothermally. But heat IS exchanged (ΔQ = ΔW ≠ 0 in general). |
| Adiabatic → temperature constant | Adiabatic means *no heat exchange*, NOT constant temperature. T changes in adiabatic processes. |
| Carnot efficiency can be 100% | Would require T₂ = 0 K (absolute zero) — unachievable by Third Law. η < 1 always. |
| All quasi-static processes are reversible | Quasi-static is *necessary* but not *sufficient*. A quasi-static process with friction is still irreversible. |
| Heat flows from high KE to low KE | Heat flows from HIGH TEMPERATURE to LOW TEMPERATURE, not necessarily high KE (a large cold body may have more total KE than a small hot body). |
| Cₚ = Cᵥ for all substances | True only for solids (approximately) where ΔV ≈ 0. For gases: Cₚ − Cᵥ = R. Always Cₚ > Cᵥ for gases. |
| A Carnot engine is the most efficient real engine | Carnot engine is the most efficient *possible* (theoretical ideal). No real engine can achieve it. |
