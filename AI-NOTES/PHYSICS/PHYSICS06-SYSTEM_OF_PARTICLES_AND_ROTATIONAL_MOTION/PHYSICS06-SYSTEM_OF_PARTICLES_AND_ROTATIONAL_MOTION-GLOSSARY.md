# CHAPTER 6 — GLOSSARY & TERMINOLOGY

### Systems of Particles and Rotational Motion

---

> All terms arranged **alphabetically** with precise scientific definitions, dimensional formulae where applicable, and exam relevance tags.

---

## A

**Angular Acceleration (α)**
The rate of change of angular velocity with respect to time. Defined as α = dω/dt. For fixed-axis rotation, α is a scalar; in general, it is a vector along the axis. SI unit: rad s⁻² = [T⁻²]. It is the rotational analogue of linear acceleration. *(All levels — critical)*

**Angular Displacement (θ)**
The angle subtended at the centre of the circular path by the position vector of a rotating particle. It is measured in radians (rad). The rotational analogue of linear displacement. Dimensionless (or [M⁰L⁰T⁰] with the radian being a supplementary unit). *(All levels)*

**Angular Momentum (L or l)**
The rotational analogue of linear momentum. For a single particle: **l** = **r** × **p** = **r** × m**v**; magnitude l = rp sinθ = r⊥p = rp⊥. For a system: **L** = Σ**lᵢ**. For rotation about a symmetry axis: L = Iω. SI unit: kg m² s⁻¹ (equivalently J s). Dimensional formula: **[ML²T⁻¹]**. *(All levels — critical)*

**Angular Velocity (ω)**
The rate of change of angular displacement. ω = dθ/dt. It is a vector directed along the axis of rotation by the right-hand screw rule. The magnitude is ω = dθ/dt. SI unit: rad s⁻¹. Dimensional formula: **[T⁻¹]**. All particles of a rigid body share the same angular velocity at any instant. *(All levels — critical)*

**Axis of Rotation**
The straight line about which a rigid body rotates. Particles on the axis have zero linear velocity. The axis may be fixed (ceiling fan) or moving (spinning top). For fixed-axis rotation, the angular velocity vector lies along this axis. *(All levels)*

---

## C

**Centre of Gravity (CG)**
The point of a body at which the total gravitational torque on the body is zero. Defined by: Σ**rᵢ** × mᵢ**g** = 0. In a uniform gravitational field (g is the same everywhere), the CG coincides with the **centre of mass**. For very large bodies in a non-uniform g, CG ≠ COM. *(Board/NEET)*

**Centre of Mass (COM)**
The unique point of a system of particles whose position is defined as the mass-weighted mean position: **R** = Σmᵢ**rᵢ**/M. The COM moves according to Newton's second law under external forces only: MA = F_ext. For uniform symmetric bodies, COM = geometric centre. The COM can lie outside the material of the body (e.g., ring, hollow sphere). SI unit: m. *(All levels — critical)*

**Centroid**
The geometric centre of a uniform lamina or figure; the point where the medians intersect for a triangle. For a uniform body, the centroid coincides with the COM. Not the same as COM for non-uniform bodies. *(Board)*

**Couple**
A pair of forces of equal magnitude, acting in opposite directions, with different lines of action (non-collinear). Net force of a couple = 0 (no translation). Net torque of a couple = F × d (the perpendicular distance between the lines of action), which is non-zero (rotation occurs). Torque of a couple is independent of the origin chosen — this gives a basis for the origin-independence of rotational equilibrium. Examples: turning a bottle cap, opening a water tap, compass needle in a magnetic field. *(All levels — critical)*

**Cross Product**
See: **Vector Product**.

## E

**Equilibrium (Mechanical)**
A rigid body is in mechanical equilibrium when: (1) Translational equilibrium — net external force is zero: ΣFᵢ = 0; and (2) Rotational equilibrium — net external torque is zero: Στᵢ = 0. This means neither linear nor angular acceleration. If only (1), it is translational equilibrium; if only (2), rotational equilibrium. *(All levels — critical)*

**Extended Body**
A body with finite size, as opposed to a point particle. It is treated as a system of particles. Its motion can be decomposed into translation of the COM and rotation about the COM. *(All levels)*

---

## F

**Fixed Axis Rotation**
Rotation in which the axis of rotation remains stationary in space. Each particle moves in a circle in a plane perpendicular to the axis. This is the main type of rotation studied in this chapter. All particles share the same ω at any instant. Examples: ceiling fan, wheel spinning about its axle. *(All levels)*

**Flywheel**
A heavy disc-shaped body with large moment of inertia used in engines (steam, automobile) to resist sudden changes in angular speed. Because of its large I, it absorbs and releases rotational kinetic energy smoothly, preventing jerky motions. *(Board/NEET)*

**Fulcrum**
The pivot point of a lever. The reaction from the support acts at the fulcrum. In rotational equilibrium of a lever: taking moments about the fulcrum — load arm × load = effort arm × effort. *(Board)*

---

## L

**Lever**
A rigid rod pivoted at a point (the fulcrum). The mechanical advantage (M.A.) = effort arm / load arm = F₁/F₂. When M.A. > 1, a small effort can lift a large load. Examples: seesaw, beam balance, scissors, wheelbarrow, human forearm. *(Board/NEET)*

**Linear Momentum of a System**
For a system of n particles: **P** = Σmᵢ**vᵢ** = M**V**, where **V** is the velocity of the COM and M is the total mass. Newton's second law for the system: d**P**/dt = **F**_ext. If **F**_ext = 0, **P** is conserved (COM moves at constant velocity). *(All levels — critical)*

---

## M

**Mechanical Advantage (M.A.)**
The ratio of the load force to the effort force in a lever: M.A. = F₁/F₂ = d₂/d₁ (effort arm / load arm). A mechanical advantage > 1 means a small effort can balance a large load. *(Board)*

**Moment of a Force**
See: **Torque**.

**Moment of Inertia (I)**
A measure of the rotational inertia of a rigid body — it resists angular acceleration (τ = Iα), exactly as mass resists linear acceleration (F = ma). Defined as I = Σmᵢrᵢ², where rᵢ is the perpendicular distance of the iᵗʰ particle from the axis. It depends on (i) mass, (ii) mass distribution, (iii) position and orientation of the axis. SI unit: kg m². Dimensional formula: **[ML²]**. I = Mk², where k is the radius of gyration. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## P

**Perpendicular Axis Theorem** *(mentioned implicitly)*
For a planar body (lamina) in the xy plane: Iz = Ix + Iy (sum of moments about two perpendicular axes in the plane = moment about the perpendicular axis to the plane through the same point). Not explicitly derived in NCERT Class XI but used to get ring/disc results. *(JEE)*

**Precession**
The motion of the axis of a spinning body (like a top) about a vertical line, sweeping out a cone. It occurs due to the torque of gravity about the contact point. The axis of the spinning top is not fixed during precession. *(Board — conceptual)*

**Pure Rotation**
Motion in which a rigid body rotates about a fixed axis and has no translational motion. Every particle moves in a circle about the axis. The body has a fixed pivot or fixed line. *(All levels)*

**Pure Translation**
Motion in which all particles of a rigid body have the same velocity at every instant. The body moves without rotating. *(All levels)*

---

## R

**Radius of Gyration (k)**
A geometric property of a body-axis system defined by I = Mk². The radius of gyration is the distance from the axis at which, if the entire mass M were concentrated, it would give the same moment of inertia I. It depends on the shape of the body and the axis. Unit: m. *(Board/NEET)*

**Rigid Body**
Ideally, a body whose shape is perfectly definite and unchanging — the distances between all pairs of particles are fixed, regardless of forces. No real body is truly rigid, but many bodies can be approximated as rigid when deformations are negligible. *(All levels)*

**Rolling Motion**
A combination of translation and rotation. A wheel or cylinder rolls without slipping when v = Rω (linear speed of centre = angular speed × radius). Total KE = ½Mv²_cm + ½Iω² (translational + rotational). *(Board/NEET — brief mention in chapter)*

**Rotational Equilibrium**
Condition when the net external torque on a rigid body is zero: Στᵢ = 0. This means the total angular momentum is not changing with time. Does not imply translational equilibrium. *(All levels)*

---

## S

**Scalar Product**
See Chapter 5. Relevant here for contrast with the vector product: work W = **F**·**d** (dot product, scalar), torque τ = **r** × **F** (cross product, vector). *(All levels)*

**System of Particles**
Any collection of particles (whether arranged as a rigid body, deformable body, or independently moving particles) whose collective behaviour is studied. The COM concept is the key tool for analysing such systems. *(All levels)*

---

## T

**Torque (τ)**
The rotational analogue of force; the physical quantity that produces angular acceleration in a rigid body. Defined as **τ** = **r** × **F**; magnitude τ = rF sinθ = r⊥F = rF⊥. It is a vector perpendicular to both **r** and **F**. Also called moment of force. SI unit: N m. Dimensional formula: **[ML²T⁻²]** — same dimensions as work, but torque is a vector and work is a scalar. τ = 0 when F passes through the origin, or F is parallel to r. For a system: τ_ext = dL/dt. *(All levels — critical)*

**Translational Equilibrium**
Condition when the net external force on a rigid body is zero: ΣFᵢ = 0. Total linear momentum is constant. Does not imply rotational equilibrium. *(All levels)*

---

## V

**Vector Product (Cross Product)**
A mathematical operation on two vectors **a** and **b** yielding a vector **c** = **a** × **b** such that: (i) |**c**| = ab sinθ; (ii) **c** is perpendicular to the plane of **a** and **b**; (iii) direction given by the right-hand screw rule (curl fingers from **a** to **b** through the smaller angle; thumb points in direction of **c**). Key properties: anti-commutative (**a** × **b** = −**b** × **a**), distributive, **a** × **a** = 0. For unit vectors: î × ĵ = k̂; ĵ × k̂ = î; k̂ × î = ĵ (cyclic order). Critical applications: torque (τ = **r** × **F**) and angular momentum (**l** = **r** × **p**). *(All levels — critical)*

## Contrast Table — Translation vs Rotation

| Aspect | Translational Motion | Rotational Motion (fixed axis) |
|---|---|---|
| Fundamental variable | Displacement x | Angular displacement θ |
| Rate quantity | Velocity v | Angular velocity ω |
| Rate of rate | Acceleration a | Angular acceleration α |
| Inertia measure | Mass M | Moment of inertia I |
| Cause of change | Force F = Ma | Torque τ = Iα |
| Energy | KE = ½Mv² | K_rot = ½Iω² |
| Momentum | p = Mv | L = Iω |
| Work | dW = F ds | dW = τ dθ |
| Power | P = Fv | P = τω |
| 2nd Law | F = dp/dt | τ = dL/dt |
| Conservation | F_ext = 0 → p = const | τ_ext = 0 → L = const |

## Common Misconceptions Clarified

| Misconception | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| COM always lies inside the body | COM can be outside (ring, hollow sphere, L-shape) |
| Torque and work are the same (same dimensions) | Same dimensions [ML²T⁻²] but torque is a vector (cross product), work is a scalar (dot product) — entirely different physical quantities |
| I is a fixed property of a body like mass | I depends on the axis of rotation; same body can have multiple values of I |
| Angular momentum of a linearly moving particle is zero | L = r⊥mv ≠ 0 for a point not on the line of motion |
| When a skater pulls in arms, energy is also conserved | L = Iω is conserved, but KE = L²/(2I) increases — extra energy comes from muscular work |
| A couple has both zero force and zero torque | A couple has zero net force BUT non-zero torque — it causes rotation without translation |
| Centre of gravity and centre of mass always coincide | They coincide only in uniform g; for very extended bodies in non-uniform g, they differ |
