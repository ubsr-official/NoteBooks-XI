# CHAPTER 4 — GLOSSARY & TERMINOLOGY

### Laws of Motion

---

> All terms arranged **alphabetically** with precise scientific definitions, dimensional formulae where applicable, and exam relevance tags.

---

## A

**Action (in Newton's Third Law)**
The force exerted by one body on another. In the Third Law, "action" simply means **force** — it does not imply temporal priority or causation. Any one of the two mutual forces between bodies A and B can be labelled the action; the other is the reaction. Action and reaction are **simultaneous** and act on **different bodies**. *(Board/NEET)*

**Angle of Friction (λ)**
The angle between the resultant contact force (vector sum of normal reaction N and limiting static friction fs) and the normal to the surface. tan λ = (fs)max/N = μs. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Angle of Repose (θr)**
The maximum angle of inclination of a surface at which a body placed on it remains stationary without sliding. At this angle, the gravity component along the incline equals the maximum static friction: tan θr = μs. Also called the **critical angle**. *(All levels — critical)*

**Apparent Weight**
The normal force exerted by a surface on a body, as measured by a weighing scale. Equal to actual weight (mg) only when acceleration = 0. In a lift accelerating upward: N = m(g+a) > mg. In free fall: N = 0 (weightlessness). Dimensional formula: **[MLT⁻²]**. *(All levels)*

**Aristotle's Fallacy**
The incorrect belief, held by Aristotle (384–322 BC), that a continuous external force is needed to maintain a body in uniform motion. The error lies in failing to account for friction as an opposing force. Corrected by Galileo in the 17th century. *(Board/NEET)*

**Atwood Machine**
A device consisting of two unequal masses (m₁ > m₂) connected by an inextensible string over a frictionless pulley. Used to study Newton's second law. Acceleration: a = (m₁−m₂)g/(m₁+m₂). Tension: T = 2m₁m₂g/(m₁+m₂). *(NEET/JEE)*

---

## B

**Banking (of Roads)**
The practice of making the outer edge of a curved road higher than the inner edge (tilting the road surface at angle θ). Purpose: to reduce reliance on friction for providing centripetal force in circular motion. The horizontal component of the normal reaction provides centripetal force at the optimum speed. *(All levels — critical)*

**Banking Angle (θ)**
The angle at which a road is banked, measured from the horizontal. Optimum speed: v₀ = √(Rg tanθ). At v₀, no friction is needed. *(All levels)*

---

## C

**Centripetal Force (fc)**
The net force directed **toward the centre** of a circular path, required to maintain circular motion. Magnitude: fc = mv²/R. **NOT a new type of force** — it is always provided by some real physical force (tension, gravity, friction, normal reaction, etc.). Dimensional formula: **[MLT⁻²]**. *(All levels — critical)*

**Coefficient of Kinetic Friction (μk)**
A dimensionless constant characteristic of a pair of surfaces in sliding contact. Defined by: fk = μk N. Always less than μs. Approximately independent of speed and area of contact. Values: steel on steel ≈ 0.1–0.2; rubber on road ≈ 0.6–0.8. *(All levels)*

**Coefficient of Static Friction (μs)**
A dimensionless constant characteristic of a pair of surfaces on the verge of sliding. Defined by: (fs)max = μs N. Always greater than μk. Equals tan(angle of repose). *(All levels)*

**Contact Force**
Any force that arises due to physical contact between two bodies. Examples: normal reaction, friction, tension, spring force, viscous drag, buoyancy. All contact forces at the microscopic level originate from **electrical forces** between charged constituents of matter. *(Board/NEET)*

**Conservation of Momentum**
The total linear momentum of an isolated system (net external force = zero) remains constant. Follows directly from Newton's second and third laws. Holds for ALL types of collisions (elastic and inelastic). **p₁ + p₂ = p₁' + p₂'**. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## E

**Equilibrium of a Particle**
The state in which the net external force on a particle is zero. The particle is either at rest (static equilibrium) or moving with constant velocity (dynamic equilibrium). Conditions: ΣFx = 0, ΣFy = 0, ΣFz = 0. *(All levels)*

---

## F

**Free-Body Diagram (FBD)**
A simplified diagram showing a **chosen body in isolation** with all external forces acting on it drawn as vectors, with their magnitudes and directions. Forces that the body exerts on other bodies are NOT included. The essential tool for applying Newton's laws systematically. *(All levels — critical)*

**Free Fall**
Motion of a body under gravity alone, with no other force (particularly no normal reaction). Acceleration = g downward. Apparent weight = 0. All objects in free fall near Earth experience the same acceleration g (independent of mass). *(Board/NEET)*

**Friction**
The component of the contact force **parallel to the surfaces in contact** that opposes relative motion (actual or impending) between the surfaces. Not a fundamental force — arises from electromagnetic interactions at the molecular scale. Laws of friction are **empirical** (approximate but practically useful). *(All levels — critical)*

---

## G

**Galileo Galilei (1564–1642)**
Italian physicist who, through inclined plane experiments, established the law of inertia: a body moving on a frictionless horizontal surface continues moving indefinitely with constant velocity. This insight directly led to Newton's First Law. Galileo also established the independence of horizontal and vertical components of motion. *(Historical/Board)*

**Gravitational Force (Weight)**
The force exerted by the Earth on a body due to gravity: W = mg. Acts vertically downward through the centre of mass. A non-contact force. Not the same as the normal reaction (they are NOT an action-reaction pair when both act on the same body). *(All levels)*

---

## I

**Impulsive Force**
A large force acting for a very short time that produces a measurable change in momentum. Examples: bat hitting ball, bullet hitting target, hammer hitting nail. Impulse = FΔt = Δp. **Not a different category of force** in Newtonian mechanics — just a large force acting briefly. *(Board/NEET)*

**Impulse**
The product of force and the time duration for which it acts. Numerically equal to the change in momentum produced: Impulse = FΔt = Δp. SI unit: **N s** = **kg m s⁻¹**. Dimensional formula: **[MLT⁻¹]**. Useful when F and Δt are individually unknown but Δp is measurable. *(All levels — critical)*

**Inertia**
The inherent property of a body to **resist any change** in its state of rest or uniform linear motion. Measured quantitatively by **mass** (greater mass → greater inertia → greater resistance). The word "inertia" comes from the Latin for "laziness" or "resistance." *(Board)*

**Inertial Frame of Reference**
A reference frame in which Newton's First Law holds — i.e., a frame that is at rest or moving with constant velocity relative to distant stars (approximately). Newton's laws are valid only in inertial frames. A non-inertial frame (accelerating frame) requires the introduction of pseudo (fictitious) forces. *(Board/JEE)*

**Isolated System**
A system on which the net external force is zero. In an isolated system, total momentum is conserved. Example: gun + bullet system before and after firing (if fired horizontally on a frictionless surface). *(All levels)*

---

## K

**Kinetic Friction**
See Coefficient of Kinetic Friction and Friction. Acts when surfaces are in relative sliding motion. fk = μkN. Opposes actual relative motion. *(All levels)*

---

## L

**Law of Inertia**
Galileo's principle that a body moving on a frictionless surface continues to move at constant velocity indefinitely. The basis of Newton's First Law. See also: **Inertia**. *(Board)*

**Limiting Friction**
The maximum value of static friction, equal to μsN. Once applied force exceeds limiting friction, the body begins to slide. Also called maximum static friction. *(Board/NEET)*

---

## M

**Momentum (Linear Momentum)**
The product of mass and velocity of a body: **p** = m**v**. A **vector quantity** in the direction of velocity. SI unit: **kg m s⁻¹** = **N s**. Dimensional formula: **[MLT⁻¹]**. The "quantity of motion" — central to Newton's Second Law and to collision analysis. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## N

**Newton (N)**
The SI unit of force. Defined as the force that produces an acceleration of 1 m s⁻² in a mass of 1 kg. 1 N = 1 kg m s⁻². Dimensional formula: **[MLT⁻²]**. Named after Isaac Newton. *(All levels)*

**Newton's First Law**
Every body continues in its state of rest or uniform motion in a straight line unless acted upon by a net external force. Defines inertia, force, and inertial frames. Simple form: ΣF = 0 ↔ a = 0. *(All levels — critical)*

**Newton's Second Law**
The rate of change of momentum of a body is proportional to the applied force and occurs in the direction of the force: **F** = d**p**/dt = m**a** (for constant mass). SI unit: newton. A **vector law** (equivalent to three scalar equations). A **local law** (F at an instant determines a at that instant). *(All levels — critical)*

**Newton's Third Law**
To every action there is always an equal and opposite reaction: **F**_AB = −**F**_BA. Forces always occur in pairs acting on different bodies simultaneously. No cause-effect relation between action and reaction. Foundation of momentum conservation. *(All levels — critical)*

**Normal Force (Normal Reaction, N or R)**
The component of the contact force **perpendicular** to the surfaces in contact. Always a push (never pull) on the body from the surface. Self-adjusting. NOT always equal to mg (different in lifts, on inclines, on curved surfaces). *(All levels)*

---

## O

**Optimum Speed (on banked road)**
The speed at which a car can navigate a banked curve without any friction: v₀ = √(Rg tanθ). At this speed, the horizontal component of N alone provides centripetal force. No tyre wear at this speed. *(NEET/JEE)*

---

## R

**Reaction**
The force exerted by body A on body B when body B exerts a force on A (Newton's Third Law). See **Action**. *(Board)*

**Recoil**
The backward motion of a gun (or any launcher) when a projectile is fired forward, due to conservation of momentum. The recoil velocity: v_gun = −(m_bullet/m_gun) × v_bullet. *(Board/NEET)*

**Rolling Friction**
The resistance to rolling motion of a sphere or cylinder on a surface, arising from deformation at the contact point. Much smaller than static or kinetic friction (2–3 orders of magnitude). Reason for the importance of wheels. *(Board)*

---

## S

**Self-Adjusting Force**
A force that automatically adjusts its magnitude to maintain equilibrium, up to a certain limit. Static friction and normal force are self-adjusting forces. If the limit is exceeded, the equilibrium breaks. *(Board)*

**Spring Constant (k)**
The constant of proportionality in Hooke's Law: F = −kx. Measures the stiffness of a spring. Higher k → stiffer spring → more force needed per unit extension. SI unit: **N m⁻¹**. Dimensional formula: **[MT⁻²]**. *(Board/NEET)*

**Static Friction (fs)**
Friction that opposes **impending** relative motion between surfaces. Self-adjusting: 0 ≤ fs ≤ μsN. At limiting condition: (fs)max = μsN. Comes into play only when there is an applied force tending to cause sliding. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## T

**Tension (T)**
The pulling force transmitted through a string, rope, cable, or rod under stretch. Acts along the string, directed away from the body (toward the string). Constant throughout a massless, inextensible string over a smooth pulley. *(All levels)*

---

## W

**Weightlessness**
The condition in which a body experiences zero apparent weight (normal force = 0). Occurs during free fall, circular orbital motion, or in the absence of gravity. A person in a freely falling lift is weightless — not because gravity has vanished, but because both the person and lift fall with the same acceleration g. *(Board/NEET)*

## Important Ancient Indian Context (from NCERT)

Ancient Indian thinkers (Vaisesika school) had concepts related to laws of motion:
* **Vega** — the tendency of a body to move in a straight line (close to inertia concept)
* **Sanskara** — persistent tendency; analogous to momentum
* **Bhaskara (1150 AD)** — introduced the concept of instantaneous motion (tatkaliki gati), anticipating instantaneous velocity
