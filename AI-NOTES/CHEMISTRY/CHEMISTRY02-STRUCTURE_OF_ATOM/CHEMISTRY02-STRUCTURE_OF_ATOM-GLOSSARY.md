## A

**Absorption Spectrum**
The spectrum produced when white (continuous) light is passed through an unexcited gaseous sample. Atoms absorb radiation at specific wavelengths corresponding to their allowed energy transitions, leaving **dark lines** on an otherwise bright continuous background. It is the photographic negative of the emission spectrum — the absorbed wavelengths are exactly the same as the emitted ones. Related: Emission spectrum, Line spectrum. *(Board/NEET)*

**Angular Momentum Quantum Number (l)**
Also called the **azimuthal quantum number** or  **subsidiary quantum number** . Defines the **shape** of an atomic orbital and the subshell to which it belongs. For a given principal quantum number n, l takes integer values from  **0 to (n − 1)** . l = 0 (s), l = 1 (p), l = 2 (d), l = 3 (f). Also determines the orbital angular momentum: L = √[l(l+1)] · h/2π. In multi-electron atoms, l contributes to orbital energy alongside n. Related: Principal quantum number, Subshell, Orbital shape. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Angular Node**
A region of zero electron probability density that takes the form of a  **plane (or cone) passing through the nucleus** . The number of angular nodes equals the azimuthal quantum number  **l** . Also called nodal planes. Examples: 2p has 1 angular node (the nodal plane through the nucleus); 3d has 2 angular nodes. Contrast with radial nodes. Related: Radial node, Node. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Atomic Number (Z)**
The number of **protons** in the nucleus of an atom. For a neutral atom, it also equals the number of electrons. Atomic number uniquely identifies an element — all atoms of the same element have the same Z regardless of their isotopic form. The chemical properties of an element are determined by Z (since it determines electron count and hence electronic configuration). Symbol: Z. Formula: Z = number of protons = number of electrons (neutral atom). Related: Mass number, Isotopes. *(Board/NEET)*

**Atomic Orbital**
The **wave function ψ** for an electron in an atom. An orbital is the three-dimensional region in space where the probability of finding the electron is high (conventionally, ~90%). Each orbital is characterised by a unique set of three quantum numbers (n, l, mₗ). An orbital can hold a maximum of **two electrons** with opposite spins (Pauli exclusion principle). Shapes: s = sphere, p = dumbbell, d = cloverleaf, f = complex. Distinct from Bohr's "orbit" — an orbital is a probability distribution, not a defined circular path. Related: Wave function, Quantum numbers, Orbit vs Orbital. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Aufbau Principle**
*(German: aufbau = "building up")*
The rule stating that in the **ground state** of an atom, electrons occupy orbitals in  **order of increasing energy** , filling lower-energy orbitals before higher-energy ones. The energy ordering follows the  **(n + l) rule** : the orbital with the lower (n + l) value has the lower energy; if two orbitals have the same (n + l), the one with the **lower n** is filled first. This gives the filling order: 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, 4p, 5s, 4d, 5p, 6s, 4f, 5d, 6p, 7s... Exceptions exist (Cr, Cu) where extra stability of half-filled/fully-filled d subshells overrides the expected order. Related: Hund's rule, Pauli exclusion principle, (n+l) rule. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## B

**Balmer Series**
The series of spectral lines of hydrogen produced by electron transitions that **end at n = 2** (from higher levels n = 3, 4, 5...). The only hydrogen spectral series that falls in the **visible region** of the electromagnetic spectrum. Named after Johann Jakob Balmer who first described it empirically in 1885. The wavenumber formula: $\bar{\nu} = 109{,}677\left(\frac{1}{4} - \frac{1}{n^2}\right)$ cm⁻¹ where n ≥ 3. The first Balmer line (n=3→n=2) at 656 nm is red; the series limit (n=∞→n=2) is at 364 nm. Related: Rydberg equation, Spectral series, Lyman series. *(Board/NEET)*

**Black Body**
An ideal body that  **absorbs and emits radiation of all frequencies uniformly** . A perfect radiator and absorber. In practice, a cavity with a small hole is a good physical approximation. The radiation emitted by a black body and its spectral distribution depend only on its  **temperature** . As temperature increases, the peak intensity shifts to shorter (higher-energy) wavelengths — this is black body radiation. Classical wave theory failed to explain its spectral distribution; Max Planck's quantum theory (1900) successfully explained it by assuming energy is emitted in discrete quanta E = hν. Related: Planck's quantum theory, Photoelectric effect. *(Board/NEET)*

**Bohr Model**
The atomic model proposed by **Niels Bohr (1913)** for the hydrogen atom, based on four postulates: (1) electrons move in circular orbits of fixed radius and energy called stationary states; (2) energy of an electron in an orbit does not change with time; (3) radiation is absorbed/emitted only during transitions between orbits; (4) angular momentum is quantised: mₑvr = nh/2π. Key equations: rₙ = n²a₀ and Eₙ = −R_H/n² where R_H = 2.18×10⁻¹⁸ J. Successfully explained H-atom stability and line spectrum. Failed for multi-electron atoms, fine structure, Zeeman/Stark effects, and contradicts Heisenberg's uncertainty principle. Related: Stationary state, Bohr radius, Limitations of Bohr's model. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Bohr Radius (a₀)**
The radius of the **first (n = 1) stationary orbit** in the hydrogen atom.
**a₀ = 52.9 pm** (picometre) = 0.529 Å.
General formula for nth orbit: rₙ = n²a₀ (for H).
For hydrogen-like species: rₙ = 52.9 · n²/Z pm.
Represents the most probable distance of the electron from the nucleus in the ground state. Related: Bohr model, Principal quantum number. *(Board/NEET)*

**Boundary Surface Diagram**
A three-dimensional surface drawn around an atomic orbital that encloses the region where the probability of finding the electron is approximately  **90%** . It is NOT possible to draw a surface enclosing 100% probability because |ψ|² never becomes exactly zero at any finite distance. The shape of the boundary surface diagram represents the shape of the orbital (sphere for s, dumbbell for p, cloverleaf for d). Related: Atomic orbital, Probability density, Wave function. *(Board/NEET)*

## C

**Canal Rays**
Positively charged rays discovered in a modified discharge tube when holes are made in the cathode, allowing the rays to pass backward through the perforations. They are the **positively charged gaseous ions** formed when gas molecules in the tube are ionised by the cathode rays. Unlike cathode rays, the charge-to-mass ratio of canal rays depends on the nature of the gas used. The smallest and lightest canal ray is obtained from hydrogen gas → identified as the  **proton** . Contrast with cathode rays (negatively charged, e/m constant). Related: Cathode rays, Proton. *(Board)*

**Cathode Rays**
Streams of **negatively charged particles (electrons)** that travel from the cathode to the anode in a cathode ray discharge tube. Key properties: travel in straight lines in absence of fields; deflected toward the positive plate in an electric field; characteristics (e/m ratio) are independent of the material of the cathode or the gas in the tube. The e/m ratio was measured by **J.J. Thomson (1897)** as 1.758820 × 10¹¹ C kg⁻¹. Established that electrons are a fundamental constituent of all matter. Related: Electron, Thomson model, e/m ratio. *(Board/NEET)*

**Continuous Spectrum**
A spectrum containing **all wavelengths** within a range with no gaps, forming a smooth, unbroken band of colours (e.g., ROYGBIV for visible light). Produced by white light passed through a prism. Differs from a  **line spectrum** , where only specific discrete wavelengths are present. The background of an absorption spectrum is a continuous spectrum. *(Board)*

## D

**de Broglie Relation**
The equation proposed by French physicist **Louis de Broglie (1924)** expressing the wave nature of matter:

$$
\lambda = \frac{h}{mv} = \frac{h}{p}
$$

where λ = de Broglie wavelength, h = Planck's constant, m = mass, v = velocity, p = momentum. Predicts that every moving particle has an associated wavelength. For macroscopic objects (large m), λ is immeasurably small (no practical significance). For electrons, λ ≈ 10⁻¹⁰ m — comparable to atomic dimensions and X-ray wavelengths — physically significant and experimentally confirmed via  **electron diffraction** . Foundation of the quantum mechanical model and wave–particle duality of matter. Related: Heisenberg uncertainty principle, Wave–particle duality, Electron microscope. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Degenerate Orbitals**
Orbitals that have the  **same energy** . In hydrogen and hydrogen-like species (one electron), all orbitals with the same principal quantum number n are degenerate (e.g., 2s and 2p have the same energy). In multi-electron atoms, degeneracy is lifted — within a given shell, s < p < d < f in energy. Within the same subshell (e.g., the three 2p orbitals), orbitals remain degenerate. Hund's rule governs how electrons are distributed among degenerate orbitals. Related: Hund's rule, Energy of orbitals, Multi-electron atoms. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Deuterium (²H or D)**
The second isotope of hydrogen (Z = 1, A = 2). Contains  **1 proton and 1 neutron** . Represents 0.015% of naturally occurring hydrogen. Its nucleus is called the deuteron. Heavy water (D₂O) is formed from deuterium. Has the same chemical properties as protium (ordinary hydrogen) but different physical properties due to its greater mass. Contrast: Protium (¹H, 0 neutrons, 99.985%) and Tritium (³H, 2 neutrons, trace). Related: Isotopes, Protium, Tritium. *(Board)*

**Dual Nature (Wave–Particle Duality)**
The property of electromagnetic radiation (and matter) of exhibiting  **both wave-like and particle-like behaviour** , depending on the type of experiment. Light behaves as a wave in phenomena like diffraction and interference, but as a stream of particles (photons) in the photoelectric effect and black body radiation. Similarly, electrons exhibit wave nature (de Broglie, diffraction) and particle nature (definite mass, charge). First proposed for light by Einstein; extended to matter by de Broglie. Related: Photoelectric effect, de Broglie relation, Photon. *(Board/NEET)*

## E

**Effective Nuclear Charge (Zeff)**
The **net positive charge experienced by an outer electron** after accounting for the shielding (screening) effect of inner electrons. Zeff = Z − σ, where σ is the shielding constant. Since inner electrons partially cancel the attractive force of the nucleus, outer electrons feel a reduced nuclear charge. For a given shell (n), electrons in different subshells experience different Zeff: s electrons penetrate closest to nucleus → highest Zeff; d electrons penetrate least → lowest Zeff. Thus Zeff order within same shell: s > p > d > f. This explains why orbital energies within a shell split in multi-electron atoms: s < p < d < f. Related: Shielding effect, Energy of orbitals, Orbital penetration. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Electron**
The **fundamental negatively charged sub-atomic particle** discovered via cathode ray experiments. Charge = −1.602176 × 10⁻¹⁹ C. Mass = 9.109382 × 10⁻³¹ kg = 0.00054 u. Occupies orbitals around the nucleus of an atom. Characterised by four quantum numbers (n, l, mₗ, ms). The number of electrons in a neutral atom equals Z (atomic number). Electrons determine the chemical behaviour of an atom. Key experiment: Thomson (e/m ratio, 1897); Millikan (charge, 1906–14). Related: Cathode rays, Quantum numbers, Electronic configuration. *(Board/NEET)*

**Electronic Configuration**
The arrangement of  **electrons in the orbitals of an atom** , written using subshell notation (e.g., 1s²2s²2p⁶) or noble gas notation (e.g., [Ne]3s¹). Determined by applying three rules in order: Aufbau principle (fill lowest energy first), Pauli exclusion principle (max 2 e⁻ per orbital, opposite spins), Hund's rule (singly occupy degenerate orbitals before pairing). Notable exceptions from expected Aufbau order: Cr ([Ar]3d⁵4s¹) and Cu ([Ar]3d¹⁰4s¹) due to extra stability of half-filled/fully-filled d subshells. Related: Aufbau principle, Pauli exclusion, Hund's rule, Valence electrons. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Emission Spectrum**
The spectrum of **radiation emitted** by a substance that has absorbed energy (by heating or electric discharge) and its excited atoms return to lower energy states. Appears as **bright coloured lines on a dark background** — a line spectrum. Each element has a unique emission spectrum (its "fingerprint"), used in spectroscopic analysis to identify elements. The hydrogen emission spectrum consists of series of lines (Lyman, Balmer, Paschen, etc.). Related: Absorption spectrum, Line spectrum, Atomic spectra. *(Board/NEET)*

**Exchange Energy**
The energy  **released when two electrons of the same spin in degenerate orbitals exchange their positions** . A stabilising quantum mechanical effect — the greater the number of possible exchanges, the lower the total energy. Exchange energy is maximum when a subshell is **half-filled** (each degenerate orbital has exactly one electron, all with parallel spin) or  **fully filled** . This is the quantum mechanical basis for: (1) Hund's rule of maximum multiplicity, and (2) the extra stability of d⁵ and d¹⁰ configurations (explaining Cr and Cu exceptions). Related: Hund's rule, Stability of half/fully-filled subshells, Cr and Cu exceptions. *(JEE)*

## G

**Ground State**
The **lowest energy state** of an atom or ion, in which electrons occupy the orbitals of lowest available energy according to the filling rules. The most stable state of the atom. For hydrogen, the ground state corresponds to n = 1 with energy E₁ = −2.18 × 10⁻¹⁸ J. An electron in the ground state is most strongly bound to the nucleus. Contrast with  **excited state** , in which one or more electrons occupy higher-energy orbitals. Related: Excited state, Bohr model, Electronic configuration. *(Board/NEET)*

## H

**Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle**
Stated by  **Werner Heisenberg (1927)** : It is **impossible to simultaneously determine both the exact position and exact momentum (or velocity)** of a microscopic particle such as an electron.

$$
\Delta x \cdot \Delta p_x \geq \frac{h}{4\pi} \qquad \text{or} \qquad \Delta x \cdot \Delta v_x \geq \frac{h}{4\pi m}
$$

This is a  **fundamental property of nature** , not a limitation of measuring instruments. The more precisely position is known (small Δx), the more uncertain is the momentum (large Δp), and vice versa. For macroscopic objects, the uncertainty is negligibly small. For electrons, it is enormously significant — rendering the concept of a definite Bohr orbit physically meaningless. This principle necessitates the probabilistic (wave function) description of electrons. Related: de Broglie relation, Wave function, Probability density, Orbit vs Orbital. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Hund's Rule of Maximum Multiplicity**
The rule governing the filling of **degenerate orbitals** (orbitals of equal energy within the same subshell): electrons occupy each orbital **singly** with **parallel spins** (same ms) before any pairing occurs. Pairing begins only after every orbital in the subshell has exactly one electron. The physical basis is **maximum exchange energy** — parallel spins in degenerate orbitals permit more exchanges, releasing more energy and stabilising the configuration. Example: Carbon (2p²) has two unpaired electrons (↑ in separate 2p orbitals), not one paired pair (↑↓ in same orbital). Related: Exchange energy, Degenerate orbitals, Electronic configuration. *(Board/NEET)*

**Hydrogen-Like Species**
Atoms or ions containing **only one electron** (regardless of atomic number Z). Examples: H (Z=1), He⁺ (Z=2), Li²⁺ (Z=3), Be³⁺ (Z=4). Bohr's model can be applied to these. The energy and radius formulas are modified by Z:

$$
E_n = -2.18 \times 10^{-18} \left(\frac{Z^2}{n^2}\right) \text{ J}, \qquad r_n = \frac{52.9 \cdot n^2}{Z} \text{ pm}
$$

Higher Z means stronger nuclear attraction → smaller radius → more negative (lower) energy. Related: Bohr model, Bohr radius, Atomic number. *(NEET/JEE)*

## I

**Isobars**
Atoms of **different elements** (different atomic numbers Z) that have the **same mass number A** (same total number of nucleons). They are different elements and have different chemical properties, but the same nucleon count. Example: ¹⁴₆C and ¹⁴₇N (both A = 14, but Z = 6 and Z = 7 respectively). Also: ⁴⁰₁₈Ar and ⁴⁰₂₀Ca. Contrast with isotopes (same Z, different A). *(Board/NEET)*

**Isotopes**
Atoms of the **same element** (same Z, same number of protons) that differ in their **mass number A** (different number of neutrons). They have identical chemical properties (same electronic configuration) but different physical properties (different masses, densities, melting points). Isotopes disprove Dalton's postulate that all atoms of the same element are identical. Examples: ¹H (protium), ²H (deuterium), ³H (tritium); ¹²C, ¹³C, ¹⁴C; ³⁵Cl, ³⁷Cl. Related: Atomic number, Mass number, Isobars, Dalton's Atomic Theory. *(Board/NEET)*

## L

**Line Spectrum (Atomic Spectrum)**
A spectrum consisting of **discrete, specific wavelengths** (appearing as bright lines in emission or dark lines in absorption) rather than a continuous range. Each element has a unique line spectrum — its spectral "fingerprint" — used for elemental identification. Line spectra arise because electrons can only occupy specific quantised energy levels; only photons of exact ΔE are emitted or absorbed. Hydrogen's line spectrum is the simplest and most studied. Related: Emission spectrum, Absorption spectrum, Quantised energy levels. *(Board/NEET)*

**Lyman Series**
The series of spectral lines of hydrogen produced by transitions that **end at n = 1** (from levels n = 2, 3, 4...). Lies in the **ultraviolet (UV) region** of the electromagnetic spectrum. Has the highest energy (shortest wavelength) of all hydrogen series. The first Lyman line (n=2→n=1) is the lowest-energy UV line; the series limit (n=∞→n=1) is at ~91 nm. Wavenumber formula: $\bar{\nu} = R_H\left(1 - \frac{1}{n^2}\right)$, n ≥ 2. Related: Rydberg equation, Spectral series, Balmer series. *(Board/NEET)*

## M

**Magnetic Orbital Quantum Number (mₗ)**
The quantum number that describes the **orientation of an atomic orbital in space** relative to a standard set of coordinate axes. For a given value of l, mₗ takes **(2l + 1) integer values** from −l to +l (including 0). Examples: l=1 (p): mₗ = −1, 0, +1 (three p orbitals — pₓ, p_y, pz); l=2 (d): mₗ = −2,−1, 0,+1,+2 (five d orbitals). The number of allowed mₗ values equals the number of orbitals in that subshell. In the absence of an external field, orbitals with the same n and l but different mₗ are degenerate. Related: Azimuthal quantum number, Orbital orientation, Zeeman effect. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Mass Number (A)**
The **total number of nucleons** (protons + neutrons) in the nucleus of an atom.

$$
A = Z + n \qquad \text{(number of neutrons } = A - Z\text{)}
$$

Represented as a superscript to the left of the element symbol: ᴬ_Z X. Mass number is always a positive integer. Unlike atomic mass (which reflects actual masses), mass number counts nucleons. Used to distinguish isotopes of the same element. Related: Atomic number, Neutron, Isotopes. *(Board/NEET)*

## N

**Neutron**
An **electrically neutral** sub-atomic particle found in the nucleus. Mass = 1.674927 × 10⁻²⁷ kg ≈ 1.00867 u (slightly greater than proton mass). Discovered by **James Chadwick (1932)** by bombarding beryllium with α-particles. Together with protons, neutrons constitute the **nucleons** and are responsible for most of the atomic mass. Number of neutrons = A − Z. Isotopes differ in their neutron count. Neutrons are NOT found outside the nucleus under normal conditions. Related: Proton, Nucleon, Isotopes, Mass number. *(Board/NEET)*

**Node (Nodal Surface)**
A region in space where the  **probability density |ψ|² = 0** , meaning there is **zero probability** of finding the electron. Two types: (1) **Radial nodes** — spherical surfaces concentric with the nucleus where ψ = 0; number = n − l − 1. (2) **Angular nodes** — planes (or cones) passing through the nucleus where ψ = 0; number = l. Total nodes = n − 1. Example: 3p orbital has 1 radial node and 1 angular node (total 2 = n−1 = 2 ✓). Important: probability is zero AT the node but can be non-zero on either side of it. Related: Wave function, Probability density, Radial node, Angular node. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Nuclear Model (Rutherford's)**
The atomic model proposed by **Ernest Rutherford (1911)** based on α-particle scattering. Key features: (1) positive charge and most mass concentrated in a tiny, dense **nucleus** at the centre; (2) electrons revolve around the nucleus in circular orbits at high speed; (3) electrons and nucleus held by electrostatic attraction. The model resembles the solar system. Drawbacks: cannot explain atomic stability (electrons should spiral in) or line spectra; gives no information on electron distribution or energies. Related: α-particle scattering, Bohr model, Drawbacks of Rutherford model. *(Board/NEET)*

**Nucleon**
A collective term for the **protons and neutrons** present in the nucleus of an atom. The total number of nucleons = mass number A. Both protons and neutrons have approximately the same mass (~1 u). Related: Proton, Neutron, Mass number. *(Board)*

## O

**Orbit (Bohr)**
A **well-defined circular path** of fixed radius and energy around the nucleus in which an electron is proposed to move, according to Bohr's model. Characterised only by the principal quantum number n. Orbits are sometimes called stationary states. The concept of a precisely defined orbit contradicts the **Heisenberg uncertainty principle** (since both exact position and velocity would be simultaneously known) and has been replaced by the concept of atomic orbitals in the quantum mechanical model. Comparison: orbit = circular 2D path (classical); orbital = 3D probability region (quantum mechanical). Related: Atomic orbital, Heisenberg uncertainty principle, Bohr model. *(Board/NEET)*

## P

**Pauli Exclusion Principle**
Proposed by  **Wolfgang Pauli (1926)** : No two electrons in an atom can have the **same set of all four quantum numbers** (n, l, mₗ, ms). Equivalently: an orbital can hold  **at most two electrons** , and if it holds two, they must have **opposite spins** (one ms = +½, the other ms = −½). This principle determines the maximum electron capacity of any subshell: 2(2l+1). For a shell of principal quantum number n: maximum electrons = 2n². The restriction imposed by Pauli's principle is fundamental to the Periodic Table structure. Related: Quantum numbers, Electronic configuration, Spin quantum number. *(Board/NEET)*

**Photoelectric Effect**
The **ejection of electrons from a metal surface** when light of sufficient frequency is incident on it. Discovered by Hertz (1887); explained by **Einstein (1905)** using Planck's quantum theory. Key equation:

$$
h\nu = h\nu_0 + \frac{1}{2}m_e v^2
$$

where hν = photon energy, hν₀ = work function (W₀ = minimum energy to eject e⁻), ½mₑv² = kinetic energy of emitted photoelectron. Critical results: (1) KE depends on **frequency** of light, not intensity; (2) intensity determines the **number** of photoelectrons; (3) no emission if ν < ν₀, regardless of intensity. Cannot be explained by wave theory alone → demonstrates particle nature of light. Related: Photon, Work function, Threshold frequency, Planck's quantum theory, Dual nature. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Photon**
A **discrete packet (quantum) of electromagnetic energy** proposed by Einstein. Energy: E = hν = hc/λ. Momentum: p = h/λ. Rest mass = 0; travels at the speed of light (c). The particle manifestation of electromagnetic radiation. In the photoelectric effect, one photon interacts with one electron, transferring all its energy instantaneously. Higher frequency → more energetic photon. A more intense beam contains more photons (not more energetic ones). Related: Planck's quantum theory, Photoelectric effect, Dual nature. *(Board/NEET)*

**Planck's Constant (h)**
A fundamental physical constant that relates the energy of a photon to its frequency.

$$
h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J·s}
$$

Units: joule-seconds (J·s) — dimensions of energy × time = angular momentum. Named after  **Max Planck** . Appears in: E = hν (photon energy), λ = h/mv (de Broglie), Δx·Δp ≥ h/4π (Heisenberg), mₑvr = nh/2π (Bohr angular momentum). Connects the quantum and classical worlds. Related: Planck's quantum theory, de Broglie relation, Heisenberg uncertainty principle. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Planck's Quantum Theory**
The theory proposed by **Max Planck (1900)** to explain black body radiation. Key postulate: atoms can  **emit or absorb energy only in discrete multiples of a fundamental quantity** , called a  **quantum** . Energy cannot be exchanged continuously — it is quantised like the steps of a staircase:

$$
E = nh\nu, \quad n = 0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots
$$

The quantum of electromagnetic radiation is the **photon** with energy E = hν. This theory marked the beginning of quantum physics and successfully explained black body radiation and (through Einstein's extension) the photoelectric effect. Related: Black body radiation, Photoelectric effect, Photon, Planck's constant. *(Board/NEET)*

**Principal Quantum Number (n)**
The quantum number that specifies the **shell** of an electron, determining primarily the **size** and **energy** of the orbital. Takes positive integer values: n = 1, 2, 3, 4... (corresponding to K, L, M, N shells). For hydrogen, energy depends only on n: Eₙ = −R_H/n². As n increases: orbital size increases, electron is farther from nucleus, energy is higher (less negative). The number of orbitals in a shell = n²; maximum electrons = 2n². Related: Azimuthal quantum number, Shell, Bohr model, Electronic configuration. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Probability Density (|ψ|²)**
The  **square of the wave function** , |ψ|², at a given point in space. Interpreted by **Max Born** as the probability of finding the electron **per unit volume** at that point — always a positive quantity. The product of |ψ|² and a small volume element gives the probability of finding the electron in that infinitesimal volume. For 1s orbital, |ψ|² is maximum at the nucleus and decreases with distance. For 2s, it has a maximum, drops to zero (radial node), then rises again. Probability density ≠ probability; to get probability, integrate |ψ|² over a volume. Related: Wave function, Node, Boundary surface diagram, Atomic orbital. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Proton**
A **positively charged sub-atomic particle** in the nucleus of an atom. Charge = +1.602176 × 10⁻¹⁹ C (equal in magnitude to electron charge). Mass = 1.672622 × 10⁻²⁷ kg ≈ 1.00727 u. The proton is **1836 times heavier** than the electron. First characterised in **1919 by Rutherford** as the lightest positive ion from hydrogen canal rays. The number of protons = atomic number Z, which defines the identity of the element. Related: Atomic number, Canal rays, Neutron, Nucleon. *(Board/NEET)*

## Q

**Quantum Mechanics**
The branch of physics that deals with the **behaviour of microscopic particles** (electrons, atoms, molecules) that exhibit both wave-like and particle-like properties. Developed independently by  **Heisenberg and Schrödinger (1926)** . Based on the Schrödinger wave equation, which gives wave functions ψ and their associated energies. Quantum mechanics incorporates wave–particle duality and the uncertainty principle, resolving the failures of classical mechanics for atomic-scale phenomena. At macroscopic scales, quantum mechanics reduces to classical mechanics. Related: Schrödinger equation, Wave function, Heisenberg uncertainty principle. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Quantum Number**
A number that **characterises the state of an electron** in an atom. Four quantum numbers together uniquely define an electron: (1) **n** — principal (size, energy); (2) **l** — azimuthal (shape); (3) **mₗ** — magnetic (orientation); (4) **ms** — spin (+½ or −½). The first three arise naturally as solutions to the Schrödinger equation for hydrogen; the fourth (spin) was proposed by Uhlenbeck and Goudsmit. No two electrons in an atom can share all four quantum numbers (Pauli). Related: Principal QN, Azimuthal QN, Magnetic QN, Spin QN, Pauli exclusion principle. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## R

**Radial Node**
A **spherical surface** (centred on the nucleus) at which the wave function ψ = 0 and therefore |ψ|² = 0 — zero probability of finding the electron. Number of radial nodes =  **n − l − 1** . Example: 2s has 1 radial node; 3s has 2 radial nodes; 3p has 1 radial node; 3d has 0 radial nodes. Radial nodes are distinct from angular nodes (which are planar). As n increases, more radial nodes appear, creating alternating regions of high and low probability. Related: Node, Angular node, Wave function, Probability density. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Rydberg Constant (R_H)**
The empirical constant in the **Rydberg equation** for the hydrogen atom's spectral lines. Value: **R_H = 109,677 cm⁻¹ = 1.09677 × 10⁷ m⁻¹** (in wavenumber units) or **R_H = 2.18 × 10⁻¹⁸ J** (in energy units). Named after the Swedish spectroscopist  **Johannes Rydberg** . The Rydberg equation:

$$
\bar{\nu} = R_H\left(\frac{1}{n_1^2} - \frac{1}{n_2^2}\right)
$$

gives the wavenumber of any spectral line of hydrogen. Bohr's model theoretically derived R_H from fundamental constants, confirming the model's correctness for hydrogen. Related: Rydberg equation, Bohr model, Spectral series. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Rydberg Equation**
The empirical formula describing all spectral series of the  **hydrogen atom** :

$$
\bar{\nu} = R_H\left(\frac{1}{n_1^2} - \frac{1}{n_2^2}\right) \text{ cm}^{-1}
$$

where n₁ = 1, 2, 3... (lower level) and n₂ = n₁+1, n₁+2... (higher level). R_H = 109,677 cm⁻¹. First derived empirically by Rydberg; later explained theoretically by Bohr's model. Each choice of n₁ generates a different series: n₁=1 (Lyman), n₁=2 (Balmer), n₁=3 (Paschen), n₁=4 (Brackett), n₁=5 (Pfund). Related: Rydberg constant, Spectral series, Bohr model. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## S

**Schrödinger Equation**
The fundamental equation of quantum mechanics, formulated by  **Erwin Schrödinger (1926)** :

$$
\hat{H}\Psi = E\Psi
$$

where Ĥ is the Hamiltonian operator (total energy — kinetic + potential), Ψ (psi) is the  **wave function** , and E is the total energy. When solved for the hydrogen atom, it yields: (1) quantised energy levels (the same as Bohr's), (2) wave functions (orbitals) characterised by quantum numbers n, l, mₗ, (3) the correct 3D orbital shapes and probability densities. Schrödinger won the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1933. For multi-electron atoms, the equation cannot be solved exactly — approximation methods are used. Related: Wave function, Quantum mechanics, Hamiltonian, Atomic orbital. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Shielding Effect (Screening Effect)**
The reduction in the **effective nuclear charge** experienced by an outer electron due to the presence of inner electrons that partially **shield** (screen) it from the full positive charge of the nucleus. Inner electrons repel outer electrons, reducing the net attractive force the nucleus exerts on them. s electrons shield more effectively than p electrons, which shield more than d electrons (for same shell), because s electrons spend more time close to the nucleus. Greater shielding → lower effective nuclear charge (Zeff) → higher (less negative) orbital energy. Explains why orbital energies within a shell split in multi-electron atoms: Es < Ep < Ed < Ef. Related: Effective nuclear charge, Orbital penetration, Energy of orbitals. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Spin Quantum Number (ms)**
The fourth quantum number, proposed by  **Uhlenbeck and Goudsmit (1925)** , describing the **intrinsic spin angular momentum** of an electron. Takes only two values: **ms = +½** (spin up, ↑) or **ms = −½** (spin down, ↓). The electron behaves as if spinning about its own axis (like Earth spinning while orbiting). Two electrons in the same orbital must have opposite spins (one ↑, one ↓) — a direct consequence of the Pauli exclusion principle. The existence of ms explains the fine doublet/triplet lines in atomic spectra that the first three quantum numbers could not account for. Related: Pauli exclusion principle, Quantum numbers, Hund's rule. *(Board/NEET)*

**Stationary State**
In Bohr's model, the **allowed energy levels** (orbits) in which electrons can exist without radiating energy. Electrons in a stationary state maintain constant energy; they do not continuously emit or absorb radiation. When an electron transitions between two stationary states, it emits or absorbs a photon of energy ΔE = hν. Also called "allowed energy states" or "quantised orbits". The concept of stationary states was Bohr's key departure from classical electromagnetic theory (which predicted continuous radiation from accelerating charges). Related: Bohr model, Postulates of Bohr, Transition. *(Board/NEET)*

## T

**Threshold Frequency (ν₀)**
The **minimum frequency of incident radiation** required to eject an electron from the surface of a metal in the photoelectric effect. Below ν₀, no photoelectric emission occurs regardless of the intensity of light. The corresponding  **threshold energy = work function** : W₀ = hν₀. Each metal has its own characteristic threshold frequency (e.g., K metal: ν₀ ≈ 5.0 × 10¹⁴ Hz). The threshold wavelength λ₀ = c/ν₀. Related: Work function, Photoelectric effect, Photon. *(Board/NEET)*

**Thomson's Model**
The atomic model proposed by **J.J. Thomson (1898)** — also called the **plum pudding** (or raisin pudding / watermelon) model. The atom is a **uniform sphere of positive charge** (~10⁻¹⁰ m radius) with electrons embedded throughout it like plums in a pudding. Mass assumed to be uniformly distributed. Successfully explained overall electrical neutrality of the atom. Disproved by Rutherford's α-particle scattering experiment, which showed that positive charge is concentrated in a tiny, dense nucleus, not spread uniformly. Related: Rutherford's model, α-particle scattering, Nuclear model. *(Board/NEET)*

**Tritium (³H or T)**
The third, radioactive isotope of hydrogen (Z=1, A=3). Contains  **1 proton and 2 neutrons** . Found only in trace amounts in nature; produced artificially. Radioactive with a half-life of ~12.3 years. Used in nuclear fusion research and as a tracer in biochemistry. Chemically identical to protium and deuterium (same electron configuration). Related: Isotopes, Deuterium, Protium. *(Board)*

## W

**Wave Function (ψ)**
A **mathematical function** of the electron's position coordinates, obtained as a solution to the Schrödinger equation. The wave function itself has no direct physical meaning (ψ can be positive, negative, or complex). However, **|ψ|² (probability density)** has physical meaning — it gives the probability of finding the electron per unit volume at a given point. Each atomic orbital is described by a unique wave function, characterised by quantum numbers n, l, mₗ. Wave functions for hydrogen-like species (one-electron systems) are called  **atomic orbitals** . All information about the electron's state is contained in ψ. Related: Schrödinger equation, Probability density, Atomic orbital, Quantum numbers. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Work Function (W₀)**
The **minimum energy required to eject an electron** from the surface of a metal in the photoelectric effect. Equal to the energy of a photon at the threshold frequency:

$$
W_0 = h\nu_0
$$

Expressed in joules or electron-volts (eV). Different metals have different work functions; metals with lower W₀ (e.g., Cs, K, Na) are more easily ionised by light. Any photon energy above W₀ appears as kinetic energy of the ejected electron. Related: Threshold frequency, Photoelectric effect, Photon. *(Board/NEET)*

## Z

**Zeeman Effect**
The **splitting of spectral lines** of an atom into multiple closely spaced components when the atom is placed in an  **external magnetic field** . Named after Dutch physicist Pieter Zeeman (1896). The magnetic field causes orbitals with the same n and l but different mₗ values (which are degenerate in absence of a field) to have slightly different energies — hence additional, closely spaced spectral lines appear. The existence of the Zeeman effect was a direct failure of Bohr's model (which could not account for it) and provided evidence for the magnetic quantum number mₗ. Contrast with the **Stark effect** (splitting in electric field). Related: Magnetic quantum number, Degenerate orbitals, Limitations of Bohr's model. *(NEET/JEE)*
