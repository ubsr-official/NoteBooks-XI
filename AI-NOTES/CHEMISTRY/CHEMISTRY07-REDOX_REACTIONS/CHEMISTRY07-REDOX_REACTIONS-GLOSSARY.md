# CHAPTER 7 — GLOSSARY & TERMINOLOGY

### Redox Reactions

---

> All terms arranged **alphabetically** with precise scientific definitions, related concepts, equations, and exam relevance tags.

## A

**Anode**
The electrode in an electrochemical cell where **oxidation** occurs. In a **galvanic cell**, the anode is the **negative** electrode (electrons flow out of it into the external circuit). In an **electrolytic cell**, the anode is the **positive** electrode (connected to the positive terminal of the external power supply). Mnemonic: **"AN OX" — ANode: OXidation**. In the Daniell cell, the Zn rod is the anode: Zn(s) → Zn²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻. Related: Cathode, Electrode potential, Oxidation, Galvanic cell. *(Board/NEET)*

**Activity Series (Metal Activity Series)**
A list of metals arranged in **decreasing order of their tendency to lose electrons** (oxidation tendency), or equivalently, in decreasing order of their reducing power. Derived from competitive electron transfer experiments. Metals higher in the series can displace metals lower in the series from their salt solutions. Also called the **electrochemical series** (a more quantitative version using standard electrode potentials). From the series: Zn > Cu > Ag: Zn displaces Cu, Cu displaces Ag, but Ag cannot displace Cu. Related: Electrochemical series, Standard electrode potential, Displacement reaction. *(Board/NEET)*

---

## C

**Cathode**
The electrode in an electrochemical cell where **reduction** occurs. In a **galvanic cell**, the cathode is the **positive** electrode (electrons flow into it from the external circuit; cations from solution are reduced here). In an **electrolytic cell**, the cathode is the **negative** electrode. Mnemonic: **"RED CAT" — REDuction at CAThode**. In the Daniell cell, the Cu rod is the cathode: Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → Cu(s). Related: Anode, Electrode potential, Reduction, Galvanic cell. *(Board/NEET)*

**Combination (Synthesis) Redox Reaction**
A type of redox reaction in which **two or more substances combine to form a single product** (A + B → C), where at least one of A or B is in the elemental form. All combustion reactions using elemental O₂ are examples. Identification: look for an element (O.N. = 0) being combined with another substance. Examples: 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO; C + O₂ → CO₂; 3Mg + N₂ → Mg₃N₂. Related: Decomposition reaction, Combustion, Oxidation. *(Board/NEET)*

**Competitive Electron Transfer**
The process by which metals compete to donate electrons to ions in solution, forming the **basis of the electrochemical series**. A metal will reduce the ion of another metal if it has a greater tendency to donate electrons (more negative E°). Demonstrated by placing Zn in CuSO₄ (Zn wins the electron competition → Cu²⁺ is reduced). Analogous to the proton competition among acids. Related: Electrochemical series, Displacement reaction, Electrode potential. *(NEET/JEE)*

## D

**Daniell Cell**
A **galvanic cell** that converts chemical energy into electrical energy via the redox reaction: Zn(s) + Cu²⁺(aq) → Zn²⁺(aq) + Cu(s). Components: (1) Zn rod in ZnSO₄ solution (anode, negative), (2) Cu rod in CuSO₄ solution (cathode, positive), (3) salt bridge connecting the two solutions, (4) external wire connecting the two rods. E°cell = E°cathode – E°anode = +0.34 – (–0.76) = +1.10 V. Invented by English chemist John Frederic Daniell (1836). Related: Galvanic cell, Electrode potential, Salt bridge, Anode, Cathode. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Decomposition Redox Reaction**
A type of redox reaction in which **a compound breaks down into two or more simpler substances, at least one of which is in the elemental form**. Distinguished from non-redox decomposition (e.g., CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂, no O.N. changes). Examples: 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂ (O: –2 → 0, oxidised; H: +1 → 0, reduced); 2KClO₃ → 2KCl + 3O₂. Related: Combination reaction, Electrolysis, Oxidation number. *(Board/NEET)*

**Displacement Redox Reaction**
A type of redox reaction in which **an ion (or atom) in a compound is replaced by an ion (or atom) of another element** (X + YZ → XZ + Y). Categories: (a) **Metal displacement** — more active metal displaces a less active one from salt solution; (b) **Non-metal displacement** — hydrogen displacement (metals displace H from water/acid) and halogen displacement (more reactive halogen displaces less reactive halide). Applications: metallurgical processes, hydrogen gas preparation, industrial halogen extraction. Related: Activity series, Electrochemical series, Redox. *(Board/NEET)*

**Disproportionation Reaction**
A special type of redox reaction in which **the same element in a single substance is simultaneously oxidised (O.N. increases) and reduced (O.N. decreases)**. Requirements: (1) The element must be in an **intermediate** oxidation state; (2) The element must be capable of at least three oxidation states. Examples: 2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂ (O: –1 → –2 and 0); Cl₂ + 2OH⁻ → ClO⁻ + Cl⁻ + H₂O (Cl: 0 → +1 and –1). **Cannot occur:** F₂ (cannot be oxidised, no positive O.N.) and ClO₄⁻ (Cl at maximum +7). Related: Oxidation number, Redox reaction, Comproportionation (reverse). *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## E

**Electrode**
A conductor (usually a metal rod or plate) that makes contact with an electrolyte solution and through which current enters or leaves. In a cell, two electrodes are present: **anode** (oxidation) and **cathode** (reduction). In a Daniell cell: Zn rod and Cu rod. The interface between the electrode and solution is where half-reactions occur. Related: Electrode potential, Galvanic cell, Anode, Cathode. *(Board/NEET)*

**Electrode Potential**
The potential (voltage) associated with each electrode, which measures the **tendency of the active species to remain in oxidised or reduced form**. Cannot be measured absolutely for a single electrode; always measured relative to a reference electrode (SHE = 0.00 V). A negative E° means the couple is a stronger reducing agent than H⁺/H₂; a positive E° means it is a weaker reducing agent. Related: Standard electrode potential, Electrochemical series, EMF. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Electrochemical Series**
A table listing **half-reactions (reduction reactions) in order of decreasing standard electrode potential (E°)**. Species at the top are the strongest oxidising agents (readily reduced); species at the bottom are the strongest reducing agents (readily oxidised). F₂/F⁻ is at the top (+2.87 V); Li⁺/Li is at the bottom (–3.05 V). Uses: (1) Predicting feasibility of redox reactions; (2) Comparing oxidising/reducing power; (3) Predicting direction of electron flow. Related: Standard electrode potential, Galvanic cell, Reduction potential. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## F

**Fractional Oxidation Number**
An **average** oxidation number that arises when an element in a compound exists in **two or more different oxidation states** in distinct structural positions, but a single number is assigned based on the empirical formula alone. Examples: C in C₃O₂ = +4/3 (reality: +2, 0, +2); S in Na₂S₄O₆ = +2.5 (reality: +5, 0, 0, +5). Important note: **electrons are never shared/transferred in fractions**; the fractional value is only a mathematical average. The true picture always requires structural knowledge. Related: Oxidation number, Stock notation, Mixed oxide. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

---

## G

**Galvanic Cell (Voltaic Cell)**
An electrochemical cell in which a **spontaneous redox reaction generates electrical energy**. The chemical energy stored in bonds is converted to electrical energy. Named after Luigi Galvani (Italian scientist). The Daniell cell is the classic example. Key features: (1) Two electrodes (anode: oxidation; cathode: reduction); (2) An electrolyte solution; (3) A salt bridge or porous partition; (4) External circuit for electron flow. EMF = E°cathode – E°anode > 0 for a spontaneous cell. Related: Daniell cell, Electrode potential, Electrolytic cell, Anode, Cathode. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

---

## H

**Half-Reaction**
One of the two separate steps into which an overall redox reaction is split, each **explicitly showing electron transfer**. An **oxidation half-reaction** shows loss of electrons (e⁻ on the right side). A **reduction half-reaction** shows gain of electrons (e⁻ on the left side). The sum of the two half-reactions gives the overall balanced equation (electrons cancel). Example for NaCl formation: Oxidation: 2Na → 2Na⁺ + 2e⁻; Reduction: Cl₂ + 2e⁻ → 2Cl⁻. Used systematically in the ion-electron (half-reaction) balancing method. Related: Ion-electron method, Oxidation, Reduction, Electrode. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Half-Reaction Method (Ion-Electron Method)**
A systematic method for **balancing redox equations** in which the overall equation is split into separate oxidation and reduction half-reactions, each balanced individually, then combined. Steps: (1) Split; (2) Balance atoms other than O, H; (3) Balance O with H₂O; (4) Balance H with H⁺ (acidic) or H⁺ then OH⁻ (basic); (5) Balance charge with electrons; (6) Equalise electrons; (7) Add half-reactions. Alternative to the oxidation number method. Related: Oxidation number method, Half-reaction, Acidic/basic medium. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Hydrogen Displacement Reaction**
A subcategory of non-metal displacement reactions in which **metals displace hydrogen from water or acids**. Very active metals (Na, K, Ca) displace H from cold water; less active metals (Mg, Fe) react with steam; many metals (Zn, Mg, Fe) displace H from acids. Very inactive metals (Ag, Au, Cu) cannot displace H₂ even from acids. The rate of H₂ evolution reflects metal activity: faster rate = more reactive metal. Related: Displacement reaction, Activity series, Electrode potential. *(Board/NEET)*

## I

**Iodometric Titration**
An indirect titration method in redox chemistry where the analyte (e.g., Cu²⁺) first reacts with excess I⁻ to liberate I₂, which is then titrated against a standard thiosulphate (S₂O₃²⁻) solution using starch as the indicator (blue → colourless at endpoint). Reactions: 2Cu²⁺ + 4I⁻ → Cu₂I₂ + I₂; I₂ + 2S₂O₃²⁻ → 2I⁻ + S₄O₆²⁻. Used for copper estimation, dissolved oxygen measurement, and bleach analysis. Related: Redox titration, Starch indicator, Thiosulphate. *(NEET/JEE)*

## L

**Layer Test**
A laboratory test for identifying bromide (Br⁻) and iodide (I⁻) ions based on **halogen displacement reactions**. Chlorine water is added to the solution, then CCl₄ is added and shaken. If Br⁻ is present, Br₂ is liberated → CCl₄ layer turns **orange-brown**. If I⁻ is present, I₂ is liberated → CCl₄ layer turns **violet/purple**. Reactions: Cl₂ + 2Br⁻ → 2Cl⁻ + Br₂; Cl₂ + 2I⁻ → 2Cl⁻ + I₂. Related: Halogen displacement, Oxidation, Cl₂. *(Board/NEET)*

## O

**Oxidation (Classical)**
The **addition of oxygen or an electronegative element to a substance, OR the removal of hydrogen or an electropositive element from a substance**. Historical development: originally only addition of O₂; broadened to include other electronegative elements; further broadened to include removal of electropositive elements. Related: Reduction, Oxidation number, Electronic definition. *(Board)*

**Oxidation (Electronic)**
**Loss of one or more electrons by any species.** The species that loses electrons is said to be oxidised. Mnemonic: OIL — Oxidation Is Loss. The species that undergoes oxidation in a reaction acts as the **reducing agent** (it causes reduction of the other species). Quantitatively: an increase in oxidation number indicates oxidation. Related: Reduction, Reducing agent, Half-reaction, OIL RIG. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Oxidation Number (Oxidation State)**
A number assigned to each atom in a compound that represents the **hypothetical charge the atom would bear if all bonds were completely ionic** (electron pair assigned to the more electronegative atom). It is a bookkeeping device, not a real charge (except for monoatomic ions). Rules: (1) Free element = 0; (2) Monoatomic ion = charge; (3) O = –2 (usually); (4) H = +1 (usually); (5) F = always –1; (6) Algebraic sum = 0 (compound) or = charge (ion). Related: Oxidation, Reduction, Stock notation, Rules for O.N. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Oxidation Number Method**
A method for **balancing redox equations** based on the principle that **total increase in oxidation number = total decrease in oxidation number** (electron conservation). Steps: (1) Write correct formulas; (2) Assign O.N.; (3) Identify changes and equalise increase/decrease; (4) Add H⁺/OH⁻ for charge balance; (5) Add H₂O for H balance; (6) Verify O. Alternative to the half-reaction method. Related: Half-reaction method, Oxidation number, Redox balancing. *(Board/NEET)*

**Oxidising Agent (Oxidant)**
A **substance that accepts electrons** from another species, thereby causing it to be oxidised. In the process, the oxidising agent itself is **reduced** (its oxidation number decreases). Strong oxidising agents: F₂ (E° = +2.87 V), KMnO₄, K₂Cr₂O₇, HNO₃ (conc.), H₂SO₄ (conc.), ozone (O₃), H₂O₂. In a galvanic cell, the oxidising agent is reduced at the cathode. Related: Reducing agent, Reduction, Oxidation, Electrode potential. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## R

**Redox Couple**
A pair consisting of the **oxidised and reduced forms of a substance** that appear in an oxidation or reduction half-reaction. Written as: **Oxidised form / Reduced form** (separated by a slash or vertical line). The oxidised form is written first. Examples: Zn²⁺/Zn; Cu²⁺/Cu; MnO₄⁻/Mn²⁺; Fe³⁺/Fe²⁺. The electrode potential of a redox couple measures the relative tendency of the couple to stay in the oxidised or reduced form. Related: Half-reaction, Standard electrode potential, Daniell cell. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Redox Reaction**
A chemical reaction involving the **simultaneous oxidation of one species and reduction of another**. The term is a portmanteau of "reduction-oxidation." Electrons are never created or destroyed; electrons lost by the reducing agent are gained by the oxidising agent. Four types: combination, decomposition, displacement, disproportionation. Modern understanding: oxidation = decrease in electron density; reduction = increase in electron density. Related: Oxidation, Reduction, Oxidising agent, Reducing agent, Half-reaction. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Redox Titration**
A volumetric analysis technique in which a **redox reaction between the analyte and a standard solution** is used to determine the concentration of the analyte. The endpoint is detected by: (1) Self-indicator (e.g., KMnO₄ — purple colour appears at endpoint); (2) External indicator (e.g., diphenylamine for Cr₂O₇²⁻ titrations — gives intense blue colour at endpoint); (3) Iodometric endpoint (starch-iodine indicator — blue colour disappears). Related: Iodometric titration, KMnO₄, Equivalence point, Indicator. *(Board/NEET)*

**Reducing Agent (Reductant)**
A **substance that donates electrons** to another species, thereby causing it to be reduced. In the process, the reducing agent itself is **oxidised** (its oxidation number increases). Strong reducing agents: Li (E° = –3.05 V), Na, Al, Zn, H₂, H₂S, SO₂, HI. The reducing agent is oxidised at the anode in a galvanic cell. In the context of halogens, hydroiodic acid (HI) is the best reductant among hydrohalic acids. Related: Oxidising agent, Oxidation, Reduction, Electrode potential. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Reduction (Classical)**
The **removal of oxygen or an electronegative element from a substance, OR the addition of hydrogen or an electropositive element to a substance**. Broadened from original definition (just removal of O₂). Example: 2HgO → 2Hg + O₂ (O removed from Hg); CH₂=CH₂ + H₂ → CH₃–CH₃ (H₂ added). Related: Oxidation, Reducing agent, Electronic definition. *(Board)*

**Reduction (Electronic)**
**Gain of one or more electrons by any species.** The species that gains electrons is said to be reduced. Mnemonic: RIG — Reduction Is Gain. The species that undergoes reduction acts as the **oxidising agent**. Quantitatively: a decrease in oxidation number indicates reduction. Related: Oxidation, Oxidising agent, Half-reaction, OIL RIG. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## S

**Salt Bridge**
A U-shaped tube containing a concentrated electrolyte solution (KCl or NH₄NO₃ in agar/gelatin) that **connects two half-cells in a galvanic cell** without allowing the solutions to mix. Its functions: (1) Maintains electrical contact (completes the circuit) through ion migration; (2) Prevents direct mixing of the two electrode solutions; (3) Keeps both solutions electrically neutral (cations migrate to cathode compartment, anions to anode compartment). Without a salt bridge, current would quickly stop due to charge build-up. Related: Daniell cell, Galvanic cell, Electrode. *(Board/NEET)*

**Self-Indicator**
A titrant that is itself so deeply coloured that it can serve as its own indicator without needing an external indicator. The classic example is **KMnO₄ (permanganate)** — deep purple/violet; its product Mn²⁺ is almost colourless. The first lasting pink colour at the endpoint (when all reductant has been consumed) signals the end of the titration. The detection limit is as low as 10⁻⁶ mol L⁻¹ MnO₄⁻, making it extremely sensitive. Related: Redox titration, KMnO₄, Endpoint, Indicator. *(Board/NEET)*

**Standard Electrode Potential (E°)**
The electrode potential measured under **standard conditions**: all ions at 1 M concentration, gas pressure at 1 atm, temperature at 298 K, relative to the standard hydrogen electrode (SHE = 0.00 V). Always tabulated as a **reduction potential** (the tendency to be reduced). More positive E° → stronger oxidising agent; more negative E° → stronger reducing agent. Used to predict feasibility of reactions: if E°(oxidant) > E°(reductant) in the table, the reaction is spontaneous. Related: Electrode potential, Electrochemical series, Daniell cell, Galvanic cell. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Standard Hydrogen Electrode (SHE)**
The **universal reference electrode** for measuring standard electrode potentials, assigned E° = **0.00 V** by convention. Consists of: a Pt wire dipped in 1 M HCl solution with H₂ gas at 1 atm bubbling over it. Half-reaction: 2H⁺(aq, 1M) + 2e⁻ ⇌ H₂(g, 1 atm). All other standard electrode potentials are measured relative to this. Absolute electrode potentials cannot be measured for a single electrode. Related: Standard electrode potential, Electrode potential, Reference electrode. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Stock Notation**
A notation proposed by German chemist **Alfred Stock** in which the **oxidation state of a metal in a compound is indicated by a Roman numeral in parentheses** after the metal's symbol or name. Used to distinguish compounds of metals with multiple oxidation states. Examples: Au(I)Cl = gold(I) chloride; Au(III)Cl₃ = gold(III) chloride; Sn(II)Cl₂ = tin(II) chloride; Fe(II)Cl₂ = iron(II) chloride; Fe(III)Cl₃ = iron(III) chloride. Related: Oxidation number, Multiple oxidation states, Nomenclature. *(Board/NEET)*

## Key Scientists in Redox Chemistry

| Scientist | Nationality | Contribution |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Antoine Lavoisier** | French | Established the concept of oxidation as addition of oxygen (1770s–1780s); discovered role of O₂ in combustion |
| **John Frederic Daniell** | English | Invented the Daniell cell (1836) — first practical galvanic cell; provided stable EMF |
| **Alfred Stock** | German | Introduced Stock notation (Roman numerals for oxidation states) — systematic nomenclature for compounds |
| **Alessandro Volta** | Italian | Invented the voltaic pile (1800) — first electrochemical battery; showed chemical reactions can produce electricity |
| **Luigi Galvani** | Italian | Discovered bioelectricity; galvanic cells named after him |
| **Michael Faraday** | British | Laws of electrolysis (1834); introduced terms anode, cathode, electrolyte, electrode |
| **Walther Nernst** | German | Nernst equation (relates E to concentration); fundamental to modern electrochemistry |
