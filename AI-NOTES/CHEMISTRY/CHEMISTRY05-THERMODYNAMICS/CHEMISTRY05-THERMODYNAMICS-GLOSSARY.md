### Thermodynamics

## A

**Adiabatic Process**
A process in which **no heat is exchanged** between the system and its surroundings; q = 0. The wall separating system and surroundings that prevents heat transfer is called the  **adiabatic wall** . Not the same as an isolated system (where both q = 0 AND w = 0). In an adiabatic process, work may still be done: ΔU = w_ad. Example: rapid compression of gas in a cylinder with insulating walls; gas in a thermos flask being compressed. Adiabatic expansion of an ideal gas into vacuum: q = 0, w = 0, ΔU = 0. Related: Isolated system, Internal energy, First law of thermodynamics. *(Board/NEET)*

**Atomization Enthalpy (Δ_a H°)**
The standard enthalpy change accompanying the  **complete conversion of one mole of a substance into gaseous atoms** . Always positive (endothermic). For diatomic molecules (H₂, Cl₂, O₂), Δ_a H° equals the bond dissociation enthalpy. For polyatomic molecules (CH₄), Δ_a H° = sum of all bond dissociation steps. Example: H₂(g) → 2H(g); Δ_a H° = +435 kJ mol⁻¹; CH₄(g) → C(g) + 4H(g); Δ_a H° = +1665 kJ mol⁻¹. Used in Hess's Law calculations and to determine mean bond enthalpies. Related: Bond dissociation enthalpy, Mean bond enthalpy, Hess's Law. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## B

**Bomb Calorimeter**
A sealed, heavy-duty **steel vessel** used to measure heat of combustion reactions at  **constant volume** . The bomb is submerged in a water bath (the calorimeter body), and the whole assembly is insulated. A combustible substance is ignited electrically in excess dioxygen. Heat evolved = C_V × ΔT (where C_V is the total heat capacity of the calorimeter). Since ΔV = 0, no PV work is done, and the measured heat gives **ΔU = q_V** directly. To obtain ΔH: use ΔH = ΔU + Δn_g RT. Related: Calorimetry, Internal energy, ΔU, Enthalpy. *(Board/NEET)*

**Bond Dissociation Enthalpy**
The **enthalpy change** when **one mole of a specific covalent bond** in a gaseous molecule is broken homolytically to form gaseous species. Always positive (endothermic — breaking bonds requires energy). For diatomic molecules: unique, well-defined value. For polyatomic molecules: the value varies with the chemical environment of the bond; successive breaking of the same type of bond in one molecule requires different amounts of energy. Example: CH₄ → CH₃ + H requires +427 kJ mol⁻¹; CH₃ → CH₂ + H requires +439 kJ mol⁻¹. Contrast with **mean bond enthalpy** which averages these values. Related: Mean bond enthalpy, Atomization enthalpy. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Born–Haber Cycle**
An **enthalpy diagram** (a specific application of Hess's Law) used to **indirectly calculate lattice enthalpy** of ionic compounds, which cannot be measured experimentally. The cycle connects formation enthalpy, sublimation enthalpy, ionization enthalpy, bond dissociation enthalpy, electron gain enthalpy, and lattice enthalpy in a closed loop. By Hess's Law: the sum of enthalpy changes around the cycle = 0. For NaCl: Δ_lattice H° = Δ_f H° + Δ_sub H° + Δ_i H° + ½Δ_bond H° + Δ_eg H° = +411.2 + 108.4 + 496 + 121 + (–348.6) = **+788 kJ mol⁻¹** (for dissociation). Named after Max Born and Fritz Haber. Related: Lattice enthalpy, Hess's Law, Ionization enthalpy, Electron gain enthalpy. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Boundary**
The **real or imaginary wall** or surface that separates a **system** from its  **surroundings** . May be physical (e.g., walls of a beaker, cylinder with piston) or conceptual (e.g., a defined region of space). The boundary can be designed to allow control of matter and energy exchange: a **permeable boundary** allows matter to pass; an **adiabatic boundary** prevents heat flow; a **rigid boundary** prevents volume change (no work). The nature of the boundary determines the type of system (open, closed, or isolated). Related: System, Surroundings, Adiabatic process. *(Board)*

## C

**Calorimetry**
The experimental science of **measuring heat changes** associated with chemical or physical processes. Performed in a **calorimeter** — a thermally insulated device. Two main types: (1) **Bomb calorimeter** (constant volume, measures ΔU); (2) **Constant-pressure calorimeter** (measures ΔH = q_p). The heat evolved or absorbed is calculated from the temperature change: q = C × ΔT, where C is the heat capacity of the calorimeter system. Calorimetry is the primary experimental source of thermochemical data. Related: Heat capacity, Internal energy, Enthalpy, Bomb calorimeter. *(Board/NEET)*

**Closed System**
A thermodynamic system in which **no exchange of matter** occurs with the surroundings, but **exchange of energy** (as heat or work) is possible. The boundaries are impermeable to matter but permeable to energy. Example: reactants in a sealed copper or steel vessel. Contrast with open systems (matter and energy exchanged) and isolated systems (neither exchanged). In chemistry, most theoretical treatments assume a closed system. Related: Open system, Isolated system, System, Surroundings. *(Board)*

## D

**Dilution Enthalpy (Enthalpy of Dilution)**
The **heat change** that occurs when a solution is diluted by adding more solvent (at constant temperature and pressure). Mathematically: enthalpy of dilution = Δ_sol H° (more dilute) – Δ_sol H° (less dilute). Example: HCl dissolved in 25 mol water (ΔH = –72.03 kJ mol⁻¹) further diluted to 40 mol water (ΔH = –72.79 kJ mol⁻¹): Δ_dilution H = –72.79 – (–72.03) = –0.76 kJ mol⁻¹. The enthalpy of solution at **infinite dilution** is the limiting value when interactions between ions/molecules become negligible. Related: Solution enthalpy, Hydration enthalpy. *(Board)*

## E

**Endothermic Reaction/Process**
A chemical or physical process in which **heat is absorbed** by the system from the surroundings. Results in an  **increase in enthalpy** : ΔH > 0 (positive). The surroundings lose heat and cool down (unless another heat source is present). Examples: decomposition of limestone CaCO₃(s) → CaO(s) + CO₂(g); dissolving NH₄NO₃ in water; photosynthesis; melting of ice. On an energy diagram, products lie **above** reactants. Related: Exothermic reaction, Enthalpy change. *(Board)*

**Enthalpy (H)**
A **thermodynamic state function** defined as H = U + pV. At constant pressure, the enthalpy change equals the  **heat absorbed or released by the system** : ΔH = q_p. H is an extensive property with units of joules (J) or kilojoules (kJ). Like internal energy U, absolute values of H cannot be measured — only changes (ΔH) are experimentally accessible. The concept of enthalpy is particularly useful for reactions at constant atmospheric pressure (most laboratory and industrial conditions). The word "enthalpy" derives from the Greek "enthalpien," meaning "to warm." Related: Internal energy, Heat capacity, Exothermic/endothermic reactions, Hess's Law. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Entropy (S)**
A **thermodynamic state function** that measures the **degree of disorder or randomness** of a system. Greater disorder = higher entropy. Units: J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹. Quantitatively: ΔS = q_rev/T (for a reversible process). Entropy increases when: solid → liquid → gas, temperature rises, volume increases (gas), substances dissolve, number of particles increases. **Second Law:** The total entropy of the universe increases for any spontaneous process: ΔS_total = ΔS_system + ΔS_surroundings > 0. At equilibrium: ΔS_total = 0. Entropy is also the thermodynamic interpretation of "heat randomization" — heat added at lower temperature causes greater disorder per unit heat. Related: Second law of thermodynamics, Third law, Gibbs energy, Spontaneity. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Exothermic Reaction/Process**
A chemical or physical process in which the system **releases heat** to the surroundings. ΔH < 0 (negative). The temperature of the surroundings rises. Examples: combustion of fuels (coal, wood, LPG), formation of water from H₂ and O₂, neutralization reactions, rusting of iron, condensation of steam. On energy diagrams, products lie **below** reactants. Most spontaneous reactions in nature are exothermic. However, some endothermic reactions are also spontaneous (entropy-driven). Related: Endothermic reaction, Enthalpy. *(Board)*

**Extensive Property**
A thermodynamic property whose value **depends on the amount of matter** present in the system. Doubling the amount of substance doubles the value of an extensive property. Examples: mass, volume, internal energy (U), enthalpy (H), entropy (S), Gibbs energy (G), heat capacity (C). Contrast with **intensive properties** (independent of amount). Molar quantities (H_m, S_m, C_m) convert extensive → intensive by dividing by n. Enthalpy is extensive: doubling all coefficients in a balanced equation doubles ΔH. Related: Intensive property, Enthalpy, Internal energy. *(Board/NEET)*

## F

**First Law of Thermodynamics**
The **law of conservation of energy** applied to thermodynamic systems. States: *"The energy of an isolated system is constant"* or equivalently, "energy can neither be created nor destroyed, only transformed." Mathematical statement: **ΔU = q + w** (using IUPAC sign convention where positive q = heat absorbed BY system; positive w = work done ON system). Establishes internal energy (U) as a state function. Does NOT tell us about the direction of spontaneous change — that requires the Second Law. Limitations: does not account for direction of change; cannot explain why some reactions are spontaneous and others are not. Related: Internal energy, Heat, Work, Adiabatic process, Isolated system. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Free Expansion**
The **expansion of a gas into vacuum** (against zero external pressure: p_ex = 0). Since w = –p_ex·ΔV = 0 (no resistance), no work is done. For an ideal gas, Joule showed experimentally that temperature does not change, so q = 0 too →  **ΔU = 0** . This confirms that for an ideal gas, internal energy depends only on temperature (not volume). Free expansion is always irreversible (gas cannot spontaneously re-compress into original volume). Related: Work, Internal energy, Irreversible process. *(Board/NEET)*

**Fusion Enthalpy (Δ_fus H°)**
The **standard enthalpy change** when **one mole of a solid substance melts** (fuses) at its melting point under standard pressure (1 bar). Always positive (endothermic — energy must be supplied to overcome intermolecular forces in the crystal). Example: H₂O(s) → H₂O(l); Δ_fus H° = +6.01 kJ mol⁻¹ at 0°C. The reverse process (freezing) releases equal heat: Δ_freeZ H° = –6.01 kJ mol⁻¹. Larger Δ_fus H° indicates stronger intermolecular forces in the solid. Related: Vaporization enthalpy, Sublimation enthalpy, Phase transformation. *(Board/NEET)*

## G

**Gibbs Energy (Gibbs Free Energy, G)**
A **thermodynamic state function** defined as G = H – TS. Named after Josiah Willard Gibbs. At constant temperature and pressure, the change in Gibbs energy gives the  **criterion for spontaneity** : ΔG < 0 (spontaneous), ΔG = 0 (equilibrium), ΔG > 0 (non-spontaneous). The Gibbs equation: ΔG = ΔH – TΔS combines energy (ΔH) and entropy (ΔS) factors. ΔG represents the **maximum non-expansion work** obtainable from a process (or minimum work required). Also called "free energy" because it represents energy available to do useful work. Standard Gibbs energy change relates to equilibrium constant: ΔG° = –RT ln K. Related: Enthalpy, Entropy, Spontaneity, Equilibrium constant, Second law. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## H

**Heat (q)**
Energy transferred between a system and its surroundings as a result of a  **temperature difference** . A **path function** — its value depends on the process, not just initial and final states. IUPAC sign convention: q > 0 when heat flows INTO the system (absorbed); q < 0 when heat flows OUT of the system (evolved). Heat is NOT the same as temperature — temperature measures the intensity of molecular motion, while heat is the total energy transferred. At constant volume: q_V = ΔU. At constant pressure: q_p = ΔH. Related: Work, Internal energy, First law, Enthalpy, Heat capacity. *(Board/NEET)*

**Heat Capacity (C)**
The **quantity of heat required to raise the temperature** of a system by 1°C (or 1 K). q = CΔT. Units: J K⁻¹. Extensive property (depends on amount). At constant volume: C_V (related to ΔU); at constant pressure: C_P (related to ΔH). For ideal gases: C_P – C_V = R. **Molar heat capacity (C_m):** heat capacity per mole = C/n; units J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹. **Specific heat capacity (c):** heat capacity per unit mass; q = mcΔT; units J K⁻¹ g⁻¹. Water has an unusually high specific heat capacity (4.18 J K⁻¹ g⁻¹) due to extensive hydrogen bonding. Related: Enthalpy, Internal energy, Calorimetry, Cp and CV. *(Board/NEET)*

**Hess's Law (Law of Constant Heat Summation)**
States: *"If a reaction takes place in several steps, its standard enthalpy change is the sum of the standard enthalpies of the intermediate reactions."* A direct consequence of **enthalpy being a state function** (path-independent). Allows calculation of ΔH values that cannot be measured directly (e.g., formation of CO from C + ½O₂; incomplete combustion always gives some CO₂). Application: manipulate known reaction equations (multiply, reverse, add) and sum their ΔH values to obtain ΔH of the target reaction. Also used in Born-Haber cycle and calculation of enthalpies of formation from combustion data. Published by Germain Hess (1840). Related: Enthalpy, State function, Thermochemical equations, Born-Haber cycle. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Hydration Enthalpy (Δ_hyd H°)**
The **enthalpy change** when **one mole of gaseous ions** are surrounded by water molecules to form hydrated ions. Always **negative** (exothermic — ion-dipole interactions release energy as water molecules orient around the ion). Larger for smaller, more highly charged ions. Related to enthalpy of solution: Δ_sol H° = Δ_lattice H° + Δ_hyd H°. Example: For NaCl, Δ_hyd H° = –784 kJ mol⁻¹, which almost exactly compensates the lattice enthalpy (+788 kJ mol⁻¹), making NaCl slightly endothermically soluble (+4 kJ mol⁻¹). Related: Lattice enthalpy, Solution enthalpy, Ionic compounds. *(Board/NEET)*

## I

**Intensive Property**
A thermodynamic property whose value is **independent of the amount of matter** present. Does not change when the sample is subdivided. Examples: temperature (T), pressure (p), density, molar volume, molar enthalpy, molar heat capacity, electronegativity, refractive index. Contrast with extensive properties (mass, volume, total internal energy, total enthalpy). Most useful physical constants (density, boiling point, melting point) are intensive. Related: Extensive property. *(Board/NEET)*

**Internal Energy (U)**
The **total energy of a thermodynamic system** — the sum of all kinetic energies (translational, rotational, vibrational) and potential energies (intermolecular, intramolecular) of all particles in the system. A **state function** — ΔU depends only on initial and final states. Cannot be measured in absolute terms (no absolute zero of energy, unlike entropy); only changes ΔU are measurable. The First Law gives: ΔU = q + w. At constant volume: ΔU = q_V. The term was established by Joule's experiments (1840-50) demonstrating the mechanical equivalent of heat. Related: Enthalpy, First law, Heat capacity, Work. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Irreversible Process**
Any process that **does not proceed through a series of equilibrium states** and **cannot be exactly reversed** without leaving a change in the surroundings. All real processes in nature are irreversible to some degree. Examples: free expansion, combustion, dissolution of a salt, rapid compression/expansion. For an irreversible process: ΔS_total > 0. In irreversible expansion: w < w_rev (less work obtained than in reversible process). In irreversible compression: w > w_rev (more work required). Related: Reversible process, Entropy, Spontaneity, Second law. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Isolated System**
A thermodynamic system in which **neither matter nor energy** is exchanged with the surroundings. The boundary is both impermeable to matter AND adiabatic (no heat) AND rigid (no work). Truly isolated systems are idealized — approximated by well-insulated containers. For an isolated system: w = 0, q = 0, so **ΔU = 0** (internal energy is constant). Entropy of an isolated system **increases** for spontaneous processes and reaches a maximum at equilibrium (Second Law). Related: Open system, Closed system, First law, Adiabatic. *(Board)*

## L

**Lattice Enthalpy (Δ_lattice H°)**
The **enthalpy change** when **one mole** of an ionic solid **dissociates** into its constituent gaseous ions at standard conditions. Always **positive** (endothermic; ionic bonds must be broken; energy must be supplied). Cannot be measured directly but is calculated using the **Born-Haber Cycle** (Hess's Law). Example: NaCl(s) → Na⁺(g) + Cl⁻(g); Δ_lattice H° = +788 kJ mol⁻¹. Larger lattice enthalpy → stronger ionic bond → less soluble compound (generally). Explains why LiF and MgO have very high melting points. Related: Born-Haber cycle, Hydration enthalpy, Solution enthalpy, Ionic bonding. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

---

## M

**Mean Bond Enthalpy**
The **average bond enthalpy** for a particular type of bond across a  **range of different molecules** . Used because bond dissociation enthalpy varies slightly from molecule to molecule for the same type of bond (e.g., C–H bond energy in CH₄ vs CH₃Cl). Calculated by dividing the total atomization enthalpy of a molecule by the number of bonds broken. Example for C–H: Mean C–H bond enthalpy = Δ_a H°(CH₄)/4 = 1665/4 =  **416 kJ mol⁻¹** . Used in the approximate formula: Δ_r H° = Σ(bond enthalpies of reactants) – Σ(bond enthalpies of products). Only valid for **gaseous** reactants and products; approximate. Related: Bond dissociation enthalpy, Atomization enthalpy, Hess's Law. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

---

## O

**Open System**
A thermodynamic system that can exchange **both matter and energy** with the surroundings. The boundary is permeable to both. Example: reactants in an open beaker (gases can escape, heat can flow). Most living organisms and many industrial processes are open systems. Contrast: closed systems (energy only) and isolated systems (neither). Related: Closed system, Isolated system. *(Board)*

---

## P

**Path Function**
A thermodynamic quantity whose value **depends on the path** (specific sequence of steps) by which a process is carried out, not merely on the initial and final states. Examples: **heat (q)** and  **work (w)** . The same initial and final states can be connected by many paths, each giving different values of q and w. However, q + w = ΔU is always the same (since ΔU is a state function). Contrast with state functions (U, H, S, G, T, p, V). Related: State function, Heat, Work, First law. *(Board/NEET)*

**Pressure-Volume Work (PV Work)**
The work done on or by a system due to a **change in volume** against an external pressure. Formula: w = –p_ex · ΔV. The negative sign ensures correct sign convention (compression gives positive w; expansion gives negative w). Types: (1) Irreversible: w = –p_ex(V_f – V_i); (2) Multi-step: w = –Σp_ex·ΔV; (3) Reversible: w = –∫p dV = –nRT ln(V_f/V_i). At constant volume: ΔV = 0 → w = 0. This is why bomb calorimeters (constant volume) measure ΔU directly without work corrections. Related: Work, Reversible process, First law, Isothermal process. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

---

## R

**Reaction Enthalpy (Δ_r H°)**
The **standard enthalpy change** accompanying a chemical reaction when stoichiometric amounts of reactants react to give products,  **all in their standard states** . Given by: Δ_r H° = Σa_i·Δ_f H°(products) – Σb_i·Δ_f H°(reactants). Has units of kJ mol⁻¹ (per mole of reaction as written). The value changes if the stoichiometric equation is written differently (extensive property). Reversal of reaction: Δ_r H° changes sign. Related: Standard enthalpy, Enthalpy of formation, Hess's Law. *(Board/NEET)*

**Reversible Process**
A thermodynamic process that is carried out **infinitely slowly** through a series of equilibrium states, such that the system and surroundings are always in near-equilibrium. Can be reversed by an **infinitesimal change** in conditions. Idealized concept — no real process is truly reversible. Properties: maximizes work obtained in expansion; minimizes work required in compression; ΔS_total = 0 (reversible → no net entropy change of universe). The concept is foundational to the second law and defines the entropy: ΔS = q_rev/T. Related: Irreversible process, Entropy, Second law, Work. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## S

**Second Law of Thermodynamics**
States: *"For any spontaneous process, the total entropy of the universe increases."* Mathematically: **ΔS_total = ΔS_system + ΔS_surroundings > 0** for spontaneous processes. At equilibrium: ΔS_total = 0. Explains: (1) why heat flows spontaneously from hot to cold (not vice versa); (2) why gases expand to fill available volume; (3) why all natural processes are irreversible at the atomic scale. Explains why spontaneous exothermic reactions are common: heat released to surroundings increases ΔS_surr, making ΔS_total positive. Multiple equivalent formulations exist (Clausius, Kelvin-Planck, entropy formulations). Related: Entropy, Gibbs energy, Spontaneity, Third law. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Solution Enthalpy (Δ_sol H°)**
The **enthalpy change** when **one mole of a substance dissolves** in a specified amount of solvent at constant temperature and pressure. For ionic compounds: Δ_sol H° = Δ_lattice H° + Δ_hyd H°. The competition between (endothermic) lattice energy and (exothermic) hydration energy determines the sign and magnitude of Δ_sol H°. Example: NaCl: Δ_sol H° = +788 + (–784) = +4 kJ mol⁻¹ (slightly endothermic but soluble due to entropy increase). **At infinite dilution:** ion-ion interactions become negligible; the limiting value represents true enthalpic hydration. Most ionic compounds dissolve endothermically; solubility usually increases with temperature. Related: Lattice enthalpy, Hydration enthalpy, Dilution enthalpy. *(Board/NEET)*

**Spontaneous Process**
A process that **proceeds without external assistance** once initiated; has an intrinsic "driving force." Thermodynamically, spontaneity is defined by: ΔS_total > 0 (Second Law), or equivalently, ΔG < 0 (at constant T and p). Important: spontaneity does NOT mean the process is fast — H₂ + O₂ is spontaneous but extremely slow at room temperature; thermodynamics addresses the direction and extent of change, not the rate. Spontaneous processes are **irreversible** — they cannot spontaneously reverse. Related: Entropy, Gibbs energy, Second law, Irreversible process. *(Board/NEET)*

**Standard Enthalpy of Combustion (Δ_c H°)**
The **standard enthalpy change** when **one mole** of a pure substance undergoes **complete combustion** with oxygen, all reactants and products in standard states. Always **negative** (exothermic). Convention: carbon → CO₂(g); hydrogen → H₂O(l); nitrogen → N₂(g); sulfur → SO₂(g). Example: CH₄(g) + 2O₂(g) → CO₂(g) + 2H₂O(l); Δ_c H° = –890.3 kJ mol⁻¹. Used in Hess's Law calculations to find enthalpies of formation. Related: Enthalpy, Combustion, Hess's Law, Thermochemical equations. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Standard Enthalpy of Formation (Δ_f H°)**
The **standard enthalpy change** for the formation of **one mole** of a compound from its **constituent elements in their most stable reference states** at 25°C (298 K) and 1 bar. By convention: Δ_f H° = 0 for all elements in their reference states (H₂(g), O₂(g), C(graphite), S(rhombic), etc.). Used to calculate Δ_r H°: Δ_r H° = Σa·Δ_f H°(products) – Σb·Δ_f H°(reactants). Key constraint: must form from ELEMENTS, not from other compounds. Key values: H₂O(l) = –285.8; CO₂(g) = –393.5; CH₄(g) = –74.81 kJ mol⁻¹. Related: Hess's Law, Standard enthalpy, Reference state, Thermochemical equations. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Standard State**
The **thermodynamic reference state** of a substance at a **specified temperature and 1 bar pressure** in its  **pure form** . Standard states enable comparison of thermodynamic data across different sources. Current IUPAC standard: 1 bar (not 1 atm, which was older convention). Denoted by superscript ⊖ or °. Examples: Standard state of liquid water at 298 K = pure water at 298 K and 1 bar; standard state of solid iron at 500 K = pure iron at 500 K and 1 bar. Related: Standard enthalpy, Gibbs energy, Reference state. *(Board)*

**State Function**
A thermodynamic property whose value **depends only on the current state** of the system and is **independent of the path** by which that state was reached. Examples: internal energy (U), enthalpy (H), entropy (S), Gibbs energy (G), temperature (T), pressure (p), volume (V). The change in a state function between two states is the same regardless of path: ΔU, ΔH, ΔS, ΔG are path-independent. Contrast with **path functions** (q, w). This path-independence is what makes Hess's Law possible. Related: Path function, Internal energy, Enthalpy, Entropy, Gibbs energy. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Sublimation Enthalpy (Δ_sub H°)**
The **standard enthalpy change** when **one mole** of a solid substance **converts directly to vapour** (gas) at constant temperature and pressure (1 bar), without passing through the liquid phase. Always **positive** (endothermic). Δ_sub H° = Δ_fus H° + Δ_vap H° (by Hess's Law). Examples: CO₂(s) → CO₂(g); Δ_sub H° = +25.2 kJ mol⁻¹; naphthalene: +73.0 kJ mol⁻¹. For metals (Na(s) → Na(g)), sublimation enthalpy equals the enthalpy of atomization. Related: Fusion enthalpy, Vaporization enthalpy, Atomization enthalpy, Hess's Law. *(Board/NEET)*

**Surroundings**
Everything **external to the system** that can interact with the system. In practice, the immediate environment (laboratory, room) rather than the entire universe. The surroundings can exchange matter and/or energy with the system depending on the type of boundary. The temperature and pressure of the surroundings are usually assumed constant (thermostat/barostat). Entropy change of surroundings: ΔS_surr = –ΔH_sys/T (at constant T and p). Related: System, Boundary, Open/closed/isolated systems. *(Board)*

**System**
The **part of the universe** under observation or study in a thermodynamic analysis. Defined by specifying what is inside the boundary. Can be a sample of gas, a solution, a reaction mixture, or even a concept (a defined volume of space). Described by macroscopic state variables: T, p, V, n, and composition. The system is what we measure and what we report thermodynamic quantities for. Related: Surroundings, Boundary, Open/closed/isolated systems, State functions. *(Board)*

## T

**Thermochemical Equation**
A **balanced chemical equation** together with the **corresponding value of Δ_r H** (enthalpy change), with all physical states specified. Conventions: (1) coefficients represent moles; (2) physical states (g, l, s, aq) and allotropic forms must be stated; (3) reversing the equation changes the sign of ΔH; (4) multiplying coefficients multiplies ΔH by the same factor. Example: C₂H₅OH(l) + 3O₂(g) → 2CO₂(g) + 3H₂O(l); Δ_r H° = –1367 kJ mol⁻¹. Related: Hess's Law, Enthalpy, Standard enthalpy. *(Board)*

**Third Law of Thermodynamics**
States: *"The entropy of any pure crystalline substance approaches zero as the temperature approaches absolute zero (0 K)."* This is because at 0 K, there is **perfect order** in a pure crystal — only one possible microstate. Allows calculation of **absolute entropy values** of substances by integrating (q_rev/T) from 0 K to 298 K. Applies only to pure crystalline solids — not to glasses, solutions, or disordered solids. Significance: provides a universal reference point (S = 0 at 0 K) for entropy, unlike U and H (which have no absolute reference). Related: Entropy, Absolute entropy, Second law. *(Board/NEET)*

---

## V

**Vaporization Enthalpy (Δ_vap H°)**
The **standard enthalpy change** when **one mole** of a liquid **vaporizes** at its boiling point and 1 bar pressure. Always **positive** (endothermic). Magnitude depends on strength of intermolecular forces: H₂O has high Δ_vap H° (+40.79 kJ mol⁻¹ at 100°C) due to strong hydrogen bonds; acetone has lower Δ_vap H° due to weaker dipole-dipole forces. The reverse process (condensation) releases the same amount of energy: Δ_cond H° = –Δ_vap H°. Related: Fusion enthalpy, Sublimation enthalpy, Phase transformations, Intermolecular forces. *(Board/NEET)*

---

## W

**Work (w)**
Energy transferred between a system and surroundings by means other than temperature difference. In thermodynamics, primarily  **pressure-volume (PV) work** : w = –p_ex · ΔV. A **path function** — value depends on how the change is brought about. IUPAC sign: positive when work is done ON the system (compression); negative when done BY the system (expansion). In reversible processes: w_rev = –nRT ln(V_f/V_i) for isothermal ideal gas. Work done in free expansion = 0 (p_ex = 0). In thermodynamics, does NOT include heat (different from common usage). Related: Heat, Pressure-volume work, First law, Reversible process, Free expansion. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*
