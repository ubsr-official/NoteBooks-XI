**Q78.** A reaction has K = 10 at 300 K and K = 100 at 400 K. Calculate ΔH° for the reaction assuming it is independent of temperature. [R = 8.314 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹]

**Solution (using van't Hoff equation):**
ln(K₂/K₁) = –ΔH°/R × (1/T₂ – 1/T₁)
ln(100/10) = –ΔH°/8.314 × (1/400 – 1/300)
ln(10) = –ΔH°/8.314 × (0.0025 – 0.00333) = –ΔH°/8.314 × (–0.000833)
2.303 = ΔH°×0.000833/8.314
ΔH° = 2.303 × 8.314 / 0.000833 = **+23,000 J mol⁻¹ ≈ +23 kJ mol⁻¹** (endothermic, K increases with T ✓)

**Q79.** Comment on the thermodynamic stability of NO(g), given:

* ½N₂(g) + ½O₂(g) → NO(g); Δ_r H° = +90 kJ mol⁻¹
* NO(g) + ½O₂(g) → NO₂(g); Δ_r H° = –74 kJ mol⁻¹

**Solution:**
NO(g) has a **positive enthalpy of formation** (+90 kJ mol⁻¹) → it is **thermodynamically unstable** with respect to its constituent elements. Energy must be supplied to form NO; it has a tendency to decompose back to N₂ and O₂.

Additionally, NO further reacts with O₂ exothermically (ΔH = –74 kJ mol⁻¹) to form NO₂, meaning NO is also unstable with respect to oxidation by O₂.

Despite being thermodynamically unstable, NO is kinetically stable (high activation energy for decomposition) — it persists in the atmosphere.

**Q80.** For a reaction at 298 K: ΔG° = –13.6 kJ mol⁻¹. Find the equilibrium constant K.

**Solution:**
ΔG° = –2.303 RT log K
log K = –ΔG°/(2.303 RT) = –(–13,600)/(2.303 × 8.314 × 298)
log K = 13,600/5708.6 = 2.382
**K = antilog(2.382) = 2.41 × 10²**
