### Classification of Elements and Periodicity in Properties

---

> All terms arranged **alphabetically** with precise scientific definitions, related concepts, equations, and exam relevance tags.

---

## A

**Actinoids (Actinides)**
The series of 14 **f-block inner-transition elements** from Thorium (Th, Z=90) to Lawrencium (Lr, Z=103), constituting the 5f-inner transition series. Placed in **Period 7** of the Periodic Table. General outer electronic configuration: (n-2)f¹⁻¹⁴(n-1)d⁰⁻¹ns², specifically (5f¹⁻¹⁴)(6d⁰⁻¹)(7s²). All actinoids are  **radioactive metals** . The early actinoids resemble transition elements due to availability of multiple oxidation states. Many are man-made (only in nanogram quantities); their chemistry is not fully studied. Elements after uranium (Z=92) are called  **transuranium elements** . Related: Lanthanoids, f-block, Inner-transition elements, Transuranium elements. *(Board/NEET)*

**Amphoteric Oxide**
An oxide that reacts with both  **acids and bases** . Found for elements lying in the **centre of a period** (e.g., Al₂O₃, As₂O₃, ZnO). This dual behaviour arises because these elements have intermediate electronegativity values and metallic/non-metallic character. Contrasts with basic oxides (Na₂O, MgO) that react only with acids, and acidic oxides (SO₃, Cl₂O₇) that react only with bases. Example: Al₂O₃ + 6HCl → 2AlCl₃ + 3H₂O (acid behaviour); Al₂O₃ + 2NaOH → 2NaAlO₂ + H₂O (base behaviour). Related: Basic oxide, Acidic oxide, Periodic trends in chemical reactivity. *(Board/NEET)*

**Atomic Radius**
An experimentally estimated measure of the  **effective size of an atom** , defined differently depending on bonding context: (1) **Covalent radius** — half the internuclear distance between two identical atoms bonded by a single covalent bond (used for non-metals). (2) **Metallic radius** — half the internuclear distance between adjacent atoms in a metallic crystal. (3) **van der Waals radius** — half the distance between nuclei of adjacent non-bonded atoms (relevant for noble gases); largest of the three types. Atomic radii are measured by X-ray or spectroscopic methods. The term "atomic radius" without qualification usually means covalent or metallic radius as appropriate. Related: Covalent radius, Metallic radius, Periodic trends, van der Waals radius. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## B

**Basic Oxide**
An oxide that reacts with **water to form a base** (alkali) or reacts with acids to form salt and water. Formed by elements on the **extreme left** of a period (alkali metals, alkaline earth metals). Example: Na₂O + H₂O → 2NaOH (strong base); MgO + H₂SO₄ → MgSO₄ + H₂O. The basicity of an oxide increases as we go down Group 1 or Group 2 (increasing metallic character). Related: Acidic oxide, Amphoteric oxide, Metallic character. *(Board/NEET)*

---

## C

**Chalcogens**
The elements of **Group 16** of the periodic table: O, S, Se, Te, Po (and Lv synthetically). These are p-block elements with outer electronic configuration ns²np⁴. They have highly negative electron gain enthalpies and readily add **two electrons** to attain stable noble gas configurations. Oxygen and sulphur are the most chemically important. Name derives from Greek "chalkos" (copper/ore) because many important metal ores are sulphides/oxides. Related: Halogens, p-block, Electron gain enthalpy. *(Board/NEET)*

**Covalent Radius**
Half the distance between the nuclei of two **identical atoms linked by a single covalent bond** in a molecule. Measured from X-ray diffraction data. Example: In Cl₂, the Cl–Cl bond distance = 198 pm → covalent radius of Cl =  **99 pm** . For different atoms bonded together, the bond length ≈ sum of their individual covalent radii (with corrections). Contrasts with metallic radius (for metals) and van der Waals radius (for non-bonded contacts). Related: Atomic radius, Bond length. *(Board/NEET)*

---

## D

**d-Block Elements (Transition Elements)**
Elements of **Groups 3–12** in the modern periodic table in which the  **last electron enters the (n-1)d subshell** . General outer electronic configuration: (n-1)d¹⁻¹⁰ns⁰⁻² (exception: Pd = 4d¹⁰5s⁰). All are metals. Key properties: form coloured ions (due to d–d transitions), exhibit variable oxidation states (multiple valences), show paramagnetism (unpaired d electrons), often act as catalysts. Form a bridge between chemically active s-block metals and less active p-block elements. Note: Zn, Cd, Hg [(n-1)d¹⁰ns²] lack most transition element properties due to completely filled d subshell. Four d-series: 3d (Sc–Zn), 4d (Y–Cd), 5d (La/Hf–Hg), 6d (Ac/Rf–Cn). Related: s-block, p-block, f-block, Transition elements. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Diagonal Relationship**
The chemical similarity observed between a **first-row element** and the **second element of the group to the right** in the periodic table (i.e., diagonal neighbours). Examples: Li–Mg, Be–Al, B–Si. Arises because diagonal elements have similar **charge-to-radius ratios** (polarising power) and thus form compounds with similar properties (similar degree of covalent character, hydrolysis behaviour, etc.). Example: Both Li and Mg form nitrides directly with N₂ (Li₃N and Mg₃N₂); both form normal carbonates; lithium carbonate decomposes on heating like magnesium carbonate. Related: Anomalous properties of second period elements, Polarising power. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Dobereiner's Triads**
An early attempt at classification of elements by **Johann Dobereiner** (German chemist, 1829). He observed that groups of three chemically similar elements ( **triads** ) could be formed where the **atomic weight of the middle element is approximately the arithmetic mean** of the atomic weights of the other two, and its properties are intermediate. Examples: (Li, Na, K), (Ca, Sr, Ba), (Cl, Br, I). Limitation: Worked for only a few elements; the law of triads was limited and dismissed as coincidence by many. Historical significance: First quantitative attempt to find numerical patterns among element properties. Related: Periodic classification, Newlands' Law, Mendeleev's Periodic Law. *(Board)*

## E

**Effective Nuclear Charge (Zeff)**
The **net positive charge experienced by a valence electron** in a multi-electron atom, after accounting for the repulsive shielding effect of inner (core) electrons. Zeff = Z − σ, where Z = actual nuclear charge and σ = shielding constant. Since inner electrons partially shield outer electrons from the full nuclear charge, Zeff < Z always. Increasing Zeff → smaller atomic radius, higher IE, more negative ΔₑgH, higher electronegativity. Zeff increases across a period (shielding from same-shell electrons is less effective) and remains roughly constant or increases slightly down a group. Related: Shielding effect, Atomic radius, Ionization enthalpy. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Electron Gain Enthalpy (ΔₑgH)**
The **enthalpy change** when an electron is added to a **neutral, isolated gaseous atom** to form a negative ion (anion): X(g) + e⁻ → X⁻(g). Units: kJ mol⁻¹. Can be exothermic (negative ΔₑgH, energy released — e.g., halogens) or endothermic (positive ΔₑgH, energy required — e.g., noble gases, N). Most negative ΔₑgH: **Cl** (–349 kJ mol⁻¹). Anomaly: F has less negative ΔₑgH than Cl due to compact n=2 shell causing electron-electron repulsion. Trend: Becomes more negative across a period (→); becomes less negative down a group (↓). Distinction from electron affinity: ΔₑgH defined at 0K with thermodynamic sign convention; Aₑ (electron affinity) defined with opposite sign. Related: Electronegativity, Ionization enthalpy, Periodic trends. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Electronegativity**
A qualitative (dimensionless) measure of the **tendency of an atom in a chemical compound to attract the shared bonding electrons** towards itself. Not a property of an isolated atom — varies with bonding environment. Most widely used scale: **Pauling scale** (F = 4.0, reference maximum). Other scales: Mulliken–Jaffé, Allred–Rochow. Trends: Increases across a period (→) as Zeff increases and atomic radius decreases; decreases down a group (↓) as atomic radius increases and Zeff felt by valence electrons decreases. High electronegativity → non-metallic, oxidising character. Low electronegativity → metallic, reducing character. Related: Electron gain enthalpy, Ionization enthalpy, Atomic radius, Ionic/covalent bond character. *(Board/NEET)*

---

## F

**f-Block Elements (Inner-Transition Elements)**
Elements in which the  **last electron enters the (n-2)f subshell** . Two series: **Lanthanoids** [Ce(Z=58)–Lu(Z=71), 4f series, Period 6] and **Actinoids** [Th(Z=90)–Lr(Z=103), 5f series, Period 7]. General outer configuration: (n-2)f¹⁻¹⁴(n-1)d⁰⁻¹ns². All are metals with quite similar properties within each series. Placed separately at the bottom of the periodic table to maintain its compact structure. Related: Lanthanoids, Actinoids, d-block, Inner-transition elements. *(Board/NEET)*

---

## G

**Group (Family)**
A **vertical column** in the modern periodic table. Elements in the same group have the **same valence shell electronic configuration** and therefore exhibit similar chemical properties. IUPAC numbering: Groups 1–18 (replacing older IA–VIIA, VIII, IB–VIIB, 0 notation). Groups 1 and 2 = s-block; Groups 3–12 = d-block; Groups 13–18 = p-block; f-block placed separately. Related: Period, Valence shell, Electronic configuration, Periodic Law. *(Board)*

---

## H

**Halogens**
Elements of  **Group 17** : F, Cl, Br, I, At (and Ts synthetically). p-Block elements with outer electronic configuration ns²np⁵ — one electron short of noble gas configuration. Have the **most negative electron gain enthalpies** (strongly exothermic when they gain an electron) among all groups. Highly electronegative, strong oxidising agents. Reactivity decreases down the group (F > Cl > Br > I). Form −1 ions (halide ions) in ionic compounds. Related: Chalcogens, p-block, Electron gain enthalpy, Electronegativity. *(Board/NEET)*

## I

**Inert Pair Effect**
The tendency of the two electrons in the outermost s-subshell to remain non-bonding (inert) in heavier p-block elements. More pronounced as we go **down a p-block group** — heavier elements prefer lower oxidation states. Example: Tl (Group 13) commonly forms Tl⁺ (not Tl³⁺ like Al and Ga); Pb (Group 14) commonly forms Pb²⁺ (not Pb⁴⁺ like C and Si); Bi (Group 15) commonly forms Bi³⁺ (not Bi⁵⁺). Attributed to relativistic contraction of the s-orbital and poor shielding by d and f electrons in heavier elements. Related: Anomalous properties, Oxidation state, Periodic trends. *(JEE)*

**Ionic Radius**
The **effective size of an ion** in an ionic crystal, estimated from internuclear distances between cations and anions in crystals. Cations are **smaller** than parent atoms (fewer electrons, same Z → higher Zeff per electron). Anions are **larger** than parent atoms (more electrons, same Z → lower Zeff per electron, more repulsion). Trends: Increases down a group (as in atomic radii). For **isoelectronic species** (same number of electrons), the ion with the greater nuclear charge (Z) has the smaller radius. Example: In isoelectronic series O²⁻, F⁻, Na⁺, Mg²⁺ (all 10e⁻): O²⁻ > F⁻ > Na⁺ > Mg²⁺. Related: Atomic radius, Isoelectronic species, Cation, Anion. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Ionization Enthalpy (Ionization Energy, ΔᵢH)**
The energy required to remove the **most loosely bound electron** from an isolated, neutral, gaseous atom in its  **ground state** . First IE: X(g) → X⁺(g) + e⁻. Second IE: X⁺(g) → X²⁺(g) + e⁻. Always **positive** (energy must be supplied). ΔᵢH₁ < ΔᵢH₂ < ΔᵢH₃... (each successive IE is larger). A large jump in consecutive IE values indicates a change in the electron being removed — crossing from valence to core (noble gas configuration). Factors: nuclear charge (↑Z → ↑IE), atomic radius (↑r → ↓IE), shielding (↑shielding → ↓IE), orbital penetration (s > p > d > f). Anomalies in Period 2: IE of B < Be (2p vs 2s electron); IE of O < N (paired 2p electron in O → repulsion). Related: Electron gain enthalpy, Electronegativity, Effective nuclear charge, Shielding effect. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Isoelectronic Species**
Atoms or ions that have the **same number of electrons** (and usually same electronic configuration). *NCERT definition extension:* Two or more species with same number of atoms, same number of valence electrons and same structure, regardless of the nature of elements involved. For ions: O²⁻, F⁻, Ne, Na⁺, Mg²⁺, Al³⁺ all have 10 electrons. For isoelectronic ions, the size depends only on nuclear charge: higher Z → smaller radius. Example sequence (10 electrons, decreasing radius): N³⁻ > O²⁻ > F⁻ > Ne > Na⁺ > Mg²⁺ > Al³⁺. Related: Ionic radius, Effective nuclear charge. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## L

**Lanthanoids (Lanthanides)**
The series of 14 **4f inner-transition elements** from Cerium (Ce, Z=58) to Lutetium (Lu, Z=71), placed in **Period 6** of the Periodic Table. General outer electronic configuration: (4f¹⁻¹⁴)(5d⁰⁻¹)(6s²). Sometimes lanthanum (Z=57) itself is included in the series, though it has no 4f electrons. Properties across the series are quite similar. Exhibit a phenomenon called **lanthanoid contraction** — gradual decrease in atomic/ionic radii across the series due to poor shielding by 4f electrons. Lanthanoid contraction has important consequences for the 5d transition series (their radii are unusually similar to the 4d counterparts). Related: Actinoids, f-block, 4f-inner transition series, Lanthanoid contraction. *(Board/NEET)*

**Law of Octaves (Newlands, 1865)**
A periodic relationship proposed by English chemist  **John Alexander Newlands** . On arranging elements in order of increasing  **atomic weights** , every **eighth element** had properties similar to the first — analogous to the octaves of music. Worked for elements up to **calcium** only; broke down for heavier elements. Awarded Davy Medal (1887) by the Royal Society despite initial rejection. Historical significance: First attempt to find a periodic numerical relationship going beyond simple triads. Related: Dobereiner's Triads, Mendeleev's Periodic Law, Periodic classification. *(Board)*

## M

**Mendeleev's Periodic Law**
Proposed by Dmitri Mendeleev (1869): *"The properties of the elements are a periodic function of their atomic weights."* Elements were arranged in **horizontal rows** and **vertical columns** (groups) in order of increasing atomic weight such that elements with similar properties fell in the same vertical group. Key features: left gaps for undiscovered elements, successfully predicted properties of Eka-aluminium (Ga) and Eka-silicon (Ge), placed some elements out of strict atomic weight order to preserve property groupings (e.g., Te before I). Limitation: Could not explain why periodicity occurs, position of isotopes, anomalies in atomic weight ordering. Superseded by the **Modern Periodic Law** (based on atomic number). Related: Modern Periodic Law, Moseley, Atomic number, Periodic classification. *(Board/NEET)*

**Metallic Radius**
Half the internuclear distance between two **adjacent metal atoms** in a metallic crystal (close-packed structure). Larger than covalent radius for the same element (metallic bonding is less directional, atoms pack loosely compared to covalent bonds). Example: In solid copper, adjacent Cu–Cu distance = 256 pm → metallic radius of Cu =  **128 pm** . Trends same as covalent radius: decreases across a period, increases down a group. Related: Atomic radius, Covalent radius. *(Board/NEET)*

**Metalloids (Semi-metals)**
Elements with properties  **intermediate between metals and non-metals** . Located along the **zig-zag borderline** in the periodic table. Examples: Si, Ge, As, Sb, Te (At is sometimes included). They are semiconductors (electrical conductivity between metals and non-metals), often have metallic lustre but are brittle like non-metals. Technologically important: Si and Ge are key semiconductor materials. Related: Metals, Non-metals, Periodic classification. *(Board/NEET)*

**Modern Periodic Law**
Formulated after Moseley's work (1913): *"The physical and chemical properties of the elements are periodic functions of their atomic numbers."* The periodicity arises from the **periodic variation in electronic configurations** as atomic number increases. This law resolved anomalies in Mendeleev's table (e.g., position of Ar/K, Co/Ni, Te/I). The Modern Periodic Table has 7 periods and 18 groups. Related: Mendeleev's Periodic Law, Moseley, Atomic number, Electronic configuration. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Moseley's Experiment (1913)**
Henry Moseley, English physicist, studied **characteristic X-ray spectra** of various elements. He found that plotting √ν (where ν = frequency of X-rays emitted) against **atomic number (Z)** gave a  **straight line** , whereas plotting against atomic mass did not. Conclusion: **Atomic number is a more fundamental property** of an element than its atomic mass. This discovery led to the modification of Mendeleev's Periodic Law into the Modern Periodic Law and resolved anomalies where elements had been placed in incorrect order of atomic mass. Related: Atomic number, Modern Periodic Law, X-ray spectra. *(Board/NEET)*

## N

**Newlands' Law of Octaves**
*See Law of Octaves.*

**Noble Gases (Group 18)**
Elements He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe, Rn (and Og synthetically). Characterised by **completely filled valence shells** (ns²np⁶, except He with 1s²). Extremely **low chemical reactivity** because it is very difficult to add or remove electrons from filled shells. **Ionization enthalpies:** Highest in each period (cannot be ionised easily). **Electron gain enthalpies:** Large positive values (added electron must enter next principal level). Atomic radii listed are **van der Waals radii** (non-bonded contacts) — not comparable to covalent/metallic radii of other elements. Helium is s-block but placed in Group 18 because of its noble gas properties. Related: p-block, Ionization enthalpy, Electron gain enthalpy, van der Waals radius. *(Board/NEET)*

## O

**Oxidation State (Oxidation Number)**
The **formal charge** assigned to an atom in a compound based on electronegativity considerations — the charge the atom would have if all bonds were completely ionic. Determined by assigning shared electrons entirely to the more electronegative atom. Example: In OF₂ (F more electronegative than O): F = −1, O = +2. In Na₂O (O more electronegative than Na): Na = +1, O = −2. Distinct from **covalency** (number of bonds formed). Example: In [AlCl(H₂O)₅]²⁺, oxidation state of Al = +3 but covalency = 6. Particularly variable in transition metals and actinoids. Related: Valence, Electronegativity, Periodic trends. *(Board/NEET)*

## P

**p-Block Elements**
Elements of **Groups 13–18** in which the **last electron enters the p-subshell** of the outermost shell. Outermost configuration: ns²np¹ to ns²np⁶ across each period. Together with s-block elements, called **Representative Elements** or Main Group Elements. Include non-metals (O, N, F, Cl, etc.), metalloids (Si, Ge, As), and some metals (Al, Ga, Sn, Pb, Bi, Tl). Noble gases end each period (closed ns²np⁶ shell). Halogens (Group 17) and chalcogens (Group 16) have most negative electron gain enthalpies. Non-metallic character increases left→right; metallic character increases down any group. Special members: H and He placed separately. Related: s-block, d-block, Halogens, Chalcogens, Noble gases. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Pauling Scale**
The most widely used scale of  **electronegativity** , proposed by Linus Pauling (American chemist, 1922). Assigns fluorine an arbitrary reference value of **4.0** (most electronegative). Other values are derived from thermochemical bond energy data. Ranges from ~0.7 (Cs) to 4.0 (F). Key values to remember: F=4.0, O=3.5, N=3.0, Cl=3.0, C=2.5, H=2.1, Na=0.9, K=0.8, Cs=0.7. Related: Electronegativity, Mulliken scale, Allred-Rochow scale. *(Board/NEET)*

**Period**
A **horizontal row** in the modern periodic table. There are  **7 periods** . The period number equals the **highest principal quantum number (n)** of elements in that period. The number of elements in a period = 2 × (number of atomic orbitals available at that energy level). Period lengths: 1→2; 2→8; 3→8; 4→18; 5→18; 6→32; 7→32. Properties change systematically across a period due to increasing nuclear charge with same principal quantum level. Related: Group, Principal quantum number, Periodic Law. *(Board)*

**Periodic Law**
*See Mendeleev's Periodic Law or Modern Periodic Law.*

## R

**Representative Elements (Main Group Elements)**
Elements of the s-block (Groups 1–2) and p-block (Groups 13–18). Their valence electrons are in the outermost s or p subshells. Include both metals (Na, Mg, Al) and non-metals (C, N, O, Cl) and metalloids (Si, Ge). Exhibit clear periodic trends in properties (atomic radius, IE, ΔₑgH, electronegativity) across periods and down groups. Contrasted with **transition elements** (d-block) and **inner-transition elements** (f-block). Related: s-block, p-block, Periodic trends. *(Board/NEET)*

---

## S

**s-Block Elements**
Elements of **Groups 1 and 2** in which the **last electron enters the s-subshell** of the outermost shell. Group 1 (alkali metals): ns¹ configuration. Group 2 (alkaline earth metals): ns² configuration. All are **reactive metals** with low ionization enthalpies. Never found free in nature (too reactive). Compounds predominantly ionic (except Li and Be). Metallic and reactive character increases down the group. Exceptions in chemical behaviour: Li resembles Mg (diagonal relationship); Be resembles Al. Related: Alkali metals, Alkaline earth metals, p-block, Diagonal relationship. *(Board/NEET)*

**Shielding Effect (Screening Effect)**
The reduction in effective nuclear charge experienced by outer electrons due to the presence of **inner (core) electrons** that partially shield them from the nucleus. Inner electrons repel outer electrons, reducing the net nuclear attraction. Effectiveness of shielding: s > p > d > f electrons (for same shell). Complete shielding occurs when inner shells are fully filled (as in noble gas cores). Greater shielding → lower Zeff → larger atomic radius, lower IE, lower electronegativity. Explains why across a period the IE increases (shielding stays approximately constant but Z increases) and why down a group IE decreases (shielding increases as more inner shells are filled). Related: Effective nuclear charge, Ionization enthalpy, Atomic radius. *(NEET/JEE)*

---

## T

**Transuranium Elements**
All elements with  **atomic number greater than 92 (uranium)** . These elements (from Z=93 neptunium onwards) do not occur naturally in significant quantities and are produced artificially by nuclear reactions. They are all radioactive with short half-lives. Many are actinoids (Z=93–103). They require sophisticated and expensive equipment to synthesise and characterise. Only a few atoms of the heaviest ones are ever produced. Related: Actinoids, f-block, IUPAC nomenclature. *(Board)*

**Triads**
*See Dobereiner's Triads.*

## V

**Valence**
The **combining capacity** of an element — the number of bonds it forms. For representative elements: valence = number of electrons in outermost orbitals, OR = 8 − (number of outermost electrons). Example: N has 5 valence electrons → valence = 3 (bonds to 3 H in NH₃) or 5 (in N₂O₅). The term **oxidation state** is now frequently used in place of valence, particularly for transition metals. Related: Oxidation state, Valence electrons, Electronic configuration. *(Board/NEET)*

**van der Waals Radius**
Half the distance between the nuclei of two **adjacent, non-bonded atoms** of the same element in the solid state or in adjacent molecules. Larger than both covalent and metallic radii for the same element because no chemical bond is involved — atoms are held only by weak van der Waals forces. Used for **noble gases** (which are monoatomic and do not form covalent bonds). Noble gas radii should not be compared directly with covalent radii of other elements. Related: Atomic radius, Covalent radius, Metallic radius. *(Board/NEET)*

## Z

**Zeff (Effective Nuclear Charge)**
*See Effective Nuclear Charge.*

**Zig-Zag Line (in Periodic Table)**
The **thick diagonal line** drawn in the periodic table that separates **metals** (to the left) from **non-metals** (to the right). Elements bordering this line — Si, Ge, As, Sb, Te — are the **metalloids** (semi-metals), with properties intermediate between metals and non-metals. The transition from metallic to non-metallic character across a period is gradual, not abrupt — reflected by this line. Related: Metals, Non-metals, Metalloids. *(Board)*
