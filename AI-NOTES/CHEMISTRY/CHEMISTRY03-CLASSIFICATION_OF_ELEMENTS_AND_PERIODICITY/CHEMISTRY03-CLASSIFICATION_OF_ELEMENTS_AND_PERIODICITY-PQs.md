**Assertion (A):** The second ionization enthalpy of sodium is much greater than that of magnesium.

**Assertion (A):** The first ionization enthalpy of oxygen is lower than that of nitrogen.

**Assertion (A):** Alkali metals are the most reactive metals in each period.
**Reason (R):** Alkali metals have the highest ionization enthalpies among metals in their respective periods.

**Reason (R):** The added electron in noble gases must enter the next higher principal quantum level, leading to a very unstable electronic configuration.

## Case Study 1 — Dobereiner to Modern Law 🟢

Read the following passage and answer Q61–Q63:

*The journey from Dobereiner's Triads (1829) to the Modern Periodic Law (1869, modified 1913) represents over 80 years of scientific effort. Dobereiner noticed that groups of three elements shared properties and had atomic weights in arithmetic progression. Newlands extended this idea by noting that every 8th element shared properties — but only up to calcium. Mendeleev arranged all known elements by atomic weight, left gaps for unknowns, and predicted properties of missing elements with remarkable accuracy. It was Moseley in 1913 who finally showed that atomic number, not atomic mass, is the true basis for periodicity.*

**Q82.** 🟡 The first ionization enthalpy values (kJ mol⁻¹) of third period elements are:
Na = 496, Mg = 737, Al = 577, Si = 786, P = 1012, S = 1000, Cl = 1251, Ar = 1521.

Identify and explain the TWO anomalies in this sequence.

**Solution:**

**Anomaly 1: Al (577) < Mg (737)**
→ Mg removes a 3s electron; Al removes a 3p electron. The 3p electron is better shielded from the nucleus by the 3s electrons (3s has greater penetration) → 3p electron of Al is easier to remove than 3s electron of Mg. Lower IE for Al than expected.

**Anomaly 2: S (1000) < P (1012)**
→ P has 3p³ (half-filled, extra stable; each electron in separate orbital, no pairing). S has 3p⁴; one 3p orbital has a paired electron → electron-electron repulsion makes it easier to remove one electron from S than from the stable half-filled 3p³ of P. Lower IE for S than expected.

**Q83.** 🔴 Using the data below, classify each element I–VI as: (a) least reactive element, (b) most reactive metal, (c) most reactive non-metal, (d) alkaline earth metal, (e) noble gas, (f) least reactive non-metal.

| Element | ΔᵢH₁ | ΔᵢH₂ | ΔₑgH |
| ------- | ------- | ------- | ------ |
| I       | 520     | 7300    | −60   |
| II      | 419     | 3051    | −48   |
| III     | 1681    | 3374    | −328  |
| IV      | 1008    | 1846    | −295  |
| V       | 2372    | 5251    | +48    |
| VI      | 738     | 1451    | −40   |

**Solution:**

* **I (alkali metal, Group 1):** Low IE₁ (520), enormously high IE₂ (7300) — stable noble gas core after losing 1 electron →  **alkali metal (e.g., Na)** . *(b) Most reactive metal*
* **II (alkali metal, possibly K):** Very low IE₁ (419), moderately high IE₂ → most reactive metal compared to I? IE₁(II) = 419 < IE₁(I) = 520 →  **II is the most reactive metal (e.g., K)** . *(b) Most reactive metal*
* **III (halogen, most reactive non-metal):** High IE₁, most negative ΔₑgH (−328) → **F or Cl** (halogen). *(c) Most reactive non-metal*
* **IV (halogen, less reactive):** High IE₁ (1008), ΔₑgH = −295 → less reactive halogen (e.g., I or Br). *(f) Least reactive non-metal* among halogens given
