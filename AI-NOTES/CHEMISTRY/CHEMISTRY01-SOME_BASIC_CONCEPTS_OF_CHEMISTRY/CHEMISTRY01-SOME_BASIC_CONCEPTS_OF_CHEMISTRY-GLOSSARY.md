# CHAPTER 1 — GLOSSARY & TERMINOLOGY

### Some Basic Concepts of Chemistry

---

> All terms arranged **alphabetically** with precise scientific definitions, related concepts, and exam relevance tags.

---

## A

**Accuracy**
The degree to which a measured value agrees with the **true or accepted value** of the quantity. A measurement can be precise without being accurate, but a truly accurate measurement must be precise. *(Board/NEET)*

**Alchemy**
A pre-scientific tradition that sought to convert base metals into gold and find the elixir of life. Chemistry developed from alchemical traditions (1300–1600 CE). In India, it existed alongside Iatrochemistry.

**Atom**
The smallest indivisible particle of an element that retains the chemical properties of that element. Proposed as 'Paramānu' by Acharya Kanda (600 BCE) and formally defined in Dalton's Atomic Theory (1808). Atoms may exist singly (Na, Cu) or as molecules (H₂, O₂).

**Atomic Mass**
The mass of an atom expressed in  **atomic mass units (u)** . Currently referenced to ¹²C = 12 u exactly (standard adopted in 1961). In practice, the **average atomic mass** is used, which accounts for isotopic abundances.

**Atomic Mass Unit (amu / u)**
A unit of mass equal to  **1/12 the mass of one ¹²C atom** .
1 u = 1 amu =  **1.66056 × 10⁻²⁴ g** .
The modern symbol is **u** (unified mass unit).

**Average Atomic Mass**
A weighted average of the masses of all naturally occurring isotopes of an element, weighted by their relative abundances.
Formula: Σ (fractional abundance × isotopic mass). *(NEET/JEE)*

**Avogadro's Constant (Nₐ)**
The number of elementary entities in **one mole** of a substance.
Nₐ =  **6.02214076 × 10²³ mol⁻¹** . Named in honour of Amedeo Avogadro. *(All levels)*

**Avogadro's Law**
Proposed in 1811: "Equal volumes of all gases at the same temperature and pressure contain equal numbers of molecules." Distinguished between atoms and molecules.

---

## B

**Balanced Chemical Equation**
A chemical equation in which the number of atoms of each element is the same on both the reactant and product sides, satisfying the Law of Conservation of Mass. Only coefficients (not subscripts) are changed to balance.

**Bhasma**
Metal preparations described in *Charaka Samhita* used in Ayurvedic medicine. Modern studies show bhasmas contain **nanoparticles** of metals — an ancient application of what is now called nanotechnology.

---

## C

**Candela (cd)**
The SI base unit of  **luminous intensity** . Defined by the luminous efficacy of monochromatic radiation of frequency 540 × 10¹² Hz.

**Chemical Property**
A property that can only be observed when a substance undergoes a **chemical change** (i.e., the substance is transformed into a different substance). Examples: combustibility, acidity, reactivity with acids.

**Compound**
A pure substance formed when two or more elements combine chemically in a  **fixed and definite ratio by mass** . Properties of a compound are different from those of its constituent elements. Components can only be separated by chemical methods.

**Concentration**
The amount of solute present in a given quantity of solution or solvent. Expressed as molarity, molality, mole fraction, or mass per cent.

---

## D

**Dalton's Atomic Theory (1808)**
Published in  *'A New System of Chemical Philosophy'* . Key postulates: atoms are indivisible; atoms of the same element are identical; compounds form from fixed atom ratios; atoms are rearranged (not created/destroyed) in reactions. Limitations: could not explain isotopes, isobars, or Gay Lussac's Law.

**Density**
Mass per unit volume of a substance: ρ = m/V. SI unit:  **kg m⁻³** ; lab unit: **g cm⁻³** or  **g mL⁻¹** . Higher density = more closely packed particles.

**Dimensional Analysis**
Also called **factor label method** or  **unit factor method** . A mathematical technique for unit conversion that involves multiplying a quantity by **unit factors** (ratios equal to 1) to cancel unwanted units and introduce desired units. *(All levels)*

---

## E

**Element**
A pure substance consisting of only  **one type of atom** . Cannot be broken down into simpler substances by chemical means. May exist as single atoms (Na, Fe) or molecules (H₂, O₂, P₄, S₈).

**Empirical Formula**
A chemical formula that shows the **simplest whole number ratio** of atoms of each element present in a compound. Derived from experimental % composition data. May or may not be same as molecular formula. Example: CH₂O is the empirical formula for both formaldehyde (CH₂O) and glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆). *(NEET/JEE)*

**Elixir of Life**
A mythical substance that alchemists sought, believed to grant immortality. Along with the Philosopher's Stone, it was a primary motivation for early chemical investigation.

---

## F

**Faience**
A type of glass made by the Harappan civilisation, used in ornaments. Evidence of early glass-making in ancient India.

**Formula Mass**
Used in place of molecular mass for **ionic compounds** (like NaCl) that do not exist as discrete molecules but as three-dimensional lattice structures. Calculated as the sum of atomic masses of all atoms in the formula unit. Example: NaCl = 23 + 35.5 =  **58.5 u** . *(Board/NEET)*

---

## G

**Gay Lussac's Law of Gaseous Volumes (1808)**
"When gases combine or are produced in a chemical reaction, they do so in a  **simple ratio by volume** , provided all gases are at the same temperature and pressure." This is essentially the Law of Definite Proportions applied to volumes. Correctly explained by Avogadro's Law.

---

## H

**Heterogeneous Mixture**
A mixture in which the composition is **not uniform** throughout. Different components may be visible. Example: soil, sand + salt, oil + water.

**Homogeneous Mixture**
A mixture in which the composition is **uniform throughout** (particles of components are evenly distributed). Also called a  **solution** . Example: air, sugar solution, vinegar.

---

## I

**Iatrochemistry**
A branch of chemistry (1300–1600 CE) focused on the application of chemistry to medicine. Preceded modern chemistry. Declined in India with the introduction of Western medicinal systems in the 20th century.

**Isotopes**
Atoms of the **same element** (same atomic number) but with **different mass numbers** (different numbers of neutrons). Example: ¹²C, ¹³C, ¹⁴C are all isotopes of carbon. The existence of isotopes is a limitation of Dalton's Atomic Theory. *(NEET/JEE)*

---

## K

**Kelvin (K)**
The SI base unit of  **thermodynamic temperature** . Defined using the Boltzmann constant. Conversion: K = °C + 273.15. The kelvin scale has no negative values (absolute zero = 0 K = −273.15°C).

**Kilogram (kg)**
The SI base unit of  **mass** . Defined by the fixed value of Planck's constant h = 6.62607015 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s.

---

## L

**Law of Conservation of Mass (Lavoisier, 1789)**
"In all physical and chemical changes, there is no net change in mass during the process. Matter can neither be created nor destroyed." This forms the basis for balancing chemical equations.

**Law of Definite Proportions (Proust)**
"A given compound always contains exactly the same proportion of elements by weight (mass)." Also called Law of Definite Composition. Confirmed by Proust's experiments on cupric carbonate.

**Law of Multiple Proportions (Dalton, 1803)**
"If two elements form more than one compound, the masses of one element that combine with a fixed mass of the other are in a ratio of small whole numbers." Example: CO and CO₂ — oxygen masses 16g and 32g for same mass of carbon → ratio 1:2.

**Limiting Reagent (Limiting Reactant)**
The reactant that is **completely consumed first** in a reaction, thereby limiting the total amount of product that can be formed. Identified by dividing moles of each reactant by its stoichiometric coefficient — smallest value indicates limiting reagent. *(NEET/JEE most tested)*

---

## M

**Matter**
Anything that has **mass** and **occupies space** (has volume). Classified into solids, liquids, and gases (by state) or elements, compounds, and mixtures (by composition).

**Metre (m)**
The SI base unit of  **length** . Defined as the length of path travelled by light in vacuum in 1/299,792,458 of a second. (Redefined in 1983 by CGPM.)

**Mixture**
A substance containing **two or more pure substances** combined in any ratio, with variable composition. Properties are intermediate between those of components. Separated by physical methods.

**Molality (m)**
Number of moles of solute dissolved per  **kilogram of solvent** . Unit: mol kg⁻¹. **Temperature-independent** (since mass doesn't change with temperature). Preferred for colligative property calculations.

**Molar Mass (M)**
The mass of **one mole** of a substance, expressed in g mol⁻¹. Numerically equal to the atomic/molecular/formula mass in u. Example: molar mass of H₂O = 18.02 g mol⁻¹.

**Molarity (M)**
Number of moles of solute per  **litre of solution** . Unit: mol L⁻¹. **Temperature-dependent** (because volume changes with temperature). Most common concentration unit in lab chemistry. Dilution: M₁V₁ = M₂V₂.

**Mole (mol)**
The SI base unit of  **amount of substance** . One mole contains exactly **6.02214076 × 10²³** elementary entities (Avogadro's number). Can represent atoms, molecules, ions, electrons, or any other specified particles. *(All levels — most fundamental concept)*

**Molecular Formula**
A chemical formula showing the **actual number** of each type of atom in one molecule of a compound. Obtained by multiplying the empirical formula by n = (Molar mass / EF mass).

**Molecular Mass**
The sum of the atomic masses of all atoms in a molecule. Expressed in atomic mass units (u). Used for covalent compounds that exist as discrete molecules. *(Board/NEET)*

**Molecule**
The smallest particle of a substance that has the chemical properties of that substance and can exist independently. May be made of atoms of the same element (H₂, O₂) or different elements (H₂O, CO₂).

---

## N

**Nanotechnology**
Technology dealing with structures/particles at the nanometre (10⁻⁹ m) scale. Mentioned in context of *Charaka Samhita* — bhasmas (Ayurvedic metal preparations) are now known to contain nanoparticles.

---

## P

**Paramānu**
The ancient Indian term for the smallest indivisible particle of matter, proposed by  **Acharya Kanda (600 BCE)** . Described as eternal, indestructible, spherical, and in motion. Comparable to the modern concept of atoms. Conceptualised ~2500 years before Dalton's atomic theory.

**Philosopher's Stone (Paras)**
A mythical substance sought by alchemists, believed to convert base metals (iron, copper) into gold. Along with the Elixir of Life, it drove early chemical investigations.

**Physical Property**
A property that can be measured or observed **without changing the chemical identity** of the substance. Examples: colour, density, melting point, boiling point, mass, volume.

**Precision**
The closeness of a set of repeated measurements to **each other** (reproducibility). Does not necessarily mean the measurements are close to the true value. *(Board/NEET)*

**Pure Substance**
A material with a **fixed composition** and definite, characteristic properties throughout. All constituent particles are chemically identical. Includes both elements and compounds.

---

## R

**Rasayan Shastra**
The ancient Indian name for chemistry (also called  *Rastantra* ,  *Ras Kriya* , or  *Rasvidya* ). Encompassed metallurgy, medicine, manufacture of cosmetics, glass, and dyes.

**Rasratnakar**
A text by Nagarjuna dealing with the formulation of mercury compounds and methods for extracting metals (gold, silver, tin, copper).

**Rounding Off**
The process of reducing the number of digits in a calculated result to match the precision of the measurements used. Follows specific rules based on whether the digit to be removed is greater than, less than, or equal to 5.

---

## S

**Scientific Notation**
A method of expressing numbers in the form **N × 10ⁿ** (where 1 ≤ N < 10), used to handle very large or very small numbers conveniently and to clarify the number of significant figures.

**Significant Figures**
All digits in a measurement that are known with certainty  **plus one final estimated (uncertain) digit** . Indicate the precision of a measurement. Rules govern which zeros are significant.

**SI System (Système International d'Unités)**
The internationally agreed-upon system of measurement, established in **1960** by the  **11th CGPM** . Based on  **7 base units** . Replaced earlier systems like the English and Metric systems.

**Stoichiometry**
*(from Greek: stoicheion = element, metron = measure)*
The quantitative study of the amounts of reactants consumed and products formed in a chemical reaction, based on the balanced chemical equation.

**Stoichiometric Coefficient**
The numerical coefficient placed before a chemical formula in a balanced equation. Represents the number of **molecules or moles** of that species participating in the reaction.

---

## U

**Unified Mass Unit (u)**
The modern preferred symbol for the atomic mass unit (amu). Defined as 1/12 the mass of one ¹²C atom =  **1.66056 × 10⁻²⁴ g** . Replaces the older notation 'amu'.

**Unit Factor**
A ratio equal to  **1** , used in dimensional analysis to convert between units. Both the numerator and denominator represent the same quantity in different units. Example: (2.54 cm / 1 in) = 1.

---

## V

**Vaiseshika Sutras**
Text authored by **Acharya Kanda (600 BCE)** containing the first formulation of the atomic theory (Paramānu theory) in India.

---

## W

**Weight**
The **force exerted by gravity** on an object. W = mg. Unlike mass, weight varies with location (changes with gravitational acceleration). SI unit: Newton (N). Measured using a spring balance (not an analytical balance).
