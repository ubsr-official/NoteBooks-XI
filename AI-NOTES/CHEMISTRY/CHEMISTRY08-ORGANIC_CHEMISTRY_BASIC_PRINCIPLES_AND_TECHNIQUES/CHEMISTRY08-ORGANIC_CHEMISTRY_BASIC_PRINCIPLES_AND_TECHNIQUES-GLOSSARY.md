## A

**Acyclic Compound (Aliphatic Compound)**
An organic compound in which carbon atoms are arranged in **open chains** (straight or branched), without any ring structure. Also called *aliphatic* (from Greek *aleiphar* = fat; early aliphatic compounds were obtained from fats). Examples: methane (CH‚āĄ), ethane (CH‚āÉCH‚āÉ), isobutane ((CH‚āÉ)‚āāCHCH‚āÉ), acetic acid (CH‚āÉCOOH). Distinguished from cyclic compounds which contain ring structures. Related: Alicyclic, Cyclic compounds, Homologous series. *(Board)*

**Adsorption Chromatography**
A type of chromatography based on the **differential adsorption** of components of a mixture on a solid adsorbent (stationary phase). Components that adsorb more strongly are retained longer; components that adsorb weakly elute first. Common adsorbents: silica gel and alumina. Two major types: column chromatography and thin layer chromatography (TLC). The Rf value quantifies relative migration. Related: TLC, Column chromatography, Rf value, Mobile phase. *(Board/NEET)*

**Alicyclic Compound**
A compound containing **carbon atoms arranged in a ring** (cyclic structure) that exhibits **aliphatic properties**. Includes both homocyclic compounds (only carbon in the ring, e.g., cyclopropane, cyclohexane) and heterocyclic non-aromatic compounds (e.g., tetrahydrofuran with O in ring). Distinguished from aromatic compounds. Related: Acyclic, Aromatic, Heterocyclic. *(Board)*

**Alkyl Group**
A fragment derived from a **saturated hydrocarbon by removing one hydrogen atom from carbon**. Named by replacing the '-ane' suffix of the corresponding alkane with '-yl'. Examples: methyl (‚ÄďCH‚āÉ), ethyl (‚ÄďCH‚āāCH‚āÉ), isopropyl (‚ÄďCH(CH‚āÉ)‚āā), tert-butyl (‚ÄďC(CH‚āÉ)‚āÉ). Important branched alkyl groups: isopropyl, sec-butyl, isobutyl, tert-butyl, neopentyl. Note: in alphabetical ordering of IUPAC names, *sec-* and *tert-* are ignored; *iso-* and *neo-* are included. Related: IUPAC nomenclature, Parent chain, Substituent. *(Board/NEET)*

**Aromatic Compound**
A class of cyclic organic compounds characterised by **special stability due to delocalisation of ŌÄ-electrons** in a cyclic conjugated system (follows H√ľckel's rule: 4n+2 ŌÄ-electrons). Benzenoid aromatics include benzene, aniline, naphthalene. Non-benzenoid aromatics include tropone (7-membered ring). Heterocyclic aromatics include furan (O), thiophene (S), pyridine (N). Related: Benzene, Resonance, Conjugated system, Delocalization. *(Board/NEET)*

## B

**Ball-and-stick Model**
A type of **molecular model** in which atoms are represented by balls and chemical bonds by sticks connecting them. Provides a three-dimensional representation showing bond lengths and angles. Useful for visualising 3D structure. For compounds with C=C (e.g., ethene), springs may replace sticks (ball-and-spring model). Compare: Framework model (bonds only), Space-filling model (volume of atoms). Related: Framework model, Space-filling model, Molecular model. *(Board)*

**Bond-line (Skeletal) Formula**
A simplified structural representation in which **carbon and hydrogen atoms are not explicitly shown**; only the carbon‚Äďcarbon bonds are drawn as a zigzag. Heteroatoms (O, N, Cl, etc.) are written explicitly. **Terminals of lines = CH‚āÉ groups; line junctions = carbon atoms** (with implicit H atoms to satisfy tetravalence). Most widely used by practising chemists due to simplicity. Compare: Lewis structure, Condensed formula. Related: Structural formula, Condensed formula. *(Board/NEET)*

## C

**Carbanion**
A highly reactive, **negatively charged carbon species** formed by heterolytic bond cleavage in which the carbon atom retains **both electrons** of the broken bond. The negatively charged carbon has a **lone pair and three bond pairs ‚Üí sp¬≥ hybridised (distorted tetrahedral)**. Carbanions are **stabilised by electron-withdrawing groups** (‚ÄďI effect) because these help disperse the negative charge. Example: CH‚āÉ‚ĀĽ (methyl anion). Extremely reactive; organic reactions proceeding via carbanions are called ionic/polar reactions. Related: Heterolytic cleavage, Carbocation, Free radical, Nucleophile. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Carbocation (Carbonium Ion)**
A highly reactive, **positively charged carbon species** formed by heterolytic bond cleavage in which carbon loses its shared electron pair. The positively charged carbon has **only six valence electrons (sextet) ‚Üí sp¬≤ hybridised (trigonal planar)**, with an empty p-orbital perpendicular to the molecular plane. Stability order (increasing): CH‚āÉ‚Āļ < RCH‚āā‚Āļ < R‚āāCH‚Āļ < R‚āÉC‚Āļ. Stabilised by electron-donating groups (+I inductive effect) and hyperconjugation. Methyl cation (‚ĀļCH‚āÉ) has zero hyperconjugation (empty p-orbital ‚ä• to C‚ÄďH bonds). Related: Heterolytic cleavage, Carbanion, Hyperconjugation, Inductive effect. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Carius Method**
The method for quantitative estimation of **halogens** in an organic compound. The compound is heated with **fuming nitric acid and silver nitrate** in a sealed hard glass tube (Carius tube) in a furnace. Halogen is converted to silver halide (AgX), which is filtered, dried, and weighed. Formula: %X = (atomic mass of X √ó mass of AgX √ó 100) / (molar mass of AgX √ó mass of compound). Also used for sulphur estimation (when compound + fuming HNO‚āÉ or Na‚āāO‚āā ‚Üí BaSO‚āĄ). Related: Quantitative analysis, AgCl, AgBr, AgI, Silver halide. *(Board/NEET)*

**Catenation**
The unique property of **carbon to form covalent bonds with other carbon atoms**, creating chains, branched structures, and rings. This ability, combined with tetravalence, explains the enormous diversity of organic compounds (>10 million known). Catenation is strongest in carbon due to the high C‚ÄďC bond enthalpy and balance of atom size vs. bond repulsion. Silicon also shows limited catenation. Related: Tetravalence, Organic chemistry, Homologous series. *(Board)*

**Chain Isomerism**
A type of structural isomerism in which two or more compounds have the **same molecular formula but different carbon skeletons** (the arrangement of the carbon backbone differs). Example: C‚āÖH‚āĀ‚āā represents pentane (straight chain), 2-methylbutane (one branch), and 2,2-dimethylpropane (two branches) ‚ÄĒ all structural isomers with different carbon skeletons. Related: Isomerism, Position isomerism, Functional group isomerism. *(Board/NEET)*

**Column Chromatography**
A type of adsorption chromatography in which the adsorbent is packed in a **vertical glass column (tube)**. The mixture is applied at the top; eluant (solvent) flows downward. Components with least adsorption elute first; those most strongly adsorbed are retained at the top. A preparative technique ‚ÄĒ can isolate compounds in usable quantities. Compare: TLC (analytical, small scale). Related: Adsorption chromatography, TLC, Eluant, Adsorbent. *(Board/NEET)*

**Condensed Structural Formula**
A structural formula in which **dashes (bonds) are omitted** and the number of identical groups attached to an atom is shown by subscripts. Examples: CH‚āÉCH‚āÉ (ethane), H‚āāC=CH‚āā (ethene), HC‚Č°CH (ethyne), CH‚āÉOH (methanol). Further condensed: CH‚āÉ(CH‚āā)‚āÜCH‚āÉ for octane. Less detailed than Lewis structures; more detailed than bond-line formulas. Related: Lewis structure, Bond-line formula, Structural formula. *(Board)*

**Conjugated System**
A molecular system with **alternating single and double bonds** (or a double bond adjacent to a lone pair), allowing **delocalisation of ŌÄ-electrons** across multiple atoms. Examples: 1,3-butadiene (CH‚āā=CH‚ÄďCH=CH‚āā), benzene, aniline, nitrobenzene. Such systems show abnormal stability and reactivity. The basis for resonance effects. Related: Resonance effect, Delocalisation, Benzene, ŌÄ-bond. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Crystallisation**
The most common purification method for **solid organic compounds**, based on the difference in **solubility** of the compound and impurities in a suitable solvent. Procedure: dissolve in minimum hot solvent ‚Üí filter (remove insoluble impurities) ‚Üí cool slowly ‚Üí pure compound crystallises ‚Üí filter, wash, dry. Coloured impurities are removed with activated charcoal. Repeated crystallisation used for impurities with comparable solubilities. Related: Purification, Sublimation, Distillation. *(Board/NEET)*

**Curved Arrow Notation**
A convention used in organic chemistry to show the **movement of electron pairs** during bond-breaking and bond-forming. The tail of the arrow shows the **source** of the electron pair; the head shows where the electrons move. A **full-headed (double barb) arrow** shows movement of a **pair of electrons**. A **half-headed (fish-hook) arrow** shows movement of a **single electron** (in radical reactions). Used to depict reaction mechanisms, resonance structures, and electron displacement effects. Related: Mechanism, Resonance, Heterolytic cleavage, Homolytic cleavage. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## D

**Differential Extraction**
A purification technique for isolating an **organic compound from an aqueous medium** by shaking it with an immiscible organic solvent in which the compound is more soluble. The organic solvent and aqueous solution form two separate layers in a separatory funnel; the organic layer is separated, and the solvent is removed by distillation. If the compound is poorly soluble in the organic solvent ‚Üí **continuous extraction** is used (same solvent repeatedly cycled). Related: Separatory funnel, Purification, Distillation. *(Board/NEET)*

**Distillation**
A purification/separation technique based on differences in **boiling points** (vapour pressures) of liquids. Vapours are cooled and condensed. Types: (1) Simple distillation ‚ÄĒ widely different BPs or non-volatile impurity; (2) Fractional distillation ‚ÄĒ close BPs (uses fractionating column with theoretical plates); (3) Vacuum distillation ‚ÄĒ high-BP or thermally labile compounds (glycerol); (4) Steam distillation ‚ÄĒ steam-volatile, water-immiscible compounds (aniline). Related: Fractional distillation, Steam distillation, Vacuum distillation, Boiling point. *(Board/NEET)*

**Dumas Method**
A method for the **quantitative estimation of nitrogen** in an organic compound. The compound is heated with **copper(II) oxide** in an atmosphere of **CO‚āā** ‚Üí gives N‚āā gas (+ CO‚āā + H‚āāO). Nitrogen gas is collected over aqueous KOH (which absorbs CO‚āā) in a graduated tube, and its volume is measured at known T and P (then converted to STP). Formula: %N = (28 √ó V_STP √ó 100) / (22400 √ó m). Applicable to a wider range of N-containing compounds than Kjeldahl's method; works for nitro, azo, and ring nitrogen compounds. Related: Kjeldahl's method, Nitrogen estimation. *(Board/NEET)*

## E

**Electromeric Effect (E-effect)**
A **temporary electron displacement effect** shown by organic compounds having a **multiple bond** (double or triple bond). It is defined as the **complete transfer of a shared pair of ŌÄ-electrons to one of the atoms** joined by a multiple bond, on the demand of an attacking reagent. This effect is immediately annulled when the attacking reagent is removed. Shown by curved arrow (full-headed). Types: +E effect (ŌÄ-electrons move towards the atom attacked by the reagent) and ‚ÄďE effect (ŌÄ-electrons move away from the atom attacked). When inductive and electromeric effects conflict, electromeric effect predominates. Related: Inductive effect, Resonance effect, Hyperconjugation. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Electrophile (E‚Āļ)**
An *electron-seeking* reactive species that **accepts an electron pair** from a nucleophile during a chemical reaction. Electrophiles attack **nucleophilic centres** (electron-rich sites, such as carbon with lone pairs or ŌÄ-electrons). Characteristics: electron-deficient (often with sextet or incomplete octet), often positively charged (or have partial positive charge). Examples ‚ÄĒ Charged: H‚Āļ, carbocations (R‚Āļ), NO‚āā‚Āļ, Br‚Āļ; Neutral: BF‚āÉ, AlCl‚āÉ, ZnCl‚āā, carbonyl group (>C=O), alkyl halides (C carries őī+). In reactions, electrophiles receive the electron pair from the substrate. Related: Nucleophile, Heterolytic cleavage, Carbocation, Lewis acid. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## F

**Fractional Distillation**
A distillation technique used to separate liquids with **close boiling points** (typically within 25 K), using a **fractionating column** placed between the distilling flask and the condenser. The column provides multiple surfaces for alternating condensation and vaporisation (each cycle = one **theoretical plate**). Vapours become increasingly enriched in the more volatile component as they rise. Used industrially in **petroleum refining** (separation of different fractions of crude oil). Related: Distillation, Theoretical plate, Fractionating column, Petroleum. *(Board/NEET)*

**Free Radical**
A **neutral, highly reactive species** containing an **unpaired electron**, formed by homolytic cleavage of a covalent bond. Represented with a dot (‚ÄĘ) to indicate the unpaired electron (e.g., CH‚āÉ‚ÄĘ). Alkyl radicals are classified as primary, secondary, or tertiary. Stability: 3¬į > 2¬į > 1¬į > methyl (same trend as carbocations). Organic reactions proceeding through free radicals are called free radical / homopolar / nonpolar reactions. Not to be confused with ions (carbocations and carbanions, which are formed by heterolytic cleavage). Related: Homolytic cleavage, Carbocation, Carbanion, Radical reaction. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Functional Group**
An **atom or group of atoms** joined to the carbon chain of an organic compound that is **responsible for its characteristic chemical properties**. Compounds with the same functional group undergo similar reactions regardless of the rest of the molecule. Examples: ‚ÄďOH (hydroxyl, alcohols), ‚ÄďCOOH (carboxyl, carboxylic acids), ‚ÄďCHO (aldehyde), >C=O (carbonyl, ketones), ‚ÄďNH‚āā (amino, amines), ‚ÄďX (halo, halides), ‚ÄďCN (cyano, nitriles). The functional group determines the suffix in IUPAC nomenclature. Related: Homologous series, IUPAC nomenclature, Polyfunctional compound. *(Board/NEET)*

**Functional Group Isomerism**
A type of structural isomerism in which two or more compounds have the **same molecular formula but different functional groups**. The same molecular formula gives rise to completely different compound classes with very different properties. Example: C‚āÉH‚āÜO ‚ÄĒ propanal (an aldehyde: CH‚āÉCH‚āāCHO) and propanone (a ketone: CH‚āÉCOCH‚āÉ). Another example: C‚āāH‚āÜO ‚ÄĒ ethanol (alcohol) and dimethyl ether (ether). Related: Isomerism, Chain isomerism, Position isomerism. *(Board/NEET)*

## H

**Heterolytic Cleavage (Heterolysis)**
The fission of a covalent bond in which **both electrons of the shared pair go to one of the bonded atoms**, forming ionic species. The atom that takes both electrons becomes an anion (‚Äď); the atom that loses both becomes a cation (+). Shown by a **full-headed (double-barb) curved arrow**. Leads to **carbocations** (when C loses electrons) or **carbanions** (when C gains both electrons). This mode of cleavage is favoured in **polar conditions** (polar solvents, ionic reactants). Organic reactions proceeding this way are called **ionic / polar / heteropolar reactions**. Related: Homolytic cleavage, Carbocation, Carbanion, Curved arrow notation. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Homologous Series**
A **group of organic compounds** that: (i) have the **same functional group**, (ii) can be represented by a **general molecular formula**, (iii) each successive member differs from the previous one by a **‚ÄďCH‚āā unit** (14 mass units), and (iv) show a gradual change in physical properties with increasing chain length. Members of a homologous series are called **homologues**. Examples: alkanes (C‚āôH‚āā‚āô‚āä‚āā), alkenes (C‚āôH‚āā‚āô), alkanols (C‚āôH‚āā‚āô‚āä‚āĀOH), alkanoic acids. Related: Functional group, IUPAC nomenclature, Acyclic compounds. *(Board/NEET)*

**Homolytic Cleavage (Homolysis)**
The fission of a covalent bond in which **each bonded atom receives one electron** of the shared pair, forming two **neutral species with unpaired electrons (free radicals)**. Represented by a **half-headed (fish-hook) curved arrow** (‚ÜĹ). Homolysis is favoured by **high temperature, UV radiation, or use of radical initiators**. Reactions proceeding via this mechanism are called **free radical / homopolar / nonpolar reactions**. Contrast: heterolytic cleavage which gives ions. Related: Free radical, Heterolytic cleavage, Radical reaction. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Hyperconjugation**
A **permanent stabilising interaction** in which the **ŌÉ-electrons of a C‚ÄďH bond** of an alkyl group directly attached to an unsaturated system or an atom with an empty/unshared p-orbital are **delocalised into that ŌÄ* or empty p-orbital**. Also called **"no-bond resonance"** (one canonical form has no C‚ÄďH bond). Requires proper orbital alignment (C‚ÄďH ŌÉ-bond must be parallel to the empty p-orbital). Applications: (1) Stability of carbocations (more őĪ C‚ÄďH bonds ‚Üí more stable cation); (2) Stability of alkenes and radicals (more substituted = more hyperconjugation = more stable). Exception: CH‚āÉ‚Āļ has **zero hyperconjugation** (empty p-orbital ‚ä• to molecular plane; no C‚ÄďH bonds can align). Formula: number of őĪ C‚ÄďH bonds = 3 √ó (no. of methyl groups attached). Related: Inductive effect, Resonance, Carbocation stability. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## I

**Inductive Effect (I-effect)**
The **transmission of polarity** (and electron density shift) through a **ŌÉ-bond framework** due to the **electronegativity difference** between bonded atoms. It is a **permanent effect** that decreases rapidly with increasing number of bonds from the substituent (virtually negligible after 3 bonds). Substituents can be **electron-withdrawing (‚ÄďI)**: halogens (F > Cl > Br > I), ‚ÄďNO‚āā, ‚ÄďCN, ‚ÄďCOOH; or **electron-donating (+I)**: alkyl groups (3¬į > 2¬į > 1¬į). Applications: (i) explaining acidity of carboxylic acids (Cl‚āÉCCOOH > Cl‚āāCHCOOH > ClCH‚āāCOOH > CH‚āÉCOOH); (ii) explaining carbocation and carbanion stability. Related: Resonance effect, Hyperconjugation, Electromeric effect. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**IUPAC Nomenclature**
The **systematic method of naming organic compounds** developed by the International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry. Names are directly correlated with molecular structure. Key steps: (1) Identify the principal functional group (highest priority) ‚Üí this gives the suffix; (2) Identify the longest carbon chain containing this group ‚Üí this gives the parent alkane name; (3) Number the chain to give lowest locants to the principal FG; (4) Name substituents in alphabetical order as prefixes. Priority order for FG: ‚ÄďCOOH > ‚ÄďSO‚āÉH > ‚ÄďCOOR > ‚ÄďCOCl > ‚ÄďCONH‚āā > ‚ÄďCN > ‚ÄďCHO > >C=O > ‚ÄďOH > ‚ÄďNH‚āā > C=C > C‚Č°C. Related: Functional group, Parent chain, Alkyl group, Locant. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Isomerism**
The phenomenon in which **two or more compounds** have the **same molecular formula but different physical and chemical properties**. Such compounds are called **isomers**. Classification: (A) Structural isomerism ‚ÄĒ same formula, different structural arrangement of atoms: (i) chain, (ii) position, (iii) functional group, (iv) metamerism; (B) Stereoisomerism ‚ÄĒ same formula, same structural arrangement but different spatial arrangement: (i) geometrical (cis-trans), (ii) optical. Related: Chain isomerism, Position isomerism, Functional group isomerism, Metamerism, Stereoisomerism. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## K

**Kjeldahl's Method**
A method for the **quantitative estimation of nitrogen** in an organic compound, particularly for amino and amide nitrogen. Procedure: (1) Digest compound with **concentrated H‚āāSO‚āĄ** ‚Üí all N converts to **(NH‚āĄ)‚āāSO‚āĄ**; (2) Add excess NaOH ‚Üí **NH‚āÉ liberated** and distilled into a known volume of standard H‚āāSO‚āĄ; (3) **Back-titrate** excess H‚āāSO‚āĄ with standard NaOH. Formula: %N = [1.4 √ó M √ó 2(V ‚Äď V‚āĀ/2)] / m. **NOT applicable** to compounds where N is in **nitro (‚ÄďNO‚āā), azo (‚ÄďN=N‚Äď), or ring positions** (e.g., pyridine) ‚ÄĒ such N does not convert to (NH‚āĄ)‚āāSO‚āĄ. Related: Dumas method, Nitrogen estimation, Back titration. *(Board/NEET)*

## L

**Lassaigne's Test (Sodium Fusion Test)**
A qualitative test for detecting **nitrogen, sulphur, halogens, and phosphorus** in organic compounds. The organic compound is **fused with metallic sodium** at high temperature ‚Üí covalent elements are converted to **ionic forms** (NaCN, Na‚āāS, NaX). The fused mass is dissolved in distilled water to give the **Sodium Fusion Extract (SFE)**. Specific tests on SFE: (N) Prussian blue test; (S) lead acetate (black PbS) or sodium nitroprusside (violet); (X) silver nitrate after acidification with HNO‚āÉ (white AgCl, pale yellow AgBr, yellow AgI); (P) ammonium molybdate (yellow precipitate). Related: Qualitative analysis, Prussian blue, Silver halide, Sodium fusion extract. *(Board/NEET)*

## M

**Metamerism**
A special type of structural isomerism arising from **different alkyl groups on either side of the same functional group** within the same molecule. Both metamers have the same molecular formula and the same functional group. Example: C‚āĄH‚āĀ‚āÄO (ethers): methoxypropane (CH‚āÉ‚ÄďO‚ÄďC‚āÉH‚āá) and ethoxyethane (C‚āāH‚āÖ‚ÄďO‚ÄďC‚āāH‚āÖ). Differs from position isomerism (where it's the same carbon skeleton with different positions) ‚ÄĒ in metamerism, it's the different alkyl groups flanking the heteroatom. Related: Isomerism, Position isomerism, Functional group isomerism. *(Board/NEET)*

**Molecular Model**
A physical device that provides **three-dimensional representation** of organic molecules. Three main types: (1) **Framework model** ‚ÄĒ only bonds shown (bond pattern without atom sizes); (2) **Ball-and-stick model** ‚ÄĒ atoms as balls, bonds as sticks (best for bond angles and 3D shape); (3) **Space-filling model** ‚ÄĒ atoms shown according to van der Waals radius, no bonds shown (shows actual volume occupied). Computer-generated models also widely used. Related: Wedge-dash formula, Three-dimensional representation. *(Board)*

## N

**Nucleophile (Nu:)**
A *nucleus-loving* reactive species that **donates an electron pair** to an electrophilic centre of the substrate during a chemical reaction. Nucleophiles are **electron-rich** species ‚ÄĒ they may be negatively charged ions with lone pairs or neutral molecules with lone pairs. Attacks sites bearing partial or full positive charge. Examples ‚ÄĒ Charged: OH‚ĀĽ, CN‚ĀĽ, Br‚ĀĽ, I‚ĀĽ, RO‚ĀĽ, R‚āÉC‚ĀĽ; Neutral: H‚āāO:, NH‚āÉ:, R‚ÄďNH‚āā:, R‚āāS:, R‚āÉP:. Nucleophiles are **Lewis bases**. Related: Electrophile, Heterolytic cleavage, Carbanion, Lewis base. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## O

**Organic Chemistry**
The branch of chemistry dealing with **carbon compounds** (specifically those containing C‚ÄďH bonds, and including compounds with C bonded to O, N, S, P, halogens, etc.). The term "organic" reflects the historical (incorrect) belief that such compounds could only be made by living organisms. Organic chemistry is said to be approximately 200 years old. The field studies structure, properties, synthesis, and reactions of carbon compounds. Related: Catenation, Vital Force Theory, Wohler's synthesis. *(Board)*

## P

**Paper Chromatography**
A type of **partition chromatography** in which **chromatography paper** (which retains water in its fibres = stationary phase) is used. A strip of paper spotted with the mixture is dipped in an organic solvent (mobile phase) which rises by capillary action. Components partition between the water (stationary) and the organic solvent (mobile) phases ‚ÄĒ components with greater affinity for the mobile phase travel further. The developed strip is a **chromatogram**. Coloured spots are visible directly; colourless spots detected by UV, iodine vapour, or spraying reagents. Related: Partition chromatography, Rf value, TLC. *(Board/NEET)*

**Partition Chromatography**
Chromatography based on **continuous differential partitioning** (distribution) of components between a **stationary liquid phase** and a **mobile liquid or gas phase**. Distinguished from adsorption chromatography by the mechanism (partitioning between two liquid phases rather than adsorption onto a solid). Paper chromatography is the most common example. Related: Paper chromatography, Adsorption chromatography, Rf value. *(Board/NEET)*

**Position Isomerism**
A type of structural isomerism in which two or more compounds have the **same molecular formula and the same carbon skeleton**, but differ in the **position of a substituent atom or functional group** on the carbon skeleton. Example: C‚āÉH‚āąO ‚ÄĒ propan-1-ol (OH at C1) and propan-2-ol (OH at C2), same 3-carbon chain but OH is at different positions. Related: Isomerism, Chain isomerism, Functional group isomerism. *(Board/NEET)*

## R

**Reaction Mechanism**
A **sequential, step-by-step account** of an organic reaction, describing: (i) which bonds are broken and formed, (ii) the movement of electrons (shown with curved arrows), (iii) the nature of intermediates formed, (iv) the energetics (enthalpies) of bond-making and bond-breaking, and (v) the kinetics (rates) of transformation. Knowledge of mechanism aids in understanding reactivity and predicting products. A mechanism must satisfy: electron bookkeeping, stereochemistry, and kinetic data. Related: Curved arrow notation, Reactive intermediate, Carbocation, Carbanion, Free radical. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Resonance (Mesomerism)**
A phenomenon in which the **actual structure of a molecule cannot be adequately represented by a single Lewis structure**, and the true structure is a **hybrid of two or more canonical (contributing) structures** that differ only in the arrangement of electrons (nuclei positions remain the same). The resonance hybrid is more stable (has lower energy) than any single contributing structure; this extra stability is the **resonance energy**. Rules: all contributing structures have the same nuclear positions, same number of unpaired electrons. More equivalent structures ‚Üí greater resonance energy. Example: benzene (two Kekul√© structures), CH‚āÉCOO‚ĀĽ (two equivalent structures). Related: Resonance effect, Conjugated system, Delocalization, Resonance energy. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Resonance Effect (Mesomeric Effect, R/M Effect)**
The **transmission of the effect of electron donation or withdrawal through a conjugated ŌÄ-system**. It arises from the interaction of a ŌÄ-bond with an adjacent ŌÄ-bond or a lone pair on a neighboring atom. **+R effect**: electrons donated towards the ŌÄ-system (making it electron-rich). Groups: ‚ÄďOH, ‚ÄďOR, ‚ÄďNH‚āā, ‚ÄďNHR, ‚ÄďNR‚āā, halogens (‚ÄďX), ‚ÄďNHCOR, ‚ÄďOCOR. **‚ÄďR effect**: electrons withdrawn from the ŌÄ-system (making it electron-poor). Groups: ‚ÄďNO‚āā, ‚ÄďCN, ‚ÄďCHO, ‚ÄďCOOH, >C=O. Different from inductive effect in that it operates through ŌÄ-bonds and is transmitted over longer distances in conjugated systems. Related: Inductive effect, Conjugated system, Resonance, +R effect, ‚ÄďR effect. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Retardation Factor (Rf value)**
A dimensionless number used in chromatography (especially TLC) that characterises the **relative migration of a compound** under defined conditions (specific adsorbent + specific solvent/eluant system).

```
Rf = Distance moved by substance from baseline (x)
```

## S

**Space-filling Model**
A type of **molecular model** in which atoms are shown as spheres with radii proportional to their **van der Waals radii**, touching neighbouring atoms at their shared bond distance. Bonds are not shown explicitly. Gives the most realistic picture of the **actual volume occupied** by atoms in a molecule. Useful for understanding steric effects. Compare: Ball-and-stick model, Framework model. Related: Molecular model, Van der Waals radius. *(Board)*

**Steam Distillation**
A distillation technique for separating **steam-volatile, water-immiscible** organic substances at a temperature **below their normal boiling points**. Principle: A mixture of water and the organic liquid boils when the **sum of their vapour pressures equals atmospheric pressure** (p‚āĀ + p‚āā = P_atm). Since p‚āĀ(organic) < P_atm alone, the mixture boils at a temperature below 100¬įC. Example: separation of aniline (b.p. 457 K) from an aniline‚Äďwater mixture (boils at ~98¬įC in steam distillation). Related: Distillation, Boiling point, Vapour pressure, Differential extraction. *(Board/NEET)*

**Stereoisomerism**
A type of isomerism in which the **molecular formula and the sequence of covalent bonds** are the same but the compounds **differ in the three-dimensional arrangement** (spatial orientation) of their atoms or groups. The two main types are: (1) **Geometrical (cis-trans) isomerism** ‚ÄĒ arises from restricted rotation about a C=C double bond or in ring systems; (2) **Optical isomerism** ‚ÄĒ arises from molecules that are non-superimposable mirror images of each other (chiral molecules). Related: Isomerism, Structural isomerism, Chiral carbon, Geometric isomerism. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Sublimation**
A **purification technique** based on the property of certain solids that convert **directly from the solid state to vapour state on heating** (without passing through the liquid state). The vapour is condensed on a cooled surface, leaving behind non-sublimable impurities. Used for: camphor, iodine, naphthalene, benzoic acid, ammonium chloride, anthracene. Purity is checked by sharp melting point of the sublimed compound. Related: Purification, Crystallisation, Distillation. *(Board)*

## T

**Tetravalence of Carbon**
The property of carbon by which it **forms exactly four covalent bonds** in virtually all stable organic compounds. Based on the electronic configuration (2s¬≤2p¬≤) and hybridisation (sp¬≥, sp¬≤, or sp). The four bonds can be: 4 single bonds (sp¬≥), 1 double + 2 single (sp¬≤), 1 triple + 1 single (sp), or 2 double bonds (sp, as in allene). Tetravalence, combined with catenation, explains the existence of millions of organic compounds. Related: Catenation, Hybridisation, sp, sp¬≤, sp¬≥. *(Board/NEET)*

**Thin Layer Chromatography (TLC)**
A type of adsorption chromatography in which a **thin layer (~0.2 mm) of adsorbent** (silica gel or alumina) is coated on a glass plate. The sample is spotted near the base, and the plate is developed by standing in a small amount of eluant. Components travel different distances depending on their affinity for the stationary phase vs. the mobile phase. Detection: coloured compounds are visible; colourless ones detected by UV light, iodine vapour, or ninhydrin spray (for amino acids). Identification by **Rf value**. An **analytical technique** (cf. column chromatography = preparative). Related: Adsorption chromatography, Rf value, Column chromatography. *(Board/NEET)*

## V

**Vital Force Theory**
The now-disproved hypothesis, proposed by Berzilius (Swedish chemist) around 1780, that **organic compounds** (those obtained from living organisms) could only be synthesised in living systems through a special "vital force" that non-living inorganic chemistry could not replicate. This theory was **definitively disproved by F. Wohler in 1828** when he synthesised urea (an organic compound) from ammonium cyanate (an inorganic compound) using purely laboratory methods. The theory's downfall opened the door to modern synthetic organic chemistry. Related: Wohler's synthesis, Organic chemistry, History of chemistry. *(Board)*

## W

**Wedge-Dash Formula**
A convention for representing the **three-dimensional structure of organic molecules** on a two-dimensional surface (paper). Three types of lines: (1) **Solid wedge (‚Ė∂)** ‚ÄĒ bond projecting **towards** the observer (out of plane); (2) **Dashed wedge (------)** ‚ÄĒ bond projecting **away** from the observer; (3) **Normal line (‚ÄĒ)** ‚ÄĒ bond **in the plane of the paper**. The broad end of each wedge is towards the observer. Used to depict chirality, conformation, and 3D structure. Example: methane drawn with 2 in-plane bonds, 1 solid wedge (H towards viewer), 1 dashed wedge (H away). Related: Stereoisomerism, Three-dimensional representation, Chiral carbon. *(Board/NEET)*

**Wohler's Synthesis (1828)**
The landmark chemical reaction performed by **Friedrich Wohler** in which **urea** (NH‚āāCONH‚āā), an organic compound previously only known from biological sources, was synthesised from **ammonium cyanate** (NH‚āĄCNO), an inorganic compound: NH‚āĄCNO ‚Üí NH‚āāCONH‚āā. This was the **first synthesis of an organic compound from an inorganic source in a laboratory**, and it effectively disproved the Vital Force Theory. Wohler's synthesis, along with Kolbe's synthesis of acetic acid (1845) and Berthelot's synthesis of methane (1856), established that organic compounds are governed by the same chemical laws as inorganic compounds. Related: Vital Force Theory, Organic chemistry, History. *(Board/NEET)*
