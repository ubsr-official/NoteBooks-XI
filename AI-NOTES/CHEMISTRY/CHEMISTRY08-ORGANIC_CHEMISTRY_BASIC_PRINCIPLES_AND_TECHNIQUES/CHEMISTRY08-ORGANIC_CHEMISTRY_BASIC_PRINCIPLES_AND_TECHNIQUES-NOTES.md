## SECTION 1 — GENERAL INTRODUCTION `[Board · NEET]`

*"Organic chemistry today is no longer the study of substances from living organisms — it is the study of carbon compounds."*

### 1.1 Historical Background

**The Vital Force Theory (Pre-1828):**
Berzilius (Swedish chemist) proposed that organic compounds could only be synthesised in living organisms through a "vital force." Inorganic chemistry covered all mineral-source compounds.

**The Turning Point — Wohler's Synthesis (1828):**
F. Wohler synthesised **urea** (an organic compound) from **ammonium cyanate** (an inorganic compound):

```
NH₄CNO ——Heat——→ NH₂CONH₂
Ammonium cyanate        Urea
```

> This single experiment **demolished the Vital Force Theory** and opened the modern era of organic chemistry.

**Subsequent Syntheses:**
| Year | Chemist | Compound Synthesised |
|------|---------|---------------------|
| 1828 | F. Wohler | Urea (from NH₄CNO) |
| 1845 | Kolbe | Acetic acid |
| 1856 | Berthelot | Methane |

These proved that organic compounds can be made from inorganic sources in a laboratory.

### 1.2 What is Organic Chemistry?

The branch of chemistry dealing with compounds of carbon (especially those containing C–H bonds) is called **organic chemistry**. Carbon forms covalent bonds with:
- Other carbon atoms (catenation)
- Hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, sulphur, phosphorus, halogens

**Significance:** Organic compounds are essential to life (DNA, proteins, enzymes), industry (fuels, polymers, dyes, medicines, plastics) and materials science.

## SECTION 2 — TETRAVALENCE OF CARBON & SHAPES OF ORGANIC MOLECULES `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

### 2.1 Hybridisation and Molecular Shapes

Carbon's electronic configuration: 1s² 2s² 2p²

By promoting one 2s electron to a 2p orbital, carbon achieves the excited state 1s² 2s¹ 2p³, enabling four bonds.

| Hybridisation | Bond Angles | Shape | Example | Bond Type |
|--------------|-------------|-------|---------|-----------|
| sp³ | 109.5° | Tetrahedral | CH₄, CH₃Cl | 4 sigma bonds only |
| sp² | 120° | Trigonal planar | C₂H₄, H₂C=O | 3 sigma + 1 pi bond |
| sp | 180° | Linear | C₂H₂, HC≡N | 2 sigma + 2 pi bonds |

**Effect of hybridisation on properties:**

```
sp   → more s-character → MORE electronegative carbon → shorter, stronger bonds
sp²  → intermediate s-character → intermediate properties
sp³  → least s-character → longest, weakest C–X bonds
```

> **Key trend:** s-character: sp(50%) > sp²(33%) > sp³(25%)  
> More s-character → greater electronegativity → shorter bond length → higher bond strength

### 2.2 Characteristic Features of π Bonds

In H₂C=CH₂ (ethene):
- All atoms lie in the **same plane**
- The two p orbitals are **mutually parallel** and **perpendicular to the molecular plane**
- The π electron cloud lies **above and below** the plane
- Rotation about C=C is **restricted** (rotation destroys p orbital overlap)

> **Why π electrons are reactive:** They are above/below the plane → easily accessible to attacking reagents → **most reactive sites in a molecule with multiple bonds**

**Counting σ and π bonds:**
- Single bond (C–C or C–H) = **1 σ bond**
- Double bond (C=C or C=O) = **1 σ + 1 π bond**
- Triple bond (C≡C or C≡N) = **1 σ + 2 π bonds**

**Worked Example:**
HC≡C–CH=CH–CH₃: σ(C–C) = 4; σ(C–H) = 6; π(C=C) = 1; π(C≡C) = 2

## SECTION 3 — STRUCTURAL REPRESENTATIONS OF ORGANIC COMPOUNDS `[Board · NEET]`

### 3.1 Types of Structural Formulas

**(i) Complete/Lewis Structural Formula:**
Shows every bond as a dash; every lone pair may be shown.

**(ii) Condensed Structural Formula:**
Dashes omitted; subscripts used for repeated groups.
- Ethane: CH₃CH₃
- Ethene: H₂C=CH₂
- Ethyne: HC≡CH
- Methanol: CH₃OH

**(iii) Bond-Line (Skeletal) Formula:**
- C and H atoms **not shown explicitly**
- Only C–C bonds drawn as zigzag lines
- Terminals = CH₃ groups
- Line junctions = C atoms (with implicit H to satisfy valency)
- Heteroatoms (O, N, Cl, etc.) are **written explicitly**

```
Example: n-hexane as bond-line formula:
     /\/\/\
    (terminal CH₃ — zigzag — terminal CH₃)
```

**Key rule for bond-line formulas:**
> Count the lines meeting at each junction/terminal; the remaining bonds are assumed to be C–H bonds (carbon is always tetravalent).

### 3.2 Three-Dimensional Representations (Wedge-Dash)

Used for depicting 3D structure on 2D paper:

| Symbol | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| Solid wedge (——▶) | Bond coming **towards** observer (out of plane) |
| Dashed wedge (------) | Bond going **away** from observer (behind plane) |
| Normal line (——) | Bond **in the plane** of paper |

Example: Methane (CH₄) — carbon at centre with two bonds in plane, one towards observer (solid wedge), one away (dashed wedge).

### 3.3 Molecular Models

| Model Type | What is Shown | Feature |
|-----------|--------------|---------|
| Framework model | Bonds only (no atoms) | Emphasises bond pattern |
| Ball-and-stick model | Both atoms AND bonds | Balls = atoms; sticks = bonds |
| Space-filling model | Volume of each atom (van der Waals radius) | No bonds shown; shows actual space occupied |

## SECTION 4 — CLASSIFICATION OF ORGANIC COMPOUNDS `[Board · NEET]`

```
Organic Compounds
├── I. Acyclic (Open Chain / Aliphatic)
│       Examples: CH₃CH₃ (ethane), (CH₃)₂CHCH₃ (isobutane)
│
└── II. Cyclic (Closed Chain / Ring)
        ├── A. Alicyclic (Aliphatic Cyclic)
        │       Homocyclic (only C in ring): Cyclopropane, Cyclohexane
        │       Heterocyclic (non-C atom in ring): Tetrahydrofuran (O in ring)
        │

                └── Heterocyclic Aromatic: Furan (O), Thiophene (S), Pyridine (N)
```

A **functional group** is an atom or group of atoms attached to the carbon chain that is responsible for the **characteristic chemical properties** of the organic compound.

| Class | Functional Group | Suffix | Example |
|-------|-----------------|--------|---------|
| Alcohols | –OH | -ol | Butan-2-ol |
| Aldehydes | –CHO | -al | Butanal |
| Ketones | >C=O | -one | Butan-2-one |
| Carboxylic acids | –COOH | -oic acid | Butanoic acid |

| Ethers | –R–O–R– | alkoxy- (prefix) | Ethoxyethane |
| Alkenes | >C=C< | -ene | But-1-ene |
| Alkynes | –C≡C– | -yne | But-1-yne |
| Halides | –X (X = F, Cl, Br, I) | halo- (prefix) | 1-Bromobutane |

| Nitro compounds | –NO₂ | nitro- (prefix) | 1-Nitrobutane |

### 4.2 Homologous Series

A **homologous series** is a group of organic compounds:
- All having the **same functional group**
- **Consecutive members differ by –CH₂** (14 mass units)
- Can be represented by a **general molecular formula**
- Members are called **homologues**

Examples: Alkanes (CₙH₂ₙ₊₂), Alkenes (CₙH₂ₙ), Alkynes (CₙH₂ₙ₋₂), Alkanols (CₙH₂ₙ₊₁OH)

## SECTION 5 — IUPAC NOMENCLATURE `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

### 5.1 IUPAC vs Common Names

**IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry)** — systematic method where the name encodes the structure.

**Common/Trivial names** — historically derived; still used for many compounds.

| Compound | Common Name | IUPAC Name |
|----------|-------------|------------|
| CH₄ | Methane | Methane |
| (CH₃)₂CO | Acetone | Propan-2-one |
| CHCl₃ | Chloroform | Trichloromethane |
| CH₃COOH | Acetic acid | Ethanoic acid |
| HCHO | Formaldehyde | Methanal |
| C₆H₆ | Benzene | Benzene |
| (H₃C)₄C | Neopentane | 2,2-Dimethylpropane |

### 5.2 IUPAC Nomenclature of Alkanes — Step-by-Step Rules

**Step 1 — Identify the longest carbon chain** (parent chain / root alkane).

**Step 2 — Number the chain** from the end that gives substituents the **lowest possible locant numbers**.

**Step 3 — Name substituents as prefixes** in alphabetical order, with their position numbers.

**Step 4 — Identical groups** → use multiplying prefixes (di-, tri-, tetra-, penta-, hexa-); these prefixes are **not considered in alphabetical ordering**.

**Step 5 — Equal-position substituents** → give lower number to the one that comes **first alphabetically**.

**Step 6 — Branched substituents** → name using parent + branch, placed in parentheses; the carbon attached to the main chain is numbered 1.

**Step 7 — Two equal-length chains** → choose the chain with **more substituents**.

| 1 | Methane | CH₄ |

### 5.4 Common Alkyl Groups

| Parent Alkane | Alkyl Group | Structural Formula |
|--------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Methane | Methyl (–Me) | –CH₃ |
| Ethane | Ethyl (–Et) | –CH₂CH₃ |

> **Prefix rules for alphabetical ordering:**
> - **iso- and neo-** ARE considered part of the fundamental name (included in alphabetical ordering)
> - **sec- and tert-** are NOT considered in alphabetical ordering

### 5.5 Nomenclature of Compounds with Functional Groups

**Priority order of functional groups** (highest to lowest):
```
–COOH > –SO₃H > –COOR > –COCl > –CONH₂ > –CN > –CHO > >C=O > –OH > –NH₂ > >C=C< > –C≡C–
```

Rules:
- **Highest-priority group** = **principal functional group** → named as suffix
- **Remaining groups** → named as prefixes
- **–R, C₆H₅–, halogens, –NO₂, alkoxy (–OR)** → ALWAYS prefix substituents (never suffix)
- Longest chain **containing the principal functional group** is the parent chain
- Chain numbered to give **lowest locant to the principal functional group**

**Examples of correct IUPAC naming:**

| Structure | IUPAC Name |
|-----------|-----------|
| HOCH₂(CH₂)₃CH₂COCH₃ | 7-Hydroxyheptan-2-one (not 2-oxoheptan-7-ol; ketone > alcohol) |
| BrCH₂CH=CH₂ | 3-Bromoprop-1-ene (not 1-bromoprop-2-ene) |
| CH₂(OH)CH₂(OH) | Ethane-1,2-diol |
| CH₂=CH–CH=CH₂ | Buta-1,3-diene (terminal 'e' of alkane dropped before vowel suffix) |

### 5.6 Nomenclature of Cyclic Compounds

- Prefix **"cyclo"** added to the parent straight-chain alkane
- Numbering begins at a substituted carbon and proceeds to give **lowest locants**

Examples: Cyclopentane, 1-Methyl-3-propylcyclohexane, 3-Ethyl-1,1-dimethylcyclohexane (not 1-ethyl-3,3-dimethylcyclohexane — more branched C gets lower number)

### 5.7 Nomenclature of Substituted Benzene

- Monosubstituted: substituent as **prefix** to "benzene" (e.g., methylbenzene, nitrobenzene)
- Disubstituted: number carbons for **lowest locants**; use **ortho (1,2), meta (1,3), para (1,4)** in trivial system
- Higher substitution: use lowest locant rule only
- When benzene ring is substituent on an alkane with functional group: it is named **phenyl** (C₆H₅–, abbreviated Ph)

```
TRIVIAL PREFIXES:
  o- (ortho): substituents at 1,2 positions
  m- (meta): substituents at 1,3 positions
  p- (para): substituents at 1,4 positions
```

## SECTION 6 — ISOMERISM `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

**Isomers:** Compounds with the **same molecular formula** but **different properties**.

```
Isomerism
├── I. Structural Isomerism (same molecular formula, different structural arrangement)
│       ├── (a) Chain isomerism: different carbon skeletons
│       │       e.g., C₅H₁₂ → Pentane, Isopentane (2-methylbutane), Neopentane
│       ├── (b) Position isomerism: same skeleton, different position of substituent/group
│       │       e.g., C₃H₈O → Propan-1-ol, Propan-2-ol
│       ├── (c) Functional group isomerism: different functional groups
│       │       e.g., C₃H₆O → Propanal (aldehyde), Propanone (ketone)
│       └── (d) Metamerism: different alkyl chains on either side of functional group
│               e.g., C₄H₁₀O → CH₃OC₃H₇ (methoxypropane), C₂H₅OC₂H₅ (ethoxyethane)
│
└── II. Stereoisomerism (same molecular formula AND same structural formula but different
        spatial arrangement of atoms)
        ├── Geometrical isomerism (cis-trans) — due to restricted rotation (C=C or ring)
        └── Optical isomerism — due to non-superimposable mirror images (chiral carbon)
```

## SECTION 7 — FUNDAMENTAL CONCEPTS IN ORGANIC REACTION MECHANISM `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

### 7.1 Fission of Covalent Bonds

When a covalent bond breaks in an organic reaction, it can do so in two ways:

#### (A) Homolytic Cleavage (Homolysis)

> Each bonded atom takes **one electron** of the shared pair → forms **free radicals** (neutral, highly reactive species with **unpaired electron**).

```
R–X ——Heat/Light——→ R• + •X
```

- Shown by **half-headed (fish-hook) curved arrow** (↽)
- Products = **free radicals** (neutral with unpaired electron)
- Reactions proceeding this way = **free radical / homopolar / nonpolar reactions**

**Stability of alkyl radicals (increasing order):**
```
•CH₃ < CH₃•CH₂ < (CH₃)₂•CH < (CH₃)₃•C
Methyl  Ethyl     Isopropyl   tert-Butyl
(least stable)              (most stable)
```

#### (B) Heterolytic Cleavage (Heterolysis)

> The shared pair of electrons goes **entirely to one atom** → forms **ions** (charged species).

```
R–X ——→ R⁺ + :X⁻      [or]      R–Z ——→ R:⁻ + Z⁺
```

- Shown by **full-headed (double-headed) curved arrow** (→)
- Products = **carbocations** (+) or **carbanions** (–)
- Reactions = **ionic / heteropolar / polar reactions**

### 7.2 Reactive Intermediates

#### (i) Carbocations (Carbonium Ions)
- Carbon with **sextet** of electrons + **positive charge**
- Hybridisation: **sp²** (trigonal planar)
- Empty p-orbital perpendicular to the molecular plane

**Stability order (increasing):**
```
⁺CH₃ < CH₃⁺CH₂ < (CH₃)₂⁺CH < (CH₃)₃C⁺
Methyl   Primary   Secondary    Tertiary
cation   cation     cation      cation
(least stable)                 (most stable)
```

Stabilised by: more alkyl groups → **inductive effect + hyperconjugation** donate electron density to the positive centre.

#### (ii) Carbanions
- Carbon with **octet** (one lone pair) + **negative charge**
- Hybridisation: **sp³** (distorted tetrahedral)
- Highly unstable and reactive

#### (iii) Free Radicals
- Neutral species with **unpaired electron**
- Highly reactive
- Stability: 3° > 2° > 1° > methyl radical (same trend as carbocations)

### 7.3 Nucleophiles and Electrophiles

**Nucleophile (Nu:)** — *nucleus-seeking*; electron-rich species that **donates an electron pair** to the reactive site. Attacks electron-deficient (positive) centres.

Examples:
- Charged: OH⁻, CN⁻, Br⁻, I⁻, R₃C:⁻
- Neutral: H₂O:, NH₃:, R₂NH:, ROH:

**Electrophile (E⁺)** — *electron-seeking*; electron-deficient species that **accepts an electron pair** from the nucleophile. Attacks electron-rich (negative) centres.

Examples:
- Charged: carbocations (R⁺), H⁺, NO₂⁺, Br⁺
- Neutral: BF₃, AlCl₃, carbonyl compounds (>C=O), alkyl halides (R–X, where δ+ on C)

**Curved arrow notation:**
> The full-headed curved arrow starts at the electron pair (or bond) and points to where those electrons move.

### 7.4 Electron Displacement Effects in Covalent Bonds

#### (A) Inductive Effect (I-effect) `[NEET · JEE]`

Polarisation of a **σ-bond** due to the electronegativity difference between atoms; the effect is **transmitted through the chain** but decreases rapidly (negligible after 3 bonds).

```
CH₃ ——→ CH₂ ——→ Cl
  δδ+      δ+     δ–
```

- **Electron-withdrawing groups (–I effect):** –NO₂, –CN, –COOH, –COOR, –OAr, halogens (F > Cl > Br > I)
- **Electron-donating groups (+I effect):** alkyl groups (–CH₃, –C₂H₅); greater carbon branching = stronger +I effect

> **Application:** Stabilisation of carbanions is enhanced by –I groups (withdrawing –ve charge); stabilisation of carbocations by +I groups.

#### (B) Resonance (Mesomeric) Effect (R/M-effect) `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

Polarity produced by **interaction of two π-bonds OR between a π-bond and a lone pair** on an adjacent atom; transmitted through conjugated systems.

**+R effect (electron release towards conjugated system):**
- Groups: –halogens, –OH, –OR, –OCOR, –NH₂, –NHR, –NR₂, –NHCOR
- E.g., –NH₂ in aniline donates lone pair into benzene ring → electron-rich ring

**–R effect (electron withdrawal from conjugated system):**
- Groups: –COOH, –CHO, >C=O, –CN, –NO₂
- E.g., –NO₂ in nitrobenzene withdraws electrons from benzene ring → electron-poor ring

**Rules for drawing resonance structures:**
1. Same positions of all **nuclei** (atoms don't move)
2. Same number of **unpaired electrons**
3. More stable contributor → more covalent bonds + all octets satisfied + less charge separation + negative charge on more electronegative atom

> **Resonance energy:** The difference in energy between the actual molecule (resonance hybrid) and the lowest-energy resonance structure. Greater number of equivalent contributing structures → greater resonance energy.

#### (C) Electromeric Effect (E-effect) `[NEET · JEE]`

A **temporary** effect; occurs only in the **presence of an attacking reagent**.

Complete transfer of a **shared π-electron pair** to one atom on demand of the attacking reagent. Annulled as soon as reagent is removed.

- **+E effect:** π-electrons transferred **towards** the atom to which the reagent attaches
  ```
  >C=C< + H⁺ → >C⁺–CH<  (π electrons go to carbon attacked by H⁺)
  ```
- **–E effect:** π-electrons transferred **away** from the atom to which the reagent attaches
  ```
  >C=C< + ⁻CN → >C–C⁻< (π electrons away from CN-attacked carbon)
  ```

> When inductive and electromeric effects conflict, **electromeric effect predominates**.

#### (D) Hyperconjugation `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

A **permanent** stabilising interaction involving **delocalisation of σ-electrons** of a C–H bond of an alkyl group directly attached to an unsaturated system or an atom with an empty/unshared p-orbital.

**In carbocations:**
One of the C–H bonds of the adjacent alkyl group aligns with the empty p-orbital → σ electrons delocalise into the empty orbital → stabilises the cation.

```
       H H              H H
       | |              | |
H — C — C⁺   ←→   H — C = C   (no-bond resonance)
       | |              | |
       H H              H H⁺
```

**Number of hyperconjugable C–H bonds:**
| Species | No. of C–H bonds available for hyperconjugation |
|---------|------------------------------------------------|
| CH₃⁺ (methyl) | 0 (p-orbital ⊥ to all C–H bonds — CANNOT hyperconjugate) |
| CH₃CH₂⁺ (ethyl) | 3 |
| (CH₃)₂CH⁺ (isopropyl) | 6 |
| (CH₃)₃C⁺ (tert-butyl) | 9 |

More C–H bonds → greater hyperconjugation → greater stability → explains carbocation stability order.

> Hyperconjugation is also called **"no-bond resonance"** (one C–H bond breaks homolytically to provide electrons).

### 7.5 Types of Organic Reactions

| Reaction Type | Description | Example |
|--------------|-------------|---------|
| Substitution | One atom/group replaced by another | CH₄ + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl + HCl |
| Addition | Two reactants join to form one product (across multiple bond) | CH₂=CH₂ + Br₂ → CH₂Br–CH₂Br |
| Elimination | Loss of small molecule from adjacent carbons → new multiple bond | CH₃CH₂Br + KOH → CH₂=CH₂ + H₂O + KBr |
| Rearrangement | Atoms reorganise within the same molecule | Carbocation rearrangements via hydride/alkyl shifts |

## SECTION 8 — PURIFICATION OF ORGANIC COMPOUNDS `[Board · NEET]`

*Purity of a compound is confirmed by a sharp, constant **melting point or boiling point**.*

### 8.1 Sublimation

**Principle:** Some solids convert **directly from solid to vapour** on heating (without melting).

**Used when:** Compound is sublimable; impurities are non-sublimable.

**Examples:** Camphor, naphthalene, iodine, anthracene, benzoic acid, ammonium chloride.

### 8.2 Crystallisation

**Principle:** Difference in **solubility** of compound and impurities in a suitable solvent.

**Procedure:**
1. Dissolve impure compound in minimum hot solvent
2. Filter hot (remove insoluble impurities)
3. Cool → pure compound crystallises out
4. Filter, wash, dry crystals

**When to use:**
- Coloured impurities → treat with activated charcoal first
- Compound highly soluble in one solvent → use solvent mixtures
- Impurities have comparable solubility → repeat crystallisation

### 8.3 Distillation

| Method | Principle | When Used |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| **Simple Distillation** | Boiling point difference | Liquids differing by >25 K in BP; separating volatile liquid from nonvolatile impurity |
| **Fractional Distillation** | Fractionating column provides multiple condensation/vaporisation cycles | Liquids with similar boiling points (e.g., petroleum refining) |
| **Distillation under Reduced Pressure (Vacuum)** | Boiling point ∝ external pressure; low pressure → lower BP | High-BP liquids that decompose at/below their BP (e.g., glycerol separation) |
| **Steam Distillation** | Mixture boils when p₁ + p₂ = P_atm; so organic liquid vaporises below its normal BP | Steam-volatile, water-immiscible substances (e.g., aniline from aniline–water mixture) |

**Steam Distillation condition:**
```
p_organic + p_water = P_atmospheric
Since p_organic < P_atm → boils at a temperature BELOW its normal boiling point
```

### 8.4 Differential Extraction

**Principle:** Organic compound more soluble in organic solvent than in water → distributed between two immiscible phases.

**Apparatus:** Separatory funnel

**When to use:** Separating organic compound from aqueous mixture. If organic compound is poorly soluble in the solvent → use **continuous extraction** (same solvent used repeatedly).

### 8.5 Chromatography

**Principle:** Differential migration of components between **stationary phase** (fixed) and **mobile phase** (moving).

**Word origin:** Greek *chroma* = colour (originally used for coloured plant pigments)

#### (A) Adsorption Chromatography

Components adsorb to different extents on the adsorbent (silica gel, alumina).

**(i) Column Chromatography:**
- Mixture applied to top of adsorbent column in glass tube
- Eluant (solvent) passed through — components elute at different rates
- Most strongly adsorbed compound stays near top; least adsorbed elutes first

**(ii) Thin Layer Chromatography (TLC):**
- ~0.2 mm adsorbent layer on glass plate
- Eluant rises by capillary action
- **Rf value** = retardation factor (characteristic of each compound in a given system)
  ```
  Rf = Distance moved by substance from baseline (x)
       ──────────────────────────────────────────────
       Distance moved by solvent from baseline (y)
  ```
- Spots detected by: colour (for coloured compounds), UV light (fluorescent compounds), iodine vapour, ninhydrin spray (amino acids)

#### (B) Partition Chromatography

Differential partitioning between two phases (not just adsorption).

**Paper Chromatography:**
- Stationary phase = water trapped in chromatography paper
- Mobile phase = organic solvent (rises by capillary action)
- Developed strip = chromatogram

## SECTION 9 — QUALITATIVE ANALYSIS OF ORGANIC COMPOUNDS `[Board · NEET]`

### 9.1 Detection of Carbon and Hydrogen

**Method:** Heat organic compound with copper(II) oxide (CuO).

```
C + 2CuO ——Δ——→ 2Cu + CO₂    [CO₂ tested with lime water → turbidity (CaCO₃↓)]
2H + CuO ——Δ——→ Cu + H₂O     [H₂O tested with anhydrous CuSO₄ → turns blue]
```

| Test for | Reagent | Positive Result |
|---------|---------|----------------|
| CO₂ | Lime water Ca(OH)₂ | White turbidity (CaCO₃↓) |
| H₂O | Anhydrous CuSO₄ (white) | Turns blue (CuSO₄·5H₂O) |

### 9.2 Lassaigne's Test (Detection of N, S, Halogens, P)

**Principle:** Fuse organic compound with **sodium metal** → converts covalent forms to **ionic forms** that can be tested in aqueous solution.

**Sodium fusion reactions:**
```
Na + C + N ——Δ——→ NaCN          (for N detection)
2Na + S ——Δ——→ Na₂S             (for S detection)
Na + X ——Δ——→ NaX               (X = Cl, Br, I)
```

The **sodium fusion extract (SFE)** is obtained by dissolving the fused mass in distilled water.

#### Test for Nitrogen (Prussian Blue Test):

```
SFE + FeSO₄ → boil → acidify with conc. H₂SO₄
6CN⁻ + Fe²⁺ → [Fe(CN)₆]⁴⁻
3[Fe(CN)₆]⁴⁻ + 4Fe³⁺ → Fe₄[Fe(CN)₆]₃·xH₂O  ← Prussian Blue colour ✓
```

> **When N and S both present:** NaSCN forms → blood-red with Fe³⁺ (no Prussian blue because no free CN⁻). Need excess Na to decompose thiocyanate: NaSCN + 2Na → NaCN + Na₂S

#### Test for Sulphur:

**(a) Lead acetate test:**
```
S²⁻ + Pb²⁺ → PbS↓  (black precipitate)
```

**(b) Sodium nitroprusside test:**
```
S²⁻ + [Fe(CN)₅NO]²⁻ → [Fe(CN)₅NOS]⁴⁻  (violet colour)
```

#### Test for Halogens (Silver Nitrate Test):

SFE first acidified with **dilute HNO₃** (to decompose CN⁻ and S²⁻ which would otherwise interfere), then AgNO₃ added.

| Halogen | Precipitate | Solubility in NH₄OH |
|---------|------------|---------------------|
| Cl | AgCl (white) | Soluble |
| Br | AgBr (pale yellow) | Sparingly soluble |
| I | AgI (yellow) | Insoluble |

> **Why HNO₃ is used (not H₂SO₄):** H₂SO₄ would form BaSO₄ or PbSO₄ and its sulphate ions could confuse the test; also HNO₃ effectively decomposes NaCN and Na₂S.

#### Test for Phosphorus:

Compound + sodium peroxide → sodium phosphate → HNO₃ + ammonium molybdate → **yellow precipitate** (ammonium phosphomolybdate, (NH₄)₃PO₄·12MoO₃)

## SECTION 10 — QUANTITATIVE ANALYSIS `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

### 10.1 Estimation of Carbon and Hydrogen

**Method:** Combustion with excess O₂ in presence of CuO at high temperature.
```
CₓHᵧ + (x + y/4)O₂ → x CO₂ + (y/2)H₂O
```
- H₂O absorbed in **anhydrous CaCl₂** (U-tube 1)
- CO₂ absorbed in **KOH solution** (U-tube 2)
- Increase in mass of each tube = mass of H₂O (m₁) and CO₂ (m₂)

```
% Carbon = (12 × m₂ × 100) / (44 × m)
% Hydrogen = (2 × m₁ × 100) / (18 × m)
```

where m = mass of organic compound.

### 10.2 Estimation of Nitrogen

#### (i) Dumas Method:
- Heat with CuO in CO₂ atmosphere → N₂ gas collected over KOH (which absorbs CO₂)
- N₂ volume measured at STP

```
% Nitrogen = (28 × V × 100) / (22400 × m)
```

where V = volume of N₂ at STP (mL), m = mass of compound (g)

**Dumas not applicable to:** Nitrogen in nitro/azo groups or ring nitrogen (e.g., pyridine) — applicable to most compounds.

#### (ii) Kjeldahl's Method:
- Compound + conc. H₂SO₄ → (NH₄)₂SO₄
- + excess NaOH → NH₃ liberated
- NH₃ absorbed in known volume of standard H₂SO₄
- Excess H₂SO₄ back-titrated with standard NaOH

```
% N = (1.4 × M × 2(V – V₁/2)) / m
```

**Kjeldahl NOT applicable to:** compounds with N in nitro, azo groups, or ring N (pyridine) — only for N that converts to (NH₄)₂SO₄.

### 10.3 Estimation of Halogens (Carius Method)

- Compound + fuming HNO₃ + AgNO₃ in **Carius tube** (sealed, heated in furnace)
- Halogen → AgX precipitate → filter, dry, weigh

```
% X = (Atomic mass of X × m₁ × 100) / (Mol. mass of AgX × m)
```

where m₁ = mass of AgX, m = mass of compound.

| AgX | Molar mass |
|-----|-----------|
| AgCl | 143.5 g/mol |
| AgBr | 188 g/mol |
| AgI | 235 g/mol |

### 10.4 Estimation of Sulphur

- Compound + fuming HNO₃ or Na₂O₂ → H₂SO₄ → precipitate as BaSO₄ (with BaCl₂)
- Filter, dry, weigh BaSO₄ (m₁)

```
% S = (32 × m₁ × 100) / (233 × m)
```

(Molar mass BaSO₄ = 233 g/mol; it contains 32 g S per mol)

### 10.5 Estimation of Phosphorus

- Compound + fuming HNO₃ → H₃PO₄ → precipitate as (NH₄)₃PO₄·12MoO₃ (molar mass = 1877 g/mol)
- **OR** precipitate as MgNH₄PO₄, ignite to Mg₂P₂O₇ (molar mass = 222 g/mol)

```
% P (via phosphomolybdate) = (31 × m₁ × 100) / (1877 × m)
% P (via Mg₂P₂O₇) = (62 × m₁ × 100) / (222 × m)
```

### 10.6 Estimation of Oxygen

Usually determined **by difference**:
```
% O = 100 – (% C + % H + % N + % S + % halogens)
```

Direct estimation: Compound decomposed in N₂ stream → O₂ + gases → passed over heated coke (O₂ → CO) → CO + I₂O₅ → CO₂ + I₂.

## SECTION 11 — SOLVED NCERT EXAMPLES (KEY ANNOTATED) `[Board · NEET]`

### Key Worked Naming Problems

**6-Methyloctan-3-ol:**
- Longest chain containing –OH: 8 C (octane)
- –OH at C3 (lowest locant → number from that end)
- –CH₃ branch at C6
- Suffix: -ol; Result: 6-methyloctan-3-ol ✓

**Hexane-2,4-dione:**
- Two C=O groups at C2 and C4
- di + -one = dione; 6 C chain = hexane
- Result: Hexane-2,4-dione ✓

**5-Oxohexanoic acid:**
- Principal FG = COOH (highest priority) → suffix -oic acid; C1 = COOH carbon
- Ketone at C5 → prefix oxo-
- 6 C chain = hexane
- Result: 5-Oxohexanoic acid ✓

### Key Worked Mechanism Problems

**Carbocation stability (Problem 8.19):**
(CH₃)₃C⁺ more stable than CH₃CH₂⁺ because it has 9 C–H bonds available for hyperconjugation vs. only 3 in ethyl cation. Methyl cation (⁺CH₃) has ZERO hyperconjugation (the empty p-orbital is perpendicular to all C–H bonds).

## SECTION 12 — KEY DISTINCTIONS `[NEET · JEE]`

| Feature | Homolytic Cleavage | Heterolytic Cleavage |
|---------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Electron distribution | 1 e⁻ to each fragment | Both e⁻ to one fragment |
| Products | Free radicals (neutral) | Carbocations (+) and carbanions (–) |
| Arrow used | Half-headed (fish-hook) | Full-headed (double) |
| Reaction type | Free radical / Nonpolar | Ionic / Polar |
| Medium | Usually non-polar solvents, gas phase | Polar solvents, ionic conditions |

| Effect | Permanent/Temporary | Transmitted via | Examples affected |
|--------|--------------------|-----------------|--------------------|
| Inductive | Permanent | σ-bonds (decreases with distance) | Acidity of carboxylic acids, carbocation stability |
| Resonance | Permanent | Conjugated π-system | Benzene stability, aniline basicity |
| Electromeric | Temporary | π-bond (reagent-initiated) | Reaction mechanism intermediates |
| Hyperconjugation | Permanent | σ → p (C–H alignment) | Carbocation stability, alkene stability |
