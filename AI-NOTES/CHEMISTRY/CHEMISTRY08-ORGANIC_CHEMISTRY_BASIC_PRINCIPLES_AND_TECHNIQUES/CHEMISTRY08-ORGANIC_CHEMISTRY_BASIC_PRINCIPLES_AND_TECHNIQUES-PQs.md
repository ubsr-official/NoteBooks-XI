# PART B: ASSERTION–REASON QUESTIONS `[NEET · Board]` 🟡

---

> **Instructions:**
> * **(a)** Both Assertion and Reason are true; Reason correctly explains the Assertion
> * **(b)** Both Assertion and Reason are true; Reason does NOT correctly explain the Assertion
> * **(c)** Assertion is true; Reason is false
> * **(d)** Assertion is false; Reason is true

---

**Q51.**
**Assertion (A):** Wohler's synthesis of urea in 1828 is considered a landmark event in the history of chemistry.
**Reason (R):** It demonstrated for the first time that organic compounds can be synthesised from inorganic starting materials in a laboratory, disproving the Vital Force Theory.

---

**Q52.**
**Assertion (A):** The rotation about a carbon–carbon double bond (C=C) is restricted.
**Reason (R):** The π-bond in C=C results from sidewise overlap of p-orbitals; rotation would destroy this overlap and break the π-bond.

---

**Q53.**
**Assertion (A):** The inductive effect decreases with increasing distance from the point of substitution.
**Reason (R):** The inductive effect is transmitted through σ-bonds and becomes negligible beyond three bonds from the substituent.

---

**Q54.**
**Assertion (A):** (CH₃)₃C⁺ (tert-butyl cation) is more stable than CH₃⁺ (methyl cation).
**Reason (R):** The methyl cation has nine α-C–H bonds available for hyperconjugation, more than the tert-butyl cation.

---

**Q55.**
**Assertion (A):** Nitric acid is added to the sodium fusion extract before adding silver nitrate in the test for halogens.
**Reason (R):** Dilute HNO₃ decomposes cyanide (CN⁻) and sulphide (S²⁻) ions, which would otherwise form AgCN or Ag₂S and give false positive results.

---

**Q56.**
**Assertion (A):** In the resonance structures of benzene, both structures are equally stable and contribute equally to the hybrid.
**Reason (R):** For equal contribution to the resonance hybrid, contributing structures must be equivalent in energy.

---

**Q57.**
**Assertion (A):** Carbanions are generally sp³ hybridised.
**Reason (R):** The negative carbon in a carbanion has three bond pairs and one lone pair, giving it a distorted tetrahedral (sp³) geometry.

---

**Q58.**
**Assertion (A):** Steam distillation can be used to purify aniline from an aniline–water mixture.
**Reason (R):** In steam distillation, the organic liquid boils when the sum of its vapour pressure and that of water equals atmospheric pressure — so aniline vaporises below its normal boiling point.

---

**Q59.**
**Assertion (A):** The electromeric effect is a permanent effect present in a molecule even in the absence of attacking reagents.
**Reason (R):** The electromeric effect involves complete transfer of a shared π-electron pair to one of the atoms in the presence of an attacking reagent.

---

## Case Study 1 — IUPAC Nomenclature: Applying the Rules 🟢🟡

**Q77.** **[Structural Analysis]** Give the number of σ and π bonds present in the following compound:

CH₂=CH–C≡C–CH=CH₂

**Solution:**

Breaking down:
- CH₂= : 2 C–H bonds (σ) + C=C at left end (1σ + 1π)
- =CH– : 1 C–H bond (σ) + part of C–C single bond to C≡C (1σ)
- –C≡C– : triple bond (1σ + 2π) + adjacent σ-bonds (part already counted)
- –CH= : 1 C–H bond (σ) + C=C at right end (1σ + 1π) + bond to C≡C (1σ)
- =CH₂ : 2 C–H bonds (σ)

Count systematically:
- C–H bonds: C1(2) + C2(1) + C3(0) + C4(0) + C5(1) + C6(2) = 6 C–H σ bonds
- C–C single bonds connecting: C2–C3(1σ), C4–C5(1σ)
- C=C double bonds: C1=C2 (1σ+1π), C5=C6 (1σ+1π)
- C≡C triple bond: C3≡C4 (1σ+2π)

**Total σ bonds = 6(C–H) + 2(C–C single) + 2(C=C σ part) + 1(C≡C σ part) = 11σ**
**Total π bonds = 1(C=C left) + 2(C≡C) + 1(C=C right) = 4π**

**Answer: 11σ and 4π bonds**

**Q81.** **[Resonance]** Write the resonance structures of CH₂=CH–CHO (propenal / acrolein) and arrange them in order of decreasing stability.

**Solution:**

```
Structure I:   CH₂=CH–CH=O:           (no charge separation, most stable)
Structure II:  ⁺CH₂–CH=CH–O:⁻        (charge separated; negative on O is more electronegative → acceptable)
Structure III: ⁻:CH₂–CH=CH–O⁺        (negative on C, positive on O → C is less electroneg. than O → LEAST stable)
```

**Stability order: I > II > III**

- I: no charge separation, maximum covalent bonds, all octets satisfied → most stable
- II: negative charge on electronegative O (acceptable), positive on terminal C
- III: negative on C, positive on O → positive charge on more electronegative atom → least stable/does not contribute significantly

**Q82.** **[Mechanism + Electron Effects]** Explain the following orders of acidity:

**(a)** Cl₃CCOOH > Cl₂CHCOOH > ClCH₂COOH > CH₃COOH

**(b)** (CH₃)₃CCOOH < (CH₃)₂CHCOOH < CH₃CH₂COOH

**Solution:**

**(a) Inductive effect of Cl (–I effect):**

Each Cl withdraws electron density from the α-carbon, which further withdraws from the –COOH group. This stabilises the –COO⁻ (conjugate base) by dispersing the negative charge. More Cl atoms = greater –I effect = greater stabilisation of –COO⁻ = stronger acid.

Cl₃C– has three Cl atoms (strongest –I); Cl₂CH– has two; ClCH₂– has one; CH₃– has none (+I effect slightly destabilises –COO⁻).

∴ Cl₃CCOOH > Cl₂CHCOOH > ClCH₂COOH > CH₃COOH ✓

**(b) +I effect of alkyl groups:**

Alkyl groups donate electrons via +I effect to –COOH, which destabilises –COO⁻ (adds electron density to an already negative species). Greater branching = stronger +I effect = more destabilisation of –COO⁻ = weaker acid.

(CH₃)₃C– has the strongest +I (branched with 3 methyls); (CH₃)₂CH– is intermediate; CH₃CH₂– is least.

∴ (CH₃)₃CCOOH is weakest acid; CH₃CH₂COOH is strongest in this series ✓
