## A

**Acid (Arrhenius)**
A substance that **dissociates in water to give hydrogen ions, H⁺ (or hydronium ions, H₃O⁺)**. The ionization of acid HX is represented as: HX(aq) → H⁺(aq) + X⁻(aq), or HX(aq) + H₂O(l) → H₃O⁺(aq) + X⁻(aq). The bare proton (H⁺) has an intense electric field and immediately bonds to a water molecule to form H₃O⁺. Strong Arrhenius acids: HCl, HBr, HI, HNO₃, H₂SO₄, HClO₄. Limitation: only applies to aqueous solutions and cannot explain basicity of NH₃ (no OH⁻ group). Related: Brønsted–Lowry acid, Lewis acid, Ionization constant, pH. *(Board/NEET)*

**Acid (Brønsted–Lowry)**
A substance capable of **donating a proton (H⁺)**. In the reaction HCl + H₂O → H₃O⁺ + Cl⁻, HCl is the Brønsted–Lowry acid (proton donor). The concept extends beyond aqueous solutions and explains the acidity of substances like ammonium ions (NH₄⁺). The stronger the tendency to donate a proton, the stronger the acid. After donating a proton, an acid becomes its **conjugate base** (which differs by one proton). Related: Conjugate acid-base pair, Base (Brønsted–Lowry), Ka. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Acid (Lewis)**
A substance that **accepts a lone pair of electrons** from a base. Lewis concept is the broadest acid-base theory. Lewis acids include: proton-deficient species (BF₃, AlCl₃, BCl₃), transition metal cations (Co³⁺, Fe³⁺), and proton H⁺ itself. Note: A Lewis acid need NOT have a proton (e.g., BF₃ is a Lewis acid but NOT a Brønsted acid). The Lewis concept explains the catalytic role of metal ions in complex reactions. Reaction: BF₃ + :NH₃ → BF₃:NH₃ (Lewis acid–base adduct). Related: Lewis base, Brønsted–Lowry acid, Coordinate bond. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Autoprotolysis (Self-ionization of Water)**
The process by which two water molecules react with each other, one acting as an acid (donating H⁺) and the other as a base (accepting H⁺): **H₂O(l) + H₂O(l) ⇌ H₃O⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq)**. The equilibrium constant for this is the **ionic product of water, Kw**. At 298 K, Kw = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴; [H⁺] = [OH⁻] = 10⁻⁷ M. Demonstrates water's amphoteric nature. The very small extent of ionization (~1.8 × 10⁻⁹ fraction) shows water is overwhelmingly in the molecular form. Related: Kw, pH, Amphoteric species. *(Board/NEET)*

## B

**Base (Arrhenius)**
A substance that **produces hydroxide ions (OH⁻)** in aqueous solution. MOH → M⁺ + OH⁻. Strong Arrhenius bases: LiOH, NaOH, KOH, CsOH, Ba(OH)₂ (completely dissociated). Limitation: does not explain basicity of NH₃, CO₃²⁻, etc. Related: Base (Brønsted–Lowry), Kb, pOH. *(Board)*

**Base (Brønsted–Lowry)**
A substance capable of **accepting a proton (H⁺)**. In NH₃ + H₂O ⇌ NH₄⁺ + OH⁻, H₂O donates a proton to NH₃; NH₃ is the Brønsted–Lowry base. After accepting a proton, a base becomes its **conjugate acid** (NH₄⁺ in this case). The stronger the tendency to accept a proton, the stronger the base. Related: Conjugate acid-base pair, Acid (Brønsted–Lowry), Kb. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Base (Lewis)**
A substance that **donates a lone pair of electrons** to a Lewis acid. Lewis bases include molecules with lone pairs: NH₃, H₂O, OH⁻, F⁻, Cl⁻, CO, etc. All Brønsted–Lowry bases are also Lewis bases (they use their lone pairs to attract H⁺). Lewis acids and bases form **coordinate bonds** in the resulting adduct. Example: In BF₃:NH₃, NH₃ donates lone pair → Lewis base. Related: Lewis acid, Coordinate bond, Lone pair. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Buffer Solution**
A solution that **resists significant change in pH** upon addition of small amounts of a strong acid or base, or upon dilution. Composed of a **weak acid and its conjugate base (salt)** (acidic buffer) or a **weak base and its conjugate acid (salt)** (basic buffer). Buffer capacity: ability to neutralize added acid or base. Best buffering: when [acid] = [salt], i.e., pH = pKa. Henderson–Hasselbalch equation: pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA]). Examples: CH₃COOH/CH₃COONa (pH ≈ 4.75); NH₄OH/NH₄Cl (pH ≈ 9.25). Biological importance: blood maintained at pH 7.35–7.45 by H₂CO₃/HCO₃⁻ buffer. Related: Henderson–Hasselbalch, pKa, Common ion effect. *(Board/NEET)*

## C

**Common Ion Effect**
The **decrease in solubility or ionization** of a weak electrolyte or sparingly soluble salt when a **soluble strong electrolyte with a common ion** is added to its solution. Explained by Le Chatelier's principle: adding a common ion shifts equilibrium to reduce the effect (backward, reducing ionization). Examples: (1) Adding CH₃COONa to acetic acid → CH₃COO⁻ added → degree of ionization of acetic acid decreases → [H⁺] decreases → pH increases. (2) Adding NaCl to saturated AgCl solution → [Cl⁻] increases → IP > Ksp → AgCl precipitates → solubility decreases. Application: gravimetric analysis (precipitate ions completely). Related: Le Chatelier's principle, Ksp, Ionization constant. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Conjugate Acid-Base Pair**
A pair of substances that **differ by exactly one proton (H⁺)**. In a Brønsted–Lowry acid-base reaction, the acid loses one proton to become the conjugate base; the base gains one proton to become the conjugate acid. Key rule: **strong acid → very weak conjugate base; weak acid → relatively stronger conjugate base**. Conjugate acid has one MORE proton; conjugate base has one LESS proton. Examples: HF/F⁻; H₂SO₄/HSO₄⁻; NH₄⁺/NH₃; H₂O/OH⁻. Mathematically: Ka(acid) × Kb(conjugate base) = Kw. Related: Brønsted–Lowry, Ka, Kb, Kw. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## D

**Degree of Ionization (α)**
The **fraction of the total concentration of a weak electrolyte that ionizes** in solution. α = (concentration ionized)/(initial concentration). For weak acid HA: Ka = cα²/(1–α) ≈ cα² (when α << 1) → α = √(Ka/c). Degree of ionization **increases with dilution** (decreasing c) and **decreases in presence of common ion**. Percentage ionization = α × 100%. Example: For 0.02 M HF (Ka = 3.2 × 10⁻⁴): α = 0.12 (12%). Related: Ka, Kb, Common ion effect, pH. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Dynamic Equilibrium**
An equilibrium state in which **both the forward and reverse processes continue to occur simultaneously at the same rate**, so there is no net change in the macroscopic properties of the system. The term "dynamic" emphasizes that the system is not static at the molecular level — molecules are continuously reacting in both directions. Distinguished from static equilibrium (no movement at all). Evidence for dynamic nature: (1) Haber's deuterium experiment — isotope scrambling proves ongoing reactions. (2) Radioactive sugar experiment — radioactivity appears in both phases of a saturated sugar solution. Related: Chemical equilibrium, Equilibrium constant, Haber's process. *(Board/NEET)*

## E

**Electrolyte**
A substance whose **aqueous solution conducts electricity**, due to the presence of ions (cations and anions) produced by dissociation or ionization. Classified by Michael Faraday. **Strong electrolytes** are nearly completely ionized (NaCl, HCl, KOH). **Weak electrolytes** are only partially ionized (CH₃COOH, NH₄OH). **Non-electrolytes** do not conduct electricity in solution (glucose, urea). The greater the ionic concentration, the higher the conductance. Related: Ionic equilibrium, Ka, Kb, Strong/weak acid-base. *(Board)*

**Equilibrium (Physical)**
A state of balance in a **physical process** (phase change, dissolution) where the rate of the forward physical change equals the rate of the reverse physical change. Examples: (1) H₂O(l) ⇌ H₂O(g) — rate of evaporation = rate of condensation, achieved in a closed container; (2) Sugar(s) ⇌ Sugar(aq) — rate of dissolution = rate of crystallization in saturated solution. Characteristics: closed system required; dynamic (ongoing); measurable property is constant. Related: Dynamic equilibrium, Vapour pressure, Solubility, Henry's Law. *(Board)*

**Equilibrium Constant (Kc)**
A dimensionless quantity that **characterizes the ratio of product concentrations to reactant concentrations at equilibrium** at a given temperature. For the reaction a A + b B ⇌ c C + d D: **Kc = [C]c[D]d/[A]a[B]b** where concentrations are in mol L⁻¹. Properties: (1) Constant at a given temperature; (2) Independent of initial concentrations; (3) K'c (reverse) = 1/Kc; (4) Multiplying equation by n → K becomes Kc^n. Also known as the **Law of Chemical Equilibrium**. Proposed by Guldberg and Waage (1864). Related: Kp, Qc, Le Chatelier's principle, Equilibrium. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Equilibrium Constant (Kp)**
The equilibrium constant for **gaseous reactions expressed in terms of partial pressures** (in bar). For a A(g) + b B(g) ⇌ c C(g) + d D(g): **Kp = (pC^c × pD^d)/(pA^a × pB^b)**. Relationship: Kp = Kc(RT)^Δn where Δn = (moles gaseous products) – (moles gaseous reactants); R = 0.0831 bar L mol⁻¹ K⁻¹. Kp = Kc only when Δn = 0. Standard state for pressure = 1 bar; pressure must be in bar for Kp calculations. Related: Kc, Δn, Partial pressure, Dalton's Law. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## H

**Henderson–Hasselbalch Equation**
The equation relating pH of a **buffer solution** to the pKa of the weak acid component and the ratio of conjugate base to acid concentrations: **pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA])**. Derived from Ka expression. When [A⁻] = [HA]: pH = pKa (maximum buffer capacity). For basic buffers: pOH = pKb + log([BH⁺]/[B]). Named after Lawrence Henderson and Karl Hasselbalch. Limitations: valid when ratio [A⁻]/[HA] is between 0.1 and 10; not applicable for concentrated solutions or strong acids/bases. Related: Buffer, pKa, Ka, pH. *(Board/NEET)*

**Henry's Law**
States that the **mass of gas dissolved in a given mass of solvent at any temperature is proportional to the pressure of the gas above the solvent**. Mathematical form: m ∝ p, or m = k × p (where k = Henry's constant). Applicable to gas-liquid equilibria: CO₂(g) ⇌ CO₂(aq). Explains: (1) CO₂ fizzing from soda when bottle is opened (pressure decreases → less gas dissolves); (2) Decompression sickness in divers; (3) Increased N₂ solubility at depth. Related: Physical equilibrium, Gas solubility, Le Chatelier's principle. *(Board/NEET)*

**Heterogeneous Equilibrium**
A chemical equilibrium in which the **reactants and products are present in more than one phase** (solid, liquid, or gaseous). Key rule: molar concentrations of **pure solids and pure liquids remain constant** (independent of amount) and are NOT included in the equilibrium constant expression — their values are absorbed into Kc. Examples: CaCO₃(s) ⇌ CaO(s) + CO₂(g); Kp = pCO₂. Ni(s) + 4CO(g) ⇌ Ni(CO)₄(g); Kc = [Ni(CO)₄]/[CO]⁴. Related: Kc, Kp, Homogeneous equilibrium. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Homogeneous Equilibrium**
A chemical equilibrium in which **all reactants and products are in the same phase**. Examples: N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g) — all gaseous; CH₃COOC₂H₅(aq) + H₂O(l) ⇌ CH₃COOH(aq) + C₂H₅OH(aq) — all in solution. For gaseous homogeneous equilibria, can write both Kc and Kp. Related: Kc, Kp, Heterogeneous equilibrium. *(Board)*

**Hydrolysis of Salts**
The reaction of **cations or anions of a salt with water** to produce an acidic or basic solution. Strong acid + strong base salt (NaCl): neither ion hydrolyses → neutral (pH = 7). Weak acid + strong base salt (CH₃COONa): anion hydrolyses (CH₃COO⁻ + H₂O ⇌ CH₃COOH + OH⁻) → basic solution (pH > 7). Strong acid + weak base salt (NH₄Cl): cation hydrolyses (NH₄⁺ + H₂O ⇌ NH₄OH + H⁺) → acidic solution (pH < 7). Weak acid + weak base (CH₃COONH₄): both hydrolyse; pH = 7 + ½(pKa – pKb). Related: Salt, pH, Ka, Kb, Buffer. *(Board/NEET)*

**Hydronium Ion (H₃O⁺)**
The species formed when a **proton (H⁺) bonds to a water molecule**: H⁺ + H₂O → H₃O⁺. Has a **trigonal pyramidal** geometry. The bare proton has an extremely small radius (~10⁻¹⁵ m) and intense electric field, making free existence in solution impossible. In dilute aqueous solution, H₃O⁺ is further hydrated to give H₅O₂⁺, H₇O₃⁺, H₉O₄⁺. In this chapter, H⁺(aq) and H₃O⁺(aq) are used interchangeably. Related: pH, Autoprotolysis, Brønsted–Lowry acid. *(Board/NEET)*

## I

**Ionic Equilibrium**
A type of dynamic equilibrium established in aqueous solutions between **ions and unionized molecules** of weak electrolytes. Examples: CH₃COOH ⇌ H⁺ + CH₃COO⁻; NH₄OH ⇌ NH₄⁺ + OH⁻. Acids, bases, and salts are electrolytes capable of establishing ionic equilibria. Characterized by ionization constants Ka and Kb. Distinguished from equilibrium of neutral molecules by the involvement of charged species (ions). Related: Ka, Kb, Kw, pH, Common ion effect. *(Board/NEET)*

**Ionic Product of Water (Kw)**
The **equilibrium constant for the autoionization of water**: H₂O + H₂O ⇌ H₃O⁺ + OH⁻. **Kw = [H⁺][OH⁻]**. Value at 298 K = **1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴ M²**. Kw is temperature-dependent: increases with temperature (autoionization is endothermic). At 298 K: [H⁺] = [OH⁻] = 10⁻⁷ M in pure water. Fundamental to all pH calculations; basis of the pH scale. pKw = –log Kw = 14 at 298 K. Relationship: pH + pOH = pKw = 14 (at 298 K). Related: pH, Autoprotolysis, Ka, Kb conjugate pairs. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Ionization Constant of Acids (Ka)**
The equilibrium constant for the **ionization of a weak acid in water**: HA + H₂O ⇌ H₃O⁺ + A⁻. **Ka = [H⁺][A⁻]/[HA]**. Larger Ka = stronger acid. For initial concentration c and degree of ionization α: Ka = cα²/(1–α) ≈ cα² (when α << 1). pKa = –log Ka: smaller pKa = stronger acid. Typical values: HF (3.5×10⁻⁴), CH₃COOH (1.74×10⁻⁵), HCN (4.9×10⁻¹⁰). Related with Kb for conjugate pair: Ka × Kb = Kw. Related: pH, Kb, Kw, Degree of ionization. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Ionization Constant of Bases (Kb)**
The equilibrium constant for the **ionization of a weak base in water**: MOH ⇌ M⁺ + OH⁻ (or B + H₂O ⇌ BH⁺ + OH⁻). **Kb = [M⁺][OH⁻]/[MOH]**. Larger Kb = stronger base. pKb = –log Kb. For conjugate acid-base pair: Ka × Kb = Kw; pKa + pKb = 14 (at 298 K). Values: Ammonia Kb = 1.77×10⁻⁵; Aniline Kb = 4.27×10⁻¹⁰. Related: Ka, Kw, pOH, Degree of ionization. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## K

**Ksp (Solubility Product Constant)**
The **equilibrium constant for the dissolution equilibrium of a sparingly soluble ionic salt** in water. For MₓXᵧ(s) ⇌ x M^(p+)(aq) + y X^(q–)(aq): **Ksp = [M^(p+)]^x[X^(q–)]^y**. Pure solid not included (constant concentration). Ksp relates to molar solubility S by: Ksp = (xS)^x × (yS)^y = x^x × y^y × S^(x+y). Ksp is temperature-dependent. Application: (1) predicting precipitation (if IP > Ksp, precipitate forms); (2) common ion effect on solubility; (3) gravimetric analysis. Solubility categories: >0.1M (soluble); 0.01–0.1M (slightly soluble); <0.01M (sparingly soluble). Related: Ionic product, Common ion effect, Solubility. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## L

**Law of Mass Action**
The historical name for the **Law of Chemical Equilibrium** (or equilibrium constant expression), coined by Norwegian chemists Guldberg and Waage (1864). States that for a chemical reaction at equilibrium, the ratio of the product of concentrations of products (each raised to its stoichiometric coefficient) to the product of concentrations of reactants (each raised to its stoichiometric coefficient) is a constant. Called "mass action" because in early chemistry, concentration was called "active mass." Modern equivalent: the equilibrium constant expression Kc. Related: Kc, Guldberg and Waage, Equilibrium constant. *(Board)*

**Le Chatelier's Principle**
States: *"A change in any of the factors that determine the equilibrium conditions of a system will cause the system to change in such a manner so as to **reduce or counteract the effect of the change**."* Originally stated by Henri Le Chatelier (French chemist, 1884). Applies to all physical and chemical equilibria. Predicts the **direction** of shift (not the magnitude, and NOT the rate). Factors: (1) Concentration — adding reactant: forward; (2) Pressure — increasing pressure: toward fewer moles of gas; (3) Temperature — increasing T: toward endothermic direction (changes Kc); (4) Catalyst — does NOT shift equilibrium, only accelerates reaching it. Related: Kc, Qc, Equilibrium constant. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## P

**pH Scale**
A **logarithmic scale for expressing hydrogen ion concentration** (or activity) in solution. **pH = –log aH⁺ ≈ –log[H⁺]** (in dilute solutions <0.01 M). pH = –log(10⁻⁷) = 7 for pure water at 298 K. Acidic: pH < 7; Neutral: pH = 7; Basic: pH > 7. Each unit change in pH = 10-fold change in [H⁺]. pOH = –log[OH⁻]; pH + pOH = 14 (at 298 K). Practical range often stated as 0–14, though solutions of very high/low concentrations can exceed this. Measured using pH paper (accuracy ±0.5) or pH meter (accuracy 0.001). Related: Kw, Ka, Kb, Hydronium ion, Buffer. *(Board/NEET)*

**Polyprotic Acid**
An acid that can **donate more than one proton per molecule** in stepwise ionization reactions. Also called polybasic acid. Each ionization has a distinct constant Ka₁, Ka₂, Ka₃. Always: **Ka₁ > Ka₂ > Ka₃** (successive ionizations are progressively harder due to increasing negative charge of anion). Examples: H₂SO₄ (diprotic: Ka₁ = very large; Ka₂ = 1.2×10⁻²); H₃PO₄ (triprotic: Ka₁ = 7.5×10⁻³; Ka₂ = 6.2×10⁻⁸; Ka₃ = 4.2×10⁻¹³); H₂CO₃. The primary reaction and H₃O⁺ come mainly from Ka₁ (first ionization step). Related: Ka, Degree of ionization, Brønsted–Lowry acid. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## Q

**Reaction Quotient (Qc)**
Defined in the same way as Kc but uses **concentrations at any instant** (not necessarily at equilibrium): **Qc = [C]c[D]d/[A]a[B]b** (using current concentrations). Comparison with Kc predicts direction of reaction: Qc < Kc → forward; Qc > Kc → backward; Qc = Kc → at equilibrium. Qp is the analogous quantity using partial pressures. Useful for determining direction when a system is disturbed. Related: Kc, Le Chatelier's principle, Equilibrium. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## S

**Saturated Solution**
A solution in which **no more solute can dissolve** at a given temperature. In a saturated solution, a dynamic equilibrium exists between the dissolved solute and undissolved solute: Sugar(s) ⇌ Sugar(aq). Rate of dissolution = Rate of crystallization. The concentration of solute in a saturated solution = solubility at that temperature. For sparingly soluble salts, this equilibrium is characterized by Ksp. Confirmed experimentally using radioactive isotopes. Related: Ksp, Dynamic equilibrium, Common ion effect. *(Board)*

**Solubility Product Constant** — *See Ksp*

**Strong Acid / Strong Base**
**Strong acids:** Nearly completely ionized (>99%) in water, providing high [H⁺]. The six strong acids: **HCl, HBr, HI, HNO₃, H₂SO₄, HClO₄** (and HClO₃). Their conjugate bases (Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻, etc.) are very weak bases (weaker than water). **Strong bases:** Completely dissociated in water. Group 1 hydroxides (LiOH, NaOH, KOH, CsOH) and Ba(OH)₂, Ca(OH)₂ (less soluble). Their conjugate acids are very weak. Contrast with weak acids (Ka = 10⁻⁴ to 10⁻¹²) and weak bases. Related: Ka, Conjugate acid-base, pH, Degree of ionization. *(Board/NEET)*

## V

**Vapour Pressure**
The **pressure exerted by the vapour** of a liquid (or solid) in equilibrium with its liquid (or solid) phase in a closed system at a given temperature. At equilibrium: rate of evaporation = rate of condensation. Vapour pressure **increases with temperature** (more molecules have sufficient energy to escape). At **normal boiling point**: vapour pressure = 1.013 bar (1 atm). Liquids with higher vapour pressure are more volatile and have lower boiling points. Difference in vapour pressure is the basis of distillation and separation of liquid mixtures. Related: Physical equilibrium, Le Chatelier's principle, Henry's Law. *(Board)*

---

## W

**Weak Acid / Weak Base**
**Weak acids:** Partially ionized in water (α << 1); establish equilibrium between ionized and unionized forms. Characterized by Ka (typically 10⁻⁴ to 10⁻¹²). Examples: CH₃COOH (Ka = 1.74×10⁻⁵), HF (Ka = 3.5×10⁻⁴), HCN (Ka = 4.9×10⁻¹⁰). The weaker the acid, the stronger its conjugate base. **Weak bases:** Partially ionized; characterized by Kb. Examples: NH₃ (Kb = 1.77×10⁻⁵), aniline (Kb = 4.27×10⁻¹⁰). Ionization of both weak acids and bases depends on: concentration (dilution increases α), temperature, and presence of common ion. Related: Ka, Kb, Degree of ionization, Common ion effect, Buffer. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*
