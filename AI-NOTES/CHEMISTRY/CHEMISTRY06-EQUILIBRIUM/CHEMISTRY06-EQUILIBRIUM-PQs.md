**Q61.**
**Assertion (A):** At chemical equilibrium, the concentrations of reactants and products do not change with time.
**Reason (R):** At equilibrium, the forward and reverse reaction rates become equal.

---

**Q62.**
**Assertion (A):** The value of Kc for a given reaction increases when more reactant is added at constant temperature.
**Reason (R):** Kc depends only on temperature and is independent of initial concentrations.

---

**Q63.**
**Assertion (A):** Increasing temperature always shifts equilibrium in the forward direction.
**Reason (R):** Endothermic reactions are favoured at higher temperatures, and increasing temperature always increases Kc.

---

**Q64.**
**Assertion (A):** For the reaction N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g), Kp < Kc at any temperature.
**Reason (R):** Kp = Kc(RT)^Δn, and for this reaction Δn = –2, so (RT)^(–2) < 1.

---

**Q65.**
**Assertion (A):** A catalyst changes the equilibrium position of a reversible reaction.
**Reason (R):** A catalyst equally increases the rates of both forward and reverse reactions.

---

**Q66.**
**Assertion (A):** The pH of an acidic buffer solution does not change significantly upon dilution.
**Reason (R):** The Henderson–Hasselbalch equation shows that pH depends on the ratio [A⁻]/[HA], which remains constant upon dilution.

**Q67.**
**Assertion (A):** Strong acids have very weak conjugate bases.
**Reason (R):** For a conjugate acid-base pair, Ka × Kb = Kw. If Ka is very large, Kb must be very small.

**Q68.**

**Q69.**

**Reason (R):** Increasing pressure favours the side with fewer moles of gaseous species to reduce pressure.

**Q81.** **[Numerical]** At 731 K, the equilibrium constant Kc for H₂(g) + I₂(g) ⇌ 2HI(g) is 46.4. If 1 mol of H₂, 1 mol of I₂, and 2 mol of HI are placed in a 2 L flask, determine the direction of net reaction.

**Solution:**
[H₂] = [I₂] = 0.5 M; [HI] = 1.0 M
Qc = [HI]²/([H₂][I₂]) = (1.0)²/(0.5 × 0.5) = 1.0/0.25 = **4.0**
Since Qc (4.0) < Kc (46.4), reaction proceeds **forward** (more HI forms).

**Q82.** **[Numerical]** Calculate the pH of 0.1 M sodium acetate solution (pKa of acetic acid = 4.74).

**Solution:**
CH₃COONa → CH₃COO⁻ (fully); salt of weak acid + strong base → basic solution.
pKb(CH₃COO⁻) = 14 – 4.74 = 9.26; Kb = 10⁻⁹·²⁶ = 5.5 × 10⁻¹⁰

CH₃COO⁻ + H₂O ⇌ CH₃COOH + OH⁻; [OH⁻] ≈ √(Kb × c) = √(5.5×10⁻¹⁰ × 0.1) = √(5.5×10⁻¹¹) = 7.4 × 10⁻⁶ M

pOH = –log(7.4×10⁻⁶) = 5.13; **pH = 14 – 5.13 = 8.87**

*(Alternatively: pH of salt of weak acid + strong base ≈ 7 + ½pKa = 7 + 2.37 = 9.37 using simpler formula)*

**Q83.** **[Numerical]** The Ksp of Ag₂SO₄ is 1.9 × 10⁻⁵. Calculate the molar solubility of Ag₂SO₄ in pure water and compare it with solubility in 0.1 M H₂SO₄.

**Solution — Pure water:**
Ag₂SO₄ → 2Ag⁺ + SO₄²⁻; Ksp = (2S)²(S) = 4S³
S = (Ksp/4)^(1/3) = (1.9×10⁻⁵/4)^(1/3) = (4.75×10⁻⁶)^(1/3) = **1.67 × 10⁻² mol L⁻¹**

**In 0.1 M H₂SO₄:**
[SO₄²⁻] ≈ 0.1 M (from H₂SO₄, >> from Ag₂SO₄)
Ksp = (2S)²(0.1) = 4S² × 0.1 = 0.4S²
S² = 1.9×10⁻⁵/0.4 = 4.75×10⁻⁵
**S = 6.89 × 10⁻³ mol L⁻¹** (less soluble due to common ion SO₄²⁻ ✓)

**Q84.** **[Numerical]** Find the equilibrium partial pressures of CO and CO₂ for the reaction: CO₂(g) + C(s) ⇌ 2CO(g); Kp = 3.0 at 1000 K. Initially P(CO₂) = 0.48 bar, P(CO) = 0.

**Solution:**
Let x = decrease in P(CO₂):
Kp = (2x)²/(0.48–x) = 3.0
4x² = 3(0.48–x) = 1.44 – 3x
4x² + 3x – 1.44 = 0
x = (–3 + √(9 + 23.04))/8 = (–3 + √32.04)/8 = (–3 + 5.66)/8 = **0.33 bar**

**P(CO) = 2 × 0.33 = 0.66 bar; P(CO₂) = 0.48 – 0.33 = 0.15 bar**

**Q85.** **[Numerical]** The ionization constant of NH₄⁺ (conjugate acid of NH₃) if Kb(NH₃) = 1.77 × 10⁻⁵.

**Solution:**
Ka(NH₄⁺) × Kb(NH₃) = Kw = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴
Ka(NH₄⁺) = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴ / 1.77 × 10⁻⁵ = **5.64 × 10⁻¹⁰**

pKa(NH₄⁺) = –log(5.64×10⁻¹⁰) ≈ **9.25**
