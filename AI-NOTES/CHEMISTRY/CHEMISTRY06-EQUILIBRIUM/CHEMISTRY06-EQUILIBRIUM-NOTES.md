## SECTION 1 — EQUILIBRIUM IN PHYSICAL PROCESSES `[Board · NEET]`

*"Chemical equilibria are important in numerous biological and environmental processes. For example, equilibria involving O₂ molecules and the protein hemoglobin play a crucial role in transport and delivery of O₂ from our lungs to our muscles."*

### 1.1 What is Equilibrium?

**Equilibrium state:** The stage where the rate of the forward process equals the rate of the reverse process, and all macroscopic properties (concentration, pressure, density, colour, etc.) remain constant with time.

> **Key Point:** Equilibrium is **DYNAMIC**, not static. Both forward and reverse processes continue simultaneously at equal rates. There is intense activity at the molecular level even though macroscopic properties appear constant.

**Equilibrium can only be established in a CLOSED system** (no exchange of matter with surroundings). In an open system, equilibrium cannot be reached.

### 1.2 Solid–Liquid Equilibrium

> Ice ⇌ Water at 273 K and 1 atm

* Rates of melting and freezing are equal
* Mass of ice and water remain constant
* Temperature remains constant
* For any pure substance at 1 atm: temperature at which solid and liquid phases coexist at equilibrium = **normal melting/freezing point**

**Inference:**
(i) Both opposing processes occur simultaneously
(ii) Both processes occur at the same rate

### 1.3 Liquid–Vapour Equilibrium

> H₂O(l) ⇌ H₂O(g)

* Rate of evaporation = Rate of condensation
* Pressure exerted by vapour at a given temperature = **equilibrium vapour pressure**
* Vapour pressure increases with temperature
* Higher vapour pressure → more volatile → lower boiling point

**Normal Boiling Point:** Temperature at which vapour pressure of liquid = 1.013 bar (1 atm). Water boils at 100°C at 1.013 bar.

> ⚠️ Equilibrium **cannot** be reached in an open system (vapour disperses, condensation rate → 0)

### 1.4 Solid–Vapour Equilibrium

> I₂(solid) ⇌ I₂(vapour); Camphor(solid) ⇌ Camphor(vapour); NH₄Cl(solid) ⇌ NH₄Cl(vapour)

* At equilibrium, intensity of colour (for iodine) becomes constant
* Solid sublimes to give vapour; vapour condenses back to solid

### 1.5 Equilibrium Involving Dissolution of Solids/Gases in Liquids

**Solids in liquids (saturated solution):**
> Sugar(solution) ⇌ Sugar(solid)

* Rate of dissolution = Rate of crystallisation
* Confirmed by radioactive sugar experiment: radioactivity is found in both phases after time, confirming dynamic exchange

**Gases in liquids (Henry's Law):**
> CO₂(gas) ⇌ CO₂(in solution)

* **Henry's Law:** Mass of gas dissolved in a given mass of solvent at any temperature is proportional to the pressure of the gas above the solvent
* Opening a soda bottle: CO₂ escapes to reach new equilibrium at lower pressure

### 1.6 General Characteristics of Physical Equilibria

| Property | Condition |
| -------- | --------- |
| System | Must be **closed** |
| Process | **Dynamic** — both opposing processes ongoing |
| Macroscopic properties | **Constant** |
| Liquid ⇌ Vapour | Vapour pressure constant at given T |
| Solid ⇌ Liquid | Melting point fixed at constant pressure |
| Solute(s) ⇌ Solute(aq) | Concentration constant at given T |
| Gas(g) ⇌ Gas(aq) | [gas(aq)]/[gas(g)] constant at given T |

## SECTION 2 — EQUILIBRIUM IN CHEMICAL PROCESSES `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

### 2.1 Dynamic Chemical Equilibrium

For a reversible reaction: **A + B ⇌ C + D**

* Initially: forward reaction rate is high; reverse rate is zero
* As products form: forward rate decreases, reverse rate increases
* At equilibrium: rate(forward) = rate(reverse) → concentrations become **constant**

**Equilibrium can be reached from both directions:**
* Start with pure reactants: concentrations of products increase until equilibrium
* Start with pure products: concentrations of reactants form until same equilibrium

### 2.2 Haber's Deuterium Experiment (Proof of Dynamic Nature)

Haber's synthesis of NH₃ was repeated with D₂ (deuterium) instead of H₂. When the two equilibrium mixtures (one with H₂/N₂/NH₃ and one with D₂/N₂/ND₃) were mixed and analysed by mass spectrometer, **all mixed forms** (NH₃, NH₂D, NHD₂, ND₃, H₂, HD, D₂) were found.

> **Conclusion:** Scrambling of H and D atoms proves that forward and reverse reactions continue even at equilibrium. If reactions had stopped at equilibrium, no mixing of isotopes would occur.

### 2.3 Key Features of Chemical Equilibrium

1. Dynamic in nature — both reactions continue simultaneously
2. Forward rate = Reverse rate
3. Concentrations of all species become constant (not necessarily equal)
4. Can be approached from either direction
5. Requires a **closed system**
6. All macroscopic properties become constant

## SECTION 3 — LAW OF CHEMICAL EQUILIBRIUM AND Kc `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

### 3.1 The Equilibrium Constant Expression

For a general reversible reaction: **a A + b B ⇌ c C + d D**

**Guldberg and Waage (1864)** proposed the equilibrium equation:

$$\boxed{K_c = \frac{[C]^c[D]^d}{[A]^a[B]^b}}$$

Where concentrations are **equilibrium** concentrations in mol L⁻¹.

* Kc = **Equilibrium constant** (concentration basis)
* The expression on the right = **equilibrium constant expression**
* Also called **Law of Mass Action** (historically)
* Powers = stoichiometric coefficients in balanced equation

> **Note:** The subscript 'eq' is usually dropped; it is understood that concentrations in Kc expression are equilibrium values.

**For H₂(g) + I₂(g) ⇌ 2HI(g):**

$$K_c = \frac{[HI]^2}{[H_2][I_2]}$$

Verified experimentally: value is constant regardless of initial concentrations (Table 6.3 in NCERT).

### 3.2 Properties of Kc

| Property | Detail |
| -------- | ------ |
| Value | Constant at a given temperature |
| Independent of | Initial concentrations of reactants/products |
| Temperature dependent | Changes when temperature changes |
| Reverse reaction | K'c = 1/Kc |
| Multiply equation by n | K_new = Kc^n |
| Divide equation by n | K_new = Kc^(1/n) |
| Net = sum of steps | K_net = K₁ × K₂ × ... |

**Example with stoichiometry:**

* H₂ + I₂ ⇌ 2HI → Kc = [HI]²/[H₂][I₂] = x
* 2HI ⇌ H₂ + I₂ → K'c = 1/x = 1/Kc
* ½H₂ + ½I₂ ⇌ HI → K"c = x^(1/2) = Kc^(1/2)

## SECTION 4 — Kp AND RELATIONSHIP WITH Kc `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

### 4.1 Equilibrium Constant in Terms of Partial Pressures

For gaseous reactions, it is convenient to express equilibrium in terms of **partial pressures**:

For **a A(g) + b B(g) ⇌ c C(g) + d D(g):**

$$\boxed{K_p = \frac{p_C^c \cdot p_D^d}{p_A^a \cdot p_B^b}}$$

* Pressure should be expressed in **bar** (standard state = 1 bar)
* 1 Pascal = 1 N m⁻²; 1 bar = 10⁵ Pa

### 4.2 Relationship Between Kp and Kc

Since p = [gas]RT (for ideal gas, p = cRT where c = n/V):

$$\boxed{K_p = K_c(RT)^{\Delta n}}$$

Where:
* R = 0.0831 bar L mol⁻¹ K⁻¹
* T = temperature in Kelvin
* **Δn = (moles of gaseous products) – (moles of gaseous reactants)** in balanced equation

| Δn | Relationship |
| -- | ------------ |
| Δn = 0 | Kp = Kc |
| Δn > 0 | Kp > Kc |
| Δn < 0 | Kp < Kc |

**Example:**

For N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g): Δn = 2 – (1+3) = **–2**

$$K_p = K_c(RT)^{-2} = \frac{K_c}{(RT)^2}$$

> ⚠️ **Important:** While calculating Kp, pressure MUST be in bar (not atm) because standard state for pressure is 1 bar.

## SECTION 5 — HOMOGENEOUS AND HETEROGENEOUS EQUILIBRIA `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

### 5.1 Homogeneous Equilibria

All reactants and products are in the **same phase**.

**Examples:**
* N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g) — all gaseous
* CH₃COOC₂H₅(aq) + H₂O(l) ⇌ CH₃COOH(aq) + C₂H₅OH(aq) — all aqueous
* Fe³⁺(aq) + SCN⁻(aq) ⇌ [Fe(SCN)]²⁺(aq) — all aqueous

### 5.2 Heterogeneous Equilibria

Reactants and products are in **more than one phase**.

**Examples:**
* H₂O(l) ⇌ H₂O(g) — liquid and gas
* CaCO₃(s) ⇌ CaO(s) + CO₂(g) — solid and gas
* Ca(OH)₂(s) + (aq) ⇌ Ca²⁺(aq) + 2OH⁻(aq) — solid and aqueous

### 5.3 Treating Pure Solids and Liquids in Kc

> **Rule:** Molar concentration of a **pure solid or pure liquid is constant** (independent of amount present). Therefore, **pure solids and pure liquids are NOT included** in the equilibrium constant expression.

**Reason:** [X(s)] or [X(l)] = constant = incorporated into Kc.

**For CaCO₃(s) ⇌ CaO(s) + CO₂(g):**

$$K_c = \frac{[CaO(s)][CO_2(g)]}{[CaCO_3(s)]}$$

Since [CaCO₃] and [CaO] are constants:

$$K'_c = [CO_2(g)] \quad \text{or} \quad K_p = p_{CO_2}$$

At 1100 K: p(CO₂) = 2.0 × 10⁵ Pa → Kp = 2.0 × 10⁵/10⁵ = 2.00

**For Ni(s) + 4CO(g) ⇌ Ni(CO)₄(g):**

$$K_c = \frac{[\text{Ni(CO)}_4]}{[\text{CO}]^4}$$

(Ni is pure solid → excluded)

> ⚠️ For heterogeneous equilibrium: pure solids/liquids MUST be present at equilibrium for the equilibrium to exist, but their concentrations do NOT appear in K expressions.

## SECTION 6 — APPLICATIONS OF EQUILIBRIUM CONSTANTS `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

### 6.1 Summary of Key Features of Kc

1. Expression is valid only when equilibrium is attained
2. Value is **independent of initial concentrations**
3. **Temperature-dependent** — one unique value at given T
4. Reverse reaction: K'c = 1/Kc
5. Multiplying equation by n: K_new = Kc^n

### 6.2 Predicting Extent of Reaction

| Value of Kc | Implication |
| ----------- | ----------- |
| **Kc > 10³** | Products predominate; reaction proceeds nearly to completion |
| **Kc < 10⁻³** | Reactants predominate; reaction proceeds to very small extent |
| **10⁻³ < Kc < 10³** | Appreciable concentrations of both reactants and products present |

**Examples:**
* H₂ + O₂ at 500 K: Kc = 2.4 × 10⁴⁷ → proceeds to completion
* H₂ + Cl₂ ⇌ 2HCl at 300 K: Kc = 4.0 × 10³¹ → proceeds to completion
* N₂ + O₂ ⇌ 2NO at 298 K: Kc = 4.8 × 10⁻³¹ → barely proceeds
* H₂ + I₂ ⇌ 2HI at 700 K: Kc = 57.0 → appreciable amounts of both

> **Important:** Kc gives information about **extent** of reaction, NOT about the **rate** at which equilibrium is reached.

### 6.3 Predicting Direction of Reaction — Reaction Quotient (Qc)

**Reaction Quotient Qc:** Defined exactly like Kc, but using **non-equilibrium concentrations** (concentrations at any arbitrary time t).

For: a A + b B ⇌ c C + d D

$$Q_c = \frac{[C]^c[D]^d}{[A]^a[B]^b} \quad \text{(concentrations not necessarily at equilibrium)}$$

| Comparison | Interpretation |
| ---------- | -------------- |
| **Qc < Kc** | Reaction proceeds **forward** (more products needed) |
| **Qc > Kc** | Reaction proceeds **backward/reverse** (more reactants needed) |
| **Qc = Kc** | System is at **equilibrium** (no net change) |

```
Qc < Kc              Qc = Kc              Qc > Kc
Reactants → Products  ← Equilibrium →  Products → Reactants
(forward)                               (reverse)
```

### 6.4 Calculating Equilibrium Concentrations — ICE Table Method

**Step-by-step approach:**

1. Write balanced equation
2. Set up ICE table (Initial, Change, Equilibrium)
3. Express change in terms of x (one unknown)
4. Substitute equilibrium expressions into Kc
5. Solve for x (may require quadratic formula)
6. Calculate equilibrium concentrations
7. Verify by substituting back into Kc

**ICE Table Template:**

```
                  aA   +   bB   ⇌   cC   +   dD
Initial (M):       I₁       I₂       0        0
Change (M):       –ax      –bx     +cx      +dx
Equilibrium (M): I₁–ax   I₂–bx    cx       dx
```

**Quadratic Formula (for equations of form ax² + bx + c = 0):**
$$x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$$

> ⚠️ Always choose the value of x that makes **physical sense** (concentrations must be positive and ≤ initial values).

## SECTION 7 — GIBBS ENERGY AND EQUILIBRIUM CONSTANT `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

### 7.1 The Connection (from Chapter 5)

From thermodynamics: ΔG = ΔG° + RT ln Q

At equilibrium: ΔG = 0 and Q = Kc, so:

$$\boxed{\Delta G° = -RT\ln K = -2.303 RT\log K}$$

$$K = e^{-\Delta G°/RT}$$

| ΔG° | K | Spontaneity |
| --- | - | ----------- |
| ΔG° < 0 | K > 1 | Forward reaction favoured; spontaneous |
| ΔG° = 0 | K = 1 | Neither forward nor reverse favoured |
| ΔG° > 0 | K < 1 | Reverse reaction favoured; non-spontaneous forward |

## SECTION 8 — FACTORS AFFECTING EQUILIBRIUM: LE CHATELIER'S PRINCIPLE `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

### 8.1 Le Chatelier's Principle

> **Le Chatelier's Principle:** "A change in any of the factors that determine the equilibrium conditions of a system will cause the system to change in such a manner so as to **reduce or counteract the effect of the change**."

Applies to all physical and chemical equilibria.

### 8.2 Effect of Concentration Change

| Change | Qc vs Kc | Net Reaction Direction |
| ------ | --------- | ---------------------- |
| Add reactant | Qc < Kc | Forward (consumes added reactant) |
| Remove reactant | Qc > Kc | Backward (replenishes removed reactant) |
| Add product | Qc > Kc | Backward (consumes added product) |
| Remove product | Qc < Kc | Forward (replenishes removed product) |

**Industrial Application:**
* **Ammonia manufacture:** NH₃ is liquified and removed → Qc < Kc → forward reaction continues
* **CaO manufacture from CaCO₃:** CO₂ removed continuously from kiln → reaction goes to completion

**Experiment — Fe³⁺/SCN⁻ system:**
> Fe³⁺(aq) + SCN⁻(aq) ⇌ [Fe(SCN)]²⁺(aq) (deep red)

* Adding SCN⁻ → deeper red (forward)
* Adding oxalic acid → removes Fe³⁺ as stable complex → equilibrium shifts backward → colour fades
* Adding HgCl₂ → removes SCN⁻ → backward shift → colour fades

### 8.3 Effect of Pressure Change

**Pressure change applies only to gaseous reactions where Δn ≠ 0.**

> Pressure change obtained by changing volume of container.

| Change | Qc vs Kc | Shift |
| ------ | --------- | ----- |
| Increase pressure (decrease volume) | Qc > Kc or < Kc | Toward side with **fewer moles of gas** |
| Decrease pressure (increase volume) | Qc changes | Toward side with **more moles of gas** |

**Examples:**

* CO(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ CH₄(g) + H₂O(g): Δn = 2–4 = –2 → Increase pressure → Forward (fewer moles)
* C(s) + CO₂(g) ⇌ 2CO(g): Δn = 2–1 = +1 → Increase pressure → Backward (fewer moles of gas)

> ⚠️ **Inert gas addition at constant volume:** Does NOT affect equilibrium (no change in partial pressures or concentrations of reactants/products)

> ⚠️ **Pressure effects on solids and liquids** can be ignored (volume nearly independent of pressure)

### 8.4 Effect of Temperature Change

> **Unlike concentration and pressure, temperature change actually changes the value of Kc (and Kp).**

| Reaction type | Increasing T | Effect on Kc |
| ------------- | ------------ | ------------ |
| Exothermic (ΔH < 0) | Shift backward | Kc **decreases** |
| Endothermic (ΔH > 0) | Shift forward | Kc **increases** |

**Logic (Le Chatelier's):**
* For exothermic reaction: heat is a "product"; adding heat (increasing T) → equilibrium shifts to consume heat (backward) → Kc decreases
* For endothermic reaction: heat is a "reactant"; adding heat → equilibrium shifts forward → Kc increases

**Example — Haber Process:**
N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g); ΔH = –92.38 kJ mol⁻¹ (exothermic)
* Low T → favours NH₃ formation (K is larger)
* But very low T → reaction too slow → **catalyst (Fe) used** to achieve reasonable rate at ~500°C

**Experiment — NO₂/N₂O₄ system:**
2NO₂(g) ⇌ N₂O₄(g); ΔH = –57.2 kJ mol⁻¹

* Low temperature (ice bath) → equilibrium shifts forward → N₂O₄ favoured → colourless (less brown NO₂)
* High temperature (hot water) → backward → NO₂ favoured → deeper brown colour

### 8.5 Effect of Catalyst

> **Catalyst does NOT affect the equilibrium composition or the value of Kc/Kp.**

* Catalyst **increases the rate** of both forward and reverse reactions **equally**
* Catalyst provides a new, lower-energy pathway (lowers activation energy) for both directions
* Equilibrium is reached **faster** but the same equilibrium position is attained
* Catalyst does not appear in the balanced equation or Kc expression

**Examples:**
* Haber process: Iron catalyst helps reach equilibrium faster at ~500°C
* Contact process (H₂SO₄): V₂O₅ or Pt catalyst for 2SO₂ + O₂ ⇌ 2SO₃ (Kc = 1.7 × 10²⁶)

## SECTION 9 — IONIC EQUILIBRIUM IN SOLUTION `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

### 9.1 Electrolytes

**Electrolytes:** Substances that conduct electricity in aqueous solution due to ions formed by dissociation/ionization.

| Type | Ionization | Example |
| ---- | ---------- | ------- |
| **Strong electrolyte** | Nearly complete (≈100%) | NaCl, HCl, NaOH, H₂SO₄ |
| **Weak electrolyte** | Partial (< 100%) | CH₃COOH (<5% ionized), NH₃ |
| **Non-electrolyte** | None | Sugar, glucose, urea |

**Dissociation vs Ionization:**
* **Dissociation:** Separation of already-existing ions (e.g., NaCl in water)
* **Ionization:** Neutral molecule splits into ions (e.g., HCl in water)
* In this chapter, both terms are used interchangeably.

## SECTION 10 — ACID-BASE THEORIES `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

### 10.1 Arrhenius Theory (1884)

* **Acid:** Substance that gives **H⁺ (or H₃O⁺)** in aqueous solution
* **Base:** Substance that gives **OH⁻** in aqueous solution

**Examples:**
* HCl(aq) → H⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq)
* NaOH(aq) → Na⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq)

**Limitation:** Only applicable to aqueous solutions; cannot explain basicity of NH₃ (which has no OH⁻ group).

### 10.2 Brønsted–Lowry Theory (1923)

* **Acid:** Proton (H⁺) **donor**
* **Base:** Proton (H⁺) **acceptor**

**Conjugate acid-base pair:** Pair that differs by exactly one proton.

```
HCl(aq)  +  H₂O(l)  ⇌  H₃O⁺(aq)  +  Cl⁻(aq)
Acid₁       Base₂        Acid₂         Base₁

Conjugate pairs:
  HCl / Cl⁻    (acid₁ / conjugate base₁)
  H₃O⁺ / H₂O  (acid₂ / conjugate base₂)
```

**Key relationships:**
* Strong acid → very weak conjugate base
* Weak acid → relatively stronger conjugate base
* **Strong acid donates proton to stronger base; equilibrium favours weaker acid and weaker base**

**Amphoteric species** (act as both acid and base):

| Species | Conjugate Acid | Conjugate Base |
| ------- | -------------- | -------------- |
| H₂O | H₃O⁺ | OH⁻ |
| HCO₃⁻ | H₂CO₃ | CO₃²⁻ |
| HSO₄⁻ | H₂SO₄ | SO₄²⁻ |
| NH₃ | NH₄⁺ | NH₂⁻ |

### 10.3 Lewis Theory (1923)

* **Lewis acid:** Electron pair **acceptor**
* **Lewis base:** Electron pair **donor**

**Much broader definition than Brønsted–Lowry:**

| Species | Lewis Classification | Reason |
| ------- | ------------------- | ------ |
| BF₃ | **Lewis acid** | Electron deficient; accepts lone pair from NH₃ |
| AlCl₃, Co³⁺, Mg²⁺ | **Lewis acid** | Electron-deficient metals |
| H₂O, NH₃, OH⁻, F⁻ | **Lewis base** | Have lone pairs to donate |
| H⁺ | **Lewis acid** | Accepts lone pairs |

**Reaction:** BF₃ + :NH₃ → BF₃:NH₃

> ⚠️ Note: **Lewis acid ≠ Brønsted acid**. BF₃ is a Lewis acid but NOT a Brønsted acid (it has no proton to donate).

## SECTION 11 — IONIZATION OF ACIDS AND BASES `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

### 11.1 Strong Acids and Bases

**Strong acids** (virtually 100% ionized in water):
HClO₄, HCl, HBr, HI, HNO₃, H₂SO₄

**Strong bases** (virtually 100% dissociated):
LiOH, NaOH, KOH, CsOH, Ba(OH)₂

Conjugate bases of strong acids (Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻, NO₃⁻, etc.) are **much weaker bases than water**.

### 11.2 Ionization Constant of Weak Acids (Ka)

For weak acid HX:
> HX(aq) + H₂O(l) ⇌ H₃O⁺(aq) + X⁻(aq)

Using ICE table with initial concentration c and degree of ionization α:

| | HX | H₃O⁺ | X⁻ |
|-|----|------|-----|
| Initial (M) | c | 0 | 0 |
| Change (M) | –cα | +cα | +cα |
| Equilibrium (M) | c–cα | cα | cα |

$$\boxed{K_a = \frac{[H^+][X^-]}{[HX]} = \frac{c\alpha^2}{1-\alpha}}$$

**When α << 1 (weak acid approximation):**
$$K_a \approx c\alpha^2 \implies \alpha = \sqrt{\frac{K_a}{c}}$$

**pKa = –log Ka** (stronger acid → smaller Ka → smaller pKa)

**Important Ka values (298 K):**

| Acid | Ka |
| ---- | -- |
| HF | 3.5 × 10⁻⁴ |
| HNO₂ | 4.5 × 10⁻⁴ |
| HCOOH | 1.8 × 10⁻⁴ |
| CH₃COOH | 1.74 × 10⁻⁵ |
| HCN | 4.9 × 10⁻¹⁰ |
| C₆H₅OH (phenol) | 1.3 × 10⁻¹⁰ |

### 11.3 Ionization Constant of Weak Bases (Kb)

For weak base MOH:
> MOH(aq) ⇌ M⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq)

$$\boxed{K_b = \frac{[M^+][OH^-]}{[MOH]} = \frac{c\alpha^2}{1-\alpha}}$$

**pKb = –log Kb**

**Important Kb values (298 K):**

| Base | Kb |
| ---- | -- |
| Dimethylamine | 5.4 × 10⁻⁴ |
| Ammonia (NH₃) | 1.77 × 10⁻⁵ |
| Pyridine | 1.77 × 10⁻⁹ |
| Aniline | 4.27 × 10⁻¹⁰ |

### 11.4 Relationship Between Ka and Kb for Conjugate Pair

For a conjugate acid-base pair (e.g., NH₄⁺ / NH₃):

$$\boxed{K_a \times K_b = K_w = 1.0 \times 10^{-14} \text{ at 298 K}}$$

$$\boxed{pK_a + pK_b = pK_w = 14 \text{ at 298 K}}$$

**For net reactions:**
$$K_{NET} = K_1 \times K_2 \times ...$$

### 11.5 Polybasic Acids

For dibasic acid H₂X:
* H₂X ⇌ H⁺ + HX⁻: first ionization constant Ka₁
* HX⁻ ⇌ H⁺ + X²⁻: second ionization constant Ka₂

**Ka₁ >> Ka₂ >> Ka₃** (each successive ionization is harder due to increasing negative charge)

## SECTION 12 — IONIZATION OF WATER AND pH SCALE `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

### 12.1 Ionic Product of Water (Kw)

Water self-ionizes (autoprotolysis):
> H₂O(l) + H₂O(l) ⇌ H₃O⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq)

$$\boxed{K_w = [H^+][OH^-] = 1.0 \times 10^{-14} \text{ M}^2 \text{ at 298 K}}$$

* [H⁺] = [OH⁻] = 1.0 × 10⁻⁷ M in pure water
* Kw is temperature-dependent

**Acidic/Neutral/Basic classification:**

| Solution | [H₃O⁺] vs [OH⁻] | pH |
| -------- | ----------------- | -- |
| Acidic | [H₃O⁺] > [OH⁻] | < 7 |
| Neutral | [H₃O⁺] = [OH⁻] | = 7 |
| Basic | [H₃O⁺] < [OH⁻] | > 7 |

### 12.2 pH Scale

$$\boxed{pH = -\log[H^+] = -\log a_{H^+}}$$

In dilute solutions (< 0.01 M): activity ≈ molarity.

$$\boxed{pOH = -\log[OH^-]}$$

$$\boxed{pH + pOH = pK_w = 14 \text{ at 298 K}}$$

**pH of common substances (298 K):**

| Substance | pH |
| --------- | -- |
| 1M HCl | ~0 |
| Gastric juice | ~1.2 |
| Lemon juice | ~2.2 |
| Soft drinks/vinegar | ~3.0 |
| Human saliva | ~6.4 |
| Pure water | 7.0 |
| Human blood | 7.4 |
| Milk of magnesia | 10 |
| 0.1M NaOH | 13 |

> ⚠️ A change of 1 pH unit = change in [H⁺] by factor of 10. A change of 2 pH units = factor of 100.

**Hydronium ion (H₃O⁺):** Bare proton (H⁺) is highly reactive; it bonds to water oxygen's lone pair to form H₃O⁺ (trigonal pyramidal). In this chapter, H⁺ and H₃O⁺ are used interchangeably.

## SECTION 13 — FACTORS AFFECTING ACID STRENGTH `[Board · NEET]`

### 13.1 H–A Bond Strength vs Polarity

**Within same group (going down):**
* H–A bond strength is more important
* Bond strength **decreases** down the group (larger atom) → acid strength **increases**
* HF << HCl << HBr << HI (acid strength increases)
* H₂O << H₂S (H₂S is stronger acid)

**Within same period (going across):**
* H–A bond polarity is more important
* Electronegativity of A increases → stronger acid
* CH₄ < NH₃ < H₂O < HF (acid strength increases)

## SECTION 14 — COMMON ION EFFECT `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

### 14.1 Definition

**Common ion effect:** The shift in equilibrium caused by the addition of an ion already present in the equilibrium mixture.

**Example:** Acetic acid equilibrium:
> CH₃COOH(aq) ⇌ H⁺(aq) + CH₃COO⁻(aq)

Adding sodium acetate (CH₃COONa) provides additional CH₃COO⁻ (common ion).
→ Qc > Kc → equilibrium shifts backward → [H⁺] decreases → **degree of ionization of acetic acid decreases**

**The common ion effect is based on Le Chatelier's principle.**

**Calculation example** — 0.05M HAc + 0.05M Ac⁻ (acetate):

```
               HAc      H⁺      Ac⁻
Initial (M):  0.05     0      0.05
Change (M):    –x     +x      +x
Equilibrium:  0.05–x   x    0.05+x
```

For weak acid: x << 0.05:
Ka = x(0.05)/0.05 = x = 1.8 × 10⁻⁵ M = [H⁺]; pH = 4.74

## SECTION 15 — HYDROLYSIS OF SALTS `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

**Hydrolysis:** Interaction of cations/anions of a salt with water to reform the parent acid or base.

| Salt type | Example | Solution pH | Reason |
| --------- | ------- | ----------- | ------ |
| Strong acid + Strong base | NaCl, KBr | **Neutral (pH = 7)** | Neither ion hydrolyses |
| Weak acid + Strong base | CH₃COONa | **Basic (pH > 7)** | CH₃COO⁻ + H₂O ⇌ CH₃COOH + OH⁻ |
| Strong acid + Weak base | NH₄Cl | **Acidic (pH < 7)** | NH₄⁺ + H₂O ⇌ NH₄OH + H⁺ |
| Weak acid + Weak base | CH₃COONH₄ | **≈ Neutral** | Degree of hydrolysis independent of concentration |

**For salt of weak acid + weak base:**
$$\boxed{pH = 7 + \frac{1}{2}(pK_a - pK_b)}$$

## SECTION 16 — BUFFER SOLUTIONS `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

**Buffer solution:** A solution that **resists change in pH** on addition of small amounts of acid or alkali, or on dilution.

**Types:**
1. **Acidic buffer:** Weak acid + its salt with strong base (e.g., CH₃COOH + CH₃COONa; pH ≈ 4.75)
2. **Basic buffer:** Weak base + its salt with strong acid (e.g., NH₄OH + NH₄Cl; pH ≈ 9.25)

### 16.1 Henderson–Hasselbalch Equation

**For acidic buffer:**

$$\boxed{pH = pK_a + \log\frac{[\text{Salt}]}{[\text{Acid}]} = pK_a + \log\frac{[A^-]}{[HA]}}$$

**For basic buffer:**

$$pOH = pK_b + \log\frac{[\text{Conjugate acid, BH}^+]}{[\text{Base, B}]}$$

**Key insight:**
* When [Salt] = [Acid]: pH = pKa (best buffering point)
* Select acid whose pKa ≈ desired pH
* **pH of buffer does NOT change on dilution** (ratio [Salt]/[Acid] unchanged)

## SECTION 17 — SOLUBILITY EQUILIBRIA AND Ksp `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

### 17.1 Solubility Product Constant (Ksp)

For sparingly soluble salt:
> BaSO₄(s) ⇌ Ba²⁺(aq) + SO₄²⁻(aq)

$$\boxed{K_{sp} = [Ba^{2+}][SO_4^{2-}]}$$

For general formula MₓXᵧ with molar solubility S:
> MₓXᵧ(s) ⇌ xM^(p+)(aq) + yX^(q–)(aq)

$$K_{sp} = [M^{p+}]^x[X^{q-}]^y = (xS)^x(yS)^y = x^x \cdot y^y \cdot S^{(x+y)}$$

$$S = \left(\frac{K_{sp}}{x^x \cdot y^y}\right)^{\frac{1}{x+y}}$$

**Example — BaSO₄:**
Ksp = 1.1 × 10⁻¹⁰; [Ba²⁺] = [SO₄²⁻] = S
→ S² = 1.1 × 10⁻¹⁰ → **S = 1.05 × 10⁻⁵ mol L⁻¹**

**Example — A₂X₃:**
Ksp = (2S)²(3S)³ = 108S⁵

### 17.2 Solubility Categories

| Category | Solubility |
| -------- | ---------- |
| Soluble | > 0.1 M |
| Slightly soluble | 0.01 M – 0.1 M |
| Sparingly soluble | < 0.01 M |

### 17.3 Comparing Solubilities Using Ksp

> ⚠️ **Cannot directly compare Ksp values of salts with different formulas to determine relative solubility.** Must calculate S for each.

**Example:**
* AgCN: Ksp = 6 × 10⁻¹⁷ → S₁ = 7.8 × 10⁻⁹ M
* Ni(OH)₂: Ksp = 2 × 10⁻¹⁵ → S₂ = 0.58 × 10⁻⁴ M
→ Ni(OH)₂ is **more soluble** despite having larger Ksp than AgCN (different formulas → different math)

### 17.4 Common Ion Effect on Solubility

Adding a common ion to a saturated solution of a sparingly soluble salt → **decreases solubility**.

**Example:** Ksp[Ni(OH)₂] = 2.0 × 10⁻¹⁵ in 0.10 M NaOH

[OH⁻] ≈ 0.10 M (since 2S << 0.10)
S × (0.10)² = 2.0 × 10⁻¹⁵ → **S = 2.0 × 10⁻¹³ M** (much less than in pure water!)

**Application of Ksp:**
* Gravimetric estimation: precipitate specific ions as sparingly soluble salts
* Purification of NaCl (passing HCl gas through saturated NaCl → common ion Cl⁻ → NaCl precipitates)

### 17.5 Precipitation Criterion

> If ionic product > Ksp → precipitation occurs
> If ionic product = Ksp → saturated solution (equilibrium)
> If ionic product < Ksp → unsaturated (no precipitation)

**Solubility and pH:**
For salt of weak acid: Solubility **increases at lower pH** (more H⁺ protonates X⁻ → equilibrium shifts forward → more salt dissolves)

$$S = \sqrt{\frac{K_{sp}([H^+] + K_a)}{K_a}}$$

## SECTION 19 — IMPORTANT FORMULAS SUMMARY `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

```
EQUILIBRIUM CONSTANTS:
  Kc = [products]^stoich / [reactants]^stoich
  Kp = Kc(RT)^Δn          [R = 0.0831 bar L mol⁻¹ K⁻¹]
  Δn = mol gaseous products – mol gaseous reactants
  Kp = Kc when Δn = 0

RELATIONSHIPS:
  K'c (reverse) = 1/Kc
  K (multiplied by n) = Kc^n
  KNET = K₁ × K₂ × ...

REACTION QUOTIENT:
  Qc < Kc → forward reaction     Qc > Kc → reverse reaction

IONIC EQUILIBRIA:
  Ka = c·α² / (1–α) ≈ c·α² (when α << 1)
  Kb = c·α² / (1–α) ≈ c·α² (when α << 1)
  Ka × Kb = Kw = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴ at 298 K
  pKa + pKb = 14 at 298 K

pH SCALE:
  pH = –log[H⁺]       pOH = –log[OH⁻]
  pH + pOH = 14 (at 298 K)
  Kw = [H⁺][OH⁻] = 10⁻¹⁴ at 298 K

BUFFER (Henderson–Hasselbalch):
  pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA])
  pOH = pKb + log([BH⁺]/[B])

SALT HYDROLYSIS:
  Weak acid + weak base salt: pH = 7 + ½(pKa – pKb)

SOLUBILITY PRODUCT:
  MₓXᵧ → xM^p+ + yX^q–
  Ksp = (xS)^x(yS)^y = x^x · y^y · S^(x+y)
  S = (Ksp / x^x · y^y)^(1/(x+y))

GIBBS–EQUILIBRIUM:
  ΔG° = –RT ln K = –2.303 RT log K
```

### Problem 6.1 — Kc Calculation for NH₃ Formation at 500K
**Given:** [N₂] = 1.5×10⁻²M, [H₂] = 3.0×10⁻²M, [NH₃] = 1.2×10⁻²M

For N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g):

$$K_c = \frac{[NH_3]^2}{[N_2][H_2]^3} = \frac{(1.2\times10^{-2})^2}{(1.5\times10^{-2})(3.0\times10^{-2})^3} = 0.355 \times 10^3 = \textbf{3.55 × 10²}$$

### Problem 6.5 — Converting Kc to Kp
**Given:** 2NOCl(g) ⇌ 2NO(g) + Cl₂(g); Kc = 3.75 × 10⁻⁶ at 1069 K

Δn = (2+1) – 2 = **1**
Kp = Kc(RT)^Δn = 3.75×10⁻⁶ × (0.0831 × 1069) = **0.033**

### Problem 6.8 — ICE Table for N₂O₄/NO₂
**Given:** 13.8g N₂O₄ in 1L at 400K; total equilibrium pressure = 9.15 bar

Moles N₂O₄ = 13.8/92 = 0.15; Initial pressure = nRT/V = 0.15 × 0.083 × 400 = **4.98 bar**

N₂O₄(g) ⇌ 2NO₂(g): (4.98–x) + 2x = 9.15 → x = 4.17 bar
* p(N₂O₄) = 0.81 bar; p(NO₂) = 8.34 bar
* Kp = (8.34)²/0.81 = 85.87; Kc = 85.87/(0.083×400) = **2.6**

### Problem 6.18 — Degree of Ionization of HF
**Given:** Ka(HF) = 3.2 × 10⁻⁴; c = 0.02M

Ka = 0.02α²/(1–α) = 3.2×10⁻⁴ → α² + 1.6×10⁻²α – 1.6×10⁻² = 0 → **α = 0.12**

[H₃O⁺] = [F⁻] = cα = 0.02 × 0.12 = 2.4 × 10⁻³M; **pH = 2.62**

### Problem 6.25 — Salt Hydrolysis pH
**Given:** pKa(CH₃COOH) = 4.76; pKb(NH₄OH) = 4.75; salt = CH₃COONH₄

pH = 7 + ½[pKa – pKb] = 7 + ½[4.76 – 4.75] = 7 + 0.005 = **7.005**
