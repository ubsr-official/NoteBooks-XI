## SECTION 1 — CLASSIFICATION OF HYDROCARBONS `[Board]`

Hydrocarbons = compounds containing **only carbon and hydrogen**.

```
Hydrocarbons
├── 1. Saturated
│       ├── Alkanes: open chain (CₙH₂ₙ₊₂)
│       └── Cycloalkanes: ring (CₙH₂ₙ)
├── 2. Unsaturated
│       ├── Alkenes: C=C double bond (CₙH₂ₙ)
│       └── Alkynes: C≡C triple bond (CₙH₂ₙ₋₂)
└── 3. Aromatic
        └── Benzene and homologues (arenes)
```

**Sources of Hydrocarbons:**

| Source | Hydrocarbons Obtained | Use |
|--------|----------------------|-----|
| Petroleum (fractional distillation) | Petrol, diesel, kerosene | Fuel |
| Natural gas / compression | CNG (Compressed Natural Gas) | Automobile fuel |
| Liquefaction | LPG (Liquefied Petroleum Gas), LNG | Domestic fuel |
| Coal (destructive distillation) | Coal gas, benzene, toluene | Industry |

## SECTION 2 — ALKANES `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

### 2.1 General Formula and Structure

- General formula: **CₙH₂ₙ₊₂**
- Carbon: **sp³ hybridised** → tetrahedral geometry
- H–C–H bond angle: **109.5°**
- C–C bond length: **154 pm**; C–H bond length: **112 pm**
- Only C–C and C–H **σ bonds** (formed by head-on sp³–sp³ or sp³–1s overlap)
- Earlier called **paraffins** (Latin: *parum* = little; *affinis* = affinity) due to chemical inertness

**First member:** Methane (CH₄) — found in coal mines (firedamp) and marshy places

### 2.2 Nomenclature and Isomerism

**Rules (recap from Ch. 8):**
1. Find the longest continuous carbon chain → parent alkane name
2. Number from the end giving **lowest locants** to substituents
3. Name substituents alphabetically (ignoring di-, tri-, etc.)

**Key isomers:**

| Formula | Name | BP (K) | Type |
|---------|------|--------|------|
| C₄H₁₀ (I) | n-Butane | 273 | Continuous chain |
| C₄H₁₀ (II) | 2-Methylpropane (isobutane) | 261 | Branched chain |
| C₅H₁₂ (III) | n-Pentane | 309 | Continuous chain |
| C₅H₁₂ (IV) | 2-Methylbutane (isopentane) | 301 | Branched chain |
| C₅H₁₂ (V) | 2,2-Dimethylpropane (neopentane) | 282.5 | Branched chain |

**Chain isomers:** Isomers differing only in the **carbon skeleton** (e.g., n-butane vs. isobutane).

**Isomer count:** C₆H₁₄ → 5 isomers; C₇H₁₆ → 9 isomers; C₁₀H₂₂ → 75 isomers.

**Classification of Carbon Atoms:**

| Type | Attached to (# other C) | Example in 2-methylpropane |
|------|--------------------------|----------------------------|
| Primary (1°) | 1 carbon | Terminal CH₃ groups |
| Secondary (2°) | 2 carbons | Middle C in n-propane |
| Tertiary (3°) | 3 carbons | Central C in isobutane |
| Quaternary (4°) | 4 carbons | C in neopentane |

> **Note:** Terminal carbon atoms are ALWAYS primary. Methane carbon (attached to NO other C) is also treated as primary.

**Alkyl Groups:** CₙH₂ₙ₊₁ — derived from alkane by removing one H.

### 2.3 Preparation of Alkanes

#### Method 1: From Unsaturated Hydrocarbons (Hydrogenation)

Alkenes and alkynes + H₂ → alkanes in presence of finely divided Pt, Pd, or Ni.

```
CH₂=CH₂ + H₂  ——Pt/Pd/Ni——→  CH₃–CH₃
(Ethene)                        (Ethane)

CH₃–C≡CH + 2H₂  ——Pt/Pd/Ni——→  CH₃–CH₂–CH₃
(Propyne)                        (Propane)
```

> Pt/Pd → work at **room temperature**; Ni → needs **higher temperature + pressure**

#### Method 2: From Alkyl Halides

**(i) Reduction with Zn/HCl:**
```
R–X + H₂  ——Zn, H⁺——→  R–H + HX

CH₃Cl + H₂  ——Zn, H⁺——→  CH₄ + HCl
```

**(ii) Wurtz Reaction** (two alkyl halides + sodium metal in dry ether):
```
R–X + 2Na + X–R  ——dry ether——→  R–R + 2NaX

CH₃Br + 2Na + BrCH₃  ——dry ether——→  CH₃–CH₃ + 2NaBr
(Bromomethane)                         (Ethane)

C₂H₅Br + 2Na + BrC₂H₅  ——dry ether——→  C₂H₅–C₂H₅ + 2NaBr
(Bromoethane)                           (n-Butane)
```

> **Limitation of Wurtz Reaction:** Best suited for alkanes with **even number of carbon atoms** (using one type of alkyl halide). Using **two different** alkyl halides gives a **mixture** of three alkanes.

#### Method 3: From Carboxylic Acids

**(i) Decarboxylation (with soda lime):**
Sodium salt of carboxylic acid + NaOH → alkane with **one C less** + Na₂CO₃.
```
CH₃COO⁻Na⁺ + NaOH  ——CaO/Δ——→  CH₄ + Na₂CO₃
(Sodium ethanoate)                (Methane)
```

> Soda lime = NaOH + CaO (CaO prevents the flask from being attacked by NaOH melt).

**(ii) Kolbe's Electrolytic Method:**
Aqueous solution of sodium/potassium salt of carboxylic acid → electrolysis → alkane (even number of C) at **anode**.
```
2CH₃COO⁻Na⁺ + 2H₂O  ——electrolysis——→  CH₃–CH₃ + 2CO₂ + H₂ + 2NaOH

At anode: 2CH₃COO⁻ → 2CH₃COO• → 2CH₃• + 2CO₂ → CH₃–CH₃
At cathode: H₂O + e⁻ → OH⁻ + ½H₂
```

> **Why methane cannot be prepared by Kolbe's method?** Because CH₃COO⁻ gives CH₃• → CH₃–CH₃ (ethane), not methane. For methane, you need HCOO⁻ (formate) → CO₂ (gas), not methane.

### 2.4 Physical Properties of Alkanes

| Property | Trend / Explanation |
|---------|-------------------|
| State | C₁–C₄: gases; C₅–C₁₇: liquids; C₁₈+: solids (at 298 K) |
| Boiling point | Increases with molecular mass (stronger van der Waals forces with larger surface area) |
| Effect of branching on BP | Branching ↑ → more spherical shape → smaller surface area → weaker vdW → lower BP |
| Colour/Odour | Colourless, odourless |
| Polarity | Almost non-polar (very small electronegativity difference C–H) |
| Solubility | Insoluble in water; soluble in non-polar solvents (like dissolves like) |

**BP Comparison (C₅H₁₂ isomers):**
- n-Pentane (309.1 K) > 2-Methylbutane (300.9 K) > 2,2-Dimethylpropane (282.5 K)
- More branching → lower BP ✓

### 2.5 Chemical Properties of Alkanes

Alkanes are generally **inert** (do not react with acids, bases, oxidising/reducing agents under normal conditions).

#### Reaction 1: Halogenation (Substitution)

```
CH₄ + Cl₂  ——hν——→  CH₃Cl + HCl  (chloromethane)
CH₃Cl + Cl₂  ——hν——→  CH₂Cl₂ + HCl  (dichloromethane)
CH₂Cl₂ + Cl₂  ——hν——→  CHCl₃ + HCl  (trichloromethane / chloroform)
CHCl₃ + Cl₂  ——hν——→  CCl₄ + HCl  (tetrachloromethane)
```

**Conditions:** High temperature (573–773 K) OR diffused sunlight / UV light (*hν*)

**Reactivity of halogens:** F₂ > Cl₂ > Br₂ > I₂
(F₂ too violent; I₂ too slow — reversible, needs oxidising agent HIO₃ or HNO₃)

**Rate of H-replacement:** 3° H > 2° H > 1° H

**Free Radical Chain Mechanism of Chlorination:**

```
(i) INITIATION:
    Cl–Cl  ——hν——→  2Cl•   (homolysis; Cl–Cl weakest bond)

(ii) PROPAGATION:
    (a) CH₄ + Cl•  ——→  •CH₃ + HCl
    (b) •CH₃ + Cl–Cl  ——→  CH₃Cl + Cl•   [steps (a) and (b) repeat → chain]

(iii) TERMINATION:
    (a) Cl• + Cl•  ——→  Cl₂
    (b) •CH₃ + •CH₃  ——→  CH₃–CH₃   [explains ethane as byproduct!]
    (c) •CH₃ + Cl•  ——→  CH₃Cl
```

> **Exam point:** The formation of **ethane as a byproduct** in the chlorination of methane is explained by the termination step (b): two methyl radicals combine.

#### Reaction 2: Combustion

Complete combustion in excess O₂/air:
```
CH₄(g) + 2O₂(g)  ——→  CO₂(g) + 2H₂O(l);  ΔHc = –890 kJ mol⁻¹
C₄H₁₀(g) + 13/2 O₂(g)  ——→  4CO₂(g) + 5H₂O(l);  ΔHc = –2875.84 kJ mol⁻¹
```

**General combustion equation:**
```
CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ + [(3n+1)/2] O₂  ——→  nCO₂ + (n+1)H₂O
```

**Incomplete combustion** (insufficient O₂) → **carbon black (soot)**:
```
CH₄(g) + O₂(g)  ——incomplete——→  C(s) + 2H₂O(l)
```
Carbon black is used in: ink, printer ink, black pigments, filters.

#### Reaction 3: Controlled Oxidation

```
2CH₄ + O₂  ——Cu/523K/100atm——→  2CH₃OH  (Methanol)
CH₄ + O₂  ——Mo₂O₃/Δ——→  HCHO + H₂O  (Methanal/Formaldehyde)
2CH₃CH₃ + 3O₂  ——(CH₃COO)Mn/Δ——→  2CH₃COOH + 2H₂O  (Ethanoic acid)
(CH₃)₃CH  ——KMnO₄——→  (CH₃)₃COH  (tertiary H → tertiary alcohol)
```

#### Reaction 4: Isomerisation

n-Alkanes → branched alkanes in presence of **anhydrous AlCl₃ + HCl(g)** at high temperature.
```
n-Hexane  ——anhy.AlCl₃/HCl——→  2-Methylpentane + 3-Methylpentane
```

#### Reaction 5: Aromatisation (Reforming)

n-Alkanes with ≥6 C atoms → benzene/homologues at 773 K, 10–20 atm, over Cr₂O₃/V₂O₅/MoO₃ on Al₂O₃:
```
n-Hexane  ——Cr₂O₃ or V₂O₅, 773K——→  Benzene + 4H₂
n-Heptane  ——Cr₂O₃ or V₂O₅, 773K——→  Toluene + 4H₂
```

#### Reaction 6: Reaction with Steam

```
CH₄ + H₂O  ——Ni/1273K——→  CO + 3H₂   (industrial H₂ production / synthesis gas)
```

#### Reaction 7: Pyrolysis (Cracking)

Higher alkanes → lower alkanes + alkenes + other products at high temperature:
```
C₆H₁₄  ——773K——→  C₆H₁₂ + H₂  (or)  C₄H₈ + C₂H₆  (or)  C₃H₆ + C₂H₄ + CH₄
C₁₂H₂₆  ——Pt/Pd/Ni, 973K——→  C₇H₁₆ + C₅H₁₀ + other products
(Dodecane)                    (Heptane)  (Pentene)
```
> Pyrolysis is a **free radical reaction**.

### 2.6 Conformations of Alkanes `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

**Definition:** Different spatial arrangements of atoms in space obtained by **rotation about C–C single bonds** are called **conformations** or **conformers** or **rotamers**.

- Free rotation about C–C σ bond is **not completely free** — hindered by a small energy barrier (1–20 kJ mol⁻¹) due to **torsional strain** (repulsion between electron clouds of adjacent C–H bonds).

#### Conformations of Ethane (C₂H₆):

Two extreme conformations:

| Conformation | H–H arrangement | Torsional strain | Stability | Energy |
|-------------|-----------------|-----------------|-----------|--------|
| **Eclipsed** | As close together as possible | Maximum | **Least stable** | Highest |
| **Staggered** | As far apart as possible | Minimum | **Most stable** | Lowest |

Any intermediate conformation is called **skew (gauche) conformation**.

> **Energy difference** between staggered and eclipsed ethane = **12.5 kJ mol⁻¹**
> This is small → easily overcome at ordinary temperatures → conformational isomers of ethane **cannot be separated and isolated**.

#### Representing Conformations:

**(i) Sawhorse Projection:**
- Molecule viewed **along molecular axis**
- Central C–C bond drawn as a long straight line
- **Front carbon** at lower end; **rear carbon** at upper end
- Three H-atoms drawn at 120° to each other on each carbon

**(ii) Newman Projection:**
- Molecule viewed **head-on** along C–C bond
- **Front carbon** = point (dot) with 3 lines at 120° for H
- **Rear carbon** = circle with 3 lines at 120° for H (shorter)
- The dihedral angle (torsional angle) is visible between front and rear H atoms

```
Newman Projection:
                          H        H
                         /          \
            H ——— •  (front C)     • ——— H   (rear C circle)
                         \          /
                          H        H
          [Eclipsed: front & rear H aligned]
          [Staggered: front & rear H at 60° to each other]
```

#### Why is staggered conformation more stable?

In the **staggered form**: electron clouds of C–H bonds are **maximally separated** → minimum repulsive forces → minimum energy → maximum stability.

In the **eclipsed form**: C–H electron clouds come **closer** → maximum repulsion (torsional strain) → higher energy → less stable.

## SECTION 3 — ALKENES `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

### 3.1 General Formula and Structure

- General formula: **CₙH₂ₙ** (for one double bond)
- Also called **olefins** (oil-forming; ethene + Cl₂ → oily liquid)
- First stable member: **Ethene (ethylene), C₂H₄**

#### Structure of C=C Double Bond:

| Bond | Type | Formation | Enthalpy | Length |
|------|------|-----------|---------|--------|
| C–C σ bond | sp²–sp² head-on overlap | Strong | ~397 kJ mol⁻¹ | 134 pm (for C=C) |
| C–C π bond | 2p–2p lateral overlap | Weak | ~284 kJ mol⁻¹ | — |

- **Total C=C bond enthalpy: 681 kJ mol⁻¹** (> C–C single bond: 348 kJ mol⁻¹)
- C=C bond length (134 pm) < C–C bond length (154 pm)
- All atoms of C=C are in the **same plane** (planar)
- **Rotation about C=C is RESTRICTED** (rotation destroys π-bond overlap)
- π electrons are loosely held → alkenes are attacked by **electrophiles**

**Orbital picture of ethene (C₂H₄):**
- Each C: sp² hybridised → 3 sp² orbitals in plane, 1 unhybridised 2p orbital ⊥ to plane
- σ framework: sp²–sp² (C=C σ) + sp²–1s (C–H bonds)
- π bond: lateral overlap of 2p orbitals → π cloud above AND below the σ-plane

---

### 3.2 Nomenclature of Alkenes

**Rules:**
- Select longest chain **containing the double bond**
- Number from the end **nearer to the double bond** (lowest locant)
- Replace '-ane' with '-ene'; for position, use lowest numbered C of double bond

| Structure | IUPAC Name |
|-----------|------------|
| CH₃–CH=CH₂ | Propene |
| CH₃–CH₂–CH=CH₂ | But-1-ene |
| CH₃–CH=CH–CH₃ | But-2-ene |
| CH₂=CH–CH=CH₂ | Buta-1,3-diene |
| CH₂=C(CH₃)–CH₃ | 2-Methylprop-1-ene |

---

### 3.3 Isomerism in Alkenes

#### (A) Structural Isomerism

**Chain isomerism + Position isomerism:**

For C₄H₈:
- **But-1-ene** (CH₂=CH–CH₂–CH₃) — position isomer with But-2-ene
- **But-2-ene** (CH₃–CH=CH–CH₃) — position isomer
- **2-Methylprop-1-ene** (CH₂=C(CH₃)–CH₃) — chain isomer of both above

#### (B) Geometrical (cis-trans) Isomerism `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

**Condition for cis-trans isomerism:** Both doubly bonded carbons must have **two DIFFERENT groups/atoms** attached to each.

Types:
- **Cis isomer:** Identical groups on the **same side** of double bond
- **Trans isomer:** Identical groups on **opposite sides** of double bond

```
Example: But-2-ene

cis-But-2-ene:        trans-But-2-ene:
  CH₃   CH₃             CH₃    H
    \   /                  \  /
     C=C                    C=C
    /   \                  /  \
   H     H                H    CH₃
  (µ = 0.33 D)           (µ ≈ 0)
  BP: 277 K               BP: 274 K
```

**Properties of cis vs trans:**
| Property | cis Isomer | trans Isomer |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| Dipole moment | **Higher** (bond moments add up) | **Lower/zero** (bond moments cancel) |
| Boiling point | Usually higher (more polar) | Usually lower |
| Melting point | Usually lower | **Higher** (more symmetric packing) |

**When is cis-trans isomerism NOT possible?**
- If two identical groups are on the **same** doubly bonded carbon
- e.g., (CH₃)₂C=CHC₂H₅ — the first C has two CH₃ (identical) → NO cis-trans
- CH₂=CBr₂ — first C has two Br → NO cis-trans

> **Types XYC=CXZ and XYC=CZW** also show cis-trans isomerism.

### 3.4 Preparation of Alkenes

#### Method 1: From Alkynes (Partial Reduction)

**(a) Lindlar's Catalyst** (palladised charcoal deactivated with quinoline or sulphur compounds) + H₂ → **cis-alkene**:
```
RC≡CR' + H₂  ——Lindlar's catalyst——→  cis-R(H)C=C(H)R'
CH≡CH + H₂  ——Pd/C——→  CH₂=CH₂   (Ethyne → Ethene)
```

**(b) Sodium in liquid NH₃** → **trans-alkene**:
```
RC≡CR' + H₂  ——Na/liq.NH₃——→  trans-R(H)C=C(H)R'
```

#### Method 2: From Alkyl Halides (Dehydrohalogenation)

Alkyl halide + **alcoholic KOH** → alkene + HX (β-elimination; Saytzeff's rule applies):
```
H    X
 |  |
–C—C–  ——alc. KOH/Δ——→  C=C  + HX
 |  |

CH₃CH₂Cl + KOH(alc.)  ——→  CH₂=CH₂ + KCl + H₂O
```

Rate for halogens: I > Br > Cl
Rate for alkyl groups: tertiary > secondary > primary

#### Method 3: From Vicinal Dihalides (Dehalogenation)

Vicinal dihalides (halogen on adjacent C atoms) + Zn → alkene + ZnX₂:
```
CH₂Br–CH₂Br + Zn  ——→  CH₂=CH₂ + ZnBr₂
CH₃CHBr–CH₂Br + Zn  ——→  CH₃CH=CH₂ + ZnBr₂
```

#### Method 4: From Alcohols (Acidic Dehydration)

Alcohol + conc. H₂SO₄ → alkene + H₂O (β-elimination):
```
CH₃CH₂OH  ——conc.H₂SO₄/443K——→  CH₂=CH₂ + H₂O
```

### 3.5 Chemical Properties of Alkenes

Alkenes are electron-rich (π electrons) → attacked by **electrophiles** → **electrophilic addition reactions**.

#### Reaction 1: Addition of H₂ (Hydrogenation)

```
CH₂=CH₂ + H₂  ——Ni/Pt/Pd——→  CH₃–CH₃
```

#### Reaction 2: Addition of Halogens (X₂)

Bromine (reddish-orange) or chlorine add to alkenes to form **vicinal dihalides**.
```
CH₂=CH₂ + Br₂  ——CCl₄——→  CH₂Br–CH₂Br   (1,2-Dibromoethane)
CH₃CH=CH₂ + Cl₂  ——CCl₄——→  CH₃CHCl–CH₂Cl   (1,2-Dichloropropane)
```

> **Test for unsaturation:** Reddish-orange bromine water / bromine in CCl₄ is **decolourised** when added to alkene.
> Mechanism: **Electrophilic addition** involving cyclic **halonium ion** intermediate (studied at higher level).

**Reactivity:** I₂ does **not** add under normal conditions.

#### Reaction 3: Addition of Hydrogen Halides (HX)

Order of reactivity: **HI > HBr > HCl**

**(a) Symmetrical alkenes:**
```
CH₂=CH₂ + HBr  ——→  CH₃–CH₂–Br   (Bromoethane)
```

**(b) Unsymmetrical alkenes — Markovnikov's Rule:**

> **Markovnikov's Rule (1869):** The negative part of the addendum (the halide X⁻) attaches to the carbon with the **lesser number of hydrogen atoms** (the more substituted C); the positive part (H⁺) attaches to the C with **more H atoms**.

```
CH₃–CH=CH₂ + HBr  ——→  CH₃–CHBr–CH₃   (2-Bromopropane, MAJOR product)
                     (NOT CH₃–CH₂–CH₂Br — 1-Bromopropane, minor product)
```

**Mechanism (why Markovnikov's rule works):**
```
Step 1: H⁺ attacks the double bond
  CH₃–CH=CH₂ + H⁺ ——→ CH₃–CH⁺–CH₃  (2° carbocation, more stable)
                      or CH₃–CH₂–CH₂⁺ (1° carbocation, less stable)
Step 2: Br⁻ attacks the more stable (2°) carbocation:
  CH₃–CH⁺–CH₃ + Br⁻ ——→ CH₃–CHBr–CH₃   (2-Bromopropane)
```
> More stable carbocation forms faster → major product follows Markovnikov's rule.

**(c) Anti-Markovnikov Addition (Peroxide Effect / Kharash Effect):**

In the presence of **peroxides** (e.g., benzoyl peroxide), HBr adds to unsymmetrical alkenes **against** Markovnikov's rule (discovered by M.S. Kharash and F.R. Mayo, 1933).

```
CH₃–CH=CH₂ + HBr  ——(C₆H₅CO)₂O₂——→  CH₃–CH₂–CH₂Br   (1-Bromopropane, MAJOR)
```

> **IMPORTANT:** Peroxide effect occurs **ONLY with HBr**, NOT with HCl or HI.
> - HCl: H–Cl bond too strong (430.5 kJ mol⁻¹) → not cleaved by free radicals
> - HI: H–I bond too weak (296.8 kJ mol⁻¹) → I• radicals combine to form I₂ instead

**Peroxide effect mechanism (Free Radical):**
```
Initiation: (C₆H₅CO)₂O₂ ——→ 2C₆H₅CO• ——→ 2C₆H₅• + 2CO₂
            C₆H₅• + HBr ——→ C₆H₆ + Br•
Propagation: CH₃–CH=CH₂ + Br• ——→ CH₃–CH•–CH₂Br  (more stable 2° radical, minor)
                                 or CH₃–CHBr–CH₂•  (less stable 1° radical, gives wrong product)
             Wait: CH₃–CH=CH₂ + Br• → CH₃–CH(Br)–CH₂•  (1° radical at terminal C is more stable
             because Br is already on the 2° carbon) → the terminal addition is correct!
             
   Actually: Br• adds to C1 (terminal, less substituted) → CH₃–CH•–CH₂Br (secondary radical — more stable)
             CH₃–CH•–CH₂Br + HBr → CH₃–CH₂–CH₂Br + Br•
```
> The secondary free radical (at C2) is **more stable** than the primary (at C1), explaining 1-bromopropane as major product in anti-Markovnikov addition.

#### Reaction 4: Addition of Sulphuric Acid

Cold conc. H₂SO₄ adds to alkenes following Markovnikov's rule → **alkyl hydrogen sulphates**:
```
CH₂=CH₂ + H–OSO₃H  ——→  CH₃–CH₂–OSO₃H   (Ethyl hydrogen sulphate)
CH₃–CH=CH₂ + H–OSO₃H  ——→  CH₃–CH(OSO₃H)–CH₃   (Propyl hydrogen sulphate)
```

#### Reaction 5: Addition of Water (Hydration)

In presence of a few drops of conc. H₂SO₄, alkenes + H₂O → **alcohols** (Markovnikov):
```
CH₃–C(CH₃)=CH₂ + H₂O  ——H⁺——→  CH₃–C(CH₃)(OH)–CH₃   (2-Methylpropan-2-ol)
```

#### Reaction 6: Oxidation

**(a) Cold, dilute KMnO₄ (Baeyer's Reagent) → vicinal diols (glycols):**
```
CH₂=CH₂ + H₂O + [O]  ——dil.KMnO₄/273K——→  CH₂OH–CH₂OH   (Ethane-1,2-diol/Glycol)
```
> Decolourisation of **Baeyer's reagent (KMnO₄)** = test for unsaturation.

**(b) Acidic KMnO₄ or acidic K₂Cr₂O₇ → ketones and/or carboxylic acids:**
```
(CH₃)₂C=CH₂  ——KMnO₄/H⁺——→  (CH₃)₂C=O + CO₂ + H₂O
CH₃–CH=CH–CH₃  ——KMnO₄/H⁺——→  2CH₃COOH   (But-2-ene → Ethanoic acid)
```

#### Reaction 7: Ozonolysis `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

Ozone (O₃) adds to C=C → **ozonide** → Zn/H₂O cleaves it → **aldehydes/ketones**:
```
CH₃CH=CH₂ + O₃  ——→  [ozonide]  ——Zn/H₂O——→  CH₃CHO + HCHO
                                               (Ethanal) (Methanal)

(CH₃)₂C=CH₂ + O₃  ——Zn/H₂O——→  (CH₃)₂C=O + HCHO
                                 (Propan-2-one) (Methanal)
```

> **Use of ozonolysis:** To determine the **position of double bond** in an unknown alkene. The ozonolysis products directly reveal where the C=C was (rejoin the fragments at the C=O groups).

#### Reaction 8: Polymerisation

```
n(CH₂=CH₂)  ——high T/P, catalyst——→  –(CH₂–CH₂)ₙ–   (Polythene)
n(CH₃–CH=CH₂)  ——high T/P, catalyst——→  –(CH–CH₂)ₙ–   (Polypropene)
                                           |
                                           CH₃
```

## SECTION 4 — ALKYNES `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

### 4.1 General Formula and Structure

- General formula: **CₙH₂ₙ₋₂**
- First stable member: **Ethyne (acetylene), C₂H₂**
- Used in **oxyacetylene flame** for welding

#### Structure of C≡C Triple Bond:

| Feature | Value |
|---------|-------|
| Hybridisation of C | sp |
| H–C–C bond angle | 180° (linear) |
| C≡C bond length | 120 pm |
| C≡C bond enthalpy | 823 kJ mol⁻¹ |
| Composition | 1 C–C σ (sp–sp head-on) + 2 C–C π (2p–2p lateral) |

**Structure of ethyne:**
- Each C has 2 sp orbitals: one for C–C σ bond, one for C–H σ bond
- Each C has 2 unhybridised p orbitals ⊥ to each other and to the C–C axis
- 2p orbitals of one C overlap with 2p orbitals of other C → 2 π bonds (one above-below plane; one in-out of plane)
- The electron cloud is **cylindrically symmetrical** about the internuclear axis → ethyne is a **linear** molecule

**Bond order comparison:**
```
C–C  (154 pm, 348 kJ mol⁻¹) < C=C (134 pm, 681 kJ mol⁻¹) < C≡C (120 pm, 823 kJ mol⁻¹)
Longer ←————————————————————————————→ Shorter
Weaker ←————————————————————————————→ Stronger
```

---

### 4.2 Nomenclature and Isomerism

IUPAC: Replace '-ane' of alkane with '-yne'; indicate position of triple bond.

| Structure | IUPAC Name | Common Name |
|-----------|------------|-------------|
| H–C≡C–H | Ethyne | Acetylene |
| CH₃–C≡C–H | Propyne | Methylacetylene |
| CH₃CH₂–C≡C–H | But-1-yne | Ethylacetylene |
| CH₃–C≡C–CH₃ | But-2-yne | Dimethylacetylene |
| HC≡C–CH₂–CH₂–CH₃ | Pent-1-yne | — |
| CH₃–C≡C–CH₂–CH₃ | Pent-2-yne | — |
| HC≡C–CH(CH₃)–CH₃ | 3-Methylbut-1-yne | — |

**Types of isomerism in alkynes:** Position isomerism (but-1-yne vs. but-2-yne) and chain isomerism.

---

### 4.3 Preparation of Alkynes

#### Method 1: From Calcium Carbide (Industrial)

```
CaCO₃  ——Δ——→  CaO + CO₂
CaO + 3C  ——Δ——→  CaC₂ + CO   (Calcium carbide)
CaC₂ + 2H₂O  ——→  Ca(OH)₂ + C₂H₂↑   (Ethyne / Acetylene)
```

#### Method 2: From Vicinal Dihalides (Double Dehydrohalogenation)

```
Vicinal dihalide + alc. KOH  ——→  alkenyl halide  ——NaNH₂——→  alkyne

   H  H                            H  H
   |  |                              \ /
H₂C–C–H + KOH ——alc.——→  H₂C=C   ——NaNH₂——→  HC≡CH
   |  |          –KBr              \
   Br Br                            Br        (Ethyne)
```

---

### 4.4 Physical Properties of Alkynes

- First 3 members (C₂–C₄): **gases**; next 8: liquids; higher: solids
- All are **colourless**; ethyne has a characteristic odour; others odourless
- **Weakly polar**; immiscible with water; soluble in organic solvents (ether, CCl₄, benzene)
- Melting point, boiling point, density increase with molar mass

---

### 4.5 Chemical Properties of Alkynes

#### A. Acidic Character `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

Ethyne reacts with Na metal and sodamide (NaNH₂) — reactions **NOT shown by ethene or ethane**:
```
HC≡CH + Na  ——→  HC≡C⁻Na⁺ + ½H₂   (Monosodium ethynide)
HC≡C⁻Na⁺ + Na  ——→  Na⁺C≡C⁻Na⁺ + ½H₂   (Disodium ethynide)
CH₃–C≡C–H + NaNH₂  ——→  CH₃–C≡C⁻Na⁺ + NH₃   (Sodium propynide)
```

**Why are terminal alkynes acidic?**
- C in ethyne: **sp hybridised** → 50% s-character (highest)
- More s-character → higher electronegativity of C
- More electronegative C attracts C–H bonding electrons more strongly → H released as H⁺ more easily
- Therefore: **terminal alkynes are acidic** (sp C–H > sp² C–H > sp³ C–H in acidity)

**Acidity trend:**
```
HC≡CH > H₂C=CH₂ > CH₃–CH₃      (Ethyne > Ethene > Ethane)
HC≡CH > CH₃–C≡CH >> CH₃–C≡C–CH₃ (terminal > internal)
```

> Only **terminal alkyne H** (on sp carbon) is acidic, not internal alkyne Hs.

**Distinction Test:** Ethyne reacts with Na/NaNH₂ → H₂ evolved. Ethene and ethane do NOT → used to distinguish alkynes from alkenes and alkanes.

#### B. Addition Reactions

Alkynes undergo **electrophilic addition** (like alkenes but add two molecules).

**(i) Addition of H₂ (Hydrogenation):**
```
HC≡CH + H₂  ——Pt/Pd/Ni——→  [CH₂=CH₂]  ——H₂——→  CH₃–CH₃
(Ethyne)                    (Ethene)              (Ethane)
```
- Lindlar's catalyst → partial reduction to **cis-alkene**
- Na/liq. NH₃ → partial reduction to **trans-alkene**

**(ii) Addition of Halogens:**
```
CH₃–C≡CH + Br₂  ——→  [CH₃CBr=CHBr] (1,2-Dibromopropene)
                ——Br₂——→  CH₃CBr₂–CHBr₂  (1,1,2,2-Tetrabromopropane)
```
> Decolourisation of bromine/CCl₄ → test for unsaturation.

**(iii) Addition of HX (Hydrogen Halides):**
Two molecules of HX add to give **gem dihalides** (both halogens on same C):
```
HC≡CH + HBr  ——→  [CH₂=CHBr]  ——HBr——→  CH₃–CHBr₂
                  (Vinyl bromide)          (1,1-Dibromoethane / gem-dihalide)

CH₃–C≡CH + HBr  ——→  [CH₃CBr=CH₂]  ——HBr——→  CH₃CBr₂–CH₃
                     (2-Bromopropene)           (2,2-Dibromopropane)
```
> Addition follows **Markovnikov's rule** in unsymmetrical cases.

**(iv) Addition of Water (Hg²⁺/H⁺ catalyst, 333 K):**
```
HC≡CH + H₂O  ——Hg²⁺/H⁺/333K——→  [CH₂=CH–OH]  ——isomerisation——→  CH₃CHO
                                  (Vinyl alcohol, unstable)           (Ethanal)

CH₃–C≡CH + H₂O  ——Hg²⁺/H⁺/333K——→  [CH₃C(OH)=CH₂]  ——→  CH₃COCH₃
                                     (unstable enol)          (Propanone)
```
> Alkynes form **carbonyl compounds** on hydration (vinyl alcohol → isomerises immediately to carbonyl form).

#### C. Polymerisation

**(i) Linear Polymerisation:**
```
nHC≡CH  ——suitable conditions——→  –(CH=CH–CH=CH)ₙ–   (Polyacetylene/Polyethyne)
```
Polyacetylene (thin films) can **conduct electricity** → used as electrodes in batteries.

**(ii) Cyclic Polymerisation (Trimerisation):**
```
3HC≡CH  ——red-hot Fe tube/873K——→  C₆H₆   (Benzene)
```
> **Best route from aliphatic to aromatic chemistry.**

## SECTION 5 — AROMATIC HYDROCARBONS `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

### 5.1 Introduction

- Also called **arenes** (Greek: *aroma* = pleasant smell)
- Benzene is the parent compound
- **Benzenoids**: contain benzene ring (benzene, toluene, naphthalene)
- **Non-benzenoids**: contain other unsaturated rings (e.g., cyclopentadienyl anion)
- All six hydrogen atoms in benzene are **equivalent**

---

### 5.2 Nomenclature and Isomerism

Disubstituted benzenes have three positional isomers:
- **1,2 or ortho (o–):** substituents on adjacent C
- **1,3 or meta (m–):** substituents with one C between them
- **1,4 or para (p–):** substituents across the ring

| Structure | Name |
|-----------|------|
| Benzene with –CH₃ | Methylbenzene (Toluene) |
| 1,2-Dimethylbenzene | o-Xylene |
| 1,3-Dimethylbenzene | m-Xylene |
| 1,4-Dimethylbenzene | p-Xylene |

---

### 5.3 Structure of Benzene `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

**Historical:**
- Isolated by Michael Faraday (1825)
- Molecular formula: **C₆H₆** (high degree of unsaturation)
- August Kekulé (1865) proposed: cyclic, alternating single/double bonds, one H per C

**Problem with Kekulé structure:**
1. Predicts two different 1,2-disubstituted products (but only one found)
2. Doesn't explain unusual stability and preference for **substitution over addition**

**Kekulé's solution:** Proposed **oscillating** double bonds (dynamic equilibrium between two structures).

**Modern explanation — Resonance:**
```
  Structure A ↔ Structure B = Resonance Hybrid C
  (2 Kekulé structures)       (circle in hexagon)
```
The circle in structure C represents **6 delocalised π electrons**.

**Orbital Picture:**
- All 6 C in benzene: **sp² hybridised**
- σ framework: 6 C–C bonds (sp²–sp²) + 6 C–H bonds (sp²–1s) → all in the same plane
- Each C has one unhybridised **2p orbital ⊥ to the plane**
- 2p orbitals overlap laterally with both neighbours → **complete delocalisation** of 6 π electrons over all 6 C atoms
- Forms two **doughnut (torus)-shaped** π clouds (one above, one below the ring plane)

**Evidence for delocalisation:**
- X-ray diffraction: **all C–C bonds EQUAL** (139 pm) — intermediate between C–C (154 pm) and C=C (133 pm)
- No pure single or double bond → explains reluctance for addition reactions

---

### 5.4 Aromaticity (Hückel's Rule) `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

A compound is **aromatic** if it satisfies ALL three conditions:

| Criterion | Requirement |
|----------|------------|
| 1. Planarity | The ring must be **flat (planar)** |
| 2. Delocalisation | **Complete delocalisation** of π electrons in the ring |
| 3. Hückel's Rule | **(4n + 2) π electrons** in the ring (n = 0, 1, 2, 3…) |

**Hückel's Rule values:**
| n | 4n+2 π e⁻ | Example |
|---|----------|---------|
| 0 | 2 | — |
| 1 | 6 | Benzene (C₆H₆), Cyclopentadienyl anion (C₅H₅⁻), Cycloheptatrienyl cation (C₇H₇⁺) |
| 2 | 10 | Naphthalene (C₁₀H₈) |
| 3 | 14 | Anthracene (C₁₄H₁₀), Phenanthrene (C₁₄H₁₀) |

**Anti-aromatic:** 4n π electrons (e.g., cyclobutadiene: 4 π e⁻; n=1) → very unstable.

---

### 5.5 Preparation of Benzene

**(i) Cyclic polymerisation of ethyne:**
```
3HC≡CH  ——red-hot Fe/873K——→  C₆H₆   (Benzene)
```

**(ii) Decarboxylation of sodium benzoate:**
```
C₆H₅COO⁻Na⁺ + NaOH  ——CaO/Δ——→  C₆H₆ + Na₂CO₃
(Sodium benzoate)                  (Benzene)
```

**(iii) Reduction of phenol:**
```
C₆H₅OH  ——Zn (heated)——→  C₆H₆ + ZnO
(Phenol, vapours over Zn dust)  (Benzene)
```

### 5.6 Physical Properties of Aromatic Hydrocarbons

- **Non-polar** → immiscible with water; miscible with organic solvents
- Usually **colourless liquids or solids** with characteristic aroma
- **Burn with sooty flame** (high C:H ratio → incomplete combustion)
- Naphthalene: used in toilets/clothes — moth repellent

---

### 5.7 Chemical Properties of Aromatic Hydrocarbons

Arenes are characterised by **electrophilic substitution reactions (SE)**.
Under special conditions: addition and oxidation also occur.

#### Electrophilic Substitution Reactions (SE Reactions)

**General Mechanism (3 steps):**

**(a) Generation of Electrophile (E⁺):**
Using Lewis acid (AlCl₃, FeCl₃, FeBr₃) or strong acid:
```
Cl₂ + AlCl₃  ——→  Cl⁺ + [AlCl₄]⁻   (for halogenation)
CH₃Cl + AlCl₃  ——→  CH₃⁺ + [AlCl₄]⁻  (for Friedel-Crafts alkylation)
HNO₃ + H₂SO₄  ——→  NO₂⁺ + HSO₄⁻ + H₂O  (for nitration; NO₂⁺ = nitronium ion)
```
(In nitration, H₂SO₄ acts as acid and HNO₃ as base — acid-base equilibrium)

**(b) Formation of σ-complex (Arenium ion):**
```
        E⁺ + benzene  ——→  σ-complex (arenium ion)
               [one C becomes sp³; aromaticity temporarily lost]
               [stabilised by resonance over remaining 5 C]
```

**(c) Removal of Proton (to restore aromaticity):**
```
σ-complex  ——[AlCl₄]⁻ or [HSO₄]⁻——→  substituted benzene + HCl (or H₂SO₄) + AlCl₃
```

---

#### SE Reaction 1: Nitration

```
Benzene + conc.HNO₃ + conc.H₂SO₄  ——323–333K——→  Nitrobenzene (C₆H₅NO₂) + H₂O
```
Electrophile: **NO₂⁺ (nitronium ion)**

#### SE Reaction 2: Halogenation

```
Benzene + Cl₂  ——anhy.AlCl₃/FeCl₃——→  Chlorobenzene (C₆H₅Cl) + HCl
Benzene + Br₂  ——anhy.AlBr₃/FeBr₃——→  Bromobenzene (C₆H₅Br) + HBr
```
Electrophile: **Cl⁺** (generated by Cl₂ + AlCl₃)

#### SE Reaction 3: Sulphonation

```
Benzene + H₂SO₄(fuming/oleum)  ——Δ——→  Benzenesulphonic acid (C₆H₅SO₃H) + H₂O
```
Electrophile: **SO₃** (from oleum)

#### SE Reaction 4: Friedel-Crafts Alkylation

```
Benzene + CH₃Cl  ——anhy.AlCl₃——→  Toluene (C₆H₅CH₃) + HCl
Benzene + C₂H₅Cl  ——anhy.AlCl₃——→  Ethylbenzene (C₆H₅C₂H₅) + HCl
```
Electrophile: **R⁺ (carbocation)**

> **Why isopropyl benzene forms with 1-chloropropane?** The initially formed n-propyl cation (1°) rearranges to the more stable isopropyl cation (2°) before attacking benzene.

#### SE Reaction 5: Friedel-Crafts Acylation

```
Benzene + CH₃COCl  ——anhy.AlCl₃/Δ——→  Acetophenone (C₆H₅COCH₃) + HCl
Benzene + (CH₃CO)₂O  ——anhy.AlCl₃/Δ——→  Acetophenone + CH₃COOH
```
Electrophile: **RC≡O⁺ (acylium ion)**

---

#### Addition Reactions of Benzene (under vigorous conditions)

**(i) Hydrogenation:**
```
C₆H₆ + 3H₂  ——Ni/Δ——→  C₆H₁₂   (Cyclohexane)
```

**(ii) Halogenation (UV light):**
```
C₆H₆ + 3Cl₂  ——UV/500K——→  C₆H₆Cl₆   (Benzene hexachloride, BHC / Gammaxane)
```

#### Combustion of Benzene

```
C₆H₆ + 15/2 O₂  ——→  6CO₂ + 3H₂O   (sooty flame)
```

### 5.8 Directive Influence of Substituents `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

When a monosubstituted benzene undergoes further substitution, the existing substituent **directs** the incoming group to specific positions. Two types:

#### Ortho/Para (o/p) Directors — ACTIVATING groups:

The group **increases electron density** at o- and p-positions (via resonance donation of electron pairs):

| Activating o/p Directors | Examples |
|--------------------------|---------|
| Strong | –NH₂, –NHR, –NR₂, –OH, –OCH₃, –NHCOCH₃ |
| Moderate | –CH₃, –C₂H₅ (alkyl groups; via hyperconjugation) |

**Mechanism (example: –OH group):**
Phenol resonance structures show **increased electron density at o- and p-positions** → electrophile attacks these positions.

**Special case: Halogens (–X):**
- Halogens are **moderately deactivating** (strong –I effect reduces overall ring density)
- BUT due to resonance (+R effect), electron density at **o- and p-positions > meta**
- → Halogens are **o/p directors but deactivating**

> **Key distinction:** Deactivating o/p directors exist (halogens) — the directive effect and activating/deactivating effect can be opposite!

#### Meta (m) Directors — DEACTIVATING groups:

The group **decreases electron density** at o- and p-positions more than at meta (via –I and –R effects → electron withdrawal from ring):

| Deactivating m-Directors | Examples |
|--------------------------|---------|
| –NO₂, –CN, –CHO, –COR, –COOH, –COOR, –SO₃H | All withdraw electrons from ring |

**Mechanism (example: –NO₂ group):**
Nitrobenzene resonance structures show positive charge development at **o- and p-positions** → electrophile prefers the **comparatively electron-rich meta** position.

**Summary Table:**

| Group Type | Direction | Ring Activation | Examples |
|-----------|-----------|----------------|---------|
| +R, +I | o/p | Activating | –OH, –NH₂, –OCH₃, alkyl |
| +R, –I (net +R) | o/p | Activating (halogens: deactivating) | –F, –Cl, –Br, –I |
| –R, –I | m | Deactivating | –NO₂, –CN, –CHO, –COOH, –SO₃H |

## SECTION 7 — SOLVED NCERT EXAMPLES (Key Annotated) `[Board · NEET]`

### Key Ozonolysis Problems

**Identifying alkene from ozonolysis products:**
- Ethanal (CH₃CHO) + Pentan-3-one (CH₃CH₂COCH₂CH₃) from ozonolysis of alkene 'A'
- Rejoin the C=O groups where ozonolysis cut: CH₃CH=CHC₂H₅–CH₂CH₃ — No, need to think:
  - Ethanal: CH₃–CHO → came from CH₃–CH= end
  - Pentan-3-one: C₂H₅–CO–C₂H₅ → came from =C(C₂H₅)–C₂H₅ or C₂H₅–C(=)–C₂H₅ Wait: pentan-3-one has the C=O at C3: CH₃CH₂–C(=O)–CH₂CH₃ → the C=O came from a C= end: =C(CH₂CH₃)–
  - **Answer:** CH₃–CH=C(CH₂CH₃)₂ → **Hex-2-ene** or 2-ethylbut-2-ene? Let's verify: CH₃–CH=C(C₂H₅)₂ — IUPAC: 2-ethylbut-2-ene? Parent = butane with ethyl at C2, double bond at C2? = **2-Ethylbut-2-ene** — actually this needs a 5-carbon chain → hex-2-ene possibility: CH₃CH=C(C₂H₅)₂ has 7 carbons — so it would be named based on the longest chain.

> **Strategy for ozonolysis identification:** Reconnect the C=O fragments at their carbonyl carbons. If the product is an **aldehyde (R–CHO)**, that C was a **=CH–** or **=CH₂** end; if a **ketone (R–CO–R')**, that C was a **=CR–** (disubstituted alkene carbon).

### Key Mechanism Problems

**Why is Markovnikov product major?**
- H⁺ adds to give **more stable carbocation** (2° > 1°)
- More stable carbocation → forms faster → predominates → nucleophile (X⁻) attacks → Markovnikov product

**Why does trans-but-2-ene have zero dipole moment?**
- CH₃ groups are on opposite sides → C–CH₃ bond dipoles are equal and opposite → cancel exactly → µ = 0
