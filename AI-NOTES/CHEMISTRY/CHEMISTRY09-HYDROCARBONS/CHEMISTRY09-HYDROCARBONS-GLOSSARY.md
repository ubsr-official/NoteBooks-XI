## A

**Acidic Character of Alkynes**
The property of terminal alkynes (R–C≡C–H) to donate a proton from the **sp-hybridised C–H bond**. Due to the **50% s-character** of sp orbitals, the sp-carbon has the highest electronegativity among sp, sp², sp³ carbons. This greater electronegativity causes stronger attraction of the C–H bonding pair towards carbon, making the H relatively easier to release as a proton (H⁺). Evidenced by reactions with Na metal and sodamide (NaNH₂) releasing H₂ or NH₃. Acidity order: ethyne (sp C–H) > ethene (sp² C–H) > ethane (sp³ C–H). Only terminal alkynes are acidic; internal alkynes (R–C≡C–R) have no terminal C–H on sp carbon. Related: sp hybridisation, Electronegativity, Sodamide, Terminal alkyne. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Acylium Ion (RCO⁺)**
A highly electrophilic carbocation formed when an acyl halide (R–CO–X) reacts with a Lewis acid (AlCl₃). The acylium ion has the structure R–C≡O⁺ (linear, positively charged carbon triple-bonded to oxygen). It is the electrophile responsible for Friedel-Crafts **acylation** of benzene. Unlike the carbocations in Friedel-Crafts alkylation, acylium ions do **not undergo rearrangement** because they are stabilised by resonance (R–CO⁺ ↔ R–C≡O⁺). This makes acylation a more reliable method for introducing substituents without carbon skeleton rearrangement. Related: Friedel-Crafts acylation, Lewis acid, Electrophile, Carbocation. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Addition Reaction**
A reaction in which two or more molecules combine to form a single product with **no atoms lost**. Common in compounds with C=C or C≡C bonds where the π-bond breaks and new σ-bonds form. Types: electrophilic addition (most common in alkenes/alkynes), free-radical addition (peroxide effect), nucleophilic addition (aldehydes/ketones — Ch. 12). Compare with substitution reactions (one group replaces another) and elimination reactions (loss of small molecule). Related: Electrophilic addition, Markovnikov's rule, Hydrogenation, Halogenation of alkenes. *(Board/NEET)*

**Aliphatic Hydrocarbon**
A hydrocarbon in which carbon atoms are arranged in **straight chains, branched chains, or non-aromatic rings** (including cycloalkanes). Distinguished from aromatic hydrocarbons (which contain delocalised π-systems obeying Hückel's rule). Aliphatic compounds can be saturated (alkanes, cycloalkanes) or unsaturated (alkenes, alkynes). Includes both open-chain and alicyclic (e.g., cyclohexane) structures. Related: Alkane, Alkene, Alkyne, Cycloalkane, Aromatic hydrocarbon. *(Board)*

**Alkane**
A saturated open-chain hydrocarbon containing only **C–C and C–H single bonds**, all formed by σ-bonds from sp³-hybridised carbon atoms. General formula: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂. Characterised by tetrahedral geometry (bond angle 109.5°), C–C bond length 154 pm, C–H bond length 112 pm. Earlier called **paraffins** (Latin: *parum* = little; *affinis* = affinity) due to low reactivity. Key reactions: free radical substitution (halogenation), combustion, controlled oxidation, isomerisation, aromatisation, pyrolysis. Physical states: C₁–C₄ = gases; C₅–C₁₇ = liquids; C₁₈+ = solids at 298 K. Related: Homologous series, Paraffin, Conformations, Methane. *(Board/NEET)*

**Alkene**
An unsaturated open-chain hydrocarbon containing at least **one C=C double bond**. General formula: CₙH₂ₙ (one double bond). Carbon of double bond: **sp² hybridised** (trigonal planar, 120°). The double bond = 1σ (head-on sp²–sp² overlap) + 1π (lateral 2p–2p overlap). π bond is weaker and more reactive → alkenes are susceptible to electrophilic addition. Also called **olefins** (from ethylene + Cl₂ → oily liquid). First stable member: ethene (C₂H₄). Show structural isomerism (chain/position) AND geometrical (cis-trans) isomerism due to restricted rotation about C=C. Related: Double bond, Markovnikov's rule, Electrophilic addition, cis-trans isomerism. *(Board/NEET)*

**Alkyl Group**
A univalent group derived from an alkane by removing one hydrogen atom: general formula CₙH₂ₙ₊₁. Named by replacing '-ane' with '-yl'. Examples: methyl (–CH₃), ethyl (–C₂H₅), isopropyl (–CH(CH₃)₂), tert-butyl (–C(CH₃)₃), neopentyl (–CH₂C(CH₃)₃). In Friedel-Crafts alkylation, alkyl carbocations are the electrophiles; they can undergo rearrangement to more stable forms. Alkyl groups show +I (electron-donating inductive) effect. Related: Alkane, Substituent, Friedel-Crafts alkylation, Carbocation. *(Board/NEET)*

**Alkyne**
An unsaturated hydrocarbon containing at least **one C≡C triple bond**. General formula: CₙH₂ₙ₋₂. Carbon of triple bond: **sp hybridised** (linear, 180°). Triple bond = 1σ (sp–sp head-on) + 2π (2p–2p lateral, in two perpendicular planes). Bond length (120 pm) < C=C (134 pm) < C–C (154 pm). Terminal alkynes have **acidic H** on sp carbon. First stable member: ethyne (C₂H₂, acetylene). Cannot show geometrical isomerism (linear geometry). Related: sp hybridisation, Acidic character, Lindlar's catalyst, Cyclic polymerisation. *(Board/NEET)*

**Anti-Markovnikov Addition (Peroxide Effect / Kharash Effect)**
The addition of HBr to unsymmetrical alkenes in the **presence of peroxides** (e.g., benzoyl peroxide), giving the product **opposite** to that predicted by Markovnikov's rule — the Br adds to the **less substituted** (terminal) carbon. Discovered by M.S. Kharash and F.R. Mayo (1933). Mechanism: free radical (Br• adds to terminal C forming more stable secondary radical → product at terminal C). Applies **ONLY to HBr** — HCl bond is too strong (not cleaved by radicals); H–I bond too weak (I• radicals recombine to I₂). Related: Markovnikov's rule, Free radical mechanism, Peroxide, HBr addition. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Arenium Ion (σ-complex)**
A carbocation intermediate formed during **electrophilic substitution reactions** of benzene when an electrophile (E⁺) attacks the benzene ring, bonding to one ring carbon. The attacked carbon becomes **sp³ hybridised**, losing its contribution to the aromatic π system. The positive charge is delocalised over the remaining 5 carbons through resonance. Also called **Wheland intermediate** or **σ-complex**. Less stable than benzene because aromaticity is partially disrupted. Regenerates aromaticity by losing a proton (H⁺) in the final step. Related: Electrophilic substitution, Benzene mechanism, Carbocation, σ-complex. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Arene (Aromatic Hydrocarbon)**
A hydrocarbon containing one or more **aromatic rings** — cyclic structures with delocalised π-electron systems obeying Hückel's rule. Term 'arene' comes from Greek *aroma* (pleasant smell). Benzene (C₆H₆) is the simplest arene; toluene (methylbenzene), naphthalene, biphenyl are homologues. **Benzenoid** arenes contain benzene rings; **non-benzenoid** arenes contain other aromatic ring systems. Characterised by preference for electrophilic substitution over addition. Related: Benzene, Aromaticity, Hückel's rule, Electrophilic substitution. *(Board/NEET)*

**Aromaticity**
The property of cyclic, planar, fully conjugated molecules that have **(4n+2) π electrons** (Hückel's rule, n = 0, 1, 2…) and experience **special stability** due to complete delocalisation of π electrons. Three criteria MUST ALL be satisfied: (i) planarity, (ii) complete delocalisation of π electrons in the ring, (iii) (4n+2)π electrons. The special stability of aromatic systems explains why benzene prefers substitution over addition. Anti-aromatic systems (4n π electrons) are exceptionally unstable. Related: Hückel's Rule, Benzene, Resonance, Delocalisation, Cyclopentadienyl anion. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## B

**Baeyer's Reagent**
**Cold, dilute, aqueous potassium permanganate (KMnO₄)**. A coloured (purple/violet) oxidising agent that reacts with C=C double bonds to form **vicinal diols (glycols)** via cis-dihydroxylation, causing the purple colour to disappear (decolourisation). Used as a **test for unsaturation**: decolourisation = positive test for alkene/alkyne. Named after Adolf von Baeyer. Alkanes and benzene (under normal conditions) do not decolourise. Acidic KMnO₄ is a stronger oxidant and cleaves C=C bonds to form acids/ketones. Related: Oxidation of alkenes, Vicinal diol, KMnO₄, Test for unsaturation. *(Board/NEET)*

**Benzene**
The simplest and parent **aromatic hydrocarbon**, molecular formula C₆H₆. Isolated by Michael Faraday (1825). Kekulé (1865) proposed a cyclic structure with alternating single and double bonds. Modern understanding: a planar, regular hexagon with all C atoms sp² hybridised, 6 equal C–C bonds of length 139 pm (intermediate between C–C 154 pm and C=C 134 pm), with 6 π electrons completely delocalised above and below the ring plane (two torus-shaped π clouds). Extremely stable due to resonance energy (~150 kJ mol⁻¹). Undergoes electrophilic substitution (not addition) preferentially. Colourless liquid, characteristic aroma, immiscible with water, burns with sooty flame. Related: Aromaticity, Hückel's rule, Resonance, Electrophilic substitution, Kekulé structure. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Benzenoid**
Aromatic compound that contains at least one **benzene ring** as part of its structure. Examples: benzene, toluene, aniline, benzoic acid, naphthalene (two fused benzene rings), anthracene (three fused rings). Distinguished from **non-benzenoid** aromatics (which satisfy Hückel's rule but lack benzene rings, e.g., cyclopentadienyl anion, azulene). Related: Benzene, Aromaticity, Non-benzenoid, Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon. *(Board)*

**Boiling Point and Branching**
In alkanes, **increased branching** leads to a **decrease in boiling point**. This is because branching makes the molecule more spherical → reduces the surface area available for intermolecular van der Waals (London dispersion) forces → weaker forces → easier to separate → lower BP. Example: n-pentane (309.1 K) > 2-methylbutane (300.9 K) > 2,2-dimethylpropane (282.5 K). Contrast: for chain length — longer chain → larger surface area → stronger vdW → higher BP. Related: Alkane physical properties, van der Waals forces, Isomerism. *(Board/NEET)*

## C

**Carcinogenicity**
The ability of a substance to cause **cancer (malignant tumours)** by damaging DNA or disrupting normal cell division. In the context of hydrocarbons, **polynuclear aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs)** with more than two fused benzene rings are carcinogenic. Formed by incomplete combustion of organic materials (tobacco smoke, coal tar, petroleum). Enter the body, undergo biochemical reactions (often epoxidation), and form adducts with DNA, causing mutations. Examples: 1,2-benzanthracene, 3-methylcholanthrene, 1,2-benzpyrene, 9,10-dimethyl-1,2-benzanthracene. Related: Polynuclear hydrocarbons, PAH, Toxicity, DNA damage. *(Board)*

**Chain Isomerism**
A type of **structural isomerism** in which compounds have the **same molecular formula but different carbon skeletons** (i.e., different number of branches in the carbon chain). Example: C₄H₁₀ → n-butane (straight chain: C–C–C–C) and isobutane/2-methylpropane (branched: one C branching from C2). C₅H₁₂ has three chain isomers: n-pentane, 2-methylbutane, 2,2-dimethylpropane. Related: Structural isomerism, Position isomerism, Functional group isomerism, Alkane. *(Board/NEET)*

**cis-trans Isomerism (Geometrical Isomerism)**
A form of **stereoisomerism** arising from the **restricted rotation** about a C=C double bond, when each doubly bonded carbon bears two **different substituents**. The **cis isomer** has identical (or similar) groups on the **same side** of the double bond; the **trans isomer** has them on **opposite sides**. They have the same structural formula but different spatial arrangements (configurations). They differ in physical properties: cis isomer typically has higher dipole moment and boiling point; trans isomer typically has higher melting point. Condition: each sp² carbon must have TWO DIFFERENT groups — if any sp² carbon has two identical groups, cis-trans isomerism is impossible. Related: Stereoisomerism, Restricted rotation, Alkene, Dipole moment. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Combustion of Hydrocarbons**
The reaction of hydrocarbons with O₂/air to produce CO₂ and H₂O with release of large amounts of heat. **Complete combustion** (excess O₂): CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ + [(3n+1)/2]O₂ → nCO₂ + (n+1)H₂O. **Incomplete combustion** (insufficient O₂): produces carbon black (soot), CO. Carbon black is used in inks, pigments, and filters. Combustion is the basis for alkanes as fuels (LPG, CNG, petrol). Aromatic hydrocarbons burn with sooty flames due to high C:H ratio. Related: Alkane reactions, Fuel, LPG, CNG, Carbon black. *(Board/NEET)*

**Conformational Isomers (Conformers / Rotamers)**
Different **spatial arrangements** of atoms in a molecule that can **interconvert by rotation** about a C–C single bond (σ-bond), without breaking any bonds. For ethane: infinite conformations possible; the two extreme cases are **eclipsed** (least stable, maximum torsional strain) and **staggered** (most stable, minimum torsional strain). Represented by **Sawhorse projections** (viewing along the bond at an angle) or **Newman projections** (viewing head-on along the C–C bond). Energy difference between eclipsed and staggered ethane = 12.5 kJ mol⁻¹ — too small to prevent interconversion at room temperature → conformational isomers **cannot be isolated**. Related: Rotation about C–C bond, Torsional strain, Sawhorse projection, Newman projection. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Cracking (Pyrolysis)**
The thermal decomposition of **higher hydrocarbons into smaller fragments** (lower alkanes, alkenes) by heating to high temperatures. Example: dodecane (C₁₂H₂₆) at 973 K over Pt/Pd/Ni → heptane (C₇H₁₆) + pentene (C₅H₁₀) + other products. Used industrially to convert heavy petroleum fractions into more valuable lighter fractions (petrol/gasoline). Mechanism: free radical. Also used to obtain alkenes from saturated hydrocarbons. Related: Pyrolysis, Alkane reactions, Free radical mechanism, Petroleum. *(Board/NEET)*

## D

**Decarboxylation**
The process of **removing CO₂ from a carboxylic acid or its salt** to form a hydrocarbon. In the preparation of alkanes: sodium salt of carboxylic acid + NaOH (with CaO as soda lime) → alkane with **one carbon less** + Na₂CO₃. Example: CH₃COONa + NaOH → CH₄ + Na₂CO₃. This is the basis for the **soda lime method** to prepare alkanes. CaO in soda lime prevents the flask from being attacked by NaOH melt. Similarly, sodium benzoate + soda lime → benzene. Related: Alkane preparation, Benzene preparation, Sodium salt, Soda lime. *(Board/NEET)*

**Dehalogenation**
The removal of a **halogen molecule (X₂)** from adjacent (vicinal) carbon atoms of a vicinal dihalide using **zinc metal**, forming an alkene. Example: CH₂Br–CH₂Br + Zn → CH₂=CH₂ + ZnBr₂. The zinc acts as a reducing agent, abstracting both halogens (as ZnX₂). Distinguished from **dehydrohalogenation** (which removes HX). Related: Alkene preparation, Vicinal dihalide, Zinc, Dehydrohalogenation. *(Board/NEET)*

**Dehydrohalogenation**
The elimination of a **hydrogen halide (HX)** from an alkyl halide in the presence of a **strong base (alcoholic KOH)** to form an alkene. It is a **β-elimination** reaction: the H is removed from the β-carbon (the carbon adjacent to the C–X carbon). Example: CH₃CH₂Cl + KOH(alc.) → CH₂=CH₂ + KCl + H₂O. Rate for halogens: iodine > bromine > chlorine. Rate for alkyl groups: tertiary > secondary > primary. Related: Alkene preparation, β-elimination, Saytzeff's rule, Alcoholic KOH. *(Board/NEET)*

**Dihedral Angle (Torsional Angle)**
The angle between two specified bonds (usually C–H bonds on adjacent carbons) as seen along the C–C bond axis in a **Newman projection**. When dihedral angle = 0° → eclipsed conformation (maximum torsional strain). When dihedral angle = 60° → staggered conformation (minimum torsional strain). Also called **angle of rotation**. The torsional strain in a conformation depends on the dihedral angle. Related: Conformation, Newman projection, Torsional strain, Eclipsed, Staggered. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Directive Influence of Substituents**
The ability of a **substituent already present on a benzene ring** to direct an incoming electrophile (during further electrophilic substitution) to a specific position — **ortho/para** or **meta**. The directing effect depends on whether the substituent increases electron density at ortho/para positions (+R effect: –OH, –NH₂, alkyl groups → ortho/para directors) or decreases it at o/p more than at meta (–R effect: –NO₂, –CN, –COOH → meta directors). **Halogens** are special: moderately deactivating (–I > +R overall) but **ortho/para directors** (because +R makes o/p relatively richer than meta). Related: Electrophilic substitution, Resonance effect, Inductive effect, Ortho, Meta, Para. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## E

**Eclipsed Conformation**
A conformation of ethane in which the hydrogen atoms on adjacent carbon atoms are as **close together as possible** when viewed along the C–C axis (Newman projection). The dihedral angle between front and rear C–H bonds = 0°. This arrangement has maximum **torsional strain** (maximum repulsion between electron clouds of C–H bonds), highest potential energy, and **least stability**. Contrast with staggered conformation (most stable). Energy of eclipsed form is 12.5 kJ mol⁻¹ higher than staggered. Related: Conformation, Torsional strain, Staggered conformation, Newman projection. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Electrophilic Addition Reaction**
An **addition reaction** initiated by an **electrophile** (E⁺, electron-deficient species) that attacks the electron-rich **π bond** of an alkene or alkyne. Mechanism: (1) E⁺ attacks one C of double bond, forming a carbocation at the other C; (2) nucleophile (Nu⁻) attacks the carbocation to give the final product. For unsymmetrical alkenes: the more stable carbocation forms → product determined by Markovnikov's rule. Examples: addition of HX, H₂SO₄, H₂O, X₂ to alkenes/alkynes. Related: π bond, Carbocation, Markovnikov's rule, Nucleophile. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Electrophilic Substitution Reaction (SE Reaction)**
A **substitution reaction** in which an **electrophile (E⁺)** replaces a hydrogen atom on a benzene ring. The driving force is the **restoration of aromaticity** (which is more stable than keeping the addition product). Three-step mechanism: (1) generation of E⁺ (using Lewis acid or strong acid), (2) formation of σ-complex (arenium ion, one C becomes sp³), (3) loss of H⁺ to restore aromaticity. Common examples: nitration, halogenation, sulphonation, Friedel-Crafts alkylation, Friedel-Crafts acylation. Related: Benzene, Lewis acid, σ-complex (arenium ion), Aromatic substitution. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## F

**Friedel-Crafts Acylation**
An electrophilic substitution reaction of benzene with an **acyl halide (RCOCl) or acid anhydride ((RCO)₂O)** in the presence of **anhydrous AlCl₃** to form an **aryl ketone**. Example: C₆H₆ + CH₃COCl → C₆H₅COCH₃ (acetophenone) + HCl. The electrophile is the **acylium ion (RCO⁺)**. Unlike Friedel-Crafts alkylation, **no rearrangement** occurs (acylium ion is stabilised by resonance). Related: Friedel-Crafts reaction, Acylium ion, Lewis acid, AlCl₃, Acetophenone. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Friedel-Crafts Alkylation**
An electrophilic substitution reaction of benzene with an **alkyl halide (R–X)** in the presence of **anhydrous AlCl₃** to form an **alkylbenzene**. Example: C₆H₆ + CH₃Cl → C₆H₅CH₃ (toluene) + HCl. The electrophile is the **carbocation R⁺**. Important caveat: primary alkyl carbocations can **rearrange** to more stable secondary or tertiary forms before reacting — explains why 1-chloropropane + benzene gives **isopropylbenzene** (via rearrangement of 1° propyl cation → 2° isopropyl cation). Related: Friedel-Crafts acylation, Carbocation, Lewis acid, Rearrangement, AlCl₃. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## G

**Geometrical Isomerism** — *see cis-trans isomerism*

**Gem-Dihalide**
A compound in which **two halogen atoms are attached to the same carbon atom** (from Latin *gemini* = twins). Formed when an alkyne reacts with 2 moles of HX by Markovnikov's rule. Example: HC≡CH + 2HBr → CH₃CHBr₂ (1,1-dibromoethane). Contrast with **vicinal dihalide** (halogens on adjacent carbons). Related: Alkyne addition, Markovnikov's rule, HX addition, Vicinal dihalide. *(Board/NEET)*

## H

**Halogenation**
The reaction of organic compounds with halogens (F₂, Cl₂, Br₂, I₂). In **alkanes**: substitution reaction under UV/Δ; free radical mechanism. Reactivity: F₂ > Cl₂ > Br₂ > I₂ (iodination is reversible, requires HIO₃). In **alkenes/alkynes**: addition of X₂ across the multiple bond (electrophilic addition); forms vicinal dihalide (alkene) or tetrahalide (alkyne). Used as a test for unsaturation (decolourisation of Br₂/CCl₄). In **benzene**: electrophilic substitution in presence of Lewis acid (FeCl₃, AlCl₃); addition is possible under UV (→ benzene hexachloride, BHC). Related: Free radical mechanism, Electrophilic addition, Lewis acid, Unsaturation test. *(Board/NEET)*

**Hydrocarbon**
An organic compound consisting of **only carbon and hydrogen atoms**. The simplest class of organic compounds. Sources: petroleum, natural gas, coal tar. Classified as: saturated (alkanes, cycloalkanes), unsaturated (alkenes, alkynes), and aromatic (arenes). Foundation for all petrochemicals. Hydrocarbons are primary energy sources: methane (natural gas), butane/propane (LPG), octane (petrol). Related: Alkane, Alkene, Alkyne, Arene, Petroleum, LPG, CNG. *(Board)*

**Hydrogenation**
The addition of **dihydrogen gas (H₂)** to a multiple bond (C=C or C≡C) in the presence of a metal catalyst (Pt, Pd, or Ni) to form a more saturated compound. Alkene + H₂ → alkane; alkyne + H₂ → alkene (partial) or alkane (complete). Catalyst mechanism: metal surface **adsorbs H₂** and activates the H–H bond. Pt and Pd work at room temperature; Ni requires higher temperature and pressure. Used for: hardening of vegetable oils (unsaturated → saturated), test reactions, and industrial production of alkanes. Related: Alkane preparation, Lindlar's catalyst, Catalyst, Mechanism of adsorption. *(Board/NEET)*

**Hückel's Rule**
The rule for predicting **aromaticity**, proposed by Erich Hückel (1931). A cyclic, planar, fully conjugated ring system is **aromatic** if it contains **(4n + 2) π electrons**, where n is a non-negative integer (n = 0, 1, 2, 3…). Values: n=0 → 2π; n=1 → 6π (benzene); n=2 → 10π (naphthalene); n=3 → 14π (anthracene). Systems with **4n π electrons** (n≥1) are **anti-aromatic** (very unstable). Systems that don't fulfill planarity/delocalisation criteria are **non-aromatic**. Related: Aromaticity, Benzene, Naphthalene, Cyclopentadienyl anion, Delocalisation. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## K

**Kekulé Structure**
The cyclic structure of benzene proposed by **August Kekulé in 1865** showing six carbon atoms in a hexagonal ring with alternating single and double bonds, and one hydrogen attached to each carbon. Two equivalent Kekulé structures exist (A and B), differing only in the position of the double bonds. Kekulé explained the fact that benzene forms only one type of monosubstituted product (all 6 H equivalent) by proposing **oscillating/dynamic double bonds**. The modern explanation is **resonance**: benzene is a hybrid of both Kekulé structures, with all C–C bonds equal (139 pm). Related: Benzene structure, Resonance, Aromaticity, Oscillating bonds. *(Board/NEET)*

**Kharash Effect** — *see Anti-Markovnikov Addition*

**Kolbe's Electrolytic Method**
A laboratory method for synthesising **alkanes containing an even number of carbon atoms** by **electrolysis** of an aqueous solution of sodium or potassium salt of a carboxylic acid. At the anode: carboxylate ions (RCOO⁻) are oxidised to give acyl radicals (RCOO•), which lose CO₂ to give alkyl radicals (R•), which combine to give alkane (R–R). At the cathode: water is reduced to give H₂ and OH⁻. Methane cannot be prepared by this method (CH₃COO⁻ → CH₃• + CO₂; CH₃• + CH₃• → C₂H₆, not CH₄). Related: Alkane preparation, Electrolysis, Decarboxylation, Free radical. *(Board/NEET)*

## L

**Lindlar's Catalyst**
**Palladised charcoal (Pd/C) partially deactivated with quinoline or sulphur compounds** — used for the **selective reduction of alkynes to cis-alkenes** (syn-addition of H₂). The catalyst's activity is reduced so that it stops at the alkene stage and does not further reduce the alkene to alkane. Produces **cis-alkene** exclusively. Contrast: Na in liquid ammonia reduces alkynes to **trans-alkenes**. Invented by Herbert Lindlar (1952). Related: Alkyne reduction, Hydrogenation, cis-Alkene, trans-Alkene. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**LPG (Liquefied Petroleum Gas)**
A fuel consisting primarily of **propane (C₃H₈) and butane (C₄H₁₀)** obtained from petroleum refining or natural gas. Stored as liquid under pressure; vaporises at atmospheric pressure. Used as **domestic fuel** with relatively **least pollution** among common fuels. Compared to kerosene (also domestic fuel, more pollution) and petrol/CNG (automobile fuels). Related: Alkane, Petroleum, Fuel, Natural gas. *(Board)*

## M

**Markovnikov's Rule**
A rule proposed by **Vladimir Markovnikov (1869)** to predict the regiochemistry (direction of addition) in the addition of hydrogen halides (HX) to unsymmetrical alkenes. The rule states: **the negative part (X⁻) of the addendum (HX) adds to that carbon atom which has a lesser number of hydrogen atoms** (the more substituted carbon); the positive part (H⁺) adds to the more hydrogen-bearing carbon. Modern mechanistic explanation: H⁺ adds to give the **more stable carbocation** (more substituted), which reacts with X⁻. Applicable to H₂SO₄ and H₂O addition too. Overridden by peroxide effect (anti-Markovnikov) for HBr only. Related: Electrophilic addition, Carbocation stability, Anti-Markovnikov, HX addition. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## N

**Newman Projection**
A structural representation showing the **conformation of a molecule** by viewing along the C–C bond **head-on**. The front carbon atom is represented by a **point (dot)** with 3 lines for its substituents at 120°. The rear carbon is represented by a **circle** with 3 shorter lines for its substituents at 120°. The dihedral angle between front and rear substituents is visible. Eclipsed conformation: front and rear lines aligned (dihedral angle 0°). Staggered conformation: front and rear lines alternating (dihedral angle 60°). Related: Conformation, Sawhorse projection, Dihedral angle, Eclipsed, Staggered. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Nitration**
An **electrophilic substitution reaction** of benzene (or other arenes) with a **nitrating mixture** (conc. HNO₃ + conc. H₂SO₄) at 323–333 K, introducing a **nitro group (–NO₂)** into the ring. The electrophile is the **nitronium ion (NO₂⁺)**, generated by H₂SO₄ (acid) protonating HNO₃ (base): HNO₃ + H₂SO₄ → H₂NO₃⁺ → NO₂⁺ + H₂O + HSO₄⁻. Product: nitrobenzene (C₆H₅NO₂). The nitro group is a meta-directing deactivating group. Related: Electrophilic substitution, Nitronium ion, Nitrobenzene, H₂SO₄ role. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Nitronium Ion (NO₂⁺)**
The electrophile responsible for the **nitration** of benzene. Formed by the interaction of concentrated sulphuric acid and concentrated nitric acid: HNO₃ + 2H₂SO₄ → NO₂⁺ + H₃O⁺ + 2HSO₄⁻ (more accurately: H₂SO₄ protonates HNO₃ → H₂NO₃⁺ → NO₂⁺ + H₂O). The highly electrophilic NO₂⁺ attacks the π-cloud of benzene to form the σ-complex. Related: Nitration, Electrophile, H₂SO₄, Benzene. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## O

**Olefins**
The older name for **alkenes** (coined because ethylene/ethene reacted with Cl₂ to form an oily liquid, 1,2-dichloroethane). The term "olefin" means "oil-forming". Still used in industrial/IUPAC contexts: "polyolefin" for polyethylene/polypropylene. Synonymous with alkene in common use. Related: Alkene, Ethylene, Addition reaction. *(Board)*

**Ozonolysis**
A reaction in which **ozone (O₃)** adds across a C=C double bond to form an **ozonide**, which is then cleaved with **Zn/H₂O** (reductive workup) to give **aldehydes and/or ketones**. The products directly reveal the position and substitution pattern of the original double bond — if a **terminal C=CH₂** (CH₂= end) gives **formaldehyde (HCHO)**; if an **internal C=CR₂** gives a **ketone (R₂C=O)**. Used to determine the structure of unknown alkenes. With oxidative workup (H₂O₂ instead of Zn), carboxylic acids are formed. Related: Alkene reactions, Aldehydes, Ketones, Structure determination. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## P

**Paraffins**
The older name for **saturated hydrocarbons (alkanes)**. Derived from Latin *parum* (little) and *affinis* (affinity), referring to the low chemical reactivity of alkanes — they do not react with acids, bases, or most reagents under normal conditions. Related: Alkane, Chemical inertness, Saturated hydrocarbon. *(Board)*

**Peroxide Effect** — *see Anti-Markovnikov Addition*

**Polymerisation**
The process in which a **large number of small molecules (monomers)** react to form a **large molecule (polymer)** by addition across double bonds. Alkenes undergo addition polymerisation: nCH₂=CH₂ → –(CH₂–CH₂)ₙ– (polythene). Alkynes can undergo both linear and cyclic polymerisation (3 ethyne → benzene). Industrially important polymers: polythene, polypropene, polystyrene. Polymerisation is initiated by heat, pressure, and appropriate catalysts (radical, anionic, Ziegler-Natta, etc.). Related: Alkene reactions, Monomer, Polythene, Polypropene, Ethyne polymerisation. *(Board/NEET)*

**Position Isomers**
Structural isomers with the **same carbon skeleton** and **same functional group type** but differing in the **position of the functional group or double bond**. Example in alkenes: but-1-ene (CH₂=CHCH₂CH₃) and but-2-ene (CH₃CH=CHCH₃) are position isomers (different position of C=C on same 4-C chain). In alkynes: but-1-yne and but-2-yne. Related: Structural isomerism, Chain isomerism, Functional group isomerism. *(Board/NEET)*

## S

**Sawhorse Projection**
A method of representing **conformations** by drawing the central C–C bond as a long diagonal line. The molecule is viewed from an angle along the bond. The **front carbon** is at the lower left; the **rear carbon** is at the upper right. Each carbon shows three substituents drawn at 120° to each other. Eclipsed: substituents on both ends aligned; Staggered: substituents alternating. Compare with Newman projection (head-on view). Related: Conformation, Newman projection, Eclipsed, Staggered. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Staggered Conformation**
A conformation of ethane in which the hydrogen atoms on adjacent carbons are as **far apart as possible** when viewed along the C–C axis. Dihedral angle between front and rear C–H bonds = 60°. Has **minimum torsional strain**, minimum energy, and **maximum stability**. The preferred conformation of ethane. Contrast: eclipsed conformation (least stable). Related: Conformation, Torsional strain, Newman projection, Dihedral angle. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Sulphonation**
An **electrophilic substitution reaction** of benzene with **fuming sulphuric acid (oleum, H₂S₂O₇ = H₂SO₄·SO₃)** to introduce a **sulphonic acid group (–SO₃H)** into the ring: C₆H₆ + H₂SO₄(oleum) → C₆H₅SO₃H + H₂O. The electrophile is **SO₃** (from oleum). The reaction is **reversible** (unlike nitration); sulphonic acid group can be removed by steaming with dilute acid. Sulphonic acid groups make compounds water-soluble (used in detergents, dyes, drug synthesis). Related: Electrophilic substitution, Fuming sulphuric acid, SO₃, Lewis acid. *(Board/NEET)*

## T

**Terminal Alkyne**
An alkyne in which the **triple bond is at the end of the carbon chain** (C1–C2 triple bond), leaving one triply bonded carbon attached to a hydrogen: **H–C≡C–R** or **H–C≡C–H**. Terminal alkynes are **acidic** because the sp-carbon has high electronegativity (50% s-character) and the C–H bond is relatively polar. React with Na metal and NaNH₂ to release H₂ or NH₃. Contrast: **internal alkynes** (R–C≡C–R') have no sp C–H bond → not acidic → do not react with Na or NaNH₂. Related: Acidic character, sp hybridisation, NaNH₂, Sodium acetylide. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Torsional Strain**
The **repulsive interaction between the electron clouds** of adjacent C–H (or C–C) bonds that **increases with decreasing dihedral angle** (i.e., as bonds become more eclipsed). This is the main source of energy difference between conformers of ethane. In eclipsed ethane, the electron clouds of front and rear C–H bonds are closest → maximum repulsion → maximum torsional strain → least stable conformation. In staggered ethane → minimum torsional strain. The term also applies to butane and other alkanes. Related: Conformations, Eclipsed, Staggered, Dihedral angle. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## V

**Vicinal Dihalide**
A compound in which **two halogen atoms are attached to two adjacent (neighbouring) carbon atoms** in the same molecule. The word *vicinal* means 'neighbouring' (Latin: *vicinalis*). Example: 1,2-dibromoethane (CH₂Br–CH₂Br) — both Br on C1 and C2. Formed by addition of X₂ to an alkene. Used as a starting material: treatment with Zn metal (dehalogenation) gives back the alkene. Contrast with **gem-dihalides** (both X on same carbon). Related: Alkene halogenation, Dehalogenation, Gem-dihalide. *(Board/NEET)*

## W

**Wurtz Reaction**
A method for the preparation of **higher alkanes** by treating two molecules of alkyl halide (same or different) with **sodium metal in dry ethereal solution**. The two alkyl groups join to give the alkane: 2R–X + 2Na → R–R + 2NaX. Best used for **even-carbon-number alkanes** (both R groups identical). Using two different alkyl halides (R–X and R'–X) produces a **mixture of three alkanes** (R–R, R'–R', R–R') — impractical for obtaining a pure product. Example: 2CH₃Br + 2Na → CH₃–CH₃ + 2NaBr. Related: Alkane preparation, Sodium metal, Dry ether, Even carbon alkanes. *(Board/NEET)*
