**Q82.** **[Concept Application]** Explain why the CвЂ“C bond length in benzene (139 pm) is intermediate between CвЂ“C single bond (154 pm) and C=C double bond (133 pm), and why this makes benzene resistant to addition reactions.

All six carbon atoms in benzene are spВІ-hybridised. Each contributes one unhybridised 2p orbital perpendicular to the ring plane. Due to the cyclic conjugated system, there is **equal probability** for lateral overlap of the 2p orbitals with both neighbours. This results in 6 ПЂ electrons being **completely delocalised** over all 6 carbons (forming two torus-shaped ПЂ clouds above and below the ring).

The delocalised ПЂ bond is intermediate in character between a full single bond and a full double bond вЂ” hence the bond length 139 pm is intermediate.

**Why resistant to addition:**
In a typical alkene, ПЂ electrons are localised в†’ easy target for electrophiles; addition destroys the ПЂ bond but leaves an spВі product (not special stability).

In benzene, the 6 delocalised ПЂ electrons provide **~150 kJ molвЃ»В№ of extra stability (resonance energy)**. Addition to benzene would disrupt this delocalisation, converting spВІ carbons to spВі and destroying the aromatic system в†’ **loss of resonance energy**. This makes addition energetically unfavorable.

**Substitution is preferred** because it preserves the aromatic ПЂ system (product is still aromatic в†’ retains the resonance stabilisation energy).
