## SECTION 1 — KÖSSEL-LEWIS APPROACH TO CHEMICAL BONDING `[Board · NEET]`

### 1.1 Background and Lewis Symbols

**Historical Context:** In 1916, **Kössel** and **Lewis** independently provided the first logical explanation of valence based on the inertness of noble gases.

**Lewis's Atomic Model:**

* Atom = positively charged **kernel** (nucleus + inner electrons) + outer valence shell
* Outer shell accommodates a **maximum of 8 electrons** (occupying corners of a cube around the kernel)
* 8 electrons (octet) = particularly stable arrangement
* Noble gases: all 8 corners occupied (stable) → other atoms seek this configuration

**Lewis Symbols:** Simple notations to represent **valence electrons** of an atom using dots.

| Element | Lewis Symbol | Valence electrons |
| ------- | ------------ | ----------------- |
| Li      | Li·         | 1                 |
| Be      | ·Be·       | 2                 |
| ·B·   | ·Ḃ·       | 3                 |
| C       | ·Ċ·       | 4                 |
| N       | :N·         | 5                 |
| O       | :Ȯ:         | 6                 |
| F       | :F̈:        | 7                 |
| Ne      | :Ṅe:        | 8                 |

**Significance:** Number of dots = number of valence electrons = group valence (or 8 − dots for heavier elements)

### 1.2 Kössel's Observations

Kössel drew attention to key facts:

* In the periodic table, **electronegative halogens** and **electropositive alkali metals** are separated by **noble gases**
* **Halogen atoms** gain electrons → negative ions (attain noble gas config)
* **Alkali metal atoms** lose electrons → positive ions (attain noble gas config)
* Ions are stabilized by **electrostatic attraction**

**Example — NaCl formation:**

```
Na → Na⁺ + e⁻           [Ne]3s¹ → [Ne]
Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻           [Ne]3s²3p⁵ → [Ne]3s²3p⁶ = [Ar]
Na⁺ + Cl⁻ → NaCl
```

**Example — CaF₂ formation:**

```
Ca → Ca²⁺ + 2e⁻         [Ar]4s² → [Ar]
F + e⁻ → F⁻             [He]2s²2p⁵ → [He]2s²2p⁶ = [Ne]
Ca²⁺ + 2F⁻ → CaF₂
```

> The **electrovalent bond** = bond formed by electrostatic attraction between positive and negative ions.
>
> **Electrovalence** = number of unit charges on the ion (Ca²⁺ → electrovalence = +2)

## SECTION 2 — OCTET RULE AND COVALENT BONDS `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

### 2.1 The Octet Rule

> **Octet Rule:** Atoms combine (by transfer or sharing of electrons) to achieve **8 electrons in their valence shell** — a configuration like the nearest noble gas.

* **Ionic bonding:** electron transfer (e.g., NaCl)
* **Covalent bonding:** electron sharing (e.g., Cl₂, H₂, F₂)

### 2.2 Covalent Bond (Lewis-Langmuir, 1919)

**Langmuir (1919)** refined Lewis's ideas by abandoning the stationary cube model and introducing the term  **covalent bond** .

**Formation conditions:**

* Each bond = result of sharing **one electron pair** between atoms
* Each combining atom contributes **at least one electron** to the shared pair
* Combining atoms attain **outer-shell noble gas configurations** (octet) by sharing

| Bond Type   | Electron Pairs Shared | Example           | Bond Order |
| ----------- | --------------------- | ----------------- | ---------- |
| Single bond | 1                     | Cl₂, H₂         | 1          |
| Double bond | 2                     | O₂, CO₂, C₂H₄ | 2          |
| Triple bond | 3                     | N₂, C₂H₂, CO   | 3          |

> ⚠️ **Special case:** H atoms need only 2 electrons (duplet) — attain He configuration.

### 2.3 Writing Lewis Dot Structures — Systematic Steps

**Step-by-step procedure:**

1. **Count total valence electrons:** Add valence electrons of all combining atoms
   * For anions: add one electron per negative charge
   * For cations: subtract one electron per positive charge
2. **Identify central atom:** Usually the **least electronegative** atom (except H which is always terminal)
3. **Draw skeletal structure:** Connect atoms with single bonds first
4. **Distribute remaining electrons:** First complete octets on terminal atoms, then central atom
5. **Use multiple bonds if needed:** If central atom lacks octet, form double or triple bonds using lone pairs from terminal atoms

**Example — CO (Problem 4.1):**

* C: 2s²2p² (4 valence e⁻); O: 2s²2p⁴ (6 valence e⁻); Total = 10
* Single bond → :C—Ö: → C lacks octet → form triple bond
* **:C≡O:** ✓ (both achieve octet)

**Example — NO₂⁻ (Problem 4.2):**

* N(5) + 2×O(6) + 1(negative charge) = 18 electrons
* Two resonance structures possible (see Section 5)

### 2.4 Formal Charge `[NEET · JEE]`

**Definition:** The charge assigned to an atom in a Lewis structure assuming **equal sharing** of bonding electrons.

$$
\text{Formal Charge} = [\text{Valence electrons (free atom)}] - [\text{Non-bonding electrons}] - \frac{1}{2}[\text{Bonding electrons}]
$$

**For ozone (O₃):**

```
    Atom 1 (central O): FC = 6 − 2 − ½(6) = +1
    Atom 2 (end O, double bond): FC = 6 − 4 − ½(4) = 0
    Atom 3 (end O, single bond): FC = 6 − 6 − ½(2) = −1
```

**Key uses of formal charge:**

* Select the **most stable Lewis structure** (lowest magnitude of formal charges)
* Track valence electrons in the molecule
* Understand charge distribution

> 🔑 **Rule:** The **lowest energy structure** has the **smallest formal charges** on atoms. Negative FC should preferably be on the  **more electronegative atom** .

## SECTION 3 — LIMITATIONS OF THE OCTET RULE `[Board · NEET]`

The octet rule is **not universal** — applies mainly to 2nd period elements. Three types of exceptions:

### 3.1 Incomplete Octet (Central Atom has < 8 electrons)

Occurs for elements with  **fewer than 4 valence electrons** :

| Compound | Central Atom | Electrons around central atom |
| -------- | ------------ | ----------------------------- |
| LiCl     | Li           | 2                             |
| BeH₂    | Be           | 4                             |
| BCl₃    | B            | 6                             |
| AlCl₃   | Al           | 6                             |
| BF₃     | B            | 6                             |

### 3.2 Odd-Electron Molecules

Molecules with an **odd total number of electrons** — impossible for all atoms to achieve octet:

* **NO** (nitric oxide): 11 electrons total — N has only 7
* **NO₂** (nitrogen dioxide): 17 electrons total

Structure: N̈=Ö (NO) and Ö=N-Ö· (NO₂)

### 3.3 Expanded Octet

Elements in **3rd period and beyond** have available 3d orbitals → can accommodate **more than 8 electrons** around central atom:

| Compound | Electrons around central atom |
| -------- | ----------------------------- |
| PF₅     | 10 (P)                        |
| SF₆     | 12 (S)                        |
| H₂SO₄  | 12 (S)                        |
| IF₇     | 14 (I)                        |

> **Note:** Sulphur in SCl₂ obeys octet (8 electrons). The expanded octet only applies when the extra electrons are needed for bonding.

### 3.4 Other Drawbacks

* Octet rule based on chemical inertness of noble gases, but **XeF₂, KrF₂, XeOF₂** etc. exist — noble gases CAN bond
* Does **not account for molecular shapes**
* Does **not explain relative stability** or energetics of molecules

## SECTION 4 — IONIC (ELECTROVALENT) BOND `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

### 4.1 Conditions Favouring Ionic Bond Formation

1. **Low ionization enthalpy** of the metal (easy electron loss)
2. **Highly negative electron gain enthalpy** of the non-metal (easy electron gain)
3. **High lattice enthalpy** of the resulting ionic compound (high stability)

> **Ionic bonds formed more easily between elements with low ionization enthalpy AND high negative electron gain enthalpy**

### 4.2 Nature of Ionic Compounds

* **Crystalline solids:** 3D arrangement of cations and anions held by **coulombic (electrostatic) forces**
* **Rock salt structure (NaCl):** Each Na⁺ surrounded by 6 Cl⁻ and vice versa
* Most have cations from  **metals** , anions from **non-metals**
* Exception: NH₄⁺ ion (non-metallic cation)

**NaCl energetics:**

* IE(Na→Na⁺) = +495.8 kJ mol⁻¹ (endothermic)
* ΔₑgH(Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻) = −348.7 kJ mol⁻¹ (exothermic)
* Sum = +147.1 kJ mol⁻¹ (endothermic overall)
* BUT lattice enthalpy (NaCl) = **−788 kJ mol⁻¹** → more than compensates
* Net: Exothermic → **NaCl is stable**

### 4.3 Lattice Enthalpy `[NEET · JEE]`

> **Lattice Enthalpy:** Energy required to **completely separate one mole of a solid ionic compound** into its gaseous constituent ions.

* For NaCl: lattice enthalpy = **788 kJ mol⁻¹**
* Involves both attractive forces (opposite charges) and repulsive forces (like charges)
* **Cannot be calculated directly** from simple coulombic interactions alone (crystal geometry factors involved)
* Used in Born-Haber cycles (higher classes)

> 🔑 **Key principle:** The qualitative measure of stability of an ionic compound is its  **lattice enthalpy** , NOT simply the achievement of noble gas configuration.

### 4.4 Fajans' Rules — Covalent Character in Ionic Bonds `[NEET · JEE]`

All bonds have partial character of the other type. Ionic bonds have **partial covalent character** when the cation **polarises** the anion.

**Fajans' Rules — Increased covalent character when:**

| Factor                 | Condition for More Covalent Character                                       |
| ---------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Cation size            | Smaller cation → more polarising power                                     |
| Anion size             | Larger anion → more polarisable                                            |
| Cation charge          | Higher charge on cation → more covalent character                          |
| Cation electron config | (n-1)dⁿns⁰ (transition metal) > ns²np⁶ (noble gas) for same size/charge |

**Mechanism:** Small, highly charged cation pulls electron density of the anion towards itself → electron density builds up between the two → resembles covalent bond.

## SECTION 5 — BOND PARAMETERS `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

### 5.1 Bond Length

> **Bond Length:** Equilibrium distance between nuclei of two bonded atoms in a molecule.

* Measured by:  **spectroscopic** ,  **X-ray diffraction** , **electron diffraction** methods
* For covalent bond: bond length ≈ sum of **covalent radii** of the two atoms (R = rₐ + r_B)
* **van der Waals radius** > covalent radius (non-bonded atoms are farther apart)

**Key bond length data (pm):**

| Bond   | Length (pm) | Bond  | Length (pm) |
| ------ | ----------- | ----- | ----------- |
| H−H   | 74          | N≡N  | 109         |
| F−F   | 144         | O=O   | 121         |
| Cl−Cl | 199         | H−F  | 92          |
| C−C   | 154         | H−Cl | 127         |
| C=C    | 133         | C−H  | 107         |
| C≡C   | 120         | C−O  | 143         |
| C=O    | 121         | N−O  | 136         |
| C≡N   | 116         | O−H  | 96          |

**General trend:** Triple < Double < Single (shorter bond = stronger bond)

### 5.2 Bond Angle

> **Bond Angle:** Angle between the orbitals containing bonding electron pairs around the central atom in a molecule/complex ion.

* Measured by spectroscopic methods
* Gives information about **shape** of the molecule
* Example: H−O−H = 104.5°, H−N−H = 107°

### 5.3 Bond Enthalpy `[Board · NEET]`

> **Bond Enthalpy:** Amount of energy required to break **one mole of bonds of a particular type** between two atoms in the gaseous state.

* Units: kJ mol⁻¹
* **Larger bond enthalpy = stronger bond**

**Examples:**

```
H₂(g) → H(g) + H(g);        ΔₐH = 435.8 kJ mol⁻¹
O₂(g) → O(g) + O(g);        ΔₐH = 498.0 kJ mol⁻¹  (O=O double)
N₂(g) → N(g) + N(g);        ΔₐH = 946.0 kJ mol⁻¹  (N≡N triple) ← among highest!
HCl(g) → H(g) + Cl(g);      ΔₐH = 431.0 kJ mol⁻¹
```

**Polyatomic molecules — Mean Bond Enthalpy:**
In H₂O:

* First O−H break: ΔₐH₁ = 502 kJ mol⁻¹
* Second O−H break: ΔₐH₂ = 427 kJ mol⁻¹  (environment changed!)
* **Average bond enthalpy** = (502 + 427)/2 = **464.5 kJ mol⁻¹**

> ⚠️ **Distinction:** Bond dissociation enthalpy (for specific bond in specific molecule) vs. Mean bond enthalpy (average across different molecules)

### 5.4 Bond Order `[Board · NEET]`

> **Bond Order:** Number of bonds (electron pairs) shared between two atoms in a molecule.

| Bond Order | Type                   | Example           |
| ---------- | ---------------------- | ----------------- |
| 1          | Single bond            | H₂, F₂, C₂H₆  |
| 2          | Double bond            | O₂, CO₂, C₂H₄ |
| 3          | Triple bond            | N₂, CO, C₂H₂   |
| 1.5        | Fractional (resonance) | O₃, CO₃²⁻     |
| 1.33       | Fractional (resonance) | NO₃⁻            |

**Isoelectronic species have identical bond orders:**

* F₂ and O₂²⁻ → bond order = 1
* N₂, CO, NO⁺ → bond order = 3

**Correlation (Very Important!):**

> **↑ Bond order → ↑ Bond enthalpy → ↓ Bond length**

### 5.5 Resonance Structures `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

> **Resonance:** When a single Lewis structure cannot accurately describe a molecule, multiple Lewis structures (**canonical forms** or  **resonance structures** ) with similar energy, same nuclear positions, and same number of bonding/non-bonding pairs are drawn. Their **weighted average** = **resonance hybrid** = actual structure.

**Resonance in O₃ (Ozone):**

* Two canonical structures (I and II): one O−O single, one O=O double
* Normal O−O = 148 pm; O=O = 121 pm
* Experimental O₃ bond lengths: **128 pm** (intermediate — equal!)
* Resonance hybrid (III) correctly predicts equal bond lengths

**Important points about resonance:**

* Canonical forms have **NO real existence**
* Molecule does NOT switch between canonical forms
* There is NO equilibrium between canonical forms (≠ tautomerism)
* The molecule has **one actual structure** = the resonance hybrid
* **Resonance stabilises the molecule** (hybrid energy < any single canonical form)
* **Resonance averages bond characteristics** across the molecule

**Other examples:**

* CO₃²⁻: 3 canonical forms; C−O bond order = 1.33 (between 1 and 2)
* CO₂: 3 canonical forms (including two structures with C≡O triple bonds)
* NO₃⁻: 3 canonical forms; N−O bond order = 1.33
* SO₃: 3 canonical forms; S−O bond order = 1.33
* NO₂: 2 canonical forms

## SECTION 6 — POLARITY OF BONDS AND DIPOLE MOMENT `[Board · NEET]`

### 6.1 Polar vs Non-polar Covalent Bonds

| Bond Type          | When formed                                                | Electron distribution                              | Example        |
| ------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- | -------------- |
| Non-polar covalent | Between identical atoms                                    | Exactly equal (electron pair at midpoint)          | H₂, Cl₂, N₂ |
| Polar covalent     | Between different atoms with different electronegativities | Unequal (shifted toward more electronegative atom) | HF, HCl, H₂O  |

**In HF:** F more electronegative → shared pair shifts toward F → H becomes δ+ and F becomes δ−
→ Resultant = **polar covalent bond**

### 6.2 Dipole Moment (µ)

> **Dipole moment:** Product of the magnitude of charge and the distance between centres of positive and negative charge.

$$
\mu = Q \times r
$$

* **Units:** Debye (D); 1 D = 3.33564 × 10⁻³⁰ C m
* **Vector quantity:** Points from positive to negative centre (convention in chemistry: crossed arrow with  **cross on positive end, arrowhead on negative end** )
* Peter Debye (Dutch, Nobel 1936) — unit named in his honour

**Dipole moments of important molecules:**

| Molecule | µ (D) | Geometry        | Reason                                |
| -------- | ------ | --------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| H₂      | 0      | Linear          | Non-polar bond (identical atoms)      |
| HF       | 1.78   | Linear          | Highly polar                          |
| HCl      | 1.07   | Linear          | Polar                                 |
| H₂O     | 1.85   | Bent            | Bond dipoles reinforce                |
| H₂S     | 0.95   | Bent            | Less polar bonds                      |
| NH₃     | 1.47   | Trig. pyramidal | Lone pair + bond dipoles              |
| NF₃     | 0.23   | Trig. pyramidal | Lone pair opposes bond dipoles        |
| BF₃     | 0      | Trig. planar    | Symmetrical — bond dipoles cancel    |
| CO₂     | 0      | Linear          | Bond dipoles cancel (opposite, equal) |
| BeF₂    | 0      | Linear          | Bond dipoles cancel                   |
| CH₄     | 0      | Tetrahedral     | All C−H dipoles cancel by symmetry   |
| CCl₄    | 0      | Tetrahedral     | All C−Cl dipoles cancel              |
| CHCl₃   | 1.04   | Tetrahedral     | Asymmetric — dipoles don't cancel    |

### 6.3 NH₃ vs NF₃ — Classic Comparison `[NEET · JEE]`

Both pyramidal with lone pair on N. But µ(NH₃) = 4.90 × 10⁻³⁰ C m > µ(NF₃) = 0.8 × 10⁻³⁰ C m

**Reason:**

* **In NH₃:** Lone pair orbital dipole acts in the **same direction** as the resultant N−H bond dipoles (both point away from N toward H) → **additive** → large net µ
* **In NF₃:** Lone pair orbital dipole acts **opposite** to the resultant N−F bond dipoles (F is more electronegative, bond dipoles point toward F, but lone pair points the other way) → **subtractive** → small net µ

> **Counterintuitive:** Even though F is more electronegative than H, NF₃ has a smaller dipole moment than NH₃ due to the opposing direction of lone pair dipole.

## SECTION 7 — VSEPR THEORY `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

### 7.1 Introduction

* Proposed by  **Sidgwick and Powell (1940)** , refined by **Nyholm and Gillespie (1957)**
* Explains and predicts **molecular shapes** (Lewis concept cannot)
* Does NOT explain bond formation energetics

### 7.2 Postulates of VSEPR Theory

1. Shape of molecule depends on **number of valence shell electron pairs** (bonded + non-bonded) around central atom
2. Electron pair clouds **repel each other** (negatively charged)
3. Electron pairs arrange themselves to **minimise repulsion** (maximise distance between them)
4. Valence shell = **sphere** with electron pairs on spherical surface at maximum mutual distance
5. **Multiple bond treated as single super pair** (but with higher repulsion)
6. If multiple resonance structures exist, VSEPR applies to any one

### 7.3 Key Repulsion Order

$$
\text{lp−lp} > \text{lp−bp} > \text{bp−bp}
$$

**Why?** Lone pairs are localised entirely on the central atom (larger electron cloud) → greater repulsion than bond pairs (shared between two atoms, more spread out)

**Effect:** Lone pairs **compress the bond angles** below the ideal geometrical angles.

### 7.4 Molecular Geometries — No Lone Pairs

| Electron pairs | Geometry             | Bond angles | Examples             |
| -------------- | -------------------- | ----------- | -------------------- |
| 2              | Linear               | 180°       | BeCl₂, HgCl₂, CO₂ |
| 3              | Trigonal planar      | 120°       | BF₃, BCl₃, AlCl₃  |
| 4              | Tetrahedral          | 109.5°     | CH₄, CCl₄, NH₄⁺  |
| 5              | Trigonal bipyramidal | 90°, 120° | PCl₅, PF₅          |
| 6              | Octahedral           | 90°        | SF₆, [CrF₆]³⁻    |

### 7.5 Molecular Geometries — With Lone Pairs

| Molecule type | Bonding pairs | Lone pairs | Shape              | Bond angle   | Example               |
| ------------- | ------------- | ---------- | ------------------ | ------------ | --------------------- |
| AB₂E         | 2             | 1          | Bent/V-shape       | 119.5°      | SO₂ (or SO₃⁻ type) |
| AB₃E         | 3             | 1          | Trigonal pyramidal | 107°        | NH₃, PH₃            |
| AB₂E₂       | 2             | 2          | Bent/Angular       | 104.5°      | H₂O, H₂S            |
| AB₄E         | 4             | 1          | See-saw            | ~90°,~120° | SF₄                  |
| AB₃E₂       | 3             | 2          | T-shape            | ~90°        | ClF₃                 |
| AB₅E         | 5             | 1          | Square pyramidal   | ~90°        | BrF₅                 |
| AB₄E₂       | 4             | 2          | Square planar      | 90°         | XeF₄                 |

### 7.6 Important Explanations

**NH₃ (107° instead of 109.5°):**

* 4 electron pairs (sp³ arrangement) → would be tetrahedral
* But 1 lone pair → lp−bp repulsion > bp−bp → compresses bond angle from 109.5° → **107°**
* Shape = **trigonal pyramidal**

**H₂O (104.5° instead of 109.5°):**

* 4 electron pairs (sp³) → tetrahedral arrangement
* **2 lone pairs** → lp−lp and lp−bp repulsions > bp−bp → further compression
* Bond angle =  **104.5°** ; shape = **bent (angular)**

**Bond angle order:** CH₄ (109.5°) > NH₃ (107°) > H₂O (104.5°)

* Each successive lone pair reduces bond angle further

**SF₄ (see-saw shape):** Lone pair preferred at **equatorial** position (only 2 lp−bp repulsions at 90°) rather than axial (3 lp−bp repulsions at 90°)

> ⚠️ **Limitation of VSEPR:** Can predict geometry accurately but provides **no theoretical basis** for why electron pairs repel — this remains a subject of discussion.

## SECTION 8 — VALENCE BOND (VB) THEORY `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

### 8.1 Introduction

* Introduced by  **Heitler and London (1927)** , developed by **Pauling**
* Explains **energetics** and **directional properties** of covalent bonds (not explained by Lewis/VSEPR)
* Based on: atomic orbitals, electronic configurations, orbital overlap, hybridisation

### 8.2 Formation of H₂ — Energy Curve

As two H atoms approach each other:

* New **attractive forces** arise: Nₐ−eB and NB−eₐ (cross-attractions)
* New **repulsive forces** arise: eₐ−eB (electron-electron) and Nₐ−NB (nuclear)
* Experimentally: attractive > repulsive → potential energy decreases
* At  **equilibrium distance (74 pm)** : minimum energy — **bond formed**
* Energy released = **bond enthalpy = 435.8 kJ mol⁻¹** (for H₂)
* Attempting to bring atoms closer → energy rises sharply (destabilisation)

### 8.3 Orbital Overlap Concept

> **Orbital Overlap:** Partial interpenetration (merging) of atomic orbitals during bond formation. Greater overlap → stronger bond.

* Covalent bond = pairing of electrons in the valence shell with **opposite spins**
* **Positive overlap:** Same sign (phase) and correct orientation → bond forms ✓
* **Negative overlap:** Opposite sign → bond does not form ✗
* **Zero overlap:** Perpendicular orientation → no bonding ✗

### 8.4 Types of Covalent Bonds

#### (i) Sigma (σ) Bond — Head-on (Axial) Overlap

* Formed by **end-to-end overlap** along the **internuclear axis**
* Strong bond (large extent of overlap)
* **Three types of sigma bonds:**

| Overlap type | Description                                   | Example             |
| ------------ | --------------------------------------------- | ------------------- |
| s−s         | Two s orbitals overlap head-on                | H₂ (1s−1s)        |
| s−p         | Half-filled s of one + half-filled p of other | HCl (H 1s + Cl 3p)  |
| p−p         | Two p orbitals overlap head-on along axis     | Cl₂ (3p−3p axial) |

#### (ii) Pi (π) Bond — Sideways (Lateral) Overlap

* Formed by **lateral overlap** of p orbitals with **axes parallel** to each other and **perpendicular to internuclear axis**
* Two saucer-shaped electron clouds **above and below** the internuclear axis (plane)
* **Weaker** than sigma bond (less overlap)
* In multiple bonds: first bond = σ, additional bonds = π

> **Important:** The first bond between two atoms is always  **σ** . In double bonds: 1σ + 1π. In triple bonds: 1σ + 2π.

### 8.5 Inability of Simple Overlap to Explain Molecular Shapes

For carbon in ground state ([He]2s²2p²):

* Excited state: [He]2s¹2pₓ¹2py¹2pz¹
* Three p-orbitals at 90° to each other → predicted HCH = **90°**
* Actual HCH in CH₄ = **109.5°** → contradiction!
* Similarly: NH₃ (predicted 90°, actual 107°) and H₂O (predicted 90°, actual 104.5°)
* **Conclusion:** Simple orbital overlap alone cannot explain actual molecular geometries → need **hybridisation**

## SECTION 9 — HYBRIDISATION `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

### 9.1 Introduction and Salient Features

> **Hybridisation:** The process of intermixing of orbitals of **slightly different energies** to form a new set of equivalent orbitals ( **hybrid orbitals** ) of equal energies and shape.

**Salient features:**

1. Number of hybrid orbitals = **number of atomic orbitals mixed**
2. Hybrid orbitals are always **equivalent in energy and shape**
3. Hybrid orbitals are more effective (form  **stronger bonds** ) than pure atomic orbitals
4. Hybrid orbitals are **directed in space** to minimise repulsion → determines molecular geometry

**Conditions for hybridisation:**

* Orbitals in the **valence shell** of the atom
* Orbitals of **almost equal energy**
* Promotion of electrons is **NOT essential** prior to hybridisation
* Even **filled orbitals** can participate in hybridisation (e.g., lone pairs in sp³ of NH₃)

### 9.2 sp Hybridisation — Linear Geometry

**Orbitals mixed:** 1 s + 1 p → **2 sp hybrid orbitals**

* Each sp orbital: **50% s character + 50% p character**
* Two sp hybrids oriented at **180°** to each other (opposite directions along z-axis)
* Remaining 2 p orbitals (pₓ and py) unhybridised → available for π bonds
* Also called **diagonal hybridisation**

**Example — BeCl₂:**

* Be ground state: 1s²2s² (no unpaired electrons)
* Excited state: 1s²2s¹2p¹ (one electron promoted)
* 2s + 2p → 2 sp hybrids → oriented at 180°
* Each sp hybrid overlaps axially with Cl 3p → 2 Be−Cl sigma bonds
* **Geometry: Linear, 180°**

**Other sp examples:** C₂H₂ (ethyne/acetylene), CO₂ (conceptually), HCN

### 9.3 sp² Hybridisation — Trigonal Planar Geometry

**Orbitals mixed:** 1 s + 2 p → **3 sp² hybrid orbitals**

* Each sp² orbital: **33% s + 67% p character**
* Three sp² hybrids in a **trigonal planar arrangement at 120°**
* Remaining 1 p orbital (pz) unhybridised → available for 1 π bond
* Also called **trigonal hybridisation**

**Example — BCl₃:**

* B ground state: 1s²2s²2p¹
* Excited state: 1s²2s¹2p¹2p¹ (3 unpaired electrons)
* 2s + 2pₓ + 2py → 3 sp² hybrids → overlap with Cl 3p
* **Geometry: Trigonal planar, ClBCl = 120°**

**Example — C₂H₄ (ethene):**

* Each C: sp² hybridised
* 3 sp² orbitals: 1 forms C−C sigma (sp²−sp²), 2 form C−H sigma bonds (sp²−s)
* Unhybridised 2p orbital on each C → sideways overlap → **1 π bond**
* C−C bond = 1σ + 1π = **double bond**
* C−C bond length = 134 pm; HCH = 117.6°, HCC = 121°

### 9.4 sp³ Hybridisation — Tetrahedral Geometry

**Orbitals mixed:** 1 s + 3 p → **4 sp³ hybrid orbitals**

* Each sp³ orbital: **25% s + 75% p character**
* Four sp³ hybrids directed towards **four corners of a tetrahedron** at **109.5°**

**Example — CH₄ (methane):**

* C excited state: 2s¹2pₓ¹2py¹2pz¹
* 4 sp³ hybrids → 4 C−H sigma bonds
* **Geometry: Tetrahedral, 109.5°**

**Example — NH₃:**

* N: 2s²2pₓ¹2py¹2pz¹ (sp³ hybridised in molecule)
* **3 bonding sp³ + 1 lone pair sp³**
* Lone pair takes more space → lp−bp repulsion → compresses angle to **107°**
* **Geometry: Trigonal pyramidal**

**Example — H₂O:**

* O: sp³ hybridised
* **2 bonding sp³ + 2 lone pair sp³**
* 2 lone pairs → greater compression → angle = **104.5°**
* **Geometry: Bent (V-shaped)**

**Example — C₂H₆ (ethane):**

* Each C: sp³
* C−C: sp³−sp³ sigma bond (bond length = 154 pm)
* C−H: sp³−s sigma bonds (bond length = 109 pm)

**Example — C₂H₂ (ethyne):**

* Each C: sp hybridised (1s + 1p → 2 sp hybrids; 2px and 2py unhybridised)
* C−C: sp−sp sigma bond
* C−H: sp−s sigma bonds
* 2pₓ and 2py of each C → sideways overlap → **2 π bonds**
* Triple bond = 1σ + 2π; C−C bond length = 120 pm

### 9.5 Hybridisation Involving d Orbitals

Available for **3rd period and beyond** (3d orbitals comparable energy to 3s, 3p):

| Hybridisation | Orbitals mixed | No. of hybrids | Geometry             | Example                      |
| ------------- | -------------- | -------------- | -------------------- | ---------------------------- |
| sp³d         | s + 3p + d     | 5              | Trigonal bipyramidal | PCl₅, PF₅                  |
| sp³d²       | s + 3p + 2d    | 6              | Octahedral           | SF₆, [CrF₆]³⁻            |
| dsp²         | d + s + 2p     | 4              | Square planar        | [Ni(CN)₄]²⁻, [PtCl₄]²⁻ |
| sp³d²       | s + 3p + 2d    | 6              | Octahedral           | [Co(NH₃)₆]³⁺ (d²sp³)   |

**PCl₅ (sp³d):**

* P: ground state 3s²3p³; excited state: 3s¹3p³3d¹ (5 unpaired electrons)
* sp³d hybridisation → 5 orbitals → trigonal bipyramidal geometry
* 3 equatorial P−Cl bonds (120° to each other, in plane)
* 2 axial P−Cl bonds (90° to equatorial plane)
* **Axial bonds > equatorial bonds in length** (axial bond pairs have greater repulsion from 3 equatorial bond pairs)

**SF₆ (sp³d²):**

* S: 3s²3p⁴; excited: 3s¹3p³3d²
* sp³d² hybridisation → 6 orbitals → **regular octahedral geometry**
* All S−F bonds equivalent (90° to each other)

## SECTION 10 — MOLECULAR ORBITAL (MO) THEORY `[Board · NEET · JEE]`

### 10.1 Introduction

* Developed by **F. Hund and R.S. Mulliken (1932)**
* In MO theory: electrons in a molecule are in **molecular orbitals** spread over the entire molecule (not localized on individual atoms like in VB theory)

### 10.2 Salient Features

1. Electrons of a molecule exist in **molecular orbitals** (MOs)
2. MOs formed from **combination of atomic orbitals** (LCAO method)
3. Electron in an  **atomic orbital** : influenced by 1 nucleus; in  **MO** : influenced by 2 or more nuclei
4. Atomic orbital =  **monocentric** ; Molecular orbital = **polycentric**
5. Number of MOs formed = **number of atomic orbitals combined**
6. Two AOs → 2 MOs: one **bonding MO** (lower energy) + one **antibonding MO** (higher energy)
7. MOs filled according to **Aufbau principle, Pauli exclusion principle, Hund's rule**

### 10.3 Formation of MOs — LCAO Method

For homonuclear diatomic molecule (e.g., H₂):

* Two 1s orbitals: ψₐ and ψB
* **Addition** → bonding MO: σ = ψₐ + ψB (constructive interference → electron density between nuclei  **increases** )
* **Subtraction** → antibonding MO: σ* = ψₐ − ψB (destructive interference → **nodal plane** between nuclei, electron density = 0)

**Key energy relationships:**

* σ (bonding): energy **lower** than parent AOs
* σ* (antibonding): energy **higher** than parent AOs
* Total energy of two MOs = total energy of two original AOs

**Conditions for LCAO:**

1. **Same or nearly equal energy** of combining AOs (1s + 1s ✓; 1s + 2s ✗)
2. **Same symmetry** about molecular axis (e.g., 2pz + 2pz ✓; 2pz + 2pₓ ✗)
3. **Maximum overlap** between combining AOs

### 10.4 Types of Molecular Orbitals

| MO designation  | Formed from            | Symmetry                                |
| --------------- | ---------------------- | --------------------------------------- |
| σ1s, σ*1s     | 1s + 1s                | Cylindrically symmetric about bond axis |
| σ2s, σ*2s     | 2s + 2s                | Cylindrically symmetric                 |
| σ2pz, σ*2pz   | 2pz + 2pz (head-on)    | Cylindrically symmetric                 |
| π2pₓ, π*2pₓ | 2pₓ + 2pₓ (sideways) | NOT symmetric (lobes above/below axis)  |
| π2py, π*2py   | 2py + 2py (sideways)   | NOT symmetric                           |

**σ MOs:** cylindrically symmetric around bond axis
**π MOs:** NOT symmetric — positive lobes above, negative below → nodal plane along internuclear axis

### 10.5 Energy Level Order of MOs

**For O₂ and F₂ (and beyond):**

```
σ1s < σ*1s < σ2s < σ*2s < σ2pz < (π2pₓ = π2py) < (π*2pₓ = π*2py) < σ*2pz
```

**For B₂, C₂, N₂ (lighter molecules — 2s-2p mixing):**

```
σ1s < σ*1s < σ2s < σ*2s < (π2pₓ = π2py) < σ2pz < (π*2pₓ = π*2py) < σ*2pz
```

> **Key difference:** For B₂, C₂, N₂ — σ2pz is **higher** in energy than π2p orbitals (due to 2s-2p interaction). For O₂, F₂ — σ2pz is **lower** than π2p.

### 10.6 Bond Order (MO Theory) `[NEET · JEE]`

$$
\text{Bond Order (b.o.)} = \frac{1}{2}(N_b - N_a)
$$

where Nb = electrons in bonding MOs, Na = electrons in antibonding MOs

**Rules:**

* b.o. > 0 → **stable** molecule (Nb > Na)
* b.o. = 0 → **unstable** molecule (e.g., He₂)
* b.o. < 0 → **unstable** (does not exist)
* **Higher b.o. → shorter, stronger bond**

### 10.7 Electronic Configurations of Key Molecules

**H₂:** (σ1s)²

* b.o. = (2−0)/2 = **1** (single bond)
* Diamagnetic (no unpaired electrons)
* Bond energy = 438 kJ mol⁻¹, bond length = 74 pm

**He₂:** (σ1s)²(σ*1s)²

* b.o. = (2−2)/2 = **0** → **does not exist** ✓

**Li₂:** KK(σ2s)²

* b.o. = (4−2)/2 = **1** (exists in vapour phase)
* Diamagnetic

**C₂:** KK(σ2s)²(σ*2s)²(π2pₓ²=π2py²)

* b.o. = (8−4)/2 = **2** (double bond)
* Diamagnetic
* **Special:** Both bonds are π bonds (unlike most double bonds with 1σ+1π)

**N₂:** KK(σ2s)²(σ*2s)²(π2pₓ²=π2py²)(σ2pz)²

* b.o. = (10−4)/2 = **3** (triple bond)
* Diamagnetic; bond energy = 946 kJ mol⁻¹ (very strong!)

**O₂:** KK(σ2s)²(σ*2s)²(σ2pz)²(π2pₓ²=π2py²)(π*2pₓ¹=π*2py¹)

* b.o. = (10−6)/2 = **2** (double bond)
* **Paramagnetic** (2 unpaired electrons in π*2p MOs) — MO theory correctly predicts this!
* Experimental proof of O₂'s paramagnetism validates MO theory

**F₂:** KK(σ2s)²(σ*2s)²(σ2pz)²(π2pₓ²=π2py²)(π*2pₓ²=π*2py²)

* b.o. = (10−8)/2 =  **1** ; Diamagnetic

**Ne₂:** b.o. = (10−10)/2 = **0** → does not exist

### 10.8 Molecular Properties from MO Theory

| Property     | MO Basis                                             |
| ------------ | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| Bond order   | b.o. = ½(Nb − Na); higher = shorter, stronger bond |
| Stability    | Stable if Nb > Na (b.o. > 0)                         |
| Diamagnetic  | All MOs doubly occupied (no unpaired electrons)      |
| Paramagnetic | One or more singly occupied MOs                      |
| Bond length  | Inversely related to bond order                      |

> ⚠️ **VB theory vs MO theory:** VB explains bonding using localised orbital overlap; MO theory uses delocalised molecular orbitals. MO theory **correctly explains paramagnetism of O₂** — VB theory fails here.

## SECTION 11 — HYDROGEN BONDING `[Board · NEET]`

### 11.1 Introduction

> **Hydrogen Bond:** The attractive force which binds a **hydrogen atom of one molecule** with the **electronegative atom (F, O, or N) of another molecule** (or the same molecule). It is **weaker than covalent bonds** but stronger than van der Waals forces.

Represented by: **dotted line (- - -)** (solid line = covalent bond)

Example in HF:

```
δ+H−Fδ⁻ - - - δ+H−Fδ⁻ - - - δ+H−Fδ⁻
```

### 11.2 Cause of Formation

* When H is bonded to a **highly electronegative element X** (F, O, N): shared electrons shift far toward X
* H becomes **highly electropositive** (δ+); X becomes **δ−**
* This δ+ hydrogen atom is attracted to the δ− electronegative atom of **another molecule** (or same molecule)
* The H-bond magnitude: maximum in  **solid state** , minimum in **gaseous state**

**Why only F, O, N?**

* H-bond requires very high electronegativity + very small size (to concentrate δ− charge)
* F, O, N satisfy both conditions
* Cl is electronegative but too large → H−Cl hydrogen bond is very weak

### 11.3 Types of Hydrogen Bonds

#### (i) Intermolecular H-bond

* Between **two different molecules** of the same or different compounds
* Examples: HF, H₂O, alcohols, carboxylic acids, DNA base pairs
* Effect:  **Higher boiling/melting points** , association of molecules

**Water:** Each H₂O molecule forms up to 4 H-bonds (2 as donor, 2 as acceptor) → highly associated → high bp (100°C)

#### (ii) Intramolecular H-bond

* Within the **same molecule** — between H of one group and electronegative atom of another group in the same molecule
* Requires 5- or 6-membered ring formation
* Example: **o-nitrophenol** (H bonded between O−H and −NO₂ oxygen in ortho position)
* Also: o-chlorophenol, 2-hydroxybenzaldehyde

**Consequence of intramolecular H-bond:** Molecule becomes **cyclic** → **lowers melting/boiling point** relative to isomers with intermolecular H-bonds (e.g., o-nitrophenol has lower bp than p-nitrophenol)
