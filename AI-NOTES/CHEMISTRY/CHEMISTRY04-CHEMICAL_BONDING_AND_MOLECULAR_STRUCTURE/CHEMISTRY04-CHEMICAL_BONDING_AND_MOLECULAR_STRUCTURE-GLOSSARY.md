### Chemical Bonding and Molecular Structure

## A

* *Antibonding Molecular Orbital (σ* , π* )**
  A molecular orbital formed by the **subtraction** of atomic wave functions in the LCAO method: σ* = ψₐ − ψB. It has energy **higher** than the parent atomic orbitals. The electron density in this orbital is concentrated  **away from the internuclear region** , with a **nodal plane** of zero electron density between the nuclei. Electrons in antibonding orbitals **destabilise the molecule** by increasing nuclear repulsion. Denoted by an asterisk (*). The σ* orbital is cylindrically symmetric; π* orbital has two lobes with a nodal plane along the bond axis. Related: Bonding molecular orbital, MO theory, Bond order, LCAO. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Axial Bond**
In trigonal bipyramidal geometry (e.g., PCl₅), the two bonds that are **perpendicular to the equatorial plane** (above and below). Axial bonds make **90° angles** with the three equatorial bonds. Axial bonds are **slightly longer and weaker** than equatorial bonds because each axial bond pair experiences repulsion from three equatorial bond pairs at 90° (maximum repulsion angle), whereas each equatorial bond pair experiences repulsion from only two equatorial neighbours at 120° and two axial pairs at 90°. Related: Equatorial bond, sp³d hybridisation, PCl₅, VSEPR. *(NEET/JEE)*

## B

**Bond Angle**
The angle between **two adjacent bonds** (the orbitals containing bonding electron pairs) at the central atom in a molecule or complex ion. Measured in degrees. Bond angles are experimentally determined by  **spectroscopic methods** . They reveal the geometry of electron pair arrangement around the central atom. Key values: H−O−H in H₂O = 104.5°; H−N−H in NH₃ = 107°; H−C−H in CH₄ = 109.5°; Cl−B−Cl in BCl₃ = 120°; Cl−Be−Cl in BeCl₂ = 180°. Related: Molecular geometry, VSEPR, Hybridisation, Lone pair. *(Board/NEET)*

**Bond Enthalpy (Bond Dissociation Enthalpy)**
The energy required to **break one mole of a specific bond** between two atoms in the gaseous state under standard conditions. Units: kJ mol⁻¹. Larger bond enthalpy = stronger bond. For **diatomic molecules** the value is precise and unique (e.g., H−H = 435.8 kJ mol⁻¹; N≡N = 946 kJ mol⁻¹). For  **polyatomic molecules** , successive bond dissociation enthalpies differ due to changing chemical environment — hence the concept of **mean (average) bond enthalpy** is used. Mean bond enthalpy = total bond dissociation enthalpy / number of bonds broken. Related: Bond order, Bond length, Mean bond enthalpy. *(Board/NEET)*

**Bond Length**
The **equilibrium internuclear distance** between two bonded atoms in a molecule — the distance at which the potential energy of the bonded system is minimum. Measured by X-ray diffraction, electron diffraction, and spectroscopic methods. For a covalent bond A−B: bond length ≈ rₐ + r_B (sum of covalent radii). **General trend:** Bond length decreases as bond order increases (triple < double < single bond). Also: atoms with larger covalent radii form longer bonds. Related: Covalent radius, Bond order, Bond enthalpy. *(Board/NEET)*

**Bond Order**
In the  **Lewis description** , bond order = number of covalent bonds (shared electron pairs) between two atoms: 1 for single, 2 for double, 3 for triple bond. In  **MO theory** : Bond order = ½(Nb − Na) where Nb = bonding electrons and Na = antibonding electrons. A positive bond order indicates a stable molecule; zero or negative bond order indicates an unstable species. **General correlation:** Higher bond order → higher bond enthalpy → shorter bond length. Fractional bond orders arise in resonance structures (e.g., 1.33 in CO₃²⁻, 1.5 in O₃). Related: Lewis structure, MO theory, Bond length, Bond enthalpy, Resonance. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Bonding Molecular Orbital (σ, π)**
A molecular orbital formed by the **addition** of atomic wave functions in the LCAO method: σ = ψₐ + ψB. It has energy **lower** than the parent atomic orbitals. The electron density in this orbital is concentrated **between the nuclei** (constructive interference), reducing nuclear repulsion and stabilising the molecule. Electrons in bonding MOs  **stabilise the molecule** . Related: Antibonding molecular orbital, MO theory, LCAO. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## C

**Canonical Forms (Resonance Structures)**
The individual Lewis structures that together constitute the **resonance hybrid** of a molecule or ion. Each canonical form has identical nuclear positions and the same total number of electrons, but differs in the arrangement of pi electrons and lone pairs. Canonical forms are hypothetical —  **they have no real existence** ; the molecule does not switch between them, and there is no equilibrium. The actual molecule is the **resonance hybrid** of all canonical forms. Related: Resonance, Resonance hybrid, Lewis structure. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Chemical Bond**
The **attractive force** that holds various constituents (atoms, ions, molecules) together in chemical species. Arises because systems tend to minimise energy — bonded systems are more stable than isolated atoms. Major types: (i) Ionic (electrovalent) bond — electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions formed by electron transfer; (ii) Covalent bond — shared electron pairs; (iii) Coordinate/Dative bond — both electrons donated by one atom; (iv) Hydrogen bond — weak electrostatic attraction involving H bonded to electronegative atom; (v) Metallic bond — sea of delocalised electrons. Related: Ionic bond, Covalent bond, Lewis structure, Kössel-Lewis approach. *(Board)*

**Covalent Bond**
A chemical bond formed by the **mutual sharing of one or more electron pairs** between two atoms. Each participating atom contributes at least one electron to the shared pair. The bond arises from the electrostatic attraction between the shared electrons and both nuclei. Types: single bond (1 shared pair), double bond (2 shared pairs), triple bond (3 shared pairs). First pair is always a  **σ bond** ; additional pairs are  **π bonds** . Related: Ionic bond, Lewis structure, Sigma bond, Pi bond, Hybridisation. *(Board/NEET)*

**Covalent Radius**
*See the Glossary in Chapter 3. Also relevant here:* In the context of bonding, covalent radius is half the internuclear distance between two identical atoms joined by a single covalent bond. It contributes to bond length calculations (R = rₐ + rB for a covalent A−B bond). *(Board)*

## D

**Debye (D)**
The unit of  **dipole moment** , named after physicist **Peter Debye** (Dutch, Nobel Prize 1936). 1 D = 3.33564 × 10⁻³⁰ C m (coulomb-metre). Dipole moments are typically in the range of 0−4 D for most polar molecules. Related: Dipole moment, Polarity of bonds. *(Board/NEET)*

**Diamagnetic**
A substance in which **all electrons are paired** (in molecular orbitals or atomic orbitals). Diamagnetic substances are **weakly repelled** by an external magnetic field. In MO theory, a molecule is diamagnetic when all its molecular orbitals are doubly occupied. Examples: H₂, N₂, F₂, He₂ (does not exist), Li₂, C₂. Contrast with paramagnetic. Related: Paramagnetism, MO theory, Unpaired electrons. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Dipole Moment (µ)**
A quantitative measure of the **polarity** of a polar molecule. Defined as the product of the magnitude of partial charge (Q) and the distance between the centres of positive and negative charge (r): µ = Q × r. Units: Debye (D). A vector quantity — conventionally, the crossed arrow in chemistry points from the positive end (δ+) to the negative end (δ−). For polyatomic molecules, the net dipole moment = **vector sum** of all individual bond dipoles. A molecule has µ = 0 if (i) all bonds are non-polar, or (ii) bond dipoles cancel due to symmetry. Related: Polar covalent bond, Polarity, Bond dipole, Electronegativity. *(Board/NEET)*

**dsp² Hybridisation**
A type of hybridisation involving **1 d + 1 s + 2 p** orbitals → 4 equivalent hybrid orbitals. Results in **square planar geometry** with bond angles of 90°. The d orbital used is one of the inner (n−1)d orbitals (e.g., 3dₓ²−y²). Found in transition metal complexes: examples [Ni(CN)₄]²⁻, [PtCl₄]²⁻. Differs from sp³ (tetrahedral) because the d orbital involved is an inner d orbital. Related: Hybridisation, Square planar, sp³ hybridisation, Coordination compounds. *(NEET/JEE)*

## E

**Electrovalent Bond (Ionic Bond)**
*See Ionic Bond.*

**Equatorial Bond**
In trigonal bipyramidal geometry, the **three bonds that lie in the horizontal plane** (equatorial plane), making angles of **120°** with each other. Equatorial bonds are **shorter and stronger** than axial bonds in the same molecule (e.g., PCl₅) because equatorial bond pairs experience less repulsion. Related: Axial bond, Trigonal bipyramidal, PCl₅, sp³d hybridisation. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Expanded Octet**
Condition where a central atom is surrounded by **more than 8 electrons** in bonding. Possible only for elements of the  **3rd period and beyond** , which have available d orbitals (3d) with energies comparable to 3s and 3p. Examples: PF₅ (10e around P), SF₆ (12e around S), H₂SO₄ (12e around S), IF₇. Not possible for 2nd period elements (maximum 4 valence orbitals: 2s, 2px, 2py, 2pz). Related: Octet rule, Limitations of octet rule, sp³d, sp³d² hybridisation. *(Board/NEET)*

## F

**Fajans' Rules**
A set of rules formulated by **Kazimierz Fajans** (1923) describing the conditions that increase the  **covalent character of an otherwise ionic bond** . The cation polarises the anion's electron cloud toward itself, creating partial covalent character. Conditions for greater covalent character: (1) Smaller cation size (higher charge density → greater polarising power); (2) Larger anion (more polarisable); (3) Higher cation charge; (4) Cation with (n−1)dⁿns⁰ configuration (transition metals) is more polarising than noble gas configuration ns²np⁶ cation of same size. Related: Polarisation, Polarisability, Ionic bond, Covalent character. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Formal Charge**
The charge assigned to an atom in a Lewis structure based on the assumption of **equal sharing** of bonding electrons: FC = (valence electrons in free atom) − (non-bonding electrons) − ½(bonding electrons). Formal charge is NOT real charge separation — it is a bookkeeping device to track electron ownership. The preferred Lewis structure is the one with the **smallest formal charges** (closest to zero) on atoms, with negative charges on more electronegative atoms. Related: Lewis structure, Resonance, Polarity. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## H

**Hybridisation**
The quantum mechanical concept of **mixing of atomic orbitals** of an atom with slightly different energies to form a new set of **equivalent hybrid orbitals** with identical energies and shapes. Proposed by **Linus Pauling** to explain the observed geometry of polyatomic molecules (e.g., tetrahedral CH₄ rather than the 90° angles predicted by unhybridised p orbitals). The number of hybrid orbitals equals the number of atomic orbitals mixed. Hybrid orbitals form stronger sigma bonds than pure atomic orbitals due to better directional overlap. Types: sp (linear, 180°); sp² (trigonal planar, 120°); sp³ (tetrahedral, 109.5°); sp³d (trigonal bipyramidal); sp³d² (octahedral); dsp² (square planar). Related: Sigma bond, Pi bond, VB theory, Molecular geometry. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Hydrogen Bond**
A weak electrostatic attraction formed when a **hydrogen atom covalently bonded to a highly electronegative atom (F, O, or N)** is attracted to another highly electronegative atom in the same or a different molecule. Represented by a  **dotted line (−−−)** . Weaker than covalent bonds but stronger than van der Waals forces. Strength depends on electronegativity and size of the electronegative atom: F > O > N (for atom X in X−H...Y). Two types: **intermolecular** (between different molecules — raises bp and mp) and **intramolecular** (within the same molecule — forms a ring, generally lowers bp relative to intermolecular). Responsible for the anomalously high boiling point of water (100°C vs −80°C expected for H₂S trend), secondary structure of proteins (α-helix, β-sheet), and DNA base pairing. Related: Electronegativity, Dipole moment, van der Waals forces, Polar molecules. *(Board/NEET)*

## I

**Incomplete Octet**
Condition where the central atom in a molecule is surrounded by  **fewer than 8 electrons** . Occurs for elements with **fewer than 4 valence electrons** (Li, Be, B, Al). Examples: BF₃ (6e around B), BCl₃ (6e around B), BeH₂ (4e around Be). These compounds are electron-deficient and act as **Lewis acids** (electron pair acceptors). Related: Expanded octet, Octet rule, Limitations of octet rule, Lewis acid. *(Board/NEET)*

**Intermolecular Hydrogen Bond**
A hydrogen bond between **two or more separate molecules** of the same or different compounds. Example: in HF, H−F...H−F chains form; in water, each H₂O forms up to 4 intermolecular H-bonds. Intermolecular H-bonds increase melting point, boiling point, viscosity, and surface tension. Related: Intramolecular hydrogen bond, Hydrogen bond. *(Board/NEET)*

**Intramolecular Hydrogen Bond**
A hydrogen bond formed **within the same molecule** between the H of one functional group and the electronegative atom of another group in the same molecule. Requires the formation of a  **5- or 6-membered ring** . Example: o-nitrophenol (H between −OH and −NO₂ oxygens), acetylacetone (enol form). Tends to **lower** boiling and melting points compared to the para-isomer (which has only intermolecular H-bonds). Related: Intermolecular hydrogen bond, Hydrogen bond. *(Board/NEET)*

**Ionic Bond (Electrovalent Bond)**
A chemical bond formed by the **complete transfer of one or more electrons** from one atom (usually a metal with low ionisation enthalpy) to another (usually a non-metal with high negative electron gain enthalpy). The resulting oppositely charged ions (cations and anions) are held together by  **electrostatic (coulombic) attraction** . Ionic compounds: (i) exist as crystalline solids (3D lattice); (ii) have high melting/boiling points; (iii) conduct electricity when dissolved or melted; (iv) are non-directional bonds. Stability determined largely by  **lattice enthalpy** . Related: Lattice enthalpy, Electrovalence, Fajans' rules, Covalent character. *(Board/NEET)*

## K

**Kernel**
In Lewis's model of the atom, the **positively charged core** consisting of the nucleus plus all inner (non-valence) electrons. The kernel is surrounded by the valence shell. The term was used by Lewis in his original description of atoms to distinguish the chemically inert core from the reactive outer shell involved in bonding. Related: Lewis symbols, Valence electrons, Lewis dot structure. *(Board)*

**Kössel-Lewis Approach**
The earliest electronic theory of chemical bonding, independently developed by **Walther Kössel** and **G.N. Lewis** in  **1916** . Lewis focused on the sharing of electron pairs (covalent bonding) and introduced Lewis symbols and Lewis dot structures. Kössel focused on ionic bond formation through electron transfer, relating it to the attainment of noble gas configurations. Both approaches are based on the **Octet Rule** (the tendency of atoms to attain 8 valence electrons). Related: Octet rule, Lewis symbols, Ionic bond, Covalent bond. *(Board/NEET)*

## L

**Lattice Enthalpy**
The energy required to **completely separate one mole of a crystalline ionic solid** into its gaseous constituent ions, infinitely far apart. Units: kJ mol⁻¹. Always positive (endothermic — energy input required). For NaCl: 788 kJ mol⁻¹. Lattice enthalpy is a measure of the **strength of the ionic bond network** in the crystal. It cannot be measured directly (due to 3D crystal geometry) but can be calculated indirectly via the **Born-Haber cycle** (studied in detail in higher classes). Higher lattice enthalpy → more stable ionic compound. Depends on: ion charges (higher charge → higher lattice enthalpy) and ionic radii (smaller ions → higher lattice enthalpy). Related: Ionic bond, Crystal lattice, Born-Haber cycle, Electrostatic energy. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**LCAO (Linear Combination of Atomic Orbitals)**
The mathematical method used in MO theory to construct molecular orbitals from atomic orbitals. The wave function of a molecular orbital = linear combination (sum or difference) of the wave functions of combining atomic orbitals: ψ(MO) = ψₐ ± ψB. **Addition** (constructive interference) → bonding MO; **Subtraction** (destructive interference) → antibonding MO. LCAO is an approximation (exact solutions of the Schrödinger equation for multielectron molecules are impossible). Conditions for LCAO: (i) similar energies, (ii) same symmetry about molecular axis, (iii) maximum overlap. Related: Molecular orbital theory, Bonding MO, Antibonding MO. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Lewis Acid/Base**
In the Lewis concept, a **Lewis acid** is an **electron pair acceptor** (e.g., BF₃, AlCl₃, H⁺) and a **Lewis base** is an **electron pair donor** (e.g., NH₃, H₂O, F⁻). This concept extends the Brønsted-Lowry concept and explains reactions involving molecules with incomplete octets. Related: Incomplete octet, Coordinate bond, Lewis structure. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Lewis Dot Structure (Lewis Structure)**
A structural formula in which **bonding electron pairs are shown as lines** and **lone (non-bonding) pairs as dots** around each atom. Provides a picture of bonding in molecules and ions in terms of the shared pairs and the octet rule. Does NOT represent actual molecular shapes (for that, VSEPR or VB/MO theory is needed). Constructed following systematic steps: count valence electrons, place central atom, distribute electrons to give octets to all atoms, use multiple bonds if necessary. Related: Lewis symbols, Octet rule, Formal charge, Resonance. *(Board/NEET)*

**Lewis Symbols**
Simple dot notations representing the **valence electrons** of an atom as dots around its symbol. Introduced by **G.N. Lewis** (American chemist). The number of unpaired dots indicates the covalence (bonding capacity) of the atom. Example: ·N̈· represents nitrogen with 5 valence electrons (3 unpaired + 1 pair). Significance: dots = valence electrons; group valence = number of dots OR 8 minus number of dots. Related: Lewis structure, Valence electrons, Kössel-Lewis approach. *(Board)*

**Lone Pair**
A pair of valence electrons that is **not involved in bonding** — present on an atom in a molecule but NOT shared with another atom. Lone pairs occupy more space than bonding pairs (localised entirely on one atom, not spread between two) → cause **greater repulsion** according to VSEPR theory. Examples: O in H₂O has 2 lone pairs; N in NH₃ has 1 lone pair; F in HF has 3 lone pairs. Lone pairs play crucial roles in determining molecular geometry, reactivity (Lewis base character), and polarity. Related: Bond pair, VSEPR, Lewis structure, Hybridisation. *(Board/NEET)*

## M

**Mean (Average) Bond Enthalpy**
For polyatomic molecules with multiple bonds of the same type, the **average of all individual bond dissociation enthalpies** for that bond type across various molecules. Used because in a polyatomic molecule, successive bonds of the same type break with different energies (the chemical environment changes as each bond breaks). Example: average O−H bond enthalpy in water = (502 + 427)/2 = 464.5 kJ mol⁻¹. Tabulated mean bond enthalpies are used to estimate enthalpy changes of reactions (ΔH ≈ Σ(bonds broken) − Σ(bonds formed)). Related: Bond enthalpy, Bond dissociation enthalpy. *(Board/NEET)*

**Molecular Orbital (MO)**
An orbital that extends over the **entire molecule** (polycentric), formed by the combination of atomic orbitals via LCAO. Electrons in MOs belong to the molecule as a whole, not to individual atoms. MOs are filled using Aufbau principle, Pauli exclusion principle, and Hund's rule — just like atomic orbitals. Types: σ MOs (symmetric around bond axis) and π MOs (not symmetric, with lobes above/below axis). Each MO can hold a maximum of 2 electrons. Related: Bonding MO, Antibonding MO, LCAO, MO theory. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Molecular Orbital Theory (MO Theory)**
A theory of chemical bonding in which electrons in a molecule are described by **molecular orbitals** (delocalized over the entire molecule), formed by the linear combination (LCAO) of atomic orbitals. Developed by  **F. Hund and R.S. Mulliken (1932)** . Key features: (i) number of MOs = number of AOs combined; (ii) bonding MOs lower energy than AOs; (iii) antibonding MOs higher energy; (iv) electrons fill MOs by Aufbau, Pauli, Hund's rules; (v) bond order = ½(Nb − Na). Major triumph over VB theory: correctly explains  **paramagnetism of O₂** . Related: VB theory, LCAO, Bond order, Antibonding MO. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## N

**Non-polar Covalent Bond**
A covalent bond between **identical atoms** or atoms of equal electronegativity, in which the shared electron pair is located **exactly midway** between the two nuclei. Results in zero charge separation (µ = 0 for the bond). Examples: H−H, Cl−Cl, N≡N, C−C (in diamond). Related: Polar covalent bond, Electronegativity, Dipole moment. *(Board)*

## O

**Octet Rule**
The rule stating that atoms tend to gain, lose, or share electrons to achieve a  **configuration of 8 electrons in their valence shell** , analogous to the nearest noble gas configuration. Proposed by Kössel and Lewis (1916). Based on the unusual chemical stability of noble gases. Exception for hydrogen and helium: these follow the **duplet rule** (2 electrons). Three exceptions to octet rule: incomplete octet (BF₃), odd-electron molecules (NO, NO₂), expanded octet (PCl₅, SF₆). Related: Lewis structure, Ionic bond, Covalent bond, Noble gases, Limitations of octet rule. *(Board/NEET)*

**Orbital Overlap**
The partial interpenetration of atomic orbitals from two atoms when they approach each other to form a chemical bond. **Positive overlap** (same phase/sign): leads to a bonding interaction → increases electron density between nuclei → lowers energy. **Negative overlap** (opposite phase): antibonding interaction. **Zero overlap** (perpendicular orientation): no bonding. Greater the extent of overlap → stronger the bond. The type of overlap determines the type of bond: head-on → σ bond; sideways → π bond. Related: Sigma bond, Pi bond, VB theory, Hybridisation. *(NEET/JEE)*

---

## P

**Paramagnetic**
A substance with **one or more unpaired electrons** (in molecular orbitals). Paramagnetic substances are attracted by an external magnetic field. In MO theory, a molecule is paramagnetic when at least one MO is singly occupied. Famous example: **O₂** is paramagnetic (predicted by MO theory — a key triumph) because its two degenerate π*2p orbitals each contain one electron. Related: Diamagnetic, Unpaired electrons, MO theory. *(NEET/JEE)*

**Pi (π) Bond**
A covalent bond formed by the **lateral (sideways) overlap** of two p orbitals from adjacent atoms, with their axes  **parallel to each other and perpendicular to the internuclear axis** . The resulting electron density forms two saucer-shaped lobes **above and below** the molecular plane. π bonds are **weaker** than σ bonds (less orbital overlap). Always formed in **addition to σ bonds** in double and triple bonds (σ is always the first bond formed). π bonds restrict rotation about the bond axis — rotation would break the parallel p-orbital alignment. Related: Sigma bond, Covalent bond, Hybridisation, Multiple bonds. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Polar Covalent Bond**
A covalent bond between atoms of  **different electronegativities** , in which the shared electron pair is displaced toward the  **more electronegative atom** . Results in partial charges: δ+ on less electronegative atom, δ− on more electronegative atom. Creates a  **bond dipole** . Degree of polarity increases with electronegativity difference. Examples: H−F (most polar), H−Cl, H−O, C−O. Related: Electronegativity, Dipole moment, Non-polar covalent bond. *(Board/NEET)*

## R

**Resonance**
A concept introduced to describe molecules that cannot be accurately depicted by a  **single Lewis structure** . Multiple equivalent Lewis structures ( **canonical forms** ) are written for the same molecule, differing only in the positions of π electrons and lone pairs (not atom positions). The actual molecule is the **resonance hybrid** — a weighted average of all canonical forms — and has **lower energy than any single canonical form** (resonance stabilisation). Examples: O₃, CO₃²⁻, NO₃⁻, SO₃, C₆H₆ (benzene). Key misconception to avoid: the molecule does NOT switch between canonical forms — it has ONE stable structure that is the hybrid. Related: Canonical forms, Resonance hybrid, Bond order, Delocalisation. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Resonance Hybrid**
The actual structure of a molecule that is described by resonance. It is a  **weighted average of all canonical forms** . Its energy is lower than any individual canonical form (extra stability =  **resonance energy** ). The bond parameters (length, order) of the hybrid are intermediate between the values in the canonical forms. Example: In O₃, the hybrid has equal O−O bonds of 128 pm (between O−O = 148 pm and O=O = 121 pm). Related: Resonance, Canonical forms, Resonance energy. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

## S

**Sigma (σ) Bond**
A covalent bond formed by the **head-on (axial) overlap** of atomic orbitals along the  **internuclear axis** . The electron density is concentrated along and symmetrically around the bond axis ( **cylindrical symmetry** ). Can be formed by: s−s overlap, s−p overlap, or p−p overlap (all head-on). Sigma bonds are **stronger** than π bonds (greater extent of overlap). The **first bond** between two atoms is always a σ bond. Sigma bonds allow free rotation (cylindrical symmetry means rotation doesn't change electron density distribution). Related: Pi bond, Orbital overlap, Hybridisation, Multiple bonds. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**sp Hybridisation**
Hybridisation involving the mixing of **one s orbital and one p orbital** of the same atom to form **two equivalent sp hybrid orbitals** directed at 180° (linear). Each sp orbital has 50% s-character and 50% p-character. Two remaining p orbitals are unhybridised and available for π bonding. Found in: BeCl₂ (no π bonds), C₂H₂ (2 π bonds per C), CO₂ (conceptually). Also called  **diagonal hybridisation** . Related: sp² hybridisation, sp³ hybridisation, Hybridisation, Linear geometry. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**sp² Hybridisation**
Hybridisation involving the mixing of **one s orbital and two p orbitals** to form **three equivalent sp² hybrid orbitals** directed at 120° in a plane (trigonal planar geometry). Each sp² orbital has 33% s-character and 67% p-character. One unhybridised p orbital remains, available for one π bond. Found in: BCl₃ (no π bond), C₂H₄ (one C=C π bond), graphite, benzene. Related: sp hybridisation, sp³ hybridisation, Trigonal planar geometry. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**sp³ Hybridisation**
Hybridisation involving the mixing of **one s orbital and three p orbitals** to form **four equivalent sp³ hybrid orbitals** directed at 109.5° toward the corners of a regular tetrahedron. Each sp³ orbital has 25% s-character and 75% p-character. No unhybridised p orbitals remain. Found in: CH₄ (4 bond pairs, tetrahedral), NH₃ (3 bond pairs + 1 lone pair, trigonal pyramidal), H₂O (2 bond pairs + 2 lone pairs, bent). Related: sp hybridisation, sp² hybridisation, Tetrahedral geometry, Hybridisation. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**sp³d Hybridisation**
Hybridisation involving **1s + 3p + 1d orbitals** → 5 hybrid orbitals in **trigonal bipyramidal geometry** (3 equatorial at 120°, 2 axial at 90° to equatorial plane). Found in PCl₅, PF₅. The d orbital involved is a 3d orbital of the same period. Related: sp³d² hybridisation, Trigonal bipyramidal, PCl₅. *(NEET/JEE)*

**sp³d² Hybridisation**
Hybridisation involving **1s + 3p + 2d orbitals** → 6 hybrid orbitals in **regular octahedral geometry** (all bond angles 90°). Found in SF₆, [CrF₆]³⁻. The two d orbitals used are from the same shell as s and p (e.g., 3d for 3s and 3p). Distinct from **d²sp³** hybridisation used in transition metal complexes where inner d orbitals (n−1)d are used (e.g., [Co(NH₃)₆]³⁺). Related: sp³d hybridisation, Octahedral geometry, SF₆. *(NEET/JEE)*

## V

**Valence Bond (VB) Theory**
A quantum mechanical theory of covalent bonding in which a bond forms when the **atomic orbitals of two atoms overlap** and the electrons pair up with opposite spins. Introduced by  **Heitler and London (1927)** , developed by  **Pauling** . VB theory explains: bond formation energetics (potential energy curve for H₂), directional nature of bonds, molecular geometry through hybridisation. Key limitation: **fails to explain paramagnetism of O₂** (predicts it should be diamagnetic). Related: MO theory, Orbital overlap, Hybridisation, Sigma bond, Pi bond. *(Board/NEET/JEE)*

**Van der Waals Radius**
Half the internuclear distance between two **adjacent, non-bonded atoms** of the same element. Always **larger than covalent radius** (no chemical bond → atoms farther apart). Relevant for noble gases and for estimating molecular sizes in non-bonded contacts. Related: Covalent radius, Bond length. *(Board)*

**VSEPR Theory (Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion)**
A model for predicting the **geometry of covalent molecules** based on the principle that electron pairs in the valence shell of the central atom repel each other and arrange themselves to  **minimize repulsion** . Proposed by  **Sidgwick and Powell (1940)** , refined by  **Nyholm and Gillespie (1957)** . Repulsion order: lp−lp > lp−bp > bp−bp. Multiple bonds treated as single super pairs. Does NOT explain the origin of repulsion or the energetics. VSEPR cannot distinguish between the energetics of different arrangements — it just predicts geometry from electron pair count.
